JULIA HART OPENS UP ABOUT EATING DISORDER DIAGNOSIS

AEW star Julia Hart has publicly revealed that she was diagnosed with Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder, commonly known as ARFID, and is using her platform to raise awareness about the often-overlooked eating disorder.

In a video posted to TikTok, Hart shared details of her November diagnosis and expressed her desire to connect with others experiencing similar challenges. She stated: "I just wanted to come on here and bring some awareness to ARFID disorder because I got diagnosed with ARFID in November and I just wanted to know if anyone else is going through that because I see a lot of people talking about anorexia, bulimia, and binging, but I don't see anyone talking about ARFID and I'm like, am I the only one here? Like, who else is going through this? How are they dealing with it?"

Hart explained that therapy has been instrumental in her understanding of the condition and identifying her personal triggers. She said: "I feel like through therapy the past few months I've really understood where my triggers come in and what has made my brain think this way."

She concluded her message by inviting others living with ARFID to share their experiences and support one another, saying: "So I just wonder if anyone else is kind of going through the same thing and what they're doing to help themselves. So just wanted to know if anyone is out there and wants to talk about it."

According to medical resources, ARFID is an eating disorder characterized by limited food intake that is not driven by concerns about body image or weight. Instead, it is often linked to fear or anxiety surrounding eating, such as concerns about choking or other adverse consequences. The condition differs from other more widely discussed eating disorders in that the restrictive eating patterns are not motivated by body image concerns. Treatment commonly includes cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), which appears to be part of Hart's current care approach.

ARFID is an eating and feeding disorder characterized by a significant restriction in the amount or variety of foods a person consumes. Unlike disorders that stem from body image or weight concerns, ARFID is typically driven by sensory sensitivities, a lack of interest in eating, or fears related to eating, such as choking or vomiting.

Hart's comments have drawn support from fans, while also bringing additional awareness to a condition that deserves more public conversation and understanding. Her willingness to share her personal experience contributes to reducing stigma around eating disorders and encourages others dealing with similar challenges to seek help and support.

Historical Title Changes

July 3rd has historically been a lot quieter of a day when it comes to title changes in pro wrestling than we've seen recently. According to historical data, only 156 major and notable title changes have occurred on this date throughout professional wrestling history. The earliest recorded July 3rd title change dates back to 1967, when Karloff Lagarde won the NWA World Welterweight Title. This marked the beginning of a long tradition of occasional championship switches on this particular date.

The late 1960s and 1970s saw several significant title changes on July 3rd. The All Asia Tag Team Titles were vacated in 1969. In 1975, Johnny Valentine captured the NWA United States Heavyweight Title (Mid-Atlantic Version). One year later in 1977, Ringo Mendoza won the NWA World Middleweight Title, continuing the pattern of championship activity during this era.

The 1980s and 1990s brought continued title changes on this date. Velvet McIntyre won the WWF Women's Title in 1986. The year 1987 proved particularly busy, with Jumbo Tsuruta and Tiger Mask #2 winning the AJPW/PWF World Tag Team Titles and Súper Halcón capturing the Mexican National Heavyweight Title. El Dandy won the CMLL World Middleweight Title in 1992, and Mano Negra won the NWA World Middleweight Title in 1993.

The 2000s brought some of the most notable July 3rd title changes in wrestling history. Billy Kidman won the WCW Cruiserweight Title in 2001. The year 2006 proved particularly significant, with Taiyo Kea winning the AJPW Unified Triple Crown Heavyweight Title and Edge capturing the WWE Title in a triple threat match featuring Rob Van Dam and John Cena. This WWE Championship change between three major stars stands as one of the biggest and most memorable title changes to occur on July 3rd.

The 2010s continued the tradition with both vacant title situations and new champions. The ROH World Title was vacated in 2013, and the TNA Television Title was vacated in 2014. In 2016, Pentagón Jr. won the AAA Latin America Title and Matt Sydal, Ricochet, and Satoshi Kojima won the NEVER Openweight 6-Man Tag Team Titles, bringing the recorded July 3rd title change history up to the modern era.

Becky Lynch orchestrated a special surprise for the newly crowned Undisputed WWE Champion Sami Zayn by arranging a reunion with his longtime friend Kevin Owens. The heartwarming moment was captured in an exclusive video shared across WWE's social media platforms on July 2, 2026.

Zayn and Owens have maintained a friendship spanning more than two decades, having first crossed paths at an independent wrestling promotion during the early 2000s. Lynch's own friendship with Zayn dates back to around 2009 when both were struggling to establish themselves in the professional wrestling industry. The trio has remained close throughout their careers, supporting one another through various stages of their respective journeys in WWE.

In the video, the three wrestlers were photographed together wearing Zayn's new championship t-shirt, commemorating his historic WWE Title win. The moment captured genuine emotion and camaraderie among the three friends.

During the encounter, Zayn opened up about his feelings regarding his championship victory and the reception he received from the WWE universe. "I'm floored by the outpouring support. I genuinely thought that six people like me and everyone else finds me annoying, and it turns out everyone loves me. I'm honestly shocked," Zayn told Lynch and Owens in the clip.

Following Zayn's Night of Champions victory, both Owens and Lynch were among the first WWE stars to congratulate and celebrate with the new champion. The support from his closest friends underscored the significance of his achievement within the WWE landscape.

Currently, Owens has been unable to compete in in-ring action since April of the previous year due to a neck injury. Despite his absence from active competition, he remains involved with WWE through the Legends & Future Greats program. Additionally, Owens recently made an appearance at NXT's Great American Bash, where he served as host for the Tailgate and Watch Party held outside the Performance Center, demonstrating his continued connection to WWE despite his injury status.

The Art of the Walkout: How Entrances Became One of Combat Sports’ Biggest Traditions

The Art of the Walkout: How Entrances Became One of Combat Sports' Biggest Traditions

Before a punch lands, the room has already picked a side. A walkout gives a fighter ninety seconds to look larger than life, calm a shaking corner, and make a rival wait under hot lights. It borrows from theater, street parades, club music, and national ritual. The ritual is loud, but it is precise. One song can say menace. Another says home.

Walkouts started as simple logistics. Fighters had to get from the dressing room to the ring without slipping, stalling, or losing focus. Then television arrived with close-ups, replay packages, and microphones near the ropes. Suddenly, the short trip became part of the product. Every step tells on a fighter.

Mike Tyson made silence feel dangerous because he did not need sparkle. No robe. No socks. Just black trunks, black shoes, and a stare that made the ring look small. Prince Naseem Hamed went the other way, with flips, thrones, magic-carpet jokes, and delays that made opponents burn energy before the first jab. Both choices worked because they matched the person. A fake entrance ages fast.

Promoters noticed the numbers. A strong walkout gives cameras easy drama, gives commentators a story, and gives ticket buyers a moment worth filming. It also buys time for officials to check gloves, mouthguards, Vaseline, and broadcast cues without making the pause feel dead.

Music turns a fighter into a flag. Nothing travels faster than a chorus. A crowd that cannot agree on a scorecard can still shout one hook together, and that shared noise gives an athlete a borrowed engine. There is a reason Conor McGregor blended "The Foggy Dew" with "Hypnotize." The first track pointed to Irish identity; the second brought swagger, bounce, and New York grit. Manny Pacquiao used songs tied to Filipino pride, so an arena in Las Vegas briefly felt like Manila. Israel Adesanya once walked out with dancers and anime references, turning niche taste into a public signature.

Bad music hurts, though. A track with no beat leaves footsteps exposed. A song chosen only because it is popular can make a champion look like a guest at someone else's party. The best picks have a job: slow the heartbeat, poke the rival, or tell the upper bowl who deserves their lungs for the next half hour.

Clothes carry meaning before anyone hears the announcer. A robe can nod to old boxing royalty. A sombrero, lavalava, keffiyeh, or tartan can point to family and place. Face paint can work too, if the fighter owns it fully. Tiny details matter. The problem starts when a look feels rented. Fans spot costume-store confidence in seconds. A fighter who enters with twelve dancers, smoke, and gold shoulder plates still has to fight at the end of the ramp. If the first round goes badly, the entrance becomes meme ammunition by breakfast.

The smartest teams test an idea under stress. They ask how long the stairs take, where the camera sits, which hand holds the flag, and whether a mask blocks breath. They rehearse with gloves on. Simple wins more than people expect. A clean jacket, a family song, and a steady pace can beat a truckload of props.

MMA borrowed from boxing, then added its own accents. In Japan, PRIDE entrances felt like pop concerts, with raised platforms and booming drums. In Mexico City, a boxing walk can sound like a football match. At Madison Square Garden, the tunnel itself carries history, so even quiet entrances seem heavy. Screens changed the ritual again. A producer can plan the lighting, but the fighter's face determines the memory. The camera only catches what nerves reveal live.

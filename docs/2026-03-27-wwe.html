🔥 WrestleMania 42 Ticket Sales and Pricing

---

WWE is making an aggressive push to fill Allegiant Stadium for WrestleMania 42, with the company rolling out repeated rounds of discounted tickets as sales continue to underperform compared to last year's event. The latest promotional effort includes a 25 percent off sale for both nights of WrestleMania 42, marking the second major discount push in recent weeks following an earlier 31.6 percent discount campaign.

The discount strategy extends beyond WrestleMania itself. WWE has applied the 25 percent reduction across the entire WrestleMania Week programming, including the WWE Hall of Fame ceremony, Monday Night Raw, and Friday Night SmackDown. The broader promotional approach suggests WWE is taking a comprehensive strategy to drive attendance across all events surrounding its flagship annual spectacle.

Current ticket distribution numbers paint a concerning picture for WWE's sales performance. Night 1 of WrestleMania 42, scheduled for April 18, has approximately 39,866 tickets distributed, while Night 2 on April 19 shows 41,330 tickets out. These figures represent a significant 13.3 percent decline compared to WrestleMania 41 at the same point in the sales cycle—though this gap has narrowed from the previous 18 percent deficit before the two major sales campaigns.

The supporting events have struggled even more notably. SmackDown at Allegiant Stadium has only 5,810 tickets distributed, described as "a major disappointment," while Raw at T-Mobile Arena sits at 7,305 tickets. The WWE Hall of Fame ceremony, held in a smaller venue setting at Dolby Live in Park MGM, has 2,183 tickets distributed.

Despite the concerning sales trajectory, WWE remains confident in ultimately filling the venue. Industry reports project that both nights of WrestleMania 42 will surpass the 50,000 attendance mark by the time the event takes place. WWE is expected to announce final attendance numbers roughly equivalent to, or slightly larger than, last year's WrestleMania 41 despite the current shortfall in ticket distribution.

A telling indicator of softer demand emerges from the secondary ticket market. Entry-level prices on resale platforms show $205 for Night 1 and $211 for Night 2—representing a 23.8 percent decrease compared to the same period last year. Industry observers consider secondary market pricing a more accurate reflection of genuine ticket demand than primary sales figures.

WrestleMania 42 will take place April 18-19 at Allegiant Stadium in Las Vegas, marking the second consecutive year the stadium has hosted the event. The card features Roman Reigns challenging CM Punk for the World Heavyweight Championship in the main event, while Randy Orton pursues his 15th WWE World Title reign against Cody Rhodes.

Beyond the in-ring action, WWE has offered an exclusive seating experience with seats positioned directly on top of the WrestleMania 42 stage—a premium option that was also available during last year's edition. This premium offering represents an effort to attract higher-value ticket purchases to offset volume concerns.

🔥 Danhausen Cursed Trademark Filing

---

Danhausen has filed to trademark one of his most recognizable catchphrases, securing legal protection for "You Are Cursed!" with the United States Patent and Trademark Office.

On March 26, 2026, Danhausen applied to trademark "You Are Cursed!" for merchandise and entertainment purposes. The application was filed under his real name, Donovan Danhausen, as an individual, with Michael E. Dockins listed as his attorney. According to the USPTO filing, Danhausen claims first use of the phrase anywhere and in commerce both date back to December 2019.

The trademark application covers two primary categories of goods and services. Under IC 025, the filing includes apparel items: Shirts; Sweatshirts; Hooded sweatshirts; Hats; Bandanas; and Socks. Under IC 041, the application encompasses entertainment services including providing wrestling news and information via a global computer network; wrestling exhibits and performances by a professional wrestler and entertainer; wrestling contests; televised appearances by a professional wrestler and sports entertainer; personal appearances by a professional wrestler and sports entertainer; live appearances by a professional wrestler and sports entertainer; and providing online interviews featuring professional wrestlers and sports entertainers in the field of professional wrestling and sports entertainment for entertainment purposes.

This individual filing is separate from several recent trademark applications submitted by WWE related to Danhausen. On March 13, 2026, WWE filed applications to trademark the terms Girlhausen, Ghoulhausen, Kidhausen, and Kid-Hausen. Those trademarks are listed to WWE, with Lauren A. Dienes-Middlen serving as the company's attorney on record.

Danhausen has built an extensive trademark portfolio throughout his career. He filed to trademark his ring name on November 5, 2020, and also trademarked his distinctive face paint at the same time. The following year, he filed to trademark the term "Love That Danhausen" and the phrase "Very Nice, Very Evil."

Danhausen's recent trademark filing comes amid a surge in popularity following his WWE debut. 🔥 Michael Cole WWE Commentary Criticism Response

**TL;DR:** Michael Cole has defended his repetitive commentary style, explaining that he deliberately reinforces key storytelling points because 90% of viewers miss subtle in-ring details. Corey Graves credits Cole with launching his commentary career and teaching him that silence can be more powerful than words, allowing crowd reactions to speak for themselves during significant moments.

---

Michael Cole has become one of wrestling's most polarizing voices, with fans frequently criticizing his tendency to repeat the same points throughout broadcasts. However, the veteran commentator is standing by his approach, offering insight into his reasoning during an appearance on The Sal Licata Show.

Cole addressed the ongoing complaint from viewers who feel he overexplains storylines and match psychology. Rather than viewing repetition as a flaw, Cole frames it as a necessary tool for ensuring WWE's narrative reaches the broadest possible audience. He explained that his role requires him to be fully informed about what's happening creatively, unlike some of his broadcast colleagues who prefer to remain in the dark about backstage decisions.

"I need to know because I need to be able to steer the audience in a certain direction of where our story is going," Cole said. He emphasized that this isn't about controlling viewer opinions but rather ensuring that creative elements land properly. "I don't want to think for them. I want them to make up their own mind, but I also want to make sure we're going down the path creatively of where our story is going."

Cole acknowledged the subtle storytelling elements woven into WWE matches — details that casual viewers might easily miss without guidance. According to him, many in-ring storytelling nuances require commentary to highlight them effectively. "There are these little subtleties in our matches that, if we don't point them out, 90% of the audience at home won't get it," he explained.

When confronted with social media criticism accusing him of "beating audiences over the head" with repeated points, Cole doubled down on his philosophy rather than backing down. "So we need to point out those subtleties. And fans say all the time on social media, 'Oh, you're beating us over the head with it! Why are you doing that?' Because 90% of our audience misses those subtleties."

While Cole's repetition strategy divides fans, his influence extends far beyond his own commentary work. Corey Graves, one of WWE's most recognizable current voices, credits Cole with fundamentally shaping his broadcasting career and teaching him one of commentary's most important lessons: the power of silence.

Graves revealed that his entire transition from in-ring performer to commentator began with a chance conversation with Cole. After suffering an injury that ended his wrestling career, Graves mentioned his past radio experience in passing. Cole immediately saw potential in him for commentary work. "He said, 'I want you to get in the booth at the PC and just try it out. Just see if you like it. See if you feel anything there,'" Graves recalled. That single suggestion set him on the path to becoming one of WWE's top broadcasters.

Beyond launching Graves' career, Cole taught him a crucial principle about live commentary: knowing when to speak matters just as much as what you say. Graves emphasized that reading crowd energy and understanding match psychology — skills he developed during his years as an in-ring performer — helped him recognize pivotal moments that deserve restraint rather than verbosity.

"The best indicator that something special is happening is just the crowd," Graves explained. "Michael Cole taught me to feel when to lay out — when to just say nothing — and to understand that as verbose as we tend to be and as many cool lines as I can come up with off the top of my head, there's nothing I can say that will top 10, 20, 60,000 people losing their minds."

Graves illustrated his point by comparing professional wrestling commentary to legendary calls in baseball, where the greatest broadcasters often allow the action itself to become the story. Once a moment's significance is established, adding more commentary can actually diminish its impact. "If you look at some of the greatest calls in baseball, once you establish the home run, you know that ball's gone. Generally, the great broadcasters won't say a word because you don't need to. Just feel the moment," he said.

He also acknowledged the challenge of resisting the urge to insert pre-planned lines during important moments. Even when broadcasters craft what they believe is the perfect quip or observation, forcing it into a natural emotional beat often backfires. "Sometimes you may have the coolest line — 'man, I wrote this thing out and I'm going to say it at this moment' — and if you try to force it, 99% of the time it feels forced and it won't live the way you want it to."

The contrast between Cole's philosophy and Graves' approach highlights a nuanced debate within wrestling commentary itself. Cole emphasizes clarity and narrative guidance through repetition, while Graves champions restraint and allowing organic crowd reactions to carry emotional weight. Both viewpoints reveal the complexity of real-time sports entertainment broadcasting, where commentators must balance storytelling, audience engagement, and authentic emotion.

Graves made clear that Cole's mentorship extended beyond launching his career — it fundamentally shaped how he approaches every broadcast. "Lo and behold, Michael Cole knew what the future would hold for me," Graves reflected, crediting Cole with seeing potential in him before he saw it in himself.

**Io Shirai vs. Meiko Satomura (World Of STARDOM Championship) - STARDOM Year End Climax 2015**

The ace of STARDOM taking on the ace and creator of Sendai Girls promised to deliver something special.

With companies like STARDOM, Joshi wrestling rose to prominence once again, with Shirai at the forefront of that resurgence.

In 2015, the World of STARDOM Championship was held by a true Joshi icon—the original final boss, Meiko Satomura. Satomura had taken the title from Shirai's close friend Kairi Hojo and later defended it against Mayu Iwatani, another close friend of Shirai's. Shirai sought to bring the title back to STARDOM, as Satomura primarily worked in Sendai Girls, the promotion she herself founded and where she reigned as world champion. The feud between these two icons needed resolution, and the date was set: December 23, 2015, at the legendary Korakuen Hall in Tokyo during STARDOM's final event of the year.

This match stands as an absolute classic. While not the easiest to locate—STARDOM lacked a proper streaming service at the time—finding it is like winning the lottery. Shirai refuses Satomura's handshake at the start, setting the tone for what unfolds as an almost teacher versus student battle. Satomura proves ferocious with her strikes, particularly her kicks, with Shirai selling them as if shot from a cannon. Yet Shirai never relents throughout, even when her aerial offense backfires, resulting in some brutal falls. These two competitors complement each other perfectly in a match that defined both careers.

Roman Reigns has revealed that his voice acting role in "Zootopia 2" presented unexpected challenges that left him questioning whether he'd continue pursuing voiceover work in the future.

He starred alongside CM Punk, who voices the character of Zebro Zebrowski. The WWE superstar explained that the role was significantly more difficult than he anticipated, primarily due to the unconventional nature of the script and the isolation of the recording process.

"This was probably the toughest thing of all of them," Reigns told Fallon. "You know how when you're performing, you need a little bit of feedback? We didn't have any lines for this. We literally said 'Zebros' the whole time. In two minutes, I said 'Zebros' 500 times, all different inflections, different deliveries, different ways."

The lack of immediate feedback from the production team compounded Reigns' struggles with the role. He noted that the recording session took place on a Zoom call with approximately ten people present, none of whom appeared to be actively engaged or providing guidance. Reigns contrasted this experience with his familiar environment in professional wrestling, where audience reactions serve as immediate validation.

"Nobody's giving me any feedback, there's like ten people on the Zoom screen all not paying attention. I'm used to, the crowd pops, oh it's good. We didn't get any of that. I thought I was just doing terrible. I was telling myself, 'I'm never gonna do this again probably, this is probably my last voiceover gig,'" Reigns explained.

Despite his initial doubts about his performance quality, the film ultimately became a commercial success. "It ended up doing great, did a great box office," Reigns acknowledged, indicating that his concerns about the final product were unfounded.

During the same interview, Reigns also praised actor Jason Momoa and suggested that the Hollywood star would be well-suited for a career in professional wrestling.

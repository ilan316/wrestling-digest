🔥 AEW MJF Interviews and Statements

MJF REVEALS HOW HE FIRST MET CODY RHODES

AEW World Champion MJF recently shared the story of his first encounter with Cody Rhodes during an appearance on the Shut Up and Wrestle podcast with Brian R. Solomon. The meeting took place at an Alpha-1 Wrestling event in Canada almost a decade ago, when MJF was still a young wrestler trying to establish himself on the independent circuit while Rhodes was already an established star in the business.

According to MJF, Rhodes initiated the encounter by playing a prank on him. Rhodes told the Alpha-1 Wrestling promoter, who MJF hinted may have been Ethan Page, that MJF was hitting on Page's sister. While MJF acknowledged the accusation was something he "wouldn't put it past" himself to do, he clarified that he actually was not pursuing Page's sister at the time.

The rumor eventually reached Ethan Page, who decided to confront MJF directly. Page approached him and asked, "Hey man, are you f***ing hitting on my sister?" MJF responded with confidence, saying, "Buddy, if I was, you would know, she'd be my girlfriend by now." When the two got into it a bit and MJF asked who had told Page this information, Page revealed that Cody Rhodes had started the rumor.

MJF then made a crucial decision. Rather than accept the situation like most 19-year-old nobodies in the wrestling business would have done, MJF chose to confront Rhodes directly. He walked into Rhodes' private locker room without knocking and demanded an explanation. "Why the f**k did you tell the booker I'm trying to bang his sister?" MJF asked. Rhodes initially denied knowing what MJF was talking about, but when MJF threatened to bring Ethan Page down to the locker room to confirm the story, Rhodes burst out laughing.

What could have been a tense confrontation turned into a friendly conversation. Rhodes was impressed by MJF's boldness and confidence in standing up to him, especially given the significant difference in their status at the time. From that moment forward, the two began a positive relationship. MJF described Rhodes as someone who loves a good lie and admires those who are willing to stand up to him directly.

This initial interaction proved to be a turning point for MJF's career. A couple of weeks later, they ran into each other at a Northeast Wrestling event promoted by Mike Lombardi. Rhodes was surprised to see that MJF was booked on numerous shows across the independent circuit. Rhodes' reaction to MJF's sudden visibility on multiple promotions, combined with their earlier interaction, directly led to MJF being booked for the inaugural All In event. MJF credits Rhodes with helping him gain opportunities during his rise through the independent wrestling scene.

MJF ON AEW'S DIVERSE ROSTER

Beyond discussing his origin story with Cody Rhodes, MJF used the podcast appearance to praise AEW's roster depth and the variety of talent within the company. MJF stated that he genuinely believes All Elite Wrestling has the best in-ring roster bell-to-bell in professional wrestling. More importantly, he emphasized that AEW offers "the most flavors of ice cream," referring to the diversity of performers, characters, and wrestling styles represented on the promotion's roster.

MJF expressed pride in being part of AEW, contrasting the company's approach with other wrestling products that he feels are "homogenized and same-same and copy-paste, and it's boring." He noted that when watching AEW programming, he is reminded of the territorial wrestling era, when every match on a card was drastically different with drastically different characters.

To illustrate his point, MJF specifically cited Mid-South Wrestling as his favorite territory. He explained that territorial wrestling appealed to him because of the wide range of characters and match types presented throughout cards. He said, "If you ask me what my favorite territory is, gun to my head, I'm probably saying Mid-South." MJF acknowledged that not everyone may agree with his assessment but emphasized that AEW's ability to present a wide range of characters and match types is one of the primary reasons he enjoys being part of the company.

Interestingly, MJF also addressed critic Jim Cornette's well-documented skepticism of AEW programming. While acknowledging that Cornette is not a fan of AEW, MJF found it interesting that Cornette doesn't seem to appreciate the variety that AEW presents, given the legendary promoter's own appreciation for territory-style wrestling. MJF concluded by stating that he personally does not find that AEW has many matches on Dynamite that feel repetitive or similar to one another.

THE PHYSICAL TOLL OF PROFESSIONAL WRESTLING

During the same podcast appearance, MJF offered candid commentary on the physical toll that professional wrestling takes on performers. He addressed criticism he had received earlier in his AEW career from fans questioning why he was not wrestling more frequently. MJF responded bluntly to these critics, challenging them to understand the physical reality of professional wrestling.

"I always laugh, especially earlier on in my AEW career when fans were like, 'Why isn't MJF wrestling more?' F**k you. You take a bump, p***y. It hurts. Do you have any idea how much it hurts?" MJF said. He went on to explain that every aspect of wrestling causes pain, not just the most dangerous spots or big bumps. "Everything we do in there hurts," he emphasized.

To illustrate his point, MJF disclosed that while conducting the interview, his knee was severely compromised. He said, "While you're interviewing me right now, my knee is f**ked. You know what I mean? It's swollen to sh*t. I'm in a sh*t ton of f**king pain." Despite these physical challenges, MJF expressed that he feels a responsibility as one of AEW's top stars to continue performing at a high level because people are counting on him to help ensure the company is doing well.

MJF was careful to clarify that he was not criticizing AEW's medical staff. He emphasized that a doctor would never clear him to compete if there were serious structural concerns with his injuries. "Obviously, a doctor is never going to let me go in there unless structurally I'm okay. So don't think I'm burying the AEW medical team. I'm in a lot of pain, there's swelling, but structurally I'm good," MJF explained.

He then addressed what he perceives as a generational gap in wrestling fandom. MJF believes that a newer generation of younger wrestling fans may not fully appreciate the sacrifices wrestlers make every time they perform. "There is this new wrestling fandom of younger kids that think this is all funsies, and they don't understand how much we're putting on the line physically every time we go through the curtain. It takes a toll," he said.

MJF also reflected on the long-term implications of his wrestling career. At just 30 years old, he is already experiencing significant pain and physical challenges from his years in the ring. "Dude, I'm 30. I'm in pain, and I'm only 30. I can't imagine what I'm going to feel like when I'm 40 or 50," he said. However, he made clear that he is not seeking sympathy and fully accepts the consequences of pursuing his dream. "But I'm also not asking for sympathy either. I'm not a b*tch. I fully understand I signed up for this sh*t. This was my decision and my dream to chase," MJF stated.

He concluded his remarks on this topic by reiterating that professional wrestling is far more physically demanding than many people realize. "However, I just wish wrestling fans understood more that every time we go out there, it's painful. It's not like it only hurts when we take a bump on the goddamn apron. It hurts every time we take a bump. No matter what, it hurts," MJF said.

BACKSTAGE ATMOSPHERE IN AEW

MJF also discussed the current state of the AEW locker room, describing it as the most harmonious environment he has experienced in his wrestling career. While acknowledging that competition among top talent naturally creates intensity, MJF emphasized that the level of animosity and toxic backstage politics seen in previous generations of professional wrestling is largely absent from AEW.

"What I will say is this, this is the most harmonious my locker room's ever been. But at the end of the day, this sh*t gets competitive, bro. It does. Some people handle it better than others. That's for damn sure," MJF said. He went on to note that while the competitive nature of wrestling remains, the atmosphere is nowhere near as nasty as it used to be in past eras.

MJF stated that he respects every person on the AEW roster unless they give him a reason not to. Specifically regarding talent in the conversation for main event spots, he expressed respect for everyone competing for the 1A, 1B, and 1C positions on the card. "There's no one on my roster that I don't respect, unless they give me a reason not to," he said.

MJF then discussed his fascination with wrestling's past while acknowledging his gratitude for the improvements in locker room dynamics. He said, "Whereas you hear so many stories from that bygone era, while I fantasize about that bygone era, and I can tell you emphatically, I truly believe, and I don't give a f*** if anyone has a problem with me saying this, you plot me into any era in any territory, I still become who I am now. I believe that in my heart of hearts. I really do. But I am so thankful that the level of chicanery of top guys trying to f***ing murder each other has really been flattened."

While acknowledging that some degree of backstage politics may never completely disappear, MJF expressed optimism about the overall improvement in wrestling's workplace culture. He recognized that some people in the industry may always be driven by unhealthy ambition and desire for more, but he views the current state as significantly better than what existed in previous generations.

WRESTLERS AS STUDENTS OF THE GAME

During the podcast discussion about old-school wrestling, MJF emphasized that he is not the only wrestler in the AEW locker room who studies wrestling history and applies historical lessons to contemporary wrestling. He stated, "I'll tell you, there are a lot of guys like me, especially in my locker room." However, he also acknowledged that not every wrestler on the AEW roster approaches the business from a historical perspective.

MJF argued that it is relatively easy to identify which performers have studied the industry's past and incorporated those lessons into their work by examining their body of work. He implied that the difference between students of the game and those who are not is evident when watching wrestlers perform.

🔥 AEW Cash Wheeler and FTR

CASH WHEELER REVEALS RANDY ORTON SPLIT AS THE FINAL STRAW FOR THE REVIVAL'S WWE EXIT

In a recent interview with LNG Productions, Cash Wheeler of FTR provided clarity on the exact moment that led him and tag team partner Dax Harwood to pursue their departure from WWE back in 2019. While many have speculated about the reasons behind The Revival's exit, Wheeler pinpointed the dissolution of their alliance with Randy Orton during WWE's draft as the pivotal moment that convinced them it was time to leave.

The Revival had begun working with Orton in August 2019, and according to Wheeler, the legendary wrestler was genuinely invested in the faction. Orton actively pushed creative to continue developing the group, and The Revival believed there was substantial potential for what they could accomplish together. However, WWE's 2019 draft would derail those plans entirely, sending The Revival to SmackDown while assigning Orton to Raw, effectively dismantling the unit before it could reach its full potential.

Wheeler explained the significance of this moment during the interview. He described Orton as the person he misses the most in terms of what could have been, emphasizing that he genuinely believed the three could have done something special together. The fact that Orton was actively advocating for the partnership made its sudden cancellation even more frustrating. When creative appeared to be fully behind the idea, having it killed by the draft felt like a clear message about WWE's broader creative vision for The Revival.

The strategic separation proved to be the breaking point. Even though Orton was engaged in his own storyline at the time—having recently faced Kofi Kingston—Wheeler and Harwood still believed they would eventually reunite and continue their work. When WWE made the decision to place them on different brands instead, it became unmistakably clear that the company had no significant plans for their future. The writing was on the wall regarding their ceiling within the organization.

Wheeler was unequivocal about how this moment led to their decision to request release. He stated that knowing WWE had killed the Orton partnership demonstrated that the company didn't really have any expectations or plans for them beyond their current standing. Once the draft separated them, Wheeler and Harwood came to the conclusion that their opportunities in WWE were severely limited, and it was time to explore other options. They subsequently requested their releases and departed WWE in April 2020.

Despite the disappointment, Wheeler expressed no regrets whatsoever about the decision to leave. He emphasized that he has never second-guessed the move, even for a moment. The subsequent move to AEW proved vindicated his choice, as FTR became integral to the new promotion's tag team division and captured the AEW Tag Team Championships multiple times. The decision to leave WWE ultimately opened doors that were never available to them in McMahon's company.

However, Wheeler did acknowledge a lingering sense of regret about not getting more opportunities to work with Orton. He emphasized that Orton remains a close friend to this day, and they maintain regular contact. Wheeler praised Orton as a genuinely funny person and expressed that he wishes they could have done more together professionally. The relationship transcended the business, and the friendship has endured even after their professional separation.

Beyond this explanation for their WWE departure, Wheeler also discussed other aspects of his career and personal philosophy during the interview. He reflected on his reduced social media presence, noting that he has become increasingly private over time and values the separation between his work and home life. He expressed frustration with internet trolls and toxic behavior online, displaying genuine empathy by saying he wishes negative people had better lives outside of their online personas.

Wheeler also addressed his future career plans, indicating that he doesn't envision a complete retirement from wrestling. Instead, he plans to have a television retirement, stepping away from major wrestling broadcasts while continuing to wrestle on independent shows that prioritize wrestling for wrestling's sake. He mentioned that after FTR eventually disbands, he may pursue some singles matches on the independent circuit, though he acknowledged his strong preference for tag team wrestling.

The story of The Revival's alliance with Randy Orton remains one of wrestling's notable "what-if" scenarios. The pairing lasted only a few months before WWE's draft dismantled it, but during that brief time, it showed promise. Wheeler's revelation about how this moment directly triggered their request for release provides important context for understanding one of the most significant talent movements in modern wrestling history. His decision to leave WWE ultimately proved beneficial not only for him and Harwood but also for professional wrestling more broadly, as AEW gained two of the finest tag team wrestlers in the industry.

🔥 AEW Owen Hart Tournament and Sareee

Sareee was scheduled to compete in the Owen Hart Foundation Women's Tournament at AEW Dynamite: Summer Blockbuster on June 10, 2026, where she would have faced Skye Blue in a first-round quarterfinal match. However, just hours before the show aired, it was announced that AEW's medical team had not cleared her to compete due to ongoing neck issues. The Japanese star had announced in May that she would take a month off for "neck maintenance," raising early questions about her tournament availability.

Takumi Iroha was expected to step in as her replacement, though Maya World ultimately filled the spot in the tournament bracket. Despite her inability to compete, Sareee remained involved with AEW during the event week, accompanying Iroha to the ring at ROH Global Wars and sharing a backstage photo with both Iroha and Tony Khan.

On June 18, Sareee addressed her absence through social media, expressing her disappointment to the wrestling community. She wrote: "I was very disappointed that I couldn't participate in this great tournament and I apologize to the fans who were excited for it. I definitely want to return to AEW. Looking forward to that day."

Khan's response came swiftly and carried an encouraging tone. The AEW President replied: "No apologies are necessary! Heal up, good luck in your recovery, and you're welcome to come back here anytime; the door to AEW is open for you!" His message made clear that while Sareee's injury forced her out of this particular tournament, AEW views her as a valued talent with a future in the promotion.

The medical decision to hold Sareee out, while disappointing in the short term, reflects AEW's commitment to wrestler safety and health protocols. Rather than risk further injury by clearing an athlete who was not at full health, the promotion chose to err on the side of caution. This conservative approach, combined with Khan's public reassurance, suggests that Sareee's AEW journey is far from over and that the promotion remains invested in bringing her back once she has fully recovered.

Shawn Michaels Opens Up About SmackDown Refusal

In a 2017 interview while promoting a comedy film, Shawn Michaels revealed he turned down opportunities to work on SmackDown broadcasts during his second WWE run, which began in 2002 after his initial retirement in 1998 due to a back injury. Michaels explained that SmackDown was taped on Tuesday nights, which would have conflicted with his wife's Bible study class—a commitment he refused to compromise. When WWE executives threatened to cut his contract in half, Michaels famously responded that he trusted God's plan for his finances and refused the offer. He told them "the good Lord's already decided how much money I'm gonna make this year. There's nothing you can do to change it." While Michaels initially resisted working SmackDown, he eventually appeared on the brand for a few notable matches toward the end of his career, including his final SmackDown appearance against Rey Mysterio in January 2010, shortly before his retirement match with The Undertaker at WrestleMania 26.

Miss Elizabeth's Broadcasting Career

WWE legend Miss Elizabeth, whose real name was Elizabeth Hulette, had a surprising career in sports broadcasting that lasted from 1993 to 1996. After retiring from wrestling in 1992 following her split from Randy Savage, Hulette spent two years working as a color commentator and correspondent for ESPN, covering offshore powerboat racing primarily on ESPN's SpeedWorld. A viral video from 1993 shows her hosting an ESPN boat racing championship in Florida. Her broadcasting stint ended when she signed with WCW in 1996 and resumed her wrestling career. Hulette tragically passed away on May 1, 2003, in Marietta, Georgia, where she was living with Lex Luger. Her death was ruled an accidental result of acute toxicity from consuming a mix of painkillers and vodka.

Wrestling Legacy and Retrospectives

When it comes to 1996 WCW PPVs, chances are the first one that will come to mind is Bash at the Beach 1996, where Hulk Hogan betrayed WCW and revealed himself as the nWo's third man. But for the most hardcore of WCW supporters, the best PPV of that year was the one that helped set the table for both Bash at the Beach and Fall Brawl, the 1996 Great American Bash.

Taking place just three weeks before Hogan's famous Leg Drop, the '96 Great American Bash has often gone forgotten based on everything else that happened that year. But in terms of wrestling and storytelling, it is arguably the best front-to-back show WCW produced during this era, and a massive step up from the Hogan vs. Dungeon of Doom PPVs from 1995 and early 1996. The show featured Rey Mysterio vs. Dean Malenko in an awesome match, Sting vs. Steven Regal in a delightful encounter, and a swell opening hoss battle between the Steiners and Fire and Ice, which consisted of Ice Train and Scott Norton. Not to mention one of the most famous angles in WCW history that set up the Bash at the Beach main event perfectly. The event demonstrates how much quality wrestling and storytelling the promotion was capable of delivering.

One of the weaker matches on the card was Konnan vs. El Gato. While Konnan was almost always a presence in WCW during his many years there, early 1996 seemed to be the time they were most behind him as a singles star, putting the US Championship on him and giving him an extended reign. This was one of Konnan's last defenses, and it fell short of expectations. WCW decided to put Pat Tanaka, a solid midcarder who was definitely not a luchador, into a Tiger Mask style mask, presenting him as the luchador El Gato. It was weird then, and it remains weird now, especially since it added nothing to the match. Even if it did, the chemistry between Konnan and Gato was simply not there. The match featured a few cool moves and flashes here and there, but overall it felt like a parody of lucha libre, and not in a positive way.

Another forgettable encounter was John Tenta vs. Big Bubba. Even with WCW on the upswing, they couldn't help but still produce matches like this one. The backstory involved John Tenta, who had been a member of the Dungeon of Doom known as The Shark, eventually leaving the group and starting to go by his real name, leading to a feud with Big Bubba.

In contrast, Chris Benoit vs. Kevin Sullivan stands out as one of the best matches on the show. This is a match that comes with significant real-life tragedy attached to it. Kevin Sullivan was accused of domestic abuse against his ex-wife Nancy in his life, while his opponent is Chris Benoit, who murdered his wife Nancy, his son Daniel, and then killed himself. That's not two different Nancys; it's the same one, as she left Sullivan for Benoit during this feud, yet another tragic element of this whole ordeal. All of this hangs over the match like a dark cloud.

But if one can somehow forget about all that for a moment, this is the best match on the show. It's a wild brawl complete with a detour to the men's restroom, and features commentators Tony Schiavone and Dusty Rhodes arguably having more fun than they ever had in their careers. The aftermath is even better, with Arn Anderson reaffirming Benoit's status in the Horseman with a post-match attack on Sullivan, and an incredible backstage promo that may be among Anderson's best work. It's terrific stuff from a pure wrestling perspective, but it's hard to enjoy it entirely knowing how everything winds up in real life.

On June 18, 1995, Sting won the WCW United States Heavyweight Title, and The Renegade won the WCW World Television Title. These championship changes occurred during the same period as the Great American Bash event and represented significant moments in WCW's ongoing storylines. The Great American Bash 1996 ultimately proved to be a crucial stepping stone in WCW's narrative arc during one of its most pivotal years.

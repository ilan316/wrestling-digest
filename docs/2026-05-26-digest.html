🔥 MVP Criticizes Triple H Over Bobby Lashley

MVP has opened up about the backstage frustrations that led to both him and Bobby Lashley leaving WWE, and he did not hold back while discussing his issues with Paul "Triple H" Levesque. Speaking on Marking Out with MVP & Dwayne Swayze, MVP claimed repeated attempts to communicate with WWE's Chief Content Officer went nowhere. According to MVP, those conversations—or lack of them—eventually convinced The Hurt Business allies that their future was elsewhere.

MVP directly addressed his feelings toward Triple H during the interview, saying, "I don't like Triple H, I think he's a coward and a liar. There were multiple times I tried to talk to Triple H, and he would always say, 'I'll get with you in a little bit.'" He also claimed Lashley struggled to get answers about his creative direction despite being a former WWE Champion. "Bobby couldn't get Triple H on the phone. Triple H wouldn't give Bobby any one on one time," MVP said. "Bobby wanted to talk to Triple H about, 'Hey, what's my direction? What am I doing here?' But Triple H always had something come up."

MVP expressed that professional communication was important to him, regardless of personal feelings. "Tell me how you feel. I'm a professional. Tell me how you feel, and we'll do business based on that. You ain't got to like me. There's lots of people throughout my career that I've worked with that I don't like. But we're making money, we're doing business." He characterized Triple H's avoidance as disrespectful, particularly noting instances when he managed to get Triple H alone in his office only to have the executive call someone else in, refusing to be alone with MVP.

MVP explained that the turning point came when he observed the broader pattern of how WWE was treating the group. The breaking point was when Shelton Benjamin, after 20 years with the company, was unceremoniously released without being offered a position as a trainer or producer despite being, in MVP's words, "one of the all-time greats" who is "under-appreciated and doesn't get the flowers he deserves."

At this point, MVP and Lashley still had contracts but were in different creative situations—MVP was paired with Omos while Lashley was working with The Pride. MVP made his decision clear about his future. With his contract coming up and Lashley's contract expiring at a similar time, MVP became convinced he no longer wanted to be with WWE, especially under Triple H's leadership.

MVP explained that those communication issues became the turning point while the group sat in catering waiting for direction. He recalled telling Lashley, "Dog, I'm not going to re-sign. Don't re-sign either. You, me, and Shelton should go to AEW. Let's go." MVP described consistently pushing the idea to Lashley over several weeks, saying, "I was in Bobby's ear every week. 'Bobby, let's go, dog. Let's get Shelton and head across the street.'"

According to MVP, Lashley initially hesitated because WWE was paying him well and he was at a level where he could show up and collect significant money. However, Lashley eventually came around to MVP's perspective. "Bobby started to kind of see the environment for what it was, and he was like, 'Yeah, P, you right. When our contracts run out, let's stop re-signing. Let's get with Shelton. And let's take this show up across the street.'"

MVP also pushed back on suggestions that WWE chose to move on from them. "So my contract ran out. Bobby's contract ran out. We chose not to re-sign. Contrary to what some people might say, that was our decision." He emphasized that other performers had all the access they wanted to Triple H, which made the disparity in treatment toward Lashley even more glaring. Lashley, despite being a two-time WWE champion and one of the most decorated champions in wrestling, could not secure one-on-one time with the Chief Content Officer to discuss his creative direction.

MVP noted that he had already filed the trademark for "The Hurt Syndicate" before the group was even free of their WWE contracts. Once their deals expired and they made the decision not to re-sign, all it took was a call to Tony Khan to make the move to AEW official.

The trio later reunited in AEW as The Hurt Syndicate, proving the group still had plenty left in the tank and seemingly vindicated in their decision to leave WWE. MVP's candid revelations provide insight into the complexities of backstage politics and talent relations within WWE under Triple H's current leadership, showing that the narrative goes beyond just what is presented in the ring. The wrestling world has seen numerous departures fueled by creative frustrations, but this episode highlights how personal conflicts and communication breakdowns can contribute to high-profile talent leaving major promotions.

🔥 AEW Stadium Stampede Production and Jack Perry Bus Crash

Jack Perry's wild bus crash at AEW Double or Nothing was confirmed to be a legitimate live stunt rather than a pre-taped segment. The incident occurred during the Stadium Stampede match, one of the highlights of the pay-per-view event. Perry drove a bus through the parking lot of the arena, where he appeared to mow down Mark Davis before crashing through a golf cart and into a parked car.

The over-the-top nature of the stunt immediately sparked speculation online about whether AEW had filmed the segment in advance. The dangerous spot took place outside the arena itself, which led many fans to question if it could have possibly been executed live during the broadcast. However, those theories were definitively put to rest when evidence emerged proving otherwise.

The confirmation came from multiple sources. Matt and Nick Jackson of the Young Bucks, who were featured wrestlers in the winning team, took to social media immediately after the show to address the production process. They stated: "Everything besides the entrances last night was shot live in real time. Kudos to the crew. We did it. Nothing but love." This statement directly addressed the question of whether any portions of the match had been pre-recorded.

Further verification came when fan footage surfaced online. Twitter user Joe Ferro shared video evidence and confirmation showing that fans inside the venue witnessed the crash sequence as it unfolded during the match. The footage showed someone in the rafters capturing the moment, with the fan posting: "Was up in the rafters but got to see this right behind my back, insanity..." This firsthand account from someone present at the arena proved conclusively that the crash happened live in front of the attending audience.

The Stadium Stampede bout itself featured a stacked lineup of competitors. Jack Perry teamed alongside Chris Jericho, The Elite's Kenny Omega, Matt Jackson and Nick Jackson of the Young Bucks, and The Hurt Syndicate duo of Bobby Lashley and Shelton Benjamin. The winning team defeated the combined opposition of the Don Callis Family representatives Andrade El Idolo and Mark Davis, The Demand faction members Bishop Kaun, Ricochet and Toa Liona, and The Dogs pairing of Clark Connors and David Finlay. Jericho earned the pinfall victory over Bishop Kaun, bringing an end to the chaotic encounter.

The match took place all throughout the arena facility. Although the action started in the ring, it quickly spilled to multiple locations. Combat occurred in backstage hallways, at the merchandise booth, up an escalator, in the catering area, on a practice tennis court, and ultimately in the parking lot where Perry's bus stunt took place. This scattered approach to the match design is consistent with the history of previous Stadium Stampede contests.

The bus crash instantly became one of the biggest and most talked-about moments from Double or Nothing. The combination of the dangerous nature of the stunt, its live execution, and the subsequent fan footage confirming its authenticity made it a standout segment. The Young Bucks' post-show comments acknowledging the crew's work in executing the entire match live in real time highlighted the impressive production effort required to pull off such an ambitious and unpredictable segment without pre-taping any of the action.

MyAEW Multi-Promotion Streaming Service

Tony Khan's long-term vision for MyAEW extends well beyond serving as a home for AEW programming alone. The streaming service, which launched earlier this year in partnership with Kiswe, is being positioned as a broader destination for professional wrestling content worldwide. During the media scrum following AEW Double or Nothing, Khan discussed the recent addition of 1 Fall Wrestling programming to MyAEW and indicated this is only the beginning of a larger expansion strategy.

MyAEW was designed primarily as an international platform, largely replacing TrillerTV for AEW viewers outside the United States and Canada. International subscribers can pay monthly subscription fees for access to AEW television programming and Ring of Honor content, with higher subscription tiers also including AEW pay-per-views. Within the U.S. and Canada, the service currently operates primarily as a FAST (Free Ad-Supported Streaming Television) channel featuring archival AEW programming alongside select free on-demand AEW and ROH content.

Khan explained that AEW wants to continue adding more wrestling promotions to MyAEW. During the post-show media scrum, he stated, "Yes, myAEW is primarily focused on international. But we do have the domestic service, and it has a lot of great content and we plan to feature wrestling from multiple promotions. We've just added QT's promotion, we have 1 Fall Wrestling now. But we're looking to add other promotions to myAEW and make it a hub for pro wrestling all over the world."

The addition of 1 Fall Wrestling was a natural fit given the promotion's ties to QT Marshall, who works both on-screen and behind the scenes within AEW. The Georgia-based independent promotion recently drew attention after Darby Allin made a surprise appearance for the company. The platform has also begun offering free on-demand access to 1FW events.

Khan also discussed making the streaming service more valuable for fans. "Of course, for a lot of fans, it's already become a great destination. It's been a key part of our international strategy. But also here in the U.S., there's great promotions we can make available on myAEW. And still, we have all of our focus here pushed to HBO Max and Warner Bros. Discovery," he said.

According to Khan, the service has already become an important piece of AEW's international distribution strategy, though the company also sees opportunities for growth domestically. The goal is for MyAEW to eventually become a centralized platform where fans can access wrestling content from multiple organizations around the world. MyAEW's website notes that an AEW podcast network is currently in development as part of this broader expansion vision. At the same time, Khan stressed that AEW's primary domestic streaming focus remains tied to its partnership with Warner Bros. Discovery and HBO Max.

🔥 John Cena Heel Turn Creative Discussion

John Cena has opened up about the creative thinking behind his shocking heel turn at WWE Elimination Chamber 2025, admitting that fan reaction ultimately pushed him back toward the version of himself audiences had known for years. Speaking during an appearance on The Rich Eisen Show, Cena discussed his approach to the villainous character and his philosophy on taking creative risks.

Cena's heel run became one of WWE's biggest talking points throughout 2025, particularly because he largely maintained the same presentation and demeanor fans had associated with him during his babyface run, while shifting his motivations and attitude instead. The turn itself generated significant discussion within the wrestling community, with the heel character eventually giving way to a return to his more recognizable fan-favorite role heading into SummerSlam and his high-profile showdown with Cody Rhodes.

When discussing his approach to the heel character, Cena explained that he intentionally approached the turn in a grounded and realistic way rather than dramatically reinventing himself. According to Cena, he chose not to alter his look, entrance music or overall presentation because he wanted the turn to feel authentic instead of cartoonish. Rather than portraying a completely different personality, he said the character was built around the idea of frustration after years of receiving such polarized reactions from WWE audiences.

"A lot of fans didn't, and honestly, talking about that—when I did turn heel, I tried to do it in the most realistic manner. There are many, many different ways I could have gone, but I kind of stayed as myself. I didn't change up the uniform. I didn't change up the music. And I started to try to find a real place of why I would choose a non-virtuous path. Me being frustrated with the audience after all these years of being such a polarizing character seemed like the strongest connection. And the audience got a taste of it and they're like, 'No, we want the old you back.'" Cena pointed out that he intentionally avoided making dramatic cosmetic changes to the character, instead focusing on the emotional reasoning behind the shift.

That reaction from the fans eventually became impossible to ignore, as many wrestling viewers quickly began gravitating back toward the more traditional version of Cena they had known for years. For years, many wrestling fans had pushed for Cena to embrace a villain role after spending most of his career positioned as WWE's top babyface. When the company finally made the change during his farewell run, however, a large portion of the audience quickly decided they preferred the older version of his character and wanted that version back on television.

Cena also reflected on his willingness to embrace creative risks throughout his career, noting that he rarely pushed back against storyline ideas presented to him. When asked whether he personally wanted to return to being a babyface once the storyline shifted again, according to Cena, he has always welcomed creative risks throughout his career and rarely pushes back against storyline ideas.

"I always like new creative challenges. That's why, very rarely—I honestly don't think I've said no to any sort of creative decision in WWE. This philosophy of accepting creative direction has defined much of Cena's approach to his career in professional wrestling.

Cena officially retired from in-ring competition in late 2025 following his loss to Gunther at Saturday Night's Main Event. Since stepping away from active competition, Cena has continued working with WWE in an ambassador-style role while balancing his growing Hollywood career.

During the same interview, Cena also revealed where he came up with the idea for The John Cena Classic, which he announced during his special appearance at WWE Backlash: Tampa. McMahon explained that the live audience inside the arena did not fully understand what had happened during the fall because WWE temporarily blacked out the live broadcast feed during the incident.

Had they seen, there was no question about it, you have to shut the show down. Those people came to see a show. They didn't come to see somebody die. McMahon also addressed the significant backlash he received after the decision, particularly from Owen Hart's brother Bret Hart. As they say, "The third time's the charm," and according to former WWE star Jimmy Wang Yang (James Yun), former WWE CEO and Chairman Vince McMahon was unaware of Yang's second release from his promotion. In his interview on "Insight with Chris Van Vliet," the former cruiserweight competitor recalled who informed him at first that he was going to be rehired in 2006.

"So, WWE SmackDown is in Cincinnati...in my hometown...I walk into SmackDown, and John Laurinaitis pulls me aside and says, 'Hey, Jim, come here. We're going to hire you again.' I said, 'Oh, really? Cool!' This is my third run with WWE...I want to go thank Vince," Yang said.

Later that day, Yang privately spoke with Mr. McMahon, who revealed in his own special way how he was uninformed that this was the third time Yang was on the WWE roster. That said, in this one-on-one meeting, McMahon instructed Yang of his new character, which included several guffaw laughs in between telling him of his new assigned role. Yang imitated each and every single laugh in the second part of his story.

"So, I go down to his office, knock on the door, 'Hey, Vince, it's Jimmy.' He said, 'Oh, Jimmy! Come on. Come on (imitates a deep belly laugh by the former chairman). You know, I didn't even know you were fired.' I was like, 'Really?'" Yang mentioned. "Then he said 'I got this idea. You know, last time I didn't let you talk because you're this Korean dude from Georgia. You know, you had a southern accent...This time...I'm going to turn you into the Asian redneck (imitates another McMahon-like laugh)...That's funny!' It was just me and him in the room, and I'm like, 'Man, this is f**king weird. Like, oh my God, this is out there.' McMahon says 'Your name is going to be Jimmy Wang Yang, because it rhymes (does the laugh again). That's hilarious!' I'm like, 'Cool, Vince. As long as that check comes every Monday, I'll do whatever you want.'"

The comedic boot scootin' cowboy would remain on the main roster until April 22, 2010, when he was released for the third time. He would briefly return for the fourth time in 2021 as a backstage producer, but that run ended months after he was hired back on. In this interview, Yang mentioned his new memoir that recounts all his runs in WWE, and where his career went after.

JBL Addresses Ludwig Kaiser’s Battery Arrest At Length: “I Think It’s Bullsh*t …”

Ludwig Kaiser, known to fans by his real name Marcel Barthel and recently performing as El Grande Americano, was arrested in Florida on a battery charge stemming from an alleged altercation with another male. The incident quickly became a topic of discussion throughout the wrestling world as details began to emerge.

During a recent edition of Something To Wrestle, WWE Hall of Famer and current Lucha Libre AAA commentator JBL addressed the situation involving Kaiser and shared his thoughts on the legal matter. JBL opened by dismissing both sides of the story, suggesting that elements may have been fabricated or exaggerated by the parties involved.

According to reports, the alleged victim made a comment about ICE to Ludwig Kaiser's girlfriend, which JBL viewed as a suspicious detail in Kaiser's account. JBL suggested that if he were facing arrest, this would be exactly the type of narrative he would construct to position himself as the victim. He questioned the credibility of the victim's version while also expressing doubt about the complete accuracy of Kaiser's account.

JBL elaborated on the circumstances leading up to the altercation, noting that a young couple was making out on an elevator when the other male apparently took issue with their behavior. JBL expressed confusion about why anyone would intervene or comment on their conduct, suggesting that the alleged victim bore responsibility for escalating the situation by inserting himself where he did not belong.

In JBL's view, the alleged victim then compounded his poor judgment by calling police after being punched rather than accepting the consequences of his own actions. JBL characterized the man who called police as a "douchebag" and criticized him for reporting the incident instead of letting the matter drop.

JBL made clear that while he does not know the absolute truth of what occurred, he believes the reality likely falls somewhere between the two conflicting accounts. He emphasized that both sides in such situations typically shade their versions of events in their own favor, making it impossible for outside observers to know exactly what happened.

Despite the uncertainty surrounding the facts, JBL expressed strong support for Kaiser, stating he is "100 percent behind Kaiser" and "unequivocally" backing the wrestler. JBL's reasoning centered on Kaiser's character and demeanor, noting that the WWE performer does not seem like a hothead and does not appear to be the type of person who would start fights arbitrarily or impulsively.

JBL concluded his remarks by expressing hope that the legal situation does not negatively affect Kaiser's career or professional standing. He suggested that Kaiser does not seem like the type of person who would engage in the kind of unprovoked behavior he was accused of committing. JBL stated that if something did occur as described, he believes the alleged victim deserved what he received as a result of his own instigation.

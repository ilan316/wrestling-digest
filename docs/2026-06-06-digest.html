WRESTLING COMMUNITY UNITES BEHIND REBEL'S ALS FIGHT

The wrestling world's support for Rebel continues to grow as she battles amyotrophic lateral sclerosis. On Friday, WWE Hall of Famer Mick Foley shared a photo on social media from a visit with Rebel, whose real name is Tanea Brooks, and AEW star Britt Baker. All three were wearing AEW's newly released "Rebel Heart" bracelets, designed to raise awareness and funds for ALS-related organizations.

Foley expressed his joy at spending time with the two, writing: "REBEL HEART! Having the BEST day, visiting with Rebel and Britt Baker – who are FORCING me to watch Love Island….which I'm kind of addicted to now!" The visit highlighted the strong bonds that remain behind the scenes within the wrestling industry, despite the serious nature of Rebel's diagnosis.

The 47-year-old Rebel publicly announced her ALS diagnosis last month after enduring a lengthy period of serious health issues during which doctors struggled to determine her condition. ALS, also known as amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, is a progressive neurodegenerative disease. Since sharing her diagnosis, an outpouring of support has come from fans and fellow wrestlers alike.

This week, Rebel announced that she has become an ambassador for I AM ALS, a patient-led organization dedicated to accelerating research, expanding access to treatment, and advancing policy changes related to the disease. She stated: "ALS may be a terminal diagnosis, but I'm showing up to fight as an Ambassador for I AM ALS."

AEW has launched a comprehensive "Rebel Heart" merchandise initiative, including bracelets and t-shirts, with all profits benefiting I AM ALS and Team Gleason, another charity supporting those living with ALS. The company's support effort has become more than merchandise—it represents a unified effort within the wrestling family to help one of its own during a difficult time.

Britt Baker, who had an on-screen partnership with Rebel in AEW, has been absent from AEW television since late 2024 with no official explanation. However, AEW CEO Tony Khan has indicated that the door remains open for her return whenever she is ready.

Foley's involvement with AEW extends beyond personal visits. MICK FOLEY DISCUSSES HIS MOVE TO AEW AND CONNECTION WITH TONY KHAN

Mick Foley has opened up about his decision to join AEW after his WWE Legends deal expired in December. The Hall of Famer described his new role as the "Swiss Army knife" of All Elite Wrestling, indicating his versatility across multiple functions within the company.

Foley identified AEW's stronger commitment to mid-card wrestling as a key factor in his decision. He noted that AEW does a better job with its mid-card divisions compared to WWE, explaining how WWE would often build tag teams over multiple years only to have them destroyed by two random main event stars, sending the message that the tag team division was never important to begin with. He stated: "I think there's just a little bit more pride" in how AEW handles its mid-card.

When tuning back into professional wrestling after a period away, Foley observed significant differences between WWE and AEW. He compared AEW favorably to his favorite family-owned theme park, noting that the company maintains love and magic in its product. In contrast, Foley suggested WWE has lost some of its magic since coming under Endeavor ownership and the subsequent formation of TKO in 2023.

"I think when you're part of a corporation and the bottom line is everything, you take away from your mid-card by making it clear that what you're selling is more important than the product in the ring," Foley explained.

Foley's first meeting with Tony Khan occurred approximately 15 years ago through a mutual connection to the Jacksonville Jaguars, an NFL team owned by Khan's father Shahid. According to Foley, a journalist friend introduced them, and Khan expressed that he was a fan. Foley recalled: "I said 'Yeah, sure,' and I haven't forgotten it. [Khan] has got that indomitable spirit."

Working with Khan has provided Foley insights into how the AEW CEO operates as a wrestling promoter and booker. Foley described Khan as "joyous," with a tendency to physically jump out from his seat while working backstage—a stark contrast to the approach taken by former WWE CEO Vince McMahon. Foley noted: "As a guy who always reacted better to a pat on the back than a kick in the butt, I would have really enjoyed that style."

Foley made his on-screen AEW appearance by co-hosting the Double or Nothing Buy In with fellow former WWE personality Renee Paquette. Later that evening, he crossed paths with MJF, who went on to defeat Darby Allin to become the new AEW World Champion.

Throughout his career, Foley has prided himself on standing out and highlighting what makes him different. Following his famous Hell in a Cell match against The Undertaker in 1998, Foley deliberately adopted a kinder, gentler persona to separate himself from tougher, more outspoken characters like "Stone Cold" Steve Austin. In AEW, Foley sees similar opportunities to embrace the different approach the company takes compared to WWE.

RICOCHET DEFENDS MODERN WRESTLING'S MULTI-FINISHER STYLE

AEW star Ricochet has engaged in a public debate defending modern professional wrestling's trend of multiple finishers and dramatic near-falls. The discussion began when WWE Hall of Famer Booker T and his Hall of Fame podcast co-host Brad Gilmore criticized what many fans refer to as "finisher spam," where wrestlers repeatedly kick out after taking finishing moves that traditionally would have ended a match.

Gilmore expressed frustration with the trend, stating: "The finisher spam, where you hit the guy with your finish three or four times and he kicks out every time—God, that bothers the hell out of me. I don't know. Maybe I'm just old school in my way of thinking." Booker agreed with the sentiment and suggested the trend has become a significant problem in modern wrestling.

Ricochet quickly responded to the criticism on Twitter/X, arguing that the style is rooted in storytelling and entertainment that influenced him growing up. He wrote: "Say you've never watched DragonBall Z without saying you've never watched DragonBall Z" and elaborated that the approach stems from "Drama, story, high level action, intensity, cliffhangers. That's what i grew up on."

When a fan argued that excessive finishers actually reduce suspense because audiences stop believing a move can end a match, Ricochet offered a different perspective on match psychology. He stated: "I disagree. It gives more suspense. Because in other promotions we KNOW the match isnt gonna end until someone hits their ONLY move that ends the match. So everyone is just sitting and waiting until that one move, knowing the match isn't gonna end until that happens."

Ricochet's comments reflect a growing divide in professional wrestling between traditional and modern views of wrestling psychology. Many veterans maintain that finishing moves should remain protected to preserve their impact and credibility. Supporters of today's style, however, argue that unpredictable kickouts and escalating drama create greater excitement and keep audiences guessing about match outcomes.

This discussion has become increasingly common across the wrestling industry as WWE, AEW, NJPW, and other major promotions continue to feature longer main events packed with dramatic near-falls and multiple signature moves. While traditionalists like Booker T and Gilmore believe the trend risks weakening the importance of finishing maneuvers, wrestlers like Ricochet see it as a way to increase suspense and create the kind of high-stakes drama that modern audiences expect from professional wrestling.

ANTHONY BOWENS REFLECTS ON SINGLES RUN CHALLENGES

AEW star Anthony Bowels has discussed his transition from tag team success to a divisive singles career. Once a marquee tag team star in The Acclaimed and multi-time AEW World Tag Team and Trios Champion alongside Max Caster, Bowels experienced a character transformation that included stints as the "5-Tool Player" and current involvement with Samoa Joe's stable, The Opps.

When discussing his character changes, Bowels explained that there was a specific creative direction being followed. He stated: "If you were actually watching the show, and not just, like, picking things up randomly, we were moving in a specific direction, where my character was starting to...it was losing a lot of matches and he was getting frustrated...The love was going to Max Caster and not to me. And then slowly, people weren't buying into this 'corny' 'Five-Tool Player' thing, which was by design."

Bowels compared his intended character arc to Rocky Maivia's transformation into The Rock in 1996. He explained: "You know, think Rocky Maivia in 1996, where it's like this hokey version of him slowly descends into The Rock, slowly descending into this character, this 'Pride of Pro Wrestling' guy, who goes, 'do all of this stuff for the industry and for the company. You should love me. You shouldn't love Max Caster...' How do I descend and fall off a cliff, and where do I get that adulation from? Never got to get to that point, creative changed."

Since joining The Opps with Samoa Joe, Bowels has gained renewed focus in his career. He has recorded one victory with Joe against The Lethal Twist, featuring Jay Lethal and Lee Johnson, and one loss to The Death Riders. Since his return last April, Bowels has maintained his eye on the TNT Championship, currently held by Kevin Knight.

Bowels expressed excitement about his new opportunity with The Opps, indicating that he is ready to sink his teeth into this direction for his character.

🔥 WWE vs AEW PPV Scheduling Conflict

WWE's decision to schedule NXT Great American Bash on June 28, 2026 at 7 PM ET represents yet another chapter in the ongoing competitive battle between wrestling's two largest American promotions. The event will air live on The CW Network for the first time in NXT's Premium Live Event history. However, the timing has drawn significant industry scrutiny because it places the show in direct competition with AEW x NJPW Forbidden Door, which begins at the same time on the same night.

Multiple industry sources confirm that this scheduling conflict was not accidental. WWE is known for being deliberate and strategic in its event planning, and internal reports suggest the company intentionally placed the NXT special against AEW's pay-per-view offering. Despite this clear positioning, WWE is expected to present the overlap as mere coincidence. This pattern of behavior has become increasingly common, reigniting debate over WWE's previous efforts to counter-program AEW events throughout 2025 and into 2026.

Dave Meltzer, a respected wrestling industry analyst, has questioned the logic behind WWE's decision in the latest Wrestling Observer Newsletter and on Wrestling Observer Radio. Meltzer noted that WWE attempted similar counterprogramming tactics multiple times in 2025 and on occasional prior instances, yet the strategy simply did not work. When WWE put both main roster and NXT shows against numerous AEW pay-per-views in 2025, AEW's shows not only performed better than expected, but WWE's competing shows actually underperformed.

A specific example illustrates this pattern clearly. During the All In: Texas weekend, WWE loaded the same market with multiple major events. The company ran an NXT show during the afternoon, followed by Bill Goldberg's retirement event later that evening, while also presenting the Evolution premium live event the following day. These shows took place around the same time as AEW's biggest event of the year. Despite internal confidence among WWE officials that AEW had been put in a difficult competitive position, All In: Texas ultimately delivered strong business results and was widely praised by fans. The numbers reportedly favored AEW considerably.

According to reports, Saturday Night's Main Event, which aired opposite these AEW events, recorded the lowest worldwide audience of any SNME special on Netflix, drawing approximately 100,000 views. Meanwhile, All In: Texas reportedly generated the fourth-highest pay-per-view buyrate in AEW history and finished as the promotion's top-performing pay-per-view event of the year. Despite these clear results, some WWE officials maintain the belief that continued counter-programming efforts could eventually have a negative impact on AEW's audience and overall business.

Meltzer questions why WWE would attempt this proven ineffective strategy again, particularly with a brand like NXT that is not as hot as it was two years ago. While Meltzer understands that on paper, running a Premium Live Event on free television against a pay-per-view that costs fans forty to fifty dollars should theoretically hurt AEW, he noted that all the strategy will do is draw direct comparisons between NXT and AEW that will not be favorable to WWE. Should AEW produce another show of the quality standards demonstrated at Double or Nothing 2026 or All In: Texas, these comparisons will only highlight the disparity between the two brands.

The scheduled landscape for June 28 is particularly complex because TNA Wrestling is also entering the mix. TNA's annual Slammiversary pay-per-view is scheduled to begin at 3 PM ET, significantly earlier in the day to avoid clashing with AEW and now NXT. Additionally, WWE presents Night of Champions from Saudi Arabia on June 27, creating a crowded weekend for wrestling fans trying to decide how to allocate their viewing time and money.

Meltzer believes that of the three wrestling shows scheduled for June 28, Slammiversary will end up taking the biggest hit from both a fan perspective and a logistical standpoint. WWE's decision to add an NXT show to that date could significantly hurt their TNA business partners. The move is particularly damaging because WWE will likely send little to no NXT talent over to participate in Slammiversary, whereas the partnership previously aided last year's Slammiversary and Bound For Glory events from a promotional standpoint. This withdrawal of talent support compounds the scheduling problem considerably.

The competitive dynamics create a difficult situation for casual fans. Fans of WWE who have watched TNA because of the partnership will likely choose to skip TNA and wait for NXT in the evening, especially with The Great American Bash airing on free television while Slammiversary exists behind a pay-per-view paywall. Casual fans will naturally prefer to save money and watch a show they have access to for free. However, some wrestling fans may experience viewer fatigue after already watching WWE Night of Champions the day before, potentially skipping both TNA and NXT entirely. This fatigue could hit NXT hard as well if fans watch Night of Champions and Slammiversary instead, meaning both shows could be hurt while AEW potentially gets to dominate the Sunday night and Monday morning wrestling discourse.

Meltzer notes that what makes the situation particularly problematic for TNA is that WWE has attempted to frame the scheduling as coincidental, though the frequency and pattern of counterprogramming makes it nearly impossible to believe this assertion. The repeated nature of this strategy across multiple years and multiple instances suggests deliberate competitive positioning rather than accidental calendar conflicts.

🔥 CM Punk Steroid Accusations

Recent photos and videos showing a noticeably more muscular CM Punk during his post-WrestleMania 42 hiatus have sparked widespread discussion among wrestling fans, with some social media users speculating about the reason behind his physical transformation. The conversation intensified after former AEW wrestler Ryan Nemeth appeared to take a shot at Punk on Twitter/X, leading to further debate about whether the WWE star's improved physique could be linked to performance-enhancing substances.

Former WWE star Ryback has offered his perspective on the matter, pushing back against the speculation and arguing that there is no evidence to suggest Punk is abusing steroids. In a statement shared via social media on June 4, Ryback emphasized that without direct confirmation from Punk himself or his medical professionals, any claims remain purely speculative. He stated: "There's been a lot of talk lately about CM Punk's improved physique and the usual speculation that comes with it. Is he on steroids? TRT? HRT? The reality is nobody knows except him and the medical professionals involved, if any."

Ryback went on to discuss the many factors that can contribute to noticeable physical improvements without necessarily involving performance-enhancing drugs. He highlighted that training heavier in lower rep ranges can add thickness and density to muscles, and that better nutrition, improved recovery, better sleep, lower stress, eliminating seed oils, and simply being more consistent can all lead to significant improvements. He stated: "When I look at Punk's physique, I don't see something that screams steroid abuse. I see a stronger, healthier version of his previous self. There are a lot of things that can create that result."

The former WWE Superstar acknowledged that treatments such as TRT or HRT are possibilities but emphasized that medically supervised hormone treatments should not automatically be viewed negatively. He explained the distinction between legitimate medical treatment and performance-enhancing drug abuse, saying: "Could TRT or HRT be involved? Sure, it's possible. But if someone has medically low testosterone or hormone levels, restoring them to healthy levels is about health and quality of life. It shouldn't be something people are shamed for. There's a big difference between optimizing health and abusing performance-enhancing drugs."

Drawing from his own experience in WWE, Ryback noted that he is familiar with the company's wellness policies and said he does not believe Punk is engaging in steroid abuse. Having been under the WWE Wellness Program himself, he stated: "I understand the rules and requirements. Personally, I don't believe CM Punk is abusing steroids. I believe he's likely made positive changes, and whatever he's doing appears to be working."

Ryback also criticized what he sees as a broader culture of body shaming within professional wrestling, arguing that performers are often criticized regardless of their appearance. He observed: "What I find interesting is how much body shaming exists in pro wrestling. People complain about Mason Rook because of how he looks. People complain about CM Punk because he improved his physique. It seems no matter what someone does, there's always a group looking to criticize."

He concluded by expressing support for Punk's apparent physical progress while referencing criticism he himself faced throughout his career. Ryback stated: "Personally, I'm happy to see him looking healthy and strong. And maybe now he understands why the term 'steroid guy' was never the right label for someone like me. I've been against steroid abuse since I was very young, but I've always been for optimal health and becoming the best version of ourselves."

At this time, CM Punk has not publicly addressed the speculation surrounding his physique. As with many discussions involving an individual's health or medical status, any claims regarding performance-enhancing drug use remain purely speculative unless confirmed by the person involved.

🔥 Indi Hartwell WWE Release and TNA Career

Indi Hartwell's WWE career came to an end on November 1, 2024, when she was released after spending five years with the company. Having signed in 2019, Hartwell spent her tenure primarily in NXT before eventually being let go. Following her departure, she has transitioned to TNA Wrestling while also pursuing independent bookings.

In reflecting on her post-WWE journey, Hartwell revealed that her longtime friend Steph De Lander, who was herself released by WWE in 2022, provided invaluable support during the transition. The two had known each other since their time together in Australian wrestling before both joining NXT. De Lander had previously received help from Matt Cardona and Chelsea Green following her own release, and she extended similar assistance to Hartwell as she adjusted to life outside WWE.

Hartwell described the adjustment to independent wrestling as a significant shift from her previous experience. She explained that working the indie circuit requires constant effort beyond simply traveling to shows and performing. The reality of being an independent wrestler involves a relentless "grind" that extends far beyond in-ring work. Hartwell spent considerable time packing and shipping her Micro Brawlers merchandise to fans while creating her own promotional graphics. She emphasized that the landscape of professional wrestling has changed dramatically since her last stint on the independents in 2019, with the emergence of new major wrestling companies creating more opportunities but also requiring more hustle.

Hartwell acknowledged the significant difference between her perceptions of independent wrestling and the actual reality. While many people assume indie wrestlers simply show up to events and go home, the reality involves promoting matches and appearances, managing merchandise, creating content, engaging with audiences on social media, and building relationships with promoters and fellow wrestlers. Beyond the business side, independent wrestlers must maintain their physical health and training regimen while managing personal relationships despite frequent absences.

The transition to TNA has exposed Hartwell to a drastically different professional environment than what she experienced in WWE. She noted that TNA provides opportunities to reconnect with wrestlers she had previously worked with while also collaborating with performers she had never encountered before. Most significantly, she highlighted fundamental differences in how the two organizations operate.

At WWE, particularly in NXT, Hartwell experienced extensive micromanagement of talent. She explained that in NXT, which functions as a developmental territory where wrestlers learn the fundamentals, every step is intentional and controlled. This level of oversight, while perhaps beneficial for training purposes, had a detrimental effect on her creativity. Hartwell felt that the micromanagement caused her creative abilities to diminish during her time with the company.

By contrast, TNA offers performers significantly more creative freedom, something Hartwell is still acclimating to even after spending roughly a year with the promotion. She acknowledged that while a year might seem like a substantial amount of time, it represents only about ten percent of her overall wrestling career spanning a decade. This perspective shaped her outlook on her ongoing development as a performer. She expressed confidence that there is still more growth ahead, more to learn, and more to accomplish as she continues her journey in TNA and on the independent circuit.

Hartwell also appeared on a recent episode of TNA iMPACT! where she teamed up with Santino Marella to compete against NXT's BirthRight, represented by Arianna Grace and Channing Lorenzo, demonstrating her continued involvement in wrestling despite her WWE departure.

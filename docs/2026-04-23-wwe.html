🔥 WrestleMania 42 General Coverage and Results

WrestleMania 42 Becomes One of WWE's Highest-Grossing Events

WWE's annual flagship event WrestleMania 42 concluded with record-breaking financial success across multiple categories. The two-night extravaganza took place on April 18-19, 2026, at Allegiant Stadium in Las Vegas, Nevada, marking the second consecutive year the venue hosted the event. Attendance across both nights reached 106,072 fans, with ticket purchases coming from all 50 states and more than 69 countries worldwide.

The financial backing from local authorities played a significant role in bringing the event to Las Vegas. WWE received a $6 million site fee from the Las Vegas Convention and Visitors Authority, an increase from the $5 million secured for WrestleMania 41 the previous year. Additionally, the company obtained $4,314,821 in tax credits from the Nevada Governor's Office of Economic Development, slightly higher than the $4,240,456 granted for last year's event. Combined, these incentives totaled more than $10 million in support for hosting the event.

The event was originally scheduled to take place in New Orleans before being relocated to Las Vegas, making Allegiant Stadium the first venue to host back-to-back WrestleMania events. WWE touted WrestleMania 42 as achieving record or near-record performances across gate sales, sponsorship deals, merchandise, WWE World experiences, on-location VIP packages, and digital engagement metrics.

Major Match Results and Storyline Developments

Saturday night's main event saw Cody Rhodes retain his Undisputed WWE Championship against Randy Orton in a brutal encounter. The match ended with an emotional twist when Orton, frustrated by his loss, delivered a vicious punt kick to Rhodes after the final bell. The blow resulted in a severely swollen left eye, with Rhodes requiring stitches to his face and four staples to close a wound on the back of his head. Despite the severity of the injury, medical evaluations determined Rhodes did not suffer a concussion or an orbital bone fracture, though he was placed in concussion protocol as a precaution.

Rhodes later shared an update from his doctor visit via Instagram Stories, posting an observation that captured the gravity of the situation: "You won't lose the eye... has to be the most positive yet absurd thing I've ever heard in a doctors office." While the exact timeline for his return to in-ring competition remained unclear, Rhodes was expected to continue his feud with Orton, though no match had been announced for the next premium live event.

Sunday night's main event delivered the historic moment of Roman Reigns defeating CM Punk to capture the World Heavyweight Championship. The victory saw Reigns standing tall at the conclusion of the show alongside his family, marking a significant chapter in his ongoing storyline dominance. CM Punk's return and subsequent championship challenge provided a major draw for the second night of the event.

Other significant developments across the two nights included Oba Femi's impressive victory over Brock Lesnar, which appeared to serve as Lesnar's retirement match, as he left his boots and gloves in the ring and embraced Paul Heyman while the crowd showed appreciation. Rhea Ripley reclaimed the WWE Women's Championship by defeating Jade Cargill. Trick Williams captured his first main roster title by winning the United States Championship from Sami Zayn, accomplishing the feat while wearing an eye-catching 80-pound, 100-yard-long mink coat for his ring entrance. During an interview, Williams acknowledged the weight of the garment, noting that carrying it down the ramp felt like a sled pull but was necessary to look good during his entrance.

Penta retained the Intercontinental Championship in a chaotic ladder match, while Finn Balor defeated Dominik Mysterio. Liv Morgan captured the Women's World Championship from Stephanie Vaquer, and Paige made her return to WWE, where she replaced Nikki Bella and teamed with Brie Bella to win the WWE Women's Tag Team Championships. Becky Lynch defeated AJ Lee to reclaim the Women's Intercontinental Championship.

Beyond the main event matches, the card featured several other notable performances. The Usos and LA Knight teamed up to face IShowSpeed, Logan Paul, and Austin Theory in a featured match. Drew McIntyre faced Jacob Fatu in an unsanctioned match that resulted in a significant victory for Fatu. Logan Paul later admitted via social media that he was "in the wrong" after attacking IShowSpeed during the match.

Broadcasting Success and Digital Records

The event's strong performance extended to its television broadcast. Night one aired on ESPN2 on Saturday and drew 1.62 million viewers despite facing heavy competition from NBA and NHL playoff games. The broadcast became the most-watched program on ESPN2 for the entire year. Night two aired on ESPN on Sunday and performed even stronger, pulling in 1.82 million viewers and ranking as the most-watched program on ESPN for the entire weekend.

Digital engagement reached unprecedented levels, with WrestleMania 42 generating more than 1.3 billion views across all social platforms, representing an 18 percent increase from the previous digital record set at WrestleMania 41. The event featured record-breaking merchandise sales and record or near-record performance at WWE World, the five-day interactive fan experience held in partnership with Fanatics. Paid experience sales reached all-time highs at WWE World, which also featured a premium lounge for attendees who signed up for Club WWE's Gold membership tier.

🔥 Ric Flair WrestleMania 42 Snub and WWE Commentary

Ric Flair's Recent WWE Controversies and Commentary

During a recent appearance on The Ariel Helwani Show, Ric Flair opened up extensively about his complicated relationship with WWE, addressing multiple grievances and offering candid thoughts on the wrestling industry's current state.

The most significant revelation concerned Flair's absence from WrestleMania 42. Flair explained that he did not receive an invitation to the Las Vegas event, which he directly attributes to an incident from 2025 involving Ludwig Kaiser. According to Flair's account, the situation stemmed from Tiffany Stratton making controversial remarks about Charlotte Flair's divorces on social media. Flair believes Stratton was not intelligent enough to have written such comments herself and suggests she was given a script. He also claimed that Kaiser made inappropriate implications about Charlotte on social media, stating that Kaiser implied interest in a one-off with her.

Flair said his response was to obtain Kaiser's phone number and call him with a direct threat. "Hey, when I see you, I'm gonna beat the shit out of you," Flair recalled telling the young wrestler. Rather than handling the situation privately, Flair believes Kaiser reported the threat to Triple H, which resulted in Flair's exclusion from WrestleMania 42. Flair expressed frustration over the situation, noting that a 24-year-old receiving a threat from a 76-year-old man should not have been taken so seriously as to warrant barring him from the event. He also made a dismissive prediction about Kaiser's wrestling future, suggesting the young competitor will "go as far in wrestling as a gnat" and will "be a fly on the wall in a year."

Beyond the immediate WrestleMania snub, Flair raised broader concerns about what he views as WWE's lack of respect toward his legacy and contributions. He emphasized that he believes his status in the wrestling industry should afford him special treatment, including a red carpet and front-row seating at major events. Flair stressed his substantial contributions to professional wrestling over many decades and expressed disappointment that his accomplishments do not seem to be adequately acknowledged by WWE management.

Another point of contention for Flair involved Dennis Rodman's induction into the WWE Hall of Fame. Flair claimed that Rodman personally called him and asked if he would induct him into the Hall of Fame. Flair said he passed this request along to WWE's decision-makers but never received a callback from the company. When Rodman was ultimately inducted on April 17, 2026, Sean Waltman and Kevin Nash performed the honors instead. While Flair stated he was happy for Waltman and Nash, he clearly felt slighted by not being given the opportunity to induct someone who had personally requested him for the role.

In discussing the broader state of WWE, Flair criticized what he sees as excessive politics within the organization. He noted that even though he was not present at WrestleMania, he remained aware of what transpired through his extensive connections in the wrestling world. Flair's frustration centered on the notion that someone of his stature should not have to deal with political maneuvering and should automatically receive the respect and consideration he believes he has earned.

On the subject of Charlotte Flair's performance at WrestleMania 42, Ric offered critical assessment. Charlotte competed in a WWE Women's Tag Team Championship match on Night One, teaming with Alexa Bliss against Lash Legend, Nia Jax, Brie Bella, and Paige. Paige had returned to replace the injured Nikki Bella, and Paige and Brie ultimately won the titles. Flair stated he did not like the match and believes his daughter should return to singles competition. He referenced Charlotte's match with Rhea Ripley from two years prior, which he characterized as unprecedented in women's wrestling history. He expressed the view that Charlotte should be featured in main event singles matches and in the title picture rather than in tag team competition.

Flair spoke positively about AEW President Tony Khan during the interview, calling him one of the greatest people he has ever met. More provocatively, Flair suggested that Khan could eventually purchase WWE given the Khan family's substantial wealth. With the $9 billion valuation of WWE, Flair noted that such a purchase would be well within the Khan family's financial reach. Beyond financial capacity, Flair praised Khan for creating opportunities for wrestlers who would never receive chances in WWE due to the company's selective nature. He highlighted Khan's fair compensation of talent and his focus on maintaining a happy workplace environment.

When discussing his relationship with Triple H, Flair acknowledged a complicated dynamic. While he noted that their relationship is good in terms of exchanging pleasantries, he also recognized that Triple H has been extremely instrumental in helping him at various points in his career. Flair specifically mentioned how Triple H brought him into WWE and helped restore his confidence through the Evolution stable after Eric Bischoff had damaged his career and self-assurance. However, Flair also acknowledged that their relationship is not what it once was and attributed this partly to the demands on Triple H as head of WWE creative.

Regarding Triple H's future, Flair pitched The Undertaker as a potential successor should Triple H ever step down from his position. Flair stated that if something were to happen with Triple H and The Undertaker was physically capable of taking on the role, Undertaker should become the next head of creative. When asked if he believed Triple H should leave WWE, Flair was quick to clarify that he was not suggesting that outcome. Instead, he referenced Triple H's well-known serious health issues and noted that nothing is guaranteed in life, implying that circumstances beyond Triple H's control could lead to him stepping aside.

Perhaps most notably, Flair expressed unwavering loyalty and gratitude toward former WWE owner Vince McMahon. He stated emphatically that everybody in the world who likes wrestling should be thanking Vince McMahon. Flair did not attempt to contact McMahon recently, as he felt that doing so would be wasting the former CEO's time. However, Flair made clear that McMahon knows how Flair feels about him.

Flair disclosed personal details about his relationship with McMahon, revealing that McMahon lent him $800,000 during a difficult period when Flair was managing three simultaneous divorces. Contrary to popular belief and how this loan has been portrayed over the years, Flair emphasized that the funds were not provided for tax issues but rather to help him navigate his personal crises. Flair further stressed that he repaid every dollar of the loan when McMahon told him he could write it off as a business loss. Flair insisted on repayment because he did not want to let McMahon down.

The repayment of this loan created significant financial hardship for Flair, as he had to pay taxes on the $800,000 as phantom income. This tax burden took him five years to overcome. Despite this difficulty, Flair characterized his repayment as the right thing to do and emphasized his integrity in keeping his word to McMahon. He even recalled that after his final WWE match against Shawn Michaels at WrestleMania 24 in 2008, he took his WrestleMania check, signed it, and gave it directly back to McMahon as part of his repayment. McMahon reportedly told Flair he did not have to do this, but Flair insisted, telling him he had promised to repay the loan in full.

Throughout the interview, Flair's comments reflected frustration with WWE's current direction and decision-making, nostalgia for earlier relationships in the company, respect for certain individuals like McMahon and The Undertaker, and a sense that his legendary status should insulate him from certain consequences or at least grant him more consideration and respect from WWE management.

🔥 Hulk Hogan Netflix Documentary Real American

The highly anticipated four-part documentary series on Netflix titled "Real American" premiered on the streaming platform and chronicles the career of Hulk Hogan while featuring what would be the wrestling legend's final interviews before his death. The production involved WWE, including current executive producer Lee Fitting and Triple H, and provides fresh perspectives on one of wrestling's most polarizing moments while exploring new territory beyond typical wrestling documentary fare.

The documentary examines the controversial finish of WrestleMania IX, which remains one of the most debated moments in wrestling history. During the main event, Bret Hart faced Yokozuna for the WWF Championship. Mr. Fuji, Yokozuna's manager, threw ceremonial salt into Hart's eyes during the match, creating an opening for Yokozuna to secure the victory and capture the title. What happened next changed the course of the evening and created a lasting rift between two wrestling titans.

After losing the championship, Hogan made his way to the ring, ostensibly to help the injured Hart. However, Hogan was quickly given an impromptu title opportunity and defeated Yokozuna to win the championship for himself. The episode shifts between Hart and Hogan's perspectives as they reflect on how the events unfolded, revealing sharply contrasting accounts of what transpired.

Hart recalled the backstage interaction that followed, describing a moment that convinced him his championship reign was already in danger. "I remember walking up to him, I was like, 'Hey Terry.' I stood up with my hand out to shake his hand for about 30 seconds but he wouldn't even look at me. He just kept talking to Brutus Beefcake," Hart explained. "I knew right then and there. I was like 'I've got the belt and I've got what he wants and I'm the enemy now.'"

When confronted with Hart's account, Hogan offered a different explanation. "I can't imagine doing that intentionally. Maybe I had an off day. Maybe I was drunk, maybe I was high. I don't know. I don't know what to tell you, but it wouldn't have been intentional," Hogan said. He further suggested that the decision ultimately came from WWE management rather than himself. "Vince made the decision, it's just one of those things," Hogan stated, while also claiming "I had that Hogan stroke back then. Me and Vince were just bonded at the hip and I could pull the plug on certain things and certain people."

Hart did not hold back in his assessment of how the situation played out. "That day I remember thinking what a step backwards. It was a whole different audience and they were liking me, not Hulk Hogan," he reflected. Hart then delivered his harshest criticism: "I said it's going to backfire and it's going to be really bad. F*ck you. Hulk came up with such a phoney, bullsh*t match. The kind of match that Hulk would dream up. Hogan came out like the hero. 'Yeah, he's my friend coming out to my rescue. Go get him Hulk. Go in there and win my belt. Win it for me.' It sucked. People would be going 'Why did they do that to Bret Hart?'. He was a good guy once upon a time, but in the end he was a backstabbing, knife-wielding piece of sh*t."

Hart also compared himself to Hogan in wrestling terms, stating "I was different than Hulk Hogan. Hulk Hogan was Elvis Presley. I was the Robert De Niro of wrestling." This comparison underscored the fundamental philosophical differences between the two wrestlers in terms of their approaches to the business and their roles within the company.

Beyond the WrestleMania IX controversy, the documentary explores significant personal aspects of Terry Bollea's life. The first episode reveals details about his family background, including a lack of apparent affection from his parents. Linda, his ex-wife, claimed that Bollea was an unplanned child, leading to a lack of compassion during his childhood. This potential early rejection may have influenced Bollea's later behavior in the wrestling business, where he was known for rarely putting others over despite being a cemented star. The documentary also reveals that Hogan had a brother, Allan Bollea, who was a biker and became estranged from the family before reconnecting with Hogan prior to his death by overdose while Hogan was on the road with the WWF. Hogan was visibly shaken when discussing his brother's death.

The documentary takes a harsh look at Linda Hogan, portraying her as interested primarily in material wealth and luxury. Most of the home movie footage shown features Linda discussing expensive purchases and the lavish home the family lived in during that era.

One of the most striking revelations in the documentary comes from Hogan's discussion of his time with TNA Wrestling from 2009 to 2013, following his divorce from Linda. Hogan joined TNA to stabilize himself financially after the divorce proceedings left him needing income. However, the physical toll of his years in the wrestling business made in-ring performance nearly impossible. Hogan detailed the severe physical consequences he endured, including the aftermath of multiple hip, knee, and back surgeries that made even basic routines difficult. At times, sleeping in a bed was too painful, forcing him to rest in a chair instead. Eric Bischoff had to assist him just to get out of bed and prepare for the day.

To manage his extreme physical pain, Hogan turned to fentanyl, revealing he took dosages that shocked medical professionals. "I was taking 80-milligram fentanyls, two in the morning, stuffing them under my gums here. I had two 300mg patches of fentanyl on my legs and they gave me six 1500mg fentanyl lollipops to eat. I went to the pharmacy, he goes, 'You should be dead. We have never seen a human being take this much fentanyl,'" Hogan disclosed. These revelations provide context for one of the darkest periods of Hogan's life, showing how the cumulative damage from his wrestling career affected him decades later.

The documentary offers what the reviewer described as fresh perspectives without relying on regurgitated footage or carbon copies of stories already covered extensively in other wrestling documentaries. This approach contrasts favorably with the previous Vince McMahon Netflix documentary from September 2024, which was criticized for being essentially a retelling of WWE history rather than a genuine examination of McMahon's life and mindset.

The documentary also includes perspectives from wrestling writer David Shoemaker, who supported Hart's view of the WrestleMania IX situation, describing the moment as a quick transition designed to move the championship back onto Hogan.

Overall, the four-part series provides an unfiltered look at both the professional wrestling career and personal life of one of the industry's biggest stars while serving as a final testament, featuring Hogan's last interview before his death in July 2025.

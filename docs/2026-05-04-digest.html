🔥 AEW Returns and Talent Updates

All Elite Wrestling delivered another eventful episode of Collision on May 2, 2026, from Peoria, Illinois, showcasing returns, emotional moments, and significant title action that will carry into the coming weeks.

The marquee return of the evening came when Colten Gunn appeared to support his brother Austin and their Bang Bang Gang partners Juice Robinson and Ace Austin during a heated confrontation with Jon Moxley and the Death Riders. The moment reunited all four members of the Gunns for the first time since Colten suffered a torn patella in July of the previous year. Colten revealed that doctors had told him recovery would take nine months to a year, but he declared "No chance!" to that timeline and made his triumphant return. In a backstage segment captured after the match, Austin Gunn emphatically stated "The Gunns are back," while Colten explained he returned early because his team needed him. Juice Robinson, meanwhile, kept his focus on an upcoming Continental Championship Eliminator Match against Jon Moxley announced for the following Wednesday's three-hour Dynamite and Collision special. Robinson made a bold statement, noting that he had defeated Moxley before seven years ago at Wrestle Kingdom and planned to do so again.

Despite Dorada executing high-flying offense including a 450 Splash and nearly connecting with a Shooting Star Press, Perry escaped the pin attempts and ultimately won with an avalanche hurricanrana from the top turnbuckle. This marked the first meeting between Perry and Dorada and was Perry's third successful National Championship defense since capturing the title at Revolution.

Women's division action featured Skye Blue earning her sixtieth AEW victory via submission, debuting a new finisher called the Descent Into Madness, which she executed on Nixi XS. The move is described as a dragon sleeper combined with a full body triangle. Jay's AEW contract expires soon, though there remains a possibility she could return full-time if she re-signs with the company. 🔥 Tony Khan and AEW Business Controversies

INTERNAL OPERATIONS AND LEADERSHIP CONCERNS

Industry insider Nick LoPiccolo has leveled a series of serious allegations against AEW President Tony Khan regarding how the company operates behind the scenes. According to LoPiccolo, who claims to be hearing directly from talent and AEW-connected sources, Khan's leadership style is defined by an avoidance of confrontation that extends far beyond simply being conflict-averse.

LoPiccolo described Khan as "allergic to conflict to the point where even if it's constructive criticism, he will ghost... he will ghost his own executives." He elaborated on this pattern, explaining that when talent or staff attempt to offer feedback that could improve the product, Khan does not engage in meaningful dialogue. Instead, LoPiccolo claims Khan responds with polite acknowledgment before disappearing from communication for extended periods.

The insider also accused Khan of refusing to accept responsibility when decisions prove problematic. "He won't ever admit that he's wrong. He won't ever accept responsibility... and he just digs his heels in," LoPiccolo stated. This unwillingness to acknowledge mistakes, according to LoPiccolo, has direct consequences for talent visibility and programming decisions.

LoPiccolo specifically connected this leadership approach to the extended benching of wrestlers. He claimed that unresolved internal issues and lack of direct communication lead to talent being kept off television for inordinate amounts of time rather than situations being addressed head-on through conversation. He suggested this represents a pattern where conflicts are avoided rather than resolved.

LoPiccolo also deflected criticism aimed at himself by claiming that Khan's paranoia level is actually higher than his own. He suggested that AEW leadership's response to outside pressure and information leaks is a more significant concern than outsiders realize.

MEDIA RIGHTS AND DEAL SHOPPING ALLEGATIONS

LoPiccolo's claims extended to AEW's current business situation following the Warner Bros. Discovery split. He alleged that privately, Warner Bros. Discovery views the situation differently than what has been presented publicly and that Khan has been acting as if everything is normal for the past seven to eight months when in reality things have been turbulent.

Most significantly, LoPiccolo claimed that AEW is not simply waiting for a media rights deal to materialize. Instead, he asserted that Khan has already begun shopping the company around in meetings with potential partners without yet securing any formal offers. "I've got confirmation that I think he's taken two or three meetings around town already, doesn't have an offer," LoPiccolo stated.

He also addressed the possibility of YouTube as a fallback option. While acknowledging that Google and YouTube might offer compensation, LoPiccolo expressed skepticism about the magnitude of such a deal. He claimed that YouTube would not come close to the figures that had been rumored for AEW's next media rights agreement. He was especially critical of earlier reporting suggesting AEW might secure five million dollars, calling this unlikely.

LoPiccolo's comments align with internal chatter suggesting YouTube has been mentioned as a possible landing spot within AEW if negotiations with other potential partners prove unsuccessful. However, this remains speculative at this stage.

PLATFORM RESTRICTIONS AND SOCIAL MEDIA CONTROVERSY

LoPiccolo's public claims about AEW's situation have been followed by restrictions on his social media accounts. He was locked out of his X/Twitter account a second time shortly after posting claims about Warner Bros. Discovery's timeline for notifying AEW about potential non-renewal.

Prior to the lockout, LoPiccolo posted a series of statements claiming that Warner Bros. Discovery had decided not to renew AEW programming months earlier and that AEW leadership had known about this decision since August. He called on journalists and podcast hosts to directly ask Khan about the timing of WBD's notification.

In his posts, LoPiccolo questioned why Khan had been "acting like nothing is wrong for the last 8 months" and challenged Khan to explain his actual distribution plan. He also made broad claims about other potential broadcast partners, stating that "the only distributor left that will take aew is google/youtube" and that AEW's internal streaming app would be "a death sentence in 2026 if youtube doesn't make an mg deal for aew."

He further claimed that other networks were no longer viable options for AEW, specifically naming Paramount, the CW/Roku combination, Amazon, and Fox as platforms that have either passed or do not want the product.

This marked the second time LoPiccolo's account had been restricted in a short timeframe. The first lockout occurred in January after he publicly accused Tony Khan of using burner accounts to harass him. Following that accusation, those accounts reportedly disappeared.

While there is no confirmation that AEW, Khan, or WBD was responsible for either lockout, LoPiccolo suggested the timing was suspicious and that platform enforcement or mass reporting could explain the restrictions. He responded to the second lockout by moving to Instagram and hinting at plans to discuss the situation further on a podcast.

DENIALS AND CAREFUL WORDING

After NFL insider John McMullen reposted his report about AEW's situation with an editor's note indicating that both AEW and Warner Bros. Discovery had denied the claims, LoPiccolo quickly responded with criticism of the denial's specific wording.

LoPiccolo focused on the phrase "pre-sale information" used in the denial, suggesting that the careful language itself raised questions. He noted that "they denied the one thing nobody normal says out loud unless a lawyer already got involved," implying the denial was lawyer-crafted rather than a straightforward refutation.

He continued his critique by comparing the denial to a flawed answer: "denying 'pre-sale information' is like asking if the house is on fire and being told the curtains are not technically in flames yet." This response suggested that LoPiccolo believed AEW and WBD were being technically precise in their denial while avoiding addressing the broader substance of his claims.

The back-and-forth between LoPiccolo's allegations and the official denials has created conflicting narratives that continue to circulate publicly. At this stage, the situation remains unresolved, with sources, denials, and interpretations continuing to clash.

BUSINESS AS USUAL MESSAGING

Despite these controversies, AEW has continued to present an image of stability publicly. Official messaging from the company has not acknowledged significant behind-the-scenes turmoil or complications with its media rights situation. However, remarks like those from LoPiccolo suggest there may be more internal discord than has been revealed to the public.

Talent in the company have generally maintained discretion about internal matters, though some public gestures have been made. Rebel, a performer with AEW, recently expressed gratitude toward Khan and the company after the AEW program Collision aired a supportive message for her following a recent health diagnosis.

AJ Styles Reflects on Career Path and 2002 WWE Decision

During recent episodes of The Phenomenally Retro Podcast, WWE Hall of Famer AJ Styles opened up about a pivotal decision that shaped his entire career trajectory. Styles explained his reasoning for turning down the deal, citing both financial and personal constraints. "It was for $500 a week, which is barely able to afford anything. He noted that he had already experienced the disappointment of WCW's closure and was cautious about his next move. Additionally, Styles pointed out that the WWE evaluators at the camp were dismissive of his high-flying style, telling him they didn't need to see that kind of offense.

Looking back, Styles expressed confidence that his decision to reject the contract was the right call. He revealed that HWA, which was WWE's developmental territory at the time, closed down approximately five months after his trial. "I guarantee you, I would have been shut out with them. This is before Daniel Bryan had showed up and kind of changed things as a guy our size. They really weren't used that well," Styles explained. Inside Look at Modern WWE Match Preparation

Styles provided educational commentary on how WWE approaches match preparation in the modern era, specifically addressing the common misconception that matches are "rehearsed." He explained that fewer live events have fundamentally changed how wrestlers prepare matches, making advance planning more necessary than in previous generations.

In past eras, matches were mostly "called in the ring," meaning wrestlers would work out sequences and psychology on the fly with only major moments and finishes planned beforehand. However, this approach has become increasingly difficult. Styles noted that without regular live event opportunities where wrestlers face the same opponents repeatedly, the chemistry and communication needed for in-ring calling is harder to develop. Additionally, modern television production demands more precise camera positioning for big moments.

When matches are rehearsed in contemporary WWE, Styles clarified that the actual wrestling moves themselves are not practiced. Instead, the focus is on "blocking" certain moments for television purposes. "Once you get to the ring, there will be some things we'll rehearse. And by 'rehearse' – we're not rehearsing the wrestling, we're not doing that. But we wanna see what cameras are going to be in the position that we're doing a certain thing so we can get the best shot. That's kind of what we rehearse. Or maybe somebody does a run-in. You kinda wanna see how everything's done so we can get the best shot. So those things happen," Styles explained.

This shift has both advantages and disadvantages. While fewer live events mean wrestlers spend less time on the road and more time at home, it can negatively impact match quality. When performers need to remember predetermined sequences and the crowd isn't responding as anticipated, wrestlers may struggle to adjust in real-time. This problem is particularly noticeable with newer talent who need more experience working with different opponents.

Long Title Reigns Impact on Modern Wrestling

In modern professional wrestling, dominance has become the standard. For the most part, champions are no longer meant to feel vulnerable. Instead, they are often presented as unstoppable forces who hold titles for months or even years. On paper, that sounds like a return to prestige. In practice, it has created a different kind of problem. Fans seem to think a title reign is a failure if not held for a lengthy amount of time. Long title reigns used to mean something special. They were rare and signaled that a wrestler had reached a level above everyone else. Today, they are far more common, especially at the top of the card. The question is no longer whether long reigns add value. It is whether they are starting to take something away. In WWE, it seems as if long title reigns are used as a way to re-write history and declare "A New Era!"

There is a reason promotions lean into long reigns. A dominant champion can elevate a title simply by holding it. When Roman Reigns carried the top championship for an extended period, it created a sense of importance around every defense. The title felt like the center of the show. A long reign also helps define an era. Fans can look back and associate a stretch of time with one central figure. That kind of consistency is valuable in a business that often shifts quickly. It gives viewers a clear top star and a clear goal for everyone chasing them. There is also the argument that modern wrestling needs fewer title changes. Weekly television and constant content can make championships feel less important if they switch hands too often. A long reign can counter that by restoring the idea that winning a title is difficult.

The downside is just as clear. When a champion holds a title for too long, outcomes start to feel obvious. Fans go into matches expecting the champion to win, not wondering if they might lose. It immediately kills curiosity. That predictability can drain tension from even the biggest matches on the card. It also highlights how different today's booking philosophy is compared to past eras. Some of the biggest stars in wrestling history did not need lengthy title reigns to feel important. The Rock, arguably one of the greatest to ever do it, had multiple world title runs, but many of them were relatively short. Titles changed hands more frequently, yet the championship still felt meaningful because the outcome was never guaranteed. Today's fans would not survive the wrestling world from twenty years ago. That sense of unpredictability made every defense feel urgent. A challenger was not just filling a spot. They had a real chance. Today, a challenger might be built up for weeks only to fall short in a result that feels inevitable. Over time, that pattern can make it harder for fans to stay invested.

Another issue is what happens to everyone else on the roster. When one wrestler sits firmly at the top for an extended period, it limits opportunities for others to break through. Challengers come and go, but few are allowed to truly rise. In earlier eras, even short title reigns could create new stars. A wrestler might win the championship briefly and gain credibility that lasted long after the loss. Now, with fewer title changes, those moments are harder to create. This can leave the upper midcard crowded with talent that feels stuck. They are presented as contenders, but rarely as equals. Over time, that gap becomes harder to close.

A noteworthy recent example is the Darby Allin title reign. At the time of this editorial, Allin has held the AEW World Title for two weeks. He has already faced two "mid card" wrestlers in Tommaso Ciampa and Brody King, and given them the spotlight in the main event scene, making them both feel like legit contenders. This demonstrates how title reigns can be used effectively to elevate the entire roster.

The solution is not to abandon long title reigns altogether. They still have a place in modern wrestling. The key is balance. Promotions must recognize when a reign has run its course and use title changes strategically to create new opportunities while maintaining the prestige of the championship itself.

Top 10 WWE What If Moments That Changed Everything

We all know professional wrestling is scripted. But its history is shaped by very real decisions, injuries and moments that cannot be undone. The bookers don't have a crystal ball. One wrong booking call or unexpected turn can ripple across years of storytelling and change how fans remember an entire era. WWE, more than any company, has built its legacy on these turning points. Looking back, it is hard not to wonder how different things might feel today if even one of these moments had gone the other way.

10. What if Nexus actually beat John Cena at SummerSlam 2010?

Nexus arrived as a fresh concept, but their loss to John Cena at Summer Slam 2010 cooled them off almost immediately. A victory could have established Wade Barrett as a defining villain of the era and given WWE a new wave of credible stars.

9. What if CM Punk never left in 2014?

CM Punk walked out in January 2014, just after the Royal Rumble, while still one of WWE's most authentic voices. If he had stayed, WWE might have handled rising fan frustration in the mid-2010s differently and leaned more into the kind of storytelling audiences were demanding. This would have a butterfly effect on All Elite Wrestling as well. The consensus is that he would have made the jump in 2019 out of frustration.

8. What if The Streak never ended at WrestleMania 30?

The decision for Brock Lesnar to defeat The Undertaker at WrestleMania 30 remains one of the most shocking calls in company history. Protecting The Streak could have preserved a once-in-a-lifetime attraction and saved that moment for a younger star who needed it more. Did Brock need it? Absolutely not. Would a Bray Wyatt victory have meant more? Without a doubt.

7. What if WCW bought WWE in 2001 instead?

This is a question worth contemplating very often. When WWE purchased World Championship Wrestling in March 2001, it ended the Monday Night Wars. If the outcome had been reversed, the industry might have developed into a more competitive landscape instead of one company dominating for decades. Until 2019, WWE was an absolute monopoly. It is still the most lucrative promotion, but with AEW, there is now a legit competitor and alternative. If WCW had won, there would be no AEW, or even TNA.

6. What if Stone Cold never turned heel at WrestleMania X-Seven?

Stone Cold Steve Austin aligned with Vince McMahon at WrestleMania X-7 in April 2001, a move that clashed with fan expectations. Keeping Austin as a rebellious hero might have extended the peak of the Attitude Era and maintained stronger audience momentum.

5. What if John Cena turned heel during his peak years?

Between 2011 and 2013, fans loudly pushed for John Cena to turn heel. Pulling the trigger then could have refreshed his character and dramatically shifted WWE storytelling during a transitional era. The company would not have gotten the poorly received heel run that was rushed during his retirement arc in 2025.

4. What if Shawn Michaels never had to step away in 1998?

A serious back injury forced Shawn Michaels out after WrestleMania XIV in March 1998, sidelining him for four years. A healthy Michaels during that stretch could have produced major rivalries with The Rock and Austin, reshaping key Attitude Era storylines. Imagine four more years of prime HBK in the mix. The impact would have been comparable to losing Roman Reigns for four full years today.

3. What if Roman Reigns was accepted as a babyface from the start?

After The Shield split in 2014, Roman Reigns was quickly positioned as WWE's next top star. He was pushed at any given opportunity, sometimes feeling forced down fans' throats. Early fan acceptance might have avoided years of uneven booking and changed how WWE built its main event scene. Would we have gotten a Tribal Chief? No. Would we have gotten a four year title run? Definitely not. Would Cody Rhodes have finished his story? Absolutely not.

2. What if Bret Hart never left WWE in 1997?

The Montreal Screwjob at Survivor Series 1997 forced Bret Hart out under controversial circumstances. Without this controversial finish, the rise of the Mr. McMahon character and its rivalry with Austin may have unfolded very differently. This moment became a catalyst for significant changes in WWE's direction.

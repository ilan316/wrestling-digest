🔥 Becky Lynch discusses WWE future and WrestleMania 42

Becky Lynch Discusses WWE Future and WrestleMania 42

As WrestleMania 42 approaches, Becky Lynch has opened up about her WWE future in a way that suggests her current run may be nearing its final chapter, though with notable hesitation that keeps fans guessing about what comes next.

Speaking on INSIGHT with Chris Van Vliet on April 16, Lynch addressed growing speculation around her contract status and whether this represents her last run with the company. When asked directly if this is her final run, Lynch didn't offer a definitive answer. Instead, she emphasized the uncertainty surrounding her future. "I didn't say it with such assuredness… I said it's probably my last one," she explained. "It's not a sure thing… but yeah, it might be. Could be." This careful wording reflects a balancing act Lynch has been navigating between her professional ambitions and personal priorities.

Lynch made it clear that her passion for wrestling hasn't diminished. She stressed that her "love of what I do… that's not going anywhere" and noted that she still feels in her prime with "plenty left in the tank." However, she also acknowledged that life outside the ring has become an increasingly important consideration. "You never want to outstay your welcome," Lynch said, pointing to time with her daughter and the possibility of expanding her family as factors weighing heavily in her thinking. This balance between maintaining her status as one of wrestling's top stars while prioritizing family represents the core of her deliberation about her future.

These comments echo earlier remarks Lynch made in an appearance on WWE Unreal, where she described her current contract as "probably" her last. The consistent use of the word "probably" rather than a definitive statement suggests that Lynch remains open to possibilities while leaning toward this being her final contract with the company. However, the latest remarks indicate the door isn't fully closed to continuing beyond this run—more of a slow count than a sudden three, suggesting she's leaving room for circumstances to change her mind.

The timing of these comments is significant given that Lynch is preparing for a high-profile WrestleMania 42 clash against AJ Lee for the Women's Intercontinental Championship. Lynch herself has hinted that this match could mirror past career-defining moments and possibly represent her own final chapter. For now, Lynch appears focused on finishing strong by telling compelling stories, delivering quality matches, and ensuring the women's division she helped elevate remains center stage.

Beyond her own wrestling situation, Lynch has been involved in other storyline developments heading into WrestleMania 42. She has reportedly made bold plays involving other wrestlers, including attempting to sway IShowSpeed away from his current alliance with The Vision faction. Speaking to IShowSpeed, Lynch didn't mince words about The Vision and offered the streamer a "bribe" of coffee while warning him about the group's dangerous track record. "These Vision boys, got a little beef with them. They're not nice," Lynch said. "They did bad things… they hurt him, they hurt his shoulder, took him out for a long time." Her comments referenced the betrayal of Seth Rollins, her husband, who was previously aligned with The Vision before the group turned on him following Crown Jewel 2025.

Lynch's message to IShowSpeed was clear—history could repeat itself, and Speed might be next in line to feel the group's treachery. IShowSpeed is set to team with Logan Paul and Austin Theory against The Usos and LA Knight in a six-man tag match at WrestleMania 42, while Lynch prepares for her Women's Intercontinental Championship bout. Speed's involvement in recent weeks has already impacted key matches, including costing LA Knight a bout on RAW and previously aiding The Vision in capturing tag team gold. Reports suggest he has also expressed feeling "scared" and "intimidated" by the intensity of WWE's top stars—a crack in his armor that Lynch may be looking to exploit strategically.

In addition to her wrestling-focused activities, Lynch has also found time for entertainment ventures outside WWE. She recently appeared on the Good One podcast, where she discussed her experience filming Happy Gilmore 2 with other entertainment figures. During those appearances, Lynch offered high praise for AEW star MJF, whom she encountered on set. "I think he's so great. I love him. And honestly, I'll be honest, I thought he'd be an asshole like his character," Lynch said. "He's so good at his character that I was like, 'Oh, I'm going to hate this guy.' And he's just the sweetest. He's so nice, and he was so great."

Lynch went on to provide additional commentary about MJF, explaining that the initial impression she had of him based on his professional wrestling character was completely inaccurate. "It was such a great conversation and chat. We bonded instantly. I totally bought the act, and now I'm killing the act, so sorry," she said with humor. "But no, I think he's great. He's wonderful, and he was so great in the movie. He's fantastic—just a fantastic speaker and actor. And he's also still so young, so I'm very excited to see where his career goes." These comments highlight Lynch's appreciation for professional wrestling talent across different promotions and her ability to connect with performers in other entertainment contexts.

Notably, Lynch also revealed significant details about her WWE creative process during these podcast appearances. She discussed her match with Bianca Belair at WrestleMania 38, explaining the story behind their feud and her role in determining how it played out. Their feud began after Lynch returned from a lengthy hiatus at SummerSlam 2021 and defeated Belair to win the Raw Women's Championship—a win that upset Belair, who felt the loss suggested WWE didn't believe in her anymore. Lynch always wanted to make it up to Belair "in the biggest way possible" by putting her back on top.

When discussing how their WrestleMania 38 match came together, Lynch revealed that WWE initially wanted to add a third participant to the match. "At one point, they wanted to put somebody else into the match and make it a triple threat. I was like, 'That's not the story.' That was going to be my hill that I died on. I was ready to really fight," Lynch explained. She pushed back against WWE's creative direction because she believed adding a third person would fundamentally damage the narrative they had built over months. "Thankfully, they changed it. Because it needed to go back to Bianca. If it had gone to the triple threat, they would've kept it on me. The story needed for Bianca to win and take that victory. It just felt like a happy and perfect ending," she said.

Lynch's willingness to advocate for her creative vision demonstrates her influence within WWE and her understanding of long-term storytelling. Her decision to fight for the one-on-one match format paid off, as Bianca Belair defeated Lynch to win back the WWE Raw Women's Championship, providing closure to their feud that satisfied both wrestlers and allowed Belair to receive the victory that validated her as a main event performer.

As WrestleMania 42 approaches, one thing is clear: whether this is truly the last run for Becky Lynch or just another chapter in her WWE career, she remains committed to leaving her mark. She isn't leaving quietly, instead continuing to be a major player in WWE's storytelling, advocating for creative decisions she believes in, and attempting to shape the outcomes of matches beyond her own. Her comments about this potentially being her final run add extra significance to her WrestleMania 42 match against AJ Lee, as fans now wonder if they might be witnessing the closing chapter of one of WWE's greatest modern careers.

Triple H stressed that Stephanie's cultural impact alone would justify her Hall of Fame induction. He noted that whenever she appears at arenas, people light up with excitement and joy at seeing her, demonstrating the lasting impression she has left on the company. Her legacy encompasses not only her on-screen work as a championship-winning performer but also her revolutionary behind-the-scenes contributions that reshaped how WWE operates as an organization.

Nevertheless, the broader legal climate surrounding her father makes his presence at the ceremony unlikely from both practical and public relations perspectives.

The focus of the ceremony will remain on celebrating Stephanie's accomplishments and legacy rather than on her father's absence. With The Undertaker scheduled to honor her during the induction, the evening promises to highlight her unique contributions to WWE's evolution both as an on-screen performer and behind-the-scenes executive. 🔥 Trish Stratus discusses her WWE career and legacy

TRISH STRATUS REFLECTS ON HER WWE CAREER AND LEGACY

Trish Stratus believes one of the biggest turning points for women in WWE came when they were finally placed alongside top male stars instead of being treated like a separate attraction. Speaking on Busted Open Radio, Stratus reflected on her career and explained how intergender storylines allowed female performers to become fully integrated into major angles, something she believes fundamentally changed how fans viewed women in WWE.

"When you had like me, Triple H, Rock, and Lita in the main event… that elevated us obviously, but it also allowed the fans to interpret these characters differently and to understand if these guys are interacting, then you're on that level suddenly," Stratus explained. She pointed to moments where women were involved directly with top stars as a major turning point for the division.

Stratus emphasized that women were once treated like a separate attraction, but that began to change once they became part of major storylines with top names. "Back then when you had the girls sort of like this separate sideshow as a division… when you started interacting with the main characters of the show, that changed everything," she said. Looking back, Stratus said those moments created lasting memories and helped reshape how women were presented in WWE. "The way we told the story together… it was magical. It was really great."

Her comments reflect how much WWE's presentation of women has evolved over time, and how those early integrations helped build what fans see today in women's wrestling.

When it comes to returning to WWE, Stratus isn't simply accepting offers for nostalgia purposes. She made clear that money isn't what convinces her to take on appearances, and she carefully evaluates what kind of role she would be playing. Rather than simply accepting bookings, she looks for opportunities that push her creatively and allow her to evolve as a performer.

"From a personal perspective, I want to make sure I'm doing something different that's challenging me as a performer," Stratus explained. She used her feud with Becky Lynch as an example, explaining how she didn't want to simply return in a predictable role. Instead, she pushed to do something different that would test her as a performer.

"The first one going back with Becky, right? It was, yeah, go back babyface. I said if I can come back and turn heel in this and make it a little bit of a longer story, I'd love that to challenge myself… just to make something different interesting," she noted.

WrestleMania 2 deserves sympathy because it is sandwiched between two of the most historic events not just in WWE history, but wrestling as a whole. It was the sequel to the very first WrestleMania that took place one year earlier in 1985, but served as the prequel to what was the biggest WrestleMania ever until the 2000s, WrestleMania 3 and the Pontiac Silverdome. Those two shows are so recognizable and important that one can sometimes forget that there was a show with a unique concept of its own right in the middle of them.

To make WrestleMania 2 feel bigger than its predecessor, the show took place in three different venues spread across three different cities. One was the Rosemont Horizon (now the Allstate Arena) in Chicago, Illinois, which later played host to WrestleMania 13 and 22. The second was the Los Angeles Memorial Sports Arena in Los Angeles, California, which ended up hosting WrestleMania 7. The third was the Nassau Memorial Veterans Coliseum in Uniondale, New York, which never hosted WrestleMania after this night.

For this spotlight, we traveled to the main event of the Chicago portion of the show for a WWE Tag Team Championship match between The British Bulldogs and The Dream Team of Greg Valentine and Brutus Beefcake. Valentine and Beefcake had won the vacant titles the previous August and had actually defended their crowns against The Dynamite Kid and Davey Boy Smith twice, with one of the wins coming by disqualification. After being challenged to one more match, the champions accepted, and just like that, there was a mid-show main event type of deal.

WrestleMania 2 never really gets mentioned because the shows either side of it are so big and important. However, what often goes unstated is that WrestleMania 2 just isn't that good overall. In fact, there would be little argument with anyone who claimed that the worst WrestleMania of all time is WrestleMania 2.

Despite how bad this show is as a whole, there had to be some sort of silver lining, and this match was that silver lining. To be transparent, this match is good at best. It's not going to make any "Greatest WrestleMania Matches" lists, but there are certainly worse ways to spend 15 minutes.

Starting off with The British Bulldogs, it's only when you go back and watch a match like this right after watching something from the modern day that you realize they were truly ahead of their time. Dynamite and Davey possessed such creative offense for the time, utilizing quick tags, cutting off the ring, and basically implementing all of the fundamentals of tag team wrestling. They mastered these fundamentals to the point where they could flesh out their offense to make it stand out at the time. Dynamite especially displayed an explosiveness that wrestling would go on to see from so many people who got their big breaks in Japan.

Valentine also put in a great performance as the workhorse of the match, rarely tagging in Beefcake and taking the bulk of the punishment.

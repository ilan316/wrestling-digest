🔥 Brody King Anti-ICE Stance in AEW

---

Brody King has become the poster-boy for the anti-ICE movement in professional wrestling. Ever since he wore the "Abolish ICE" t-shirt for a match at Arena Mexico nearly a year ago, the former AEW Tag-Team and Trios Champion has become a figure that fans associate with the anti-ICE movement, leading to regular "F**k ICE!" chants from crowds whenever he appears in the ring.

The chants received mainstream media attention following the February 4, 2026 episode of AEW Dynamite, when fans chanted "F**k ICE" before King's match against MJF at the Pearl Theater at Palms Casino Resort in Las Vegas. Since then, fans have continued the chants at AEW shows, sometimes combining them with chants of "F**k Don Callis." The chants even spread internationally, with Australian crowds participating during King's match against MJF at AEW Grand Slam in Sydney on February 14.

During a recent interview with Denise Salcedo, King addressed the subject directly. He explained that his activism stems from a personal desire to represent others the way he wished someone had represented him when he was younger. "I feel like when I was younger I always wanted somebody that felt like me on TV or wherever to express the way that they felt so I can have someone to champion for me," King said. "And if I can be that for other people that are being marginalized then I want to be that for them."

King emphasized that he does not view his stance as political, but rather as a matter of basic morality. "Everyone tries to bring up the political thing, in my opinion, it's just what's right and what's wrong," he continued. "And there's a lot of wrong happening in the world and if I can speak out against it then I will." He also cited other wrestlers taking similar stances, noting that "we got guys like Mick Foley who also do the same thing."

Reflecting on how his activism gained momentum, King recalled the timeline of events. "I wore the Abolish ICE shirt at Arena Mexico almost a year ago, I think it was June of last year, and the chants happened this year right before Australia," King said. "It got a lot of attention for the shirt and then out of nowhere kind of picked up more attention." He contextualized the growth of the movement within broader social tensions, noting that "obviously the state of things gets more boiled over, people are frustrated and that's going to come out in certain ways, and I can't think of a more positive way for it to come out than them chanting it at a wrestling show."

King revealed that hearing the chants during his February 4 match took him by surprise but had an energizing effect. "It took me off guard, but I also felt like I could run through a wall at that moment," he admitted. He also commented on MJF's reaction to the chants, saying, "I feel like the way that MJF looked at that camera, he knew I was going to run through a wall as well."

Beyond his in-ring presence, King has been actively involved in activism related to ICE. He has been vocal in his opposition to U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement and raises money through merchandise and comic books for families impacted by ICE raids. His commitment to the cause extends beyond symbolic gestures, demonstrating a sustained effort to support those affected by immigration enforcement actions.

King's journey from wearing a single t-shirt to becoming a symbol of anti-ICE activism in wrestling illustrates how personal conviction can resonate with audiences on a broader scale. His willingness to use his platform, combined with fan enthusiasm for the cause, has created a movement that transcends typical wrestling storylines and speaks to real-world social justice concerns.

🔥 Babes of Wrath Tag Team Breakup in AEW

---

The Babes of Wrath have called it quits. Following their loss to Divine Dominion on the March 28 episode of AEW Collision, Willow Nightingale and Harley Cameron decided to put their tag team alliance on ice and pursue separate endeavors.

The match itself saw Divine Dominion (Megan Bayne and Lena Kross) retain their AEW Women's World Tag Team Championships in what was a thrilling encounter. This marked the second consecutive victory for the champions over the Babes of Wrath, following their initial title win at AEW Revolution on the Zero Hour portion of the event. Due to how their first bout concluded, a rematch was warranted, and it delivered an entertaining contest between both teams.

Despite selling the effects of their match at Revolution just 13 days prior, both Nightingale and Cameron brought their A-game to the rematch. The action began before the bell even rang, with both teams eager to get started. Divine Dominion used their environment to their advantage, taking Nightingale out of the ring and isolating her on the outside to amplify the pain she was already experiencing from pulling double duty at Revolution.

Throughout the match, the Babes of Wrath showed resilience and competitive fire. Cameron hit Bayne with a big powerbomb, while Nightingale connected with a spinebuster on Kross. The challengers even came close to capturing the titles with several near falls, and both women managed to slap single-legged Boston crabs on their opponents simultaneously. However, Bayne was able to reach the ropes to break the submission hold.

In the end, Divine Dominion's teamwork proved to be the difference. Kross threw Nightingale to the outside to isolate Cameron, and with the odds stacked against her, Cameron fell victim to the Divine Intervention finisher. Bayne secured the victory with a high stack pin, retaining the titles for her team.

The aftermath of the match proved to be bittersweet. Backstage, Cameron apologized to Nightingale for costing her team the win. However, rather than blame and resentment, the two women shared an emotional moment. Both appreciated what they had accomplished together during their time as a team. Cameron, showing maturity and selflessness, encouraged Nightingale to focus on herself and her TBS Championship, telling her she would always be in her corner to cheer her on as she defended the title going forward.

With that conversation, the Babes of Wrath agreed to part ways—at least for now. They left each other on good terms, having formed a genuine bond during their time together.

The Babes of Wrath had enjoyed significant success during their partnership. They were the inaugural AEW Women's World Tag Team Champions, winning the tournament to crown the first title holders in December. In that tournament final, they defeated the Timeless Love Bombs (Toni Storm and Mina Shirakawa) to claim the belts.

Now that the Babes of Wrath are out of the picture, Divine Dominion can shift their attention to defending their championship against new challengers. Other tag teams in the AEW women's division include the recently formed Brawling Birds duo of Alex Windsor and Jamie Hayter, as well as the Sisters of Sin (Julia Hart and Skye Blue). These teams are potential future contenders for the championship. The team of Anna Jay and Tay Melo could also be brought back into the equation, though that appears dependent on whether Jay remains with the company.

Chris Jericho Reflects on Past WWE Feud

---

Chris Jericho has reflected on two significant chapters of his wrestling career that he feels deserve greater recognition than they've historically received.

The first involves his rivalry with The Rock during WWE's Invasion angle following the company's acquisition of WCW in March 2001. Jericho considers this feud one of the most underrated of his in-ring career, praising both his own abilities and The Rock's skills as a performer. "A really great one that I think deserves more recognition is Jericho versus the Rock," he said. "We could go toe to toe in the ring, and Rock was a great wrestler. Rock liked to talk things through in promos first, which I can do too, but there were very few people who could genuinely stand up to him on the mic."

Jericho emphasized the dynamic quality of their exchanges, noting that he and The Rock complemented each other perfectly as competitors. "To have a great dragon slayer, you need a great dragon, and I think we were very good from that aspect. There was a whole run where he accused me of not being able to win the big one — and then I went ahead and beat him for the Undisputed Championship. We were even tag team champions together at one point, for about two weeks... Every time we were in the ring together or did a promo or a backstage segment, it was excellent."

The feud saw several championship exchanges. Jericho first defeated The Rock for the WCW World Championship at WWE No Mercy in October 2001. Shortly afterward, the two briefly aligned to capture the WWE Tag Team Championships from The Dudley Boyz, though their reign lasted only about two weeks before their on-screen tension escalated. They faced each other again for the WCW world title, with The Rock reclaiming the championship. Jericho then regained the WCW World Championship by defeating The Rock once more at WWE Vengeance in December 2001. That same night, he cemented his legacy by becoming the first-ever Undisputed WWE Champion after also defeating Steve Austin for the WWE Title.

Beyond his WWE career, Jericho also highlighted an underrated moment from his time in All Elite Wrestling: the iconic acapella performance of "Judas" by the crowd during his storyline with MJF. In 2021, MJF introduced a stipulation banning Jericho's theme song during their feud, which lasted exactly 366 days. The creative team brainstormed various ways to handle the moment, including displaying lyrics on screen with a bouncing ball or placing lyric sheets under seats for fans to reference.

However, Jericho and Tony Khan ultimately decided against these approaches. Khan drew a parallel to concert experiences, explaining that fans either know the words or they don't, and forcing it would diminish the organic nature of the moment. "When you go to a concert, people know the words or they don't," Khan reasoned. "That's the organic nature of it. If you force it, it's not going to be as cool."

Jericho agreed with this philosophy, noting that even if the moment failed, it would still enhance the heel storyline, but if it succeeded, something truly special could emerge. The initial seconds felt slightly awkward, but by the third line, the crowd had fully engaged. Some fans even pulled up the lyrics on their phones, which Jericho viewed as a natural response.

He described the acapella singing as one of the coolest and most underrated moments of his career. "But that was one of the coolest moments I've ever been part of, and I think it's actually a fairly underrated moment in AEW history and in wrestling history. That's the only time I can ever remember that happening that way. It was the one time where Fozzy and wrestling collided perfectly." For Jericho, this rare intersection between his music career and professional wrestling created something truly unique and memorable.

Okada attempted to control Knight through mind games, threatening to chop him just to make the challenger flinch, while also dishing out punishment on the outside with a DDT on the floor. Knight refused to be intimidated, rising to the occasion and even turning the tables by making Okada flinch in return. The challenger matched Okada's Drop Kick, hit a huge Springboard Clothesline to the outside, and executed a leaping Hurricanrana that dragged the champion down to the mat.

Knight appeared to have victory within reach after landing a Coast-To-Coast Drop Kick. Ascending to the top rope for his finishing UFO Splash, Knight seemed poised to claim the title. However, Okada rolled away, but that didn't prevent "The Jet" from launching himself almost the entire length of the ring in an attempt to land his finisher. Knight came crashing down hard on Okada's knees instead.

In the closing sequence, Okada attempted The Rainmaker, but Knight rolled him up for a near fall. When Okada went for the move a second time, Knight attempted another roll up. This time, Okada dropped down and placed all his weight on Knight, and as the referee counted, Okada grabbed the ring ropes for extra leverage to secure the victory. Both men are now tied at one win each, with a potential trilogy bout potentially on the horizon given the manner in which this match concluded.

AEW's Jeff Jarrett On Working With Chuck Norris At WWE Survivor Series: 'A Pro's Pro'

The main event of WWE Survivor Series 1994 pitted The Undertaker against Yokozuna in a Casket Match, with multiple interferences expected in the midst of the action. To combat that, WWE recruited MMA and acting icon Chuck Norris as the special guest enforcer.

In this role, Norris notably blocked the involvement of Bam Bam Bigelow, King Kong Bundy, and Jeff Jarrett, the latter of whom ate a superkick for his offense. Nevertheless, Jarrett looks back on that moment fondly as he considers Norris, who died earlier this month at the age of 86, a one-of-a-kind figure.

"Casket Match, and just how it all laid out, Chuck Norris was a special enforcer, so of course, a heel got to roll down there and show his chops," Jarrett said on the "My World" podcast. "I'll never forget having the quick conversation. I'm thinking okay, we're probably going to go over this, and then we just had that conversation. Very briefly he said, 'Well, what do you think?' And I said, 'Well, I'm better off stepping into you, not you coming into me, but we'll meet each other.' He just kind of nodded his head. He said, 'Okay.' That's kind of what I posted on my tweet, just a very brief [interaction]. But boy oh boy, he's a pros pro. 'Was it stiff, Jeff?' Let's just say it was in there, but he was a pro. He knew what he was doing."

Despite the multiple attempted interferences and the successful one from Irwin R. Schyster, The Undertaker ultimately won the match, courtesy of a big boot and a dramatic close of the casket. Satisfied with the outcome, Norris then looked on at ringside with a smirk.

Thunder Rosa Recalls WWE PPV Experience

AEW's Thunder Rosa Recalls Attending WWE Pay-Per-View That Changed Her Life

While professional wrestling is a staple of Thunder Rosa's life now, it was once almost nonexistent. Speaking on "REAL 92.3 LA," the former AEW Women's Champion noted that wrestling, aside from recognizing lucha legend Rey Mysterio, didn't enter her life until her 20s. And it wasn't until WWE WrestleMania 25 in 2009 that the industry truly hooked her.

"I didn't start watching wrestling until I was in my 20s," Rosa said. "I remember bringing people to my house to watch some of the pay-per-views. Then I watched TNA. The Knockouts division at the time was hot and I was like 'These ladies can go!' What I used to watch on WWE, it was not of my interest. I remember watching some of the bra and panties [matches] and my friend was like '[Thunder], don't ever do that.' We watched really bad women's wrestling, but it was what it was at the time."

Rosa explained that her initial skepticism about professional wrestling eventually gave way to curiosity and genuine interest. "I wasn't a fan, but life changes you and you start getting other tastes for things," she continued. "So I went to WrestleMania 25 in Houston and that really changed a lot of things. There was a lot of people in that arena that became part of my life later on in life, so it's a full circle."

The event, emanating from Houston's Reliant Stadium, featured a main event title match between Randy Orton and then-WWE Champion Triple H. Elsewhere, John Cena claimed the World Heavyweight Championship by defeating the Big Show and Adam "Edge" Copeland, while Chris Jericho overcame three WWE legends in a handicap elimination match. Big Show, Copeland, and Jericho all currently reside in All Elite Wrestling alongside Rosa, as does John Morrison, who competed in a Lumberjack match to unify WWE and World Tag Team Championships at the 2009 event.

**AEW Criticized Over Handling Of Ronda Rousey's Surprise Appearance**

Ronda Rousey's AEW appearance generated significant attention, but the way the company allegedly handled her backstage before the surprise has drawn criticism from veteran wrestling personality Vince Russo.

During a discussion on the Coach and Bro Show, Russo explained that AEW has employed similar secretive methods with other returning stars in the past. He referenced a previous incident involving Adam Cole, who was returning to AEW after an extended absence due to injury. According to Russo, the company showed backstage footage of Cole arriving in a car, after which he was immediately placed in a wheelchair and covered with a black blanket before being wheeled throughout the backstage area.

"Coach, you got to know the backstory. First guys, years ago, they showed backstage footage of Adam Cole was returning to AEW after a long absence because of an injury. And they literally showed backstage Adam Cole showing up in a car. They immediately put him in a wheelchair and covered him with a black blanket and wheeled him throughout the back. They showed that — I saw this. So I'm like, 'Bro, that is the stupidest most ridiculous thing I've ever seen in my life. All my years in wrestling, we had hundreds of surprises. We never ever did that,'" Russo said.

However, Russo's frustration intensified when he learned that AEW had apparently employed the same tactic with Rousey. "Then I'm doing a show this morning and I am smartened up — oh no. Oh no. They did that to Ronda Rousey. They sat Ronda Rousey in a wheelchair and covered her with a tarp and rolled her around the back."

Russo went further in his criticism, questioning the logic behind such an approach for a star of Rousey's caliber. He suggested that Rousey herself should have objected to the undignified treatment, given her status as one of the biggest names in both wrestling and combat sports.

"Number one, at what point does Ronda Rousey say, 'Are you out of your effing mind, bro? I'm freaking Ronda Rousey. I'm not some wrestling mark.' That's the stupidest thing I've ever heard," Russo stated.

Beyond questioning Rousey's willingness to participate in such a scenario, Russo argued that AEW's strategy was fundamentally flawed from a business perspective. He contended that advertising her appearance in advance would have been far more effective at driving viewership than keeping it secret.

"Number two, this is your typical case, coach… you're k-fabing yourself. Why wouldn't you want them to know Ronda Rousey is going to be on the show? Then guess what — YOU MIGHT GET PEOPLE TO tune into the show. That's called k-fabing yourself. They wanted Ronda to be this big surprise. Why? Why, bro? Advertise she's going to be on the show. Don't hide her. Get it out there in social media. Oh my god — Ronda Rousey's going to be on AEW — and guess what? Maybe you'll have some more people watching the show. So all around, bro, this was absolutely ridiculous."

Russo's comments shifted the conversation away from the surprise itself and toward the decision-making process behind the scenes. His criticism centered on the belief that Rousey's presence as one of the biggest crossover names in wrestling and combat sports should have been leveraged as a promotional tool rather than concealed from the audience.

The debate raises questions about the most effective ways to generate interest in wrestling programming and whether keeping major talent hidden is ever the right call in an era where social media and advance promotion can significantly impact viewership numbers.

**Tony Schiavone Felt 'A Sense Of Relief' Upon Leaving Wrestling After WCW Closure**

Although he's been a regular presence in AEW since 2019, there was a lengthy period of time when Tony Schiavone had stepped away from the pro wrestling industry entirely. The sabbatical began when WCW was shut down in 2001, and speaking on "What Happened When," Schiavone recalled how he felt after the last "WCW Monday Nitro" broadcast.

"I was glad to leave that day," Schiavone said. "Very honestly, I don't know if it was anger, or relief, or excitement about what was ahead of me, but I knew that I had to reinvent myself, because I knew the WWE would not want me, though I did check with them a couple of times about going to work with them."

Schiavone had already seen the writing on the wall before the actual end, and had started looking for another job outside of wrestling despite it being against his WCW contract to do so. The broadcaster got an interview with a radio station and wound up being hired immediately after WCW's closure. Immediately after leaving WCW, Schiavone began working at a radio station and later became a sports broadcaster before his eventual return to wrestling.

Looking back at the final "Nitro," Schiavone recalled leaving the arena without saying goodbye to anyone. "Got in my car and drove to Atlanta that night," Schiavone continued. "There was a sense of relief from that."

"I hit the ground running and that's why, when I look back on it, f**t it," Schiavone said. "It didn't really bother me at all." He then clarified that he was bothered by the way the company's final days were handled by some executives, but by the time the actual closure came around, Schiavone was simply happy to go.

AEW's Maya World Reflects On 'Hard Work Paying Off'

Following a series of tag team matches in late 2025, All Elite Wrestling announced Hyan and Maya World as the newest members of the company. For World, it marked a full-circle moment as she'd be joining Athena, her former coach, in the AEW-ROH women's locker room. According to World herself, it also served as validation of the efforts she's put forth in her career so far.

The contract offer came in an unexpected manner during a late-night drive. "I remember I was driving back from Houston to Austin where I was at the time. It was like 2am, and we were asked to be back the next week," World told Denise Salcedo, reflecting on the moment. "We were like, 'Yeah,' and then five minutes later they were like 'Oh, by the way [you're being offered a contract]'. That happened and I immediately pulled over to the side of the road."

Filled with a rush of emotions, World's first instinct was to share the news with Athena. "I just cried," World said. "I called my coach first, Athena, of course, because she's been with me through everything, my first match on Ring of Honor ever back in 2023 was with her." With it being late at night, the call initially went unanswered, so World then reached out to Billie Starkz, Athena's on-screen protege, to help get in touch with her.

When World finally connected with Athena, her former coach provided valuable guidance. "She was just giving me all this advice business-wise. It felt like all my hard work paying off. I didn't think it'd be this soon, but thank God it was."

As an official member of the AEW-ROH family, World has continued to compete alongside Hyan, a veteran of the squared circle. Additionally, she's challenged Athena for the ROH Women's Championship in a singles match.

**AEW Star Started Marvel-Inspired Gimmick Because He Hated Shaving**

All Elite Wrestling's Brian Cage has opened up about the surprisingly humble origins of one of his most recognizable character traits. Appearing on The Hot Seat podcast with eight-time Olympian Wendy Fortino, Cage explained how Marvel Comics' Wolverine became woven into his wrestling presentation — and it all came down to personal grooming preferences.

The journey began with Cage's simple aversion to shaving. He initially grew out large sideburns as a practical solution to avoid the daily chore. The look caught the attention of a friend, who referenced former WCW wrestler Lash LeRoux and his distinctive pointed sideburns. This inspired Cage to experiment with a similar style.

"Well, I hated shaving, so I grew these huge sideburns just so I didn't have to shave," Cage explained. "And then there was an old wrestler named Lash LeRoux that had these cool little sideburns that point. And my friend's like, 'Oh, you should shave like that one time,' and so I did. People called me like Wolverine, or Hugh Jackman wishes he was this buff."

The pivotal moment came when a fan suggested a name that would stick. According to Cage, an audience member dubbed him "Swolverine" — a clever portmanteau combining his physique with the Marvel character. The name resonated so strongly that Cage considered trademark and patent protection for it.

"And then a fan was like, 'Oh, it's cause you're the Swolverine.' I went, 'Oh man, Swolverine! I'm gonna write that down, get that trademarked and patented,'" Cage recalled. Once he had embraced the nickname, he began intentionally incorporating Wolverine references into his in-ring work. Being a longtime fan of the character, Cage drew inspiration from various sources to create move names with thematic significance.

"So I was like, 'Oh, that's pretty cool, let me add that into it.' Since I was such a fan of Wolverine anyways, even like moves I do — Drill Claw, Weapon X, Tornado Claw — those are all Wolverine references from different fighting games and stuff like that," he said.

As Cage's appearance evolved over time, the connection to Wolverine became even more pronounced. When he grew out his beard and longer hair, particularly following the release of the film *Logan* and newer Wolverine portrayals, fans increasingly pointed out the resemblance.

Cage emphasized that this alignment was never an intentional goal on his part. However, he views the repeated comparisons as a genuine compliment to both his physique and presentation.

"When I changed my look and grew the beard and hair, especially from Logan and the new Wolverine, they're like, 'Man, you look more like Wolverine now! Is that what you're going for?' I was like, 'I wasn't trying to at all!' But the fact that everyone keeps thinking I do, I'm like, that's great. That's an awesome compliment to me," Cage concluded.

🔥 Tony Khan AEW State of Company Comments

AEW President Tony Khan has provided an update on the state of his promotion, highlighting record-breaking viewership numbers and sharing an amusing origin story about one of wrestling's most recognizable annual events.

During recent interviews, Khan revealed that AEW's signature Blood and Guts match owes its name to an unexpected source: Vince McMahon. The WWE chairman made the comment during a WWE investor call in 2019 while reassuring shareholders that WWE would maintain its PG programming rather than embrace what he described as the style of "blood and guts guys"—a veiled reference to AEW, which was planning its entry into the wrestling industry at that time.

Khan explained the serendipitous naming in detail: "The name actually comes from taking something and twisting it around because Vince McMahon, on an investor call, said, 'We're not like those blood and guts guys.' At that point, I said, 'We have to use that. They have to use that. That's such a great quote.' So Vince actually named it."

When AEW decided to create its own version of the classic two-ring steel cage match, the promotion needed a distinct name since WWE owns the WarGames trademark. McMahon's inadvertent suggestion proved perfect, capturing the spirit of the violent multi-person cage match while also providing AEW with a memorable brand identity. The name has remained ever since, becoming an annual edition of AEW Dynamite built around the company's signature cage match concept.

Regarding the current state of AEW, Khan expressed optimism about the promotion's trajectory. He stated that last week's broadcast on TBS delivered the company's highest viewership in years, noting it was the most-watched program since AEW's major Blood and Guts cage match in 2024.

Khan emphasized that AEW's true viewership extends beyond traditional cable ratings when factoring in streaming numbers. "Two years ago, we weren't streaming on HBO Max yet. So the viewership now, the true viewership is actually much higher because not only do we have the TBS viewers, but also we have streaming viewership," Khan said. "So in reality, this is the hottest and most viewers we've had in a long time, if not ever. And it feels like the excitement around AEW today is at an all-time high."

The AEW President credited the wrestlers across the roster for driving this momentum. "There's a lot of excitement around AEW. So many of the wrestlers have been delivering at a high level—women and men—and I think the excitement right now is at an all-time high," Khan commented.

Khan also praised a promising young talent in the AEW system. "We're training Sting's son, Steven, right now," Khan said of Steven Borden Jr. "He wrestles before all the shows in what we call the 'dark match.' And I think he's going to be a tremendous wrestler. I'm really, really proud of the work he's doing."

Borden Jr., the son of wrestling legend Sting, has been working dark matches before AEW's televised events as he continues his in-ring development in front of live AEW crowds. Khan's comments suggest the promotion is investing in building the next generation of talent while capitalizing on current momentum across the roster.

AEW Broadcast Future and Court Merger Delay

A California judge has granted a temporary restraining order that pauses the proposed merger between Paramount and Warner Bros. Discovery, extending the uncertainty surrounding AEW's long-term broadcast home. Judge Araceli Martinez-Olguin ordered the halt on Monday in response to a lawsuit filed by a coalition of U.S. state attorneys general challenging the transaction. The temporary restraining order prevents the merger from closing for at least 14 days, though some reports suggest it could extend up to 28 days while the legal challenge moves forward.

The coalition challenging the merger includes attorneys general from California, Nevada, Massachusetts, New York, New Jersey, and several other states. Their argument centers on the claim that combining Paramount and Warner Bros. Discovery would violate a century-old antitrust law by creating an anticompetitive market. Specifically, they contend the merger would "harm competition in the basic cable and theatrical markets by combining two of the top three cable programmers and two of the top five film distributors."

Despite the legal challenges, Paramount has defended the proposed transaction, stating that the "merger is lawful, pro-competitive, and will benefit consumers, creators, workers, and the entertainment industry." Warner Bros. Discovery has declined to comment publicly on the matter. According to court proceedings, a Paramount attorney indicated that the company had suggested the deal could close as early as Wednesday before the restraining order was granted.

The stakes for completing this merger have become increasingly high for Paramount. Under the terms of the merger agreement, if the deal is not finalized by the end of September, Warner Bros. Discovery shareholders will receive an additional 25 cents per share each quarter until the transaction closes, amounting to roughly 650 million dollars per quarter. This financial pressure has prompted Paramount to seek an expedited hearing on a preliminary injunction, pushing for the hearing to take place in late August with live witnesses. A ruling in September could potentially allow Paramount to avoid these escalating payments to shareholders.

However, the stakes are even higher should regulators ultimately block the merger entirely. Paramount would be required to pay Warner Bros. Discovery a 7 billion dollar termination fee under the terms of their agreement. The preliminary injunction filing is viewed as particularly significant, as a judge's decision on this matter could determine the merger's ultimate fate. If granted, it would block the Paramount-WBD merger until a judge could rule on the coalition's full lawsuit, potentially putting the deal at serious risk. If denied, it would likely allow the merger to close with minimal additional difficulty.

The situation has created notable implications for AEW, which currently relies on Warner Bros. Discovery as its media partner. AEW's current media rights agreement with Warner Bros. Discovery runs through 2027 and includes a one-year extension option that is controlled by WBD. The uncertainty surrounding the Paramount-WBD merger therefore creates uncertainty about the wrestling promotion's broadcast future beyond 2027. AEW President Tony Khan has spoken positively in recent months about Paramount Skydance CEO David Ellison and the company's potential future relationship with Paramount should the merger ultimately be completed, suggesting the company has been considering the implications of this deal.

The Paramount-WBD merger is not the only major media transaction currently facing legal delays. The proposed 6.2 billion dollar merger between Nexstar Media and Tegna is also on hold following a similar lawsuit and injunction filed by California Attorney General Rob Bonta. That case is not expected to go to trial until mid-2027, suggesting that major media consolidation battles could extend well into the future.

AEW Star Will Ospreay Recalls Winning Top NJPW Title During Pandemic

At the 2021 Sakura Genesis event, Will Ospreay defeated Kota Ibushi to capture New Japan's top prize, the IWGP World Heavyweight Championship. Unfortunately, his post-match celebration didn't pan out exactly as he would have liked.

According to Ospreay, that can be attributed to the hazardous state of the world and Japan's corresponding limitations at the time. "The title win was great, but it was during COVID," he told Marking Out. "COVID was not a good time for any of us in Japan. I was living there at the time. I was severely beaten up and they had restrictions. So it was like all the restaurants would close at 8:00 p.m. You can have fans in attendance, but it was half full, and they wasn't allowed to make any noise. So they would wear the mask and clap."

Ospreay first learned of his IWGP World Heavyweight Title victory from NJPW head booker Gedo, who informed him that he would initially win the 2021 New Japan Cup to earn the title opportunity against Ibushi. Following the good news, Ospreay then cried tears of joy in the nearby bathroom, realizing that his long-time dream would soon come true. Ospreay even imagined what the crowd would sound like in his crowning moment.

Due to the environmental restrictions enforced by NJPW, the audience ultimately stifled their shock when Ospreay dethroned Ibushi at Sakura Genesis. "I remember hitting my finish and it was like, one two three, and they went, 'What?' They were shocked, then they quickly went back in," Ospreay recalled. "I wasn't allowed to go out and party afterwards. You know after you have one of those performances, you get the boys together, you go out, you get absolutely trounced. Honestly, it was 'Good job. See you next week.'"

🔥 Janel Grant Lawsuit Moving to Arbitration

Janel Grant's lawsuit against Vince McMahon and WWE is officially headed to private arbitration following a joint filing by all remaining parties in Connecticut federal court. The agreement represents a significant procedural shift in one of professional wrestling's most high-profile legal cases, though it does not resolve the underlying allegations.

According to the joint status report, Grant, McMahon and WWE have executed an agreement to arbitrate their dispute. Under the terms of this agreement, Grant is required to file paperwork voluntarily dismissing the lawsuit against the two remaining defendants within five business days. The legal language is precise and consequential: the dismissal will be filed "with prejudice," meaning Grant cannot turn around and refile the same claims in federal court at a later date. However, the agreement preserves each party's ability to continue presenting their respective claims and defenses during the private arbitration process.

The move to arbitration comes after months of contentious litigation over whether the case should leave the federal court system. McMahon and WWE had consistently argued that an arbitration clause contained in a nondisclosure agreement Grant signed with McMahon in 2022 required the dispute to be resolved privately. Grant's legal team fought this position vigorously, maintaining that she signed the NDA under duress and that the arbitration provision should not be enforced. Despite these arguments, a Connecticut judge approved the move to arbitration on July 13, 2026, which ultimately paved the way for all parties to reach their current agreement.

Grant, a former WWE employee, initially filed her lawsuit in January 2024 against McMahon, WWE and John Laurinaitis in Connecticut federal court. The complaint accused McMahon of sexual assault and sex trafficking. Laurinaitis, a former WWE executive, was dismissed from the case at an earlier stage of litigation. McMahon resigned from TKO/WWE shortly after the lawsuit was filed, though his resignation did not end the legal dispute.

The shift to private arbitration carries major implications for public transparency. Federal lawsuits typically generate public hearings, written court rulings, and filed documents available to the press and public. Arbitration, by contrast, generally occurs away from public view, meaning that future testimony, evidence presented, witness statements, and any potential financial resolution may never be disclosed to the public. Unless someone involved chooses to speak publicly about developments, the wrestling community and media may never learn the specific details of how the case concludes.

Grant's original allegations included claims that McMahon engaged in sexual abuse and trafficking and that WWE either enabled or concealed his conduct. Grant claimed that McMahon agreed to pay her 3 million dollars under the NDA but stopped making payments after providing only 1 million dollars. She sought to void the nondisclosure agreement entirely and recover the remaining financial damages, along with additional compensation. McMahon has denied wrongdoing, while WWE has disputed Grant's allegations and claims.

The decision to move the case into arbitration does not constitute an admission of wrongdoing by McMahon or WWE, nor does it mean Grant has abandoned her allegations. Rather, the same fundamental dispute that was being litigated in federal court will now continue through a confidential process overseen by a private arbitrator. The claims, defenses and evidence will remain the same, only the venue and procedural rules have changed. The jurisdictional change merely moves the battleground from a public courtroom to a private arbitration proceeding.

Grant's legal team had previously argued that federal laws designed to prevent sexual assault and harassment claims from being forced into private arbitration should apply to her case. Despite those arguments, all parties have now voluntarily agreed to proceed through arbitration, which represents a substantial development after months of public court filings and legal opposition to the arbitration clause.

One of professional wrestling's most significant legal matters is now set to proceed entirely behind closed doors.

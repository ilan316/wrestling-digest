The high-profile event, which drew notable figures from the wrestling and combat sports worlds, was abruptly disrupted by the security threat. President Donald Trump and members of his cabinet were swiftly evacuated by Secret Service agents as the situation developed.

Among those in attendance were Shane McMahon and Linda McMahon, the current Secretary of Education. Linda McMahon was captured being escorted out of the event by security personnel, with photographic evidence of the evacuation circulating widely. Shane McMahon was evacuated alongside his mother during the immediate response to the incident.

Dana White, the TKO executive, also attended the dinner and was seated prominently near where the president had been sitting. He later recounted his experience in an interview with USA Today, offering a detailed account of the chaotic moments as the situation unfolded. According to White's account, the atmosphere inside the venue quickly deteriorated as the security threat became apparent. "Inside, the whole event just started getting noisy, tables getting flipped over, guys running in with guns and they were screaming `Get down. I didn't get down. It was f****** awesome. I literally took every minute of it in, it was a pretty crazy, unique experience. We were sitting right in front of the table, right in front of where the president was. Nobody got tackled but a guy came in looking for shooters and then came toward the table. I thought the shooter was by us or something."

Authorities responded quickly to the incident. A suspect identified as Cole Thomas Allen was taken into custody immediately after the incident occurred. The rapid response by security and law enforcement personnel prevented further escalation of the situation, and the evacuation of high-profile attendees including the president proceeded without reported injuries.

Utami Hayashishita Marigold Departure

Utami Hayashishita's time with Dream Star Fighting Marigold is coming to an end. This past Saturday, the former Marigold World Champion announced her imminent departure from the promotion at Korakuen Hall following a World Championship match against current titleholder Miku Aono. Despite challenging for her former title, Hayashishita fell short as Aono successfully retained the championship. After the match concluded, Hayashishita addressed the crowd and informed them that her final Marigold appearance will take place on May 23 at Ota Ward Gym.

Following the weekend's show, Marigold owner Rossy Ogawa was interviewed by Tokyo Sports regarding Hayashishita's departure. Ogawa revealed that he had been aware of her decision to leave since January of this year. While expressing sadness about losing her from the company, Ogawa made clear that he did not attempt to persuade her to remain with Marigold. He explained his reasoning by stating that if a wrestler wants to quit, it indicates they no longer wish to work for the promotion, making any attempt at convincing them to stay pointless.

Ogawa's philosophical approach to the departure emphasized acceptance over resistance. He stated: "I didn't try to convince her to stay. Because if she wants to quit, it means she doesn't want to work here anymore. There's no point in trying to convince her to stay. We don't chase after those who leave. But there's no point in dwelling only on the negatives. This is precisely the time to turn a crisis into an opportunity. All we can do is hope the wrestlers do their best."

Hayashishita's departure marks the loss of one of Marigold's most prominent stars. The 27-year-old was a standout performer in Ogawa's formerly owned and operated promotion, World Wonder Ring STARDOM, before choosing to join Marigold in 2024 alongside several other major names. She quickly established herself as a major force in the new promotion, capturing the Marigold World Championship on January 3, 2025, at First Dream in Tokyo. She defeated inaugural champion Sareee to claim the title, becoming the second-ever Marigold World Champion. Her reign lasted 296 days, making her the longest-reigning champion of that title to date.

Man Like DeReiss Made New Friends And Wrestling Fans In Las Vegas

Independent wrestler Man Like DeReiss had an unexpected and heartwarming encounter during his travels to Las Vegas for WrestleMania that resulted in new friendships and a story worth telling.

According to DeReiss's account on social media, the story began on a flight from London Heathrow to Las Vegas. DeReiss was traveling alone to attend WrestleMania week, but found himself seated in the middle of a group of six women who had booked two rows of three seats. When one of the women needed help rearranging their seating, DeReiss stepped in to assist, which opened the door to conversation.

During their chat, the woman in the adjacent seat asked DeReiss why he was traveling to Vegas alone. He explained that he was heading to WrestleMania and described the concept of Mania week from an independent wrestler's perspective. The woman shared that she used to love watching wrestling, particularly World of Sport programming featuring Big Daddy and Giant Haystacks. She also mentioned that her grandchildren are wrestling fans.

The conversation naturally progressed to DeReiss mentioning his upcoming match. When the women asked what time and place he would be wrestling, DeReiss invited them to Joey Janela's Spring Break event. He gave them specific details: the Horseshoe venue, 7pm on Friday night, and told them it would be a GCW show. Since the woman didn't have social media, this was his only way to communicate the details to her.

When the plane landed, DeReiss said goodbye to the group, sharing a group hug with his new friends. However, he was skeptical about whether they would actually attend the event, thinking to himself that there was no way they would actually show up.

But the women proved him wrong. When DeReiss competed at Spring Break and later stood at the merchandise table following his match, his new friends arrived at the event. The moment was significant enough that DeReiss posted about it on social media, sharing a photo with two of the six women who had made the trip to support him.

Unfortunately, after the show ended, DeReiss was unable to reconnect with the group, and since they weren't on social media, he had no way to contact them. This prompted him to make a public appeal on social media, writing: "After the show I never got to see them and now have no contact as they don't have social media. So if anyone knows these women PLEASE LET ME KNOW. I know they're from Cardiff, Wales and want to invite them to a show in the UK!"

The story had a happy ending. DeReiss successfully reconnected with the women, as evidenced by a follow-up post on April 25, 2026, where he shared: "HAHAHAHA WE FOUND THEM!!" The discovery provided closure to the story and allowed DeReiss to maintain the friendships he had unexpectedly formed during his journey to Las Vegas.

Werner Herzog Didn’t Want To Know Hulk Hogan’s Real Name

In the documentary, Herzog discusses the performative nature of humans and the separation of performance and reality. He reflects on Hogan's dual nature, stating "In the case of Hulk Hogan, it would be interesting to look into the performer and into the real Hulk Hogan, if he still exists."

When asked directly if he knows Hogan's real name, Herzog gave a memorable response that speaks to his philosophical approach to the subject matter. "I don't even want to know his real name," he replied. "Keep it your secret." This answer perfectly encapsulates Herzog's artistic perspective on Hogan as a character and cultural phenomenon, prioritizing the mystique of the performer over biographical details.

All four episodes of "Real American" are now streaming on Netflix. The documentary explores various aspects of Hogan's life and career, featuring interviews and perspectives from multiple figures within professional wrestling. During the documentary, Triple H revealed that he was the person who called Hogan to cut ties with him after tapes leaked of him using racist language. Triple H was able to separate the performer from the real person during what was clearly a difficult professional situation.

Stephanie McMahon Reveals Surprising WWE Hall Of Fame Insight

Stephanie McMahon, a WWE Hall of Famer herself, appeared on an episode of her podcast "What's Your Story?" where she opened up about the philosophy behind the McMahon family's role in professional wrestling.

During the discussion, McMahon explained the foundational principle that guided how her family operated within WWE. She stated that the McMahons were never supposed to be in the Hall of Fame, noting this was something she was raised to understand from an early age.

"Well, the McMahons were never supposed to be in the Hall of Fame, as I was always brought up. We were characters that were intentionally designed to get other talent over. So it was never about us. And that's just sort of like ingrained in my brain," Stephanie explained.

This insight reveals the selfless approach the McMahon family took toward their on-screen personas, prioritizing the elevation of wrestlers and talent rather than seeking personal accolades and recognition. The concept was deeply embedded in Stephanie's mindset from childhood, forming a core part of her professional philosophy within the wrestling industry.

Despite this original intention, Stephanie McMahon was ultimately inducted into the WWE Hall of Fame, joining her father Vince McMahon and other family members who have received the honor, demonstrating how the landscape of professional wrestling and the McMahon family's role within it has evolved over time.

Jerry Lawler Admits Taz Was Upset Over Jokes About Him Being Small on Commentary

Jerry Lawler is reflecting on his time as a WWE commentator and acknowledging that not all of his on-air remarks landed the way he intended. During an appearance on the Not Sam podcast, the legendary announcer discussed his commentary style during WWE's peak eras and admitted that his tendency to push boundaries sometimes created real tension with other performers.

Lawler was candid about his approach, explaining that while he always aimed to connect with the audience, some of his comments ended up being taken more personally than he anticipated. He explained that certain wrestlers in the business still thought he harbored actual animosity toward them based on things he said during broadcasts.

"I just—I tried to remember some of the things that I said about those guys, but I sort of went over the line on some of the stuff. I haven't—I mean, some of the guys in the business still thought I hated them," Lawler said.

The King of Memphis then highlighted a specific instance involving Taz, the former ECW World Heavyweight Champion. Lawler recalled making jokes about Taz's stature during commentary and acknowledged that the Human Suplex Machine was not pleased with those remarks at the time.

"I'll never forget when Taz came in on their thing, and me and Vince were doing commentary, and I said, 'Whoa, this is Taz—this guy's…' I don't know, I made some jokes about him being small. And he didn't like it—no, he never liked it," Lawler recounted.

Despite Taz's negative reaction to the on-air comments, Lawler emphasized that the tension did not result in lasting heat between the two. According to Lawler, they were eventually able to move past the situation and maintain a positive working relationship.

"But we got along fine. It was just—I just went after the real stuff that the people at home could relate to, and it all worked out well," Lawler explained.

Lawler's candid reflection illustrates how wrestling commentary operated during certain eras, where pushing boundaries and making edgy remarks were often considered part of the job. However, his acknowledgment also demonstrates how that approach sometimes blurred the line between entertainment and what could be perceived as personal attacks on performers.

By revisiting these moments now, Lawler is not attempting to deflect responsibility but rather owning his past behavior and offering perspective on how his commentary style evolved throughout his career.

How do you release Kairi Sane? | Column

Kairi Sane being part of WWE roster cuts on Friday is a riddle that's difficult to solve. At the time of her departure, there was no evidence she was on the chopping block at all. WWE had invested screen time in her story, and she'd even beaten IYO SKY in her last singles match on the 4/13 Raw. Everyone was waiting for her to finally stand up for herself against Asuka, leading to what many assumed would be a long-running program between the two.

By all public accounts, nobody saw Sane's departure coming. IYO SKY mentioned this in her post to Sane on Saturday, writing: "I understand that everyone, as an athlete, eventually faces a turning point when thinking about their life and career, but I feel so heartbroken that it came at such an unexpected time." Bayley wrote that she was having trouble "wrapping her head around this one" in regards to Sane's departure as well. Bayley stated: "I'm having a hard time finding words for this one. I love you and it was an honor to tell stories with you." When exactly the decision was made that Kairi would be part of the cuts is not clear.

The situation with Sane and Aleister Black's releases share interesting similarities. Sane and Black being cut from WWE was either a late decision, or the old idea of beating someone before they leave the territory is not known to TKO. Black was released after having won his final three singles matches against Randy Orton on 2/20, Sami Zayn on 3/20, and Matt Cardona on 4/3. Even when he left AEW, he'd lost his final two matches to Adam Cole and Adam Copeland, after having only dropped one throughout the rest of his time with the company. Sane was coming off a singles win over IYO SKY on the 4/13 Raw. Her absence will be a giant elephant in the room during anything to do with Asuka now for fans who had been invested in the story the two had been telling. All of this suggests WWE either didn't prepare for Sane or Black leaving, didn't know it was going to happen, or it was decided at the last minute.

The decision to cut Kairi Sane may not feel like a Triple H decision, but nobody forced his hand here. Roster cuts are a Triple H decision with Nick Khan input. Dave Meltzer has been explaining this to people online in the last few days. According to his analysis: "Nobody above Levesque is saying who to cut. Maybe Nick Khan, maybe not. Ari Emanuel isn't sitting there saying we have to fire Zelina and Kairi. By and large the decision maker, for good or for bad, here is Paul. It's like in other years where people blamed this person or that person when the decision was 100 percent Vince. There's not a chance TKO is ordering who to cut. It is possible they've got an unofficial salary cap and Levesque as the manager has to keep the number below that. Most likely the salary total would be a Khan decision, and as far as the key in the personnel decision, that's Levesque although Khan having input and others having input is likely. It's like Vince would consult with a few people but at the end, it was Vince's call, not evil Jim Ross or evil Johnny Ace or evil JJ Dillon."

Meltzer continued with this assessment: "You think a guy at the level of Ari Emanuel is deciding on whether or not to keep Kairi Sane? You're kidding yourself." This clarifies the chain of command and responsibility for Sane's unexpected release.

Looking ahead, whatever comes next, it will likely be whatever Kairi Sane wants. Everyone will want her. Tony Khan will want her. Stardom will want her back. TNA probably thinks she'd make an excellent addition to the Undead Realm, and Juggalo Championship Wrestling would love to put her in an angle that has something to do with who controls the company. Kairi Sane's departure from WWE isn't bad for wrestling as a whole, it's just bad for WWE itself.

Chelsea Green: From WWE Release to Reinvention

Chelsea Green: From WWE Release to Reinvention

Just days after a mass release of WWE talent, I caught myself scrolling through old independent wrestling matches on YouTube. It started with GCW Presents Joey Janela's Spring Break X. Then about an hour later I stumbled across some Matt Cardona GCW videos. What stood out most was not just Cardona's transformation from WWE to the independents and back to WWE—it was Chelsea Green. It caught me off guard in the best way. Seeing where she was compared to where she is now made me stop and think about how impressive her journey really was—not just returning to WWE, but turning that second chance into a legitimate rise to stardom.

There is something fitting about the chaos that follows Chelsea Green. Not just the character with the exaggerated, loud, "can I speak to your manager?!" energy, but the journey itself. Because if you zoom out, her return to WWE was not a clean redemption arc. It was messy, unpredictable and, at times, unfair. And that is exactly why it worked.

When WWE released Green in 2021, it felt abrupt. She had barely scratched the surface of what she could be on that stage. After a November 2020 debut, she was released just a few months later while sidelined with a broken wrist. The odds seemed stacked against her. For many wrestlers, that is where momentum dies. Green did not disappear; she recalibrated.

Across the independent scene and other promotions, she leaned harder into what made her different. The "Hot Mess" persona that made her a fan favorite evolved into something sharper, more intentional and impossible to ignore. A return to Impact Wrestling followed. Teaming with Deonna Purrazzo, the duo captured the Impact Knockouts World Tag Team Championship on August 12, 2022, holding the titles for 56 days. More importantly, her attitude and charisma did not change—they grew. That evolution quickly became an attraction for those watching back in WWE. She was not trying to prove she could hang in the ring; everyone knows she can wrestle. She was proving she could stand out.

Standing out is what made WWE pick up the phone. By the time she returned in 2023, Green was not the same performer who had been released. She understood something crucial: Being memorable matters just as much as being technically sound. Almost immediately, she carved out a role. While others chased dominance, Green embraced disruption. Complaining, scheming and overreacting, she became a character audiences could recognize instantly—one that you cannot ignore. That kind of clarity is rare and valuable.

For years, the knock on Green was simple: She was entertaining, but not someone you built a division around. Then she started stacking accomplishments. Her first major statement came in the tag division, capturing the WWE Women's Tag Team Championships alongside Sonya Deville and later Piper Niven. She quickly became a fan favorite. The real turning point came with the introduction of the Women's United States Championship. Green became the inaugural titleholder and later the first two-time champion. The narrative shifted. When you're that talented, it's really hard to ignore. She did not abandon the comedy; she proved it could carry gold.

What separates Green is not just the character—it is the commitment. Every entrance, every segment and every exaggerated reaction feels deliberate. There is no half-measure. From podcasts and interviews to dumpsters, red carpets and the WWE ring, every appearance feels intentional. There is 100 percent effort no matter what the task at hand is. She has a knack for turning the smallest moments into something memorable. In an era where many blur the line between performer and person, Green leans fully into performance—and that is what makes it work. It would not be surprising if Hollywood comes calling.

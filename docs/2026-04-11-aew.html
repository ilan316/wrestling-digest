🔥 Darby Allin on Four Pillars of AEW

Darby Allin is pushing back against one of professional wrestling's most recognizable labels. As one of AEW's original breakout stars alongside MJF, Jack Perry, and Sammy Guevara, Allin has long been grouped into the so-called "four pillars" of AEW. However, during recent interviews, Allin made it abundantly clear he's not a fan of the term and believes it doesn't reflect the full picture of the company's rise.

"I really don't concern myself honestly too much with that term," Allin said. "I really don't like that term, four pillars, because I believe it takes more than four to actually get this thing up and going." Allin didn't dismiss the group's contributions but emphasized that AEW's growth has always been a collective effort from the entire roster, not just a select few names.

Allin highlighted the unprecedented nature of AEW's accomplishment. "The beauty to me with being on the ground level of AEW is you could see when the company got to the highest points, it was just through us. It was not through us as in the four pillars, but through everybody on the roster working together because this wasn't a machine that was built years and years and years ago. This was only in 2019. So the fact that we got to 81,000 plus people at Wembley is incredible. And I think people really don't stop and think about that."

The perspective comes from Allin's unique vantage point of experiencing AEW's ups and downs since day one. For him, it's always been about the team dynamic rather than individual accolades. "As high as we go and then as low as we go and now we're going back up, it's so rewarding when you're a part of something from the ground up and it truly feels like your baby," Allin explained.

Ego management emerged as a critical theme in Allin's comments about the company's culture. "You can't have an ego with this because when there's egos that's when the ship starts to sink. But if we all work together it pulls it up and you can feel that energy," he said. This philosophy extends to his broader view of the AEW locker room, where he believes maintaining focus on the collective good is essential.

"I just feel like as long as everybody can keep the egos in check and actually understand that this is a team effort, and if you just take your ego out of the equation, dude, honestly, that's the only thing that's going to hold anybody back," Allin told TMZ Sports. "To me, that's not ever what AEW was built on. It was never built on, 'Hey, I want to be this A-list celebrity.' It was always built on, 'Hey, I just want to give the best pro wrestling.'"

Allin contextualized AEW's mission within wrestling history, drawing parallels to the work of wrestlers like Tiger Mask and Dynamite Kid in Japan during the 1980s. "The fact that we're able to do our version of wrestling on national TV in the US, and it's not like – because, at the time, New Japan also, way back like in 2014, the in-ring wrestling was so amazing, but it was in Japan. But now that the fact that type of wrestling is in the US every single week, that's what the beauty of AEW is to me is that we can actually form our version of this art on national TV every week. So, it's nothing that I take for granted."

Beyond his commentary on company dynamics, Allin also offered insights into his personal approach to his physically demanding wrestling style. Known for performing some of the craziest stunts in modern wrestling, including diving through a pane of glass at AEW Revolution 2024 and smashing his car at Full Gear 2024, Allin has earned a reputation for an unusually high pain tolerance.

When asked about his pain management and what medical professionals have told him, Allin attributed much of his ability to recover to mindset rather than physical anomalies. "Nothing. Everybody always says I need to be tested in a laboratory because I'm able to just do the most outrageous stuff and keep coming back. I'm not lying to you when I say this. I don't take any pain pills. I don't drink. I don't smoke. Literally, it's more mind than anything. Because if your mind goes, your body follows."

Allin emphasized his strict regimen of physical recovery and mental discipline. "So I just keep my mind very sharp with my meditating on my physical recovery aspects that I do. I am very strict on all of that, and I feel incredible. Every day I'm not wrestling, I'm skating. If I'm not skating, I'm jumping off a four-wheeler, jumping off a roof, or something. It never ends. That's the thing. It never ends. It's not like I just sit at home and watch cat compilation videos on YouTube. Like I'm always moving, which I think is really good."

Outside the wrestling ring, Allin continues to pursue extreme physical challenges. He successfully climbed Mount Everest on May 18, 2025, reaching the summit after weeks of climbing. When asked if he'd consider attempting the climb again, he expressed his desire to repeat the experience. "Yeah, absolutely. You can only climb it two months out of the year essentially. That season is coming up right now in April, so everybody is going to go climb Mount Everest right now. I'm looking at the videos and I miss it so much. Everybody's getting ready to do it, and I'm like 'Man, I miss it so much.' Yeah, it was incredible."

However, any future Everest attempt would need to wait at least a year given the narrow climbing window during spring months, which would most likely affect his schedule in 2026. For now, Allin's focus remains on his wrestling career at AEW.

Looking to the immediate future, Allin is set to compete at AEW Dynasty on Sunday, April 12, in Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada. He will face Andrade El Idolo in a match with significant implications. Should Allin defeat Andrade, MJF has agreed to grant him a future shot at the AEW World Championship. Additionally, Kenny Omega, who is challenging MJF for the title at Dynasty, has stated he will honor the same stipulation should he win the championship.

Allin's recent comments underscore his role as a leader within the AEW locker room who prioritizes collective success over individual achievement. His rejection of the "four pillars" label and emphasis on team effort reflects a philosophy that has shaped AEW since its inception in 2019. For Allin, the company's accomplishments—from building a dedicated fanbase to drawing record crowds like the 81,000 plus at Wembley—represent a shared achievement that transcends any individual accolade or label.

🔥 Will Ospreay AEW Career and Health

Will Ospreay has opened up extensively about his recent neck surgery and the major adjustments he is making to his wrestling career while continuing his work with AEW. The interviews reveal both the serious nature of his injury and his determination to protect his long-term health moving forward.

Ospreay explained that his neck injury became far more severe than many realized before he finally underwent surgery. He described losing feeling in his left arm during matches without fully understanding the extent of the damage at the time. According to Ospreay, there would be moments in matches where he would take a bump and simply lose control of his arm, which was an alarming physical response his body was experiencing. However, since undergoing surgery, he has returned to the ring and has taken major bumps without those symptoms returning, giving him confidence that his recovery is progressing well. He noted that after being thrown off the top rope with a belly-to-belly suplex and traveling across more than half the ring, his neck remained intact and felt normal.

The surgery has forced Ospreay to make permanent changes to his in-ring style. Most notably, he has removed the Shooting Star Press from his arsenal because he no longer trusts his neck to handle the risk. He explained that executing the move properly requires him to fully arch his back and look back, which puts excessive stress on his neck. While he can still perform other flipping movements like moonsaults and front flips, the Shooting Star Press crosses a line he is no longer willing to test. Ospreay stated that he approaches his matches now while keeping his anxiety in check regarding certain moves, and he simply does not want to risk or test overextending his neck.

This decision becomes even more significant when considering Chris Jericho's warning from years earlier. Jericho had reached out to Ospreay after witnessing dangerous moments during his early career, particularly after an incident where Ospreay performed a Spanish Fly off the apron against Marty Scurll in April, during which his foot caught the rope and his neck folded underneath his own body weight. Jericho told him directly to stop taking such risks, and Ospreay now acknowledges how difficult it is to ignore advice from someone genuinely looking out for his health and safety. With surgery now behind him, Jericho's earlier concerns feel vindicated, and Ospreay appears focused on heeding that veteran's wisdom.

Despite his elevated status in AEW, Ospreay has made another significant life decision by refusing to relocate to the United States. While many expected him to move to America once his career reached this level, Ospreay explained that he never felt that was the right move for his life. He remains based in the United Kingdom, which he acknowledged is unusual given the conventional wisdom in professional wrestling. His primary concern involves his son and the uncertainty of moving him into an American school system he does not fully understand. He stated that uprooting his son's life and taking him to America simply does not sit right with him, as the intimidation factor of navigating an unfamiliar system is too great. Ospreay admitted that anxiety plays a part in his thinking, and he feels more comfortable staying somewhere familiar where he understands how daily life works. His decision demonstrates that even at the highest level of wrestling, family stability can outweigh career convenience.

Looking forward, Ospreay has discussed his goals and aspirations within AEW. He has been making the media rounds to promote AEW Dynasty 2026 and has touched on several topics including his ambitions. One of his stated objectives is to hit the Tiger Driver on Jon Moxley at Dynasty, describing it as more of a blind rage with one objective rather than a calculated game plan. He also reflected on his matches against Kenny Omega, noting that while they have won one match each, something bothers him about needing outside assistance to win their second encounter. He expressed a desire to someday be in a position where he could honestly say he believes he could beat Kenny Omega cleanly. Ospreay described how watching Kenny Omega rise to prominence at the Tokyo Dome inspired him to set Omega as the wall he needed to climb, and he continues to be inspired by Omega's comeback from a near-death health situation and his current peak performance level.

Additionally, Ospreay has discussed the possibility of starting a new faction in AEW. He acknowledged that founding factions is something he has experience with, having created the United Empire in Japan with Great-O-Khan. The thought of starting his own group in AEW has crossed his mind, and he has spoken frequently with Speedball Mike Bailey and Jet Kevin Knight about potentially forming a good guy faction. He envisions a group that would serve as a counterbalance to the backstage chaos, where when the bad guys are causing trouble, his group would step in and discourage such behavior. However, starting something new without calling it the United Empire would not feel quite right to him.

Regarding his match with Vader, Ospreay was more reserved, admitting that the day was quite difficult for him and that Vader was an odd character to deal with. While he does not want to speak ill of someone no longer with them, he found it somewhat amusing to say he had one of Vader's last matches.

Overall, Ospreay's recent interviews paint a picture of a wrestler who is taking his long-term health seriously while continuing to pursue his ambitions in AEW. His willingness to drop signature moves, remain based outside the United States for family reasons, and reflect on previous warnings about his risky style all suggest a maturing approach to his career that balances professional success with personal well-being.

🔥 MJF and AEW World Championship

Maxwell Jacob Friedman sits atop All Elite Wrestling as the current AEW World Champion, but his position faces a significant challenge as he prepares to defend his title against Kenny Omega at AEW Dynasty on April 12, 2026. This rematch holds special significance as it marks the second encounter between these two competitors over the richest prize in AEW.

Their history dates back to October 28, 2023, when the two men squared off on AEW Collision in what was, at the time, the biggest match in that show's short history and remains one of only three AEW Men's World Championship matches to take place on the Saturday night program. In that encounter, MJF was dangerously close to surpassing Omega's then-record as the longest reigning AEW Men's World Champion in history. Despite being chased by contenders like Samoa Joe, Jay White, and Wardlow throughout 2023, MJF maintained his grip on the championship until Omega stepped up to do what the rest of the AEW roster had failed to accomplish. Omega decided to ensure that Friedman couldn't possibly break his record by challenging him on the last possible date before the anniversary of Omega's record reign expired.

That October 2023 match was a spectacle that lived up to its billing. It featured an incredible amount of action with both men bringing their full arsenals to bear. MJF, despite being technically positioned as a babyface at the time, played the heel role expertly, which has always suited his wrestling style better. He strutted around the ring, poked Omega in the eye, and attempted sportsmanlike handshakes while planning to strike. MJF even hit surprise offensive moves including a Fosbury Flop in the early going and a Poisonrana to escape Omega's One-Winged Angel finisher. Omega responded by bringing power-based offense to the match, including a devastating powerbomb on the barricade and another through a table on the outside that provided one of the most satisfying visuals in professional wrestling. By the match's conclusion, both men were exhausted yet still attempting to land their biggest moves, though the match ran out of steam as interference from Don Callis played a role in the finish.

Now, nearly three years later, MJF and Omega are set to clash once again with championship stakes on the line. In the lead-up to Dynasty, the two men have engaged in considerable verbal warfare, with MJF attempting to downplay Omega's current condition by bringing up that his past battle with diverticulitis might not be entirely resolved. Many observers expected their first match to main event a pay-per-view, but Omega prioritized ensuring MJF couldn't break his record, which necessitated the hastily arranged Collision encounter.

Beyond his championship pursuits, MJF has positioned himself as one of the top stars in AEW capable of building his own faction. His history with group affiliations is mixed, but there is significant potential for MJF to start a new stable that could become a prominently featured act. Several notable wrestlers have been suggested as potential members, including Wardlow, who previously served as MJF's bodyguard before betraying him and going on to become a three-time TNT Champion. Wardlow is currently sidelined with a torn pectoral muscle but could potentially reunite with MJF upon his return.

Other potential faction members include Big Bill and Bryan Keith of Paid In Full, who were proteges to Chris Jericho before he took time off from AEW. MJF, who introduced Big Bill to the company, could recruit them. Alec Price and Jordan Oliver, known as Bustah and The Brain on the independent scene, were signed to AEW in early 2026 with their signing originally announced by MJF following a title defense against Price on a Limitless Wrestling show. Harley Cameron has received significant praise from MJF, who called her "the most improved women's wrestler in the world today" and predicted she would become an AEW Women's World Champion. Cameron, a former inaugural AEW Women's World Tag Team Champion alongside Willow Nightingale, could find renewed opportunity in MJF's stable now that her tag team partnership has dissolved.

Kris Statlander, a former AEW Women's World Champion and longtime friend of MJF, has been going through a recent downswing after losing her title to Thekla in February and could seek renewed momentum through an alliance with the AEW World Champion. The Demand, consisting of Ricochet, Bishop Kaun, and Toa Liona, represents another possible merger point, as MJF has frequently interacted with Ricochet on social media and traded praise with him consistently. Finally, FTR (Dax Harwood and Cash Wheeler) and Stokely Hathaway could reunite with MJF, as he previously formed The Pinnacle with Wardlow, Shawn Spears, and FTR back in 2021, though that faction didn't maintain its prominence for an extended period.

MJF's success as a performer stems largely from his exceptional promo abilities and his unique character work. Speaking on the podcast "Games with Names" with former NFL player Julien Edelman, MJF detailed his meticulous process for developing his cutting insults and verbal attacks. According to MJF, he spends hours at home thinking of horrible things to say to his opponents, maintaining an extensive notes app filled with what he describes as "a cornucopia of ruthlessly unhinged, mean things to say." He organizes these insults meticulously, listing each wrestler's name followed by approximately seventy brutal barbs that are ultra-specific to that individual competitor.

MJF acknowledged that while some of these insults are crude and contain references to sensitive topics like sexually-transmitted infections, many are so wrestler-specific that they wouldn't translate to a wider audience. He admitted that his cutting remarks sometimes hurt wrestlers' feelings, with his boss occasionally approaching him to express concern about a particular performer's hurt feelings. To this, MJF responded with characteristic dismissiveness, stating "I kind of don't give a s--t," and noted that such interactions tend to escalate tensions, which he views as part of professional wrestling's natural progression.

In discussing the importance of promo work in professional wrestling, MJF cited WWE Hall of Famer "Stone Cold" Steve Austin as a major influence, describing Austin as like a "father figure" to him. According to MJF, Austin understood that the most crucial element of professional wrestling was establishing a well-defined character that audiences could grasp and connect with emotionally. This philosophy has clearly shaped MJF's approach to his own character development and championship presentation.

The AEW women's division currently features Thekla as the reigning AEW Women's World Champion, a position she captured in less than a year after her arrival in mid-2025. She won the title in February by defeating Kris Statlander in a strap match on Dynamite and has been praised by AEW owner Tony Khan as the "MVP of the new arrivals as world champion." Khan noted that while Thekla arrived with the goal of winning the world title, her first priority in the company was pursuing a rivalry with Jamie Hayter, a longtime AEW star and former world champion. Khan expressed enthusiasm for their upcoming Dynasty match, calling it "a really compelling, exciting rivalry." Thekla's rapid ascension and immediate focus on high-profile opposition demonstrates the company's confidence in her abilities and drawing power.

The broader context of AEW's current championship landscape shows a company investing heavily in both established talent and newer acquisitions. Beyond Thekla's women's title reign, the AEW World Trios Champions consist of Mistico, Speedball Bailey, and Kevin Knight, all signed within the past year. Khan expressed particular enthusiasm about these signings, noting they represent different places in their careers, experiences, and backgrounds, yet all demonstrate exceptional in-ring ability. Khan stated he is a massive fan of Speedball Bailey and marveled that these wrestlers have only been in AEW for approximately a year given their impressive impact. The trios champions will defend their titles against the Dogs of War on AEW Collision on April 11, the night before Dynasty.

As AEW approaches Dynasty on April 12, 2026, the event promises significant championship action. MJF's defense against Kenny Omega will serve as a high-profile encounter between two of the company's most talented and charismatic performers, with considerable history and stakes attached. The storyline builds on their previous encounters and Omega's concern about his record being broken, creating natural narrative tension. Simultaneously, Thekla's women's championship defense against Jamie Hayter will showcase the depth of AEW's women's division and the company's ability to elevate newer talent while maintaining respect for its established performers. These championship matches, combined with the trios title defense, demonstrate AEW's focus on strong championship storylines and competitive balance across its various title divisions heading into one of its major pay-per-view events.

🔥 Kenny Omega AEW Return and Health

Kenny Omega's comeback to professional wrestling has been a long and challenging journey, marked by serious health complications and a premature return that nearly derailed his overall recovery. The AEW star opened up about his experience during an interview with Haley Miller of Vice Magazine ahead of the 2026 AEW Dynasty pay-per-view event.

Omega spent all of 2024 out of action battling diverticulitis, an intestinal condition that was nearly fatal. He made his return to in-ring competition on January 5, 2025, at New Japan's Wrestle Dynasty event at the Tokyo Dome, facing Gabe Kidd in a match that would later be voted Match of the Year in the Wrestling Observer Newsletter awards. However, Omega now acknowledges that his decision to return to competition so quickly was a mistake that extended his overall recovery timeline.

"I'm just still a little tiny bit on the mend, because it's a process that takes a long time," Omega explained. "Plus, I came back way too early, so I hindered my own full recovery because of the schedule that I took probably a year and a half too soon, which it is what it is, but here we are."

The premature return made Omega's comeback significantly more difficult than it needed to be. The struggle to regain his physical capabilities was particularly challenging, as he found it difficult to recapture the energy and strength that defined his career. "Finally, I've been able to find the equation," he said. "I've been able to figure out a diet, a workout plan, and a way to stay as healthy as possible and get results, which is the most important part. It was really difficult to regain usable energy. It was tough to feel like I had any spring left in my step."

Despite the ongoing recovery process, Omega has been experiencing encouraging signs of improvement. He described the inconsistent nature of his current condition, where some days feel significantly better than others. "There are days when I don't feel so great still, but then there are days when I do feel great," he said. "When I'm at the gym, whether it be with a large group of people, or whether I'm just with one training partner, even heck, just by myself, I'm monitoring myself when I have those great days. I think, 'My goodness, where is this energy coming from today? Where is the strength coming from today?' This is the type of stuff the old Kenny Omega could be doing."

The positive developments in Omega's health recovery come as he prepares for one of the biggest matches of his career. He is scheduled to challenge MJF for the AEW World Championship in the main event of AEW Dynasty 2026, taking place on Sunday, April 12, 2026, live from Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada.

Beyond his current health focus, Omega has also been reflecting on his legendary career in New Japan Pro Wrestling. During an appearance on the Battleground Podcast, he discussed some of his favorite rivalries from his time in Japan. While acknowledging that many fans default to his series with Kazuchika Okada, Omega highlighted that hardcore New Japan fans particularly enjoyed his matches against Tetsuya Naito and Tomohiro Ishii.

However, Omega expressed particular pride in his long-term storytelling with his former Golden Lovers partner Kota Ibushi. "Some of the people know me more because of the Golden Lovers stuff, which is also fantastic," he said. "And I think that was some of the most proud storytelling that I've ever done in my career because the onus was all on us to continue that story forward and to always sort of implant that in the things we had done when maybe the promoters or the bookers didn't see the value in it."

What made the Golden Lovers story particularly special to Omega was the way he and Ibushi managed to tell it across multiple promotions over an extended period. "We had always sort of wanted to not insult the fans intelligence," Omega explained. "Regardless of what promotion we were in, we wanted to show that there was this interconnectivity and these human emotions, and that life doesn't end just because of promotion changes. Relationships don't end because of promotion changes. And it was cool that we could see this multiple-promotion-spanning storyline that took about a decade to see the grand finale and to be able to be at the forefront of an actual real long-term story where the onus was completely on us to tell."

🔥 Alex Windsor Joins AEW

Alex Windsor Explains Why AEW Was The Company She Wanted To Join

Alex Windsor didn't hesitate when asked why she chose AEW. During an interview with The Wrestling Classic, she broke down exactly what made the company stand out and why signing there felt like the right move for her career.

While discussing her decision, Windsor explained that AEW's presentation was a major factor. She admitted that she didn't grow up deeply watching wrestling, but said AEW's style made it easy to imagine how it could inspire someone new to the sport. "I think it was a great—not an alternative to WWE—but I just think how it looked. I can imagine, obviously, like I said, I hadn't really seen wrestling before. I can imagine if I saw an AEW show as a fan as a kid and that was my first introduction to wrestling, I would want to be a wrestler after watching it," she explained.

She continued by pointing to AEW's matches and overall presentation as key reasons why she felt drawn to the company. In her view, AEW reflects what professional wrestling is supposed to be. "I just think how it's presented, how the matches are, I just think as a company it embodies what great wrestling is," Windsor stated.

Windsor made it clear that AEW wasn't just another option—it was the place she believed wrestlers should want to be. She said the company's reputation and identity made it feel like the right destination for anyone serious about making it in the business. "So I just think whether you're a fan or you know, a wrestler who's, you know, aspiring to, you know, to get signed somewhere, I just think AEW is the place that again it doesn't lie like it's where the best wrestle and I just think as a company I just think it that's how it comes across and that's I think anyone who wants to to be anything in wrestling, honestly, I think it is the company to go for."

Windsor's comments give a clear look at what pushed her toward AEW and why the company stood out when it came time to choose her next step in wrestling. With more talent continuing to sign, her words show how AEW's presentation is still playing a big role in attracting new names.

AEW Dynasty 2026 is set to take place on Sunday, April 12, 2026, live from Rogers Arena in Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada. The Zero Hour preshow begins at 7:00 PM ET / 4:00 PM PT, with the main card bell time set for 8:00 PM ET / 5:00 PM PT. This will be the third annual Dynasty pay-per-view event and the first AEW pay-per-view ever held in Vancouver, making it a historic occasion for the promotion.

For viewers across different time zones, the event presents various viewing windows. In the US Central time zone, the Zero Hour begins at 6:00 PM with the main card at 7:00 PM. Mountain time viewers will see the preshow at 5:00 PM and main card at 6:00 PM. Pacific time fans get the earliest start with Zero Hour at 4:00 PM and main card at 5:00 PM. International viewers have significantly different times: United Kingdom fans will catch the preshow at midnight and main card at 1:00 AM Monday morning. Central Europe times are 1:00 AM for Zero Hour and 2:00 AM for the main card. India Standard Time shows 5:30 AM for Zero Hour and 6:30 AM for the main card. Australian Eastern Daylight Time offers 10:00 AM for Zero Hour and 11:00 AM for the main card on Monday, April 13. New Zealand Standard Time provides noon for Zero Hour and 1:00 PM for the main card.

The Zero Hour preshow will be available free on YouTube, X (Twitter), and Facebook for all regions. The main card viewing options vary by region. In the United States, fans can watch via HBO Max at $39.99 or through Amazon Prime, PPV.com, Sling, and YouTube at $49.99. United Kingdom viewers can access the event through MyAEW at £19.99 PPV or £14.99 monthly subscription, or through Amazon Prime and TrillerTV. Australia and New Zealand audiences can watch on TrillerTV and MyAEW for approximately $35 AUD. For the rest of the world, PPV.com, TrillerTV, and MyAEW offer regional pricing options.

The event has already sold over 10,000 tickets. The main event features MJF defending the AEW World Championship against Kenny Omega in what is being called arguably one of the most anticipated AEW World Championship matches in recent years. Another major matchup sees Adam Copeland and Christian Cage challenging FTR for the AEW Tag Team Championship on home soil. The city's crowd is expected to heavily support the Canadian legends in this match.

Tony Khan has been actively promoting Dynasty 2026 across media appearances. During an interview with The Masked Man Show, Khan discussed several prominent matches and recent talent acquisitions. Regarding Thekla, the current AEW Women's World Champion, Khan called her the MVP of AEW's most recent batch of signings. He stated: "I think when you look at the champions on the women's side, I would say absolutely Thekla would be the MVP of the new arrivals as world champion. And she's been fantastic."

Khan elaborated on Thekla's trajectory since arriving in AEW, noting that while she arrived with the goal of winning the world title, her first target was Jamie Hayter. Khan explained: "When [Thekla] arrived, she had a goal of winning the world title, but the very first thing she did was go after Jamie Hayter. And Jamie Hayter been a huge part of AEW, one of our most popular stars, a former world champion. And Jamie Hayter versus Thekla I think is a really compelling, exciting rivalry, and I'm excited for their match this weekend at AEW Dynasty. I think Thekla's been a huge part of the company on the women's side."

Khan also praised the current AEW Trios Champions, who were among recent signings: Speedball Mike Bailey, The Jet Kevin Knight, and Mistico. Khan stated: "All three of them. They represent very different places in their careers, different experiences, different backgrounds, but three great wrestlers that we've signed in the past year in Mistico and Speedball Bailey and of course the Jet Kevin Knight. I can't believe those two have only been around for a year. I'm the biggest Speedball fan in the world. They've had an incredible run." Khan's enthusiasm for these performers underscores the success AEW has had with its recent international acquisitions.

Don Callis Family Injuries in AEW

Don Callis has spoken out about what he perceives as an unfair double standard in how fans react to injuries involving different members of his wrestling family. During an interview, Callis addressed the recent meniscus injury sustained by Kyle Fletcher, which is expected to sideline the athlete for several months.

Callis expressed his frustration using a pointed comparison with Kenny Omega. According to Callis, whenever Omega sustains an injury, no matter how minor, the wrestling community and fanbase react with overwhelming concern and sympathy. Callis used a hangnail as an example of the type of minor injury that would trigger widespread worry about Omega's wellbeing.

In stark contrast, Callis highlighted that Fletcher's injury, which he characterized as catastrophic and horrible, has received virtually no attention or sympathy from fans. The stable leader suggested that this lack of reaction is particularly unjust given the severity of Fletcher's condition and the extended recovery period he faces.

Callis made clear his intent was to point out what he views as an allegory for the disparate nature of fan reactions based on the prominence or drawing power of individual wrestlers. He emphasized that he finds this situation unfortunate, suggesting that wrestlers in his stable deserve the same level of concern and support that Kenny Omega receives from the fanbase.

The interview also touched on other remarks from Callis, including comments about wanting to take an eye from Nick Jackson to wear as a talisman, indicating Callis was in character during portions of the interview promoting ongoing storylines.

Speedball Mike Bailey on Exclusive Contracts

AEW superstar Speedball Mike Bailey has voiced strong opinions about the concept of exclusive contracts in professional wrestling. Speaking to Adrian Hernandez in a recent interview, Bailey explained that while he understands the necessity of exclusive deals, he believes they are ultimately harmful to both wrestlers and their development as performers.

"This is my hot take when it comes to professional wrestling but exclusive contracts are bad for pro wrestlers, for their development," Bailey stated. "I understand there is a need for them. The ecosystem, the companies, it doesn't work without them, and that makes sense. But if everyone in the world could wrestle absolutely anyone else regardless of contracts, just imagine the match-ups you would see."

Bailey acknowledged that while unlimited cross-promotional wrestling may not be feasible, the closest alternative currently available is what AEW is implementing through strategic partnerships. He highlighted the company's collaborations with CMLL and New Japan Pro Wrestling as examples of how to showcase top wrestlers from around the globe while still maintaining necessary business structures.

Beyond the contract issue, Bailey addressed another significant concern in professional wrestling: the lack of diversity among champions. He pointed out that requiring wrestlers to speak English as a first language to be considered championship material is both ridiculous and counterproductive to the industry's growth and global appeal.

"It's been a problem in professional wrestling for a long time, the lack of diversity in terms of seeing champions from different cultures and races," Bailey explained. "And I think the fact that in order to be considered eligible to become a champion in pro wrestling, you need to speak English, especially as a first language, is completely ridiculous and extremely counterproductive."

Despite identifying these longstanding issues, Bailey expressed optimism about recent changes within the wrestling industry. He credited inter-promotional partnerships and the expansion of opportunities for international talent as driving forces behind the gradual elimination of these barriers.

"And I'm glad that it's become less and less of a thing, and I think it's due to partnerships like that and opening up the playing fields to literally letting anyone who is the best at pro wrestling wrestle at the very top, as it should be," Bailey said. "And I think putting any kind of barrier on that is absolutely ridiculous."

Bailey concluded by endorsing AEW's current direction, noting that other wrestling companies are following the company's lead in removing artificial limitations on talent. He believes these industry-wide changes represent exactly what professional wrestling needs to thrive and reach its full potential on a global scale.

AEW and CAP Pat Buck on TNA Talent

AEW and Create A Pro's Pat Buck has publicly criticized TNA and Carlos Silva over the cancellation of a scheduled main event match between MJF and Nic Nemeth for Create A Pro's May 1 Monumental Moment show. The situation developed this week when rumors emerged that TNA might be pulling wrestlers from already announced independent matches with AEW talent, leading to the cancellation of multiple high-profile bouts.

The fallout was swift. Leon Slater's anticipated WrestleMania week showdown with Ricochet was cancelled, as was the MJF versus Nemeth match for Create A Pro. Bryan Myers, who co-owns Create A Pro with Buck, announced changes to the May 1 card on Wednesday, confirming that both MJF and Nemeth would still appear on the show but no longer against each other.

Pat Buck, who serves as an AEW talent development executive alongside his role at Create A Pro, provided a detailed statement to address what he described as an unnecessary and unprofessional decision. According to Buck's account, TNA specifically forced the change to the main event just three weeks after it had already been approved and promoted. The timing of the change is particularly frustrating given that the match had been finalized and already marketed to fans.

Buck expressed frustration at the lack of understanding regarding Create A Pro's role in the wrestling ecosystem. He emphasized that Create A Pro functions as a training school and development ground, not as a competing media company. The promotion runs approximately 25 events annually and reinvests all revenue back into the business, covering facility leases, ring maintenance, and necessary equipment. Buck noted that the live events themselves are essential training tools for wrestlers, especially as such opportunities become increasingly rare at higher levels of professional wrestling due to cost concerns and the industry's focus on television and streaming content.

Buck articulated the broader implications of TNA's decision, questioning how it serves anyone's interests. He pointed out that Create A Pro serves as a pipeline for developing talent that eventually moves into major wrestling companies. By supporting independent promotions, larger wrestling organizations benefit from wrestlers who have been trained and kept sharp through consistent live event experience. Buck suggested that TNA's decision ultimately undermines the system that helps wrestlers develop their skills and earn income.

While technically TNA did not pull talent from the shows, Buck argued that altering a main event match carries the same practical impact as talent withdrawal. He emphasized that anyone with actual event promotion experience understands how disruptive such last-minute changes are to a card. Buck stated that when one promoter makes a decision that negatively affects another promoter's show, professional etiquette dictates that compensation or remediation should follow, whether through sending additional talent, strengthening the card, or finding another way to return value. He noted that no such attempt was made.

Buck also referenced the history of Create A Pro's relationship with TNA. He noted that years ago, TNA used Create A Pro students for meaningful work when the larger company needed additional content and wrestlers. However, he suggested that the current dynamic has shifted, with TNA now using Create A Pro talent for roles like ring crew and catering rather than meaningful wrestling opportunities. Buck acknowledged that Carlos Silva may not be familiar with his background, and stated that's acceptable, but he asserted that executives in Silva's position should understand the environment in which they operate and follow proper industry etiquette.

Buck's statement also included a subtle comparison to how AEW handles similar situations. He suggested that Tony Khan could potentially offer Carlos Silva guidance on how to protect one's brand while still supporting the broader wrestling scene. This comment implied that AEW manages to balance its own interests with industry relationships in a more constructive manner.

Buck concluded by expressing his annoyance at what he characterized as an unnecessary and "cringe" situation. He emphasized that professional interactions should be conducted with grace rather than chaos, and lamented that the opportunity to handle the situation appropriately was missed. Create A Pro's role as an independent training ground that has historically supported major wrestling companies ultimately makes the decision appear short-sighted from Buck's perspective.

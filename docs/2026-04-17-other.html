🔥 WrestleCon Main Event drama and TNA controversy

WrestleCon Main Event Drama and TNA Controversy

The WrestleCon Mark Hitchcock Memorial Supershow on April 16-17, 2026, became defined by a series of behind-the-scenes scrambles and fan frustration after TNA made a last-minute decision that rippled through the entire event.

The primary issue stemmed from TNA's sudden withdrawal of Leon Slater from a scheduled singles match against Ricochet just days before the supershow. The company cited "partner conflicts" as the reason for pulling its talent, though the timing and lack of direct communication raised questions about the company's motivations. WrestleCon promoter Michael Bochicchio revealed that he learned about the cancellation through online reports rather than receiving direct notification from TNA officials. He only had brief contact with TNA President Carlos Silva, and even that conversation failed to provide clear answers about why the match was being scrapped.

Bochicchio addressed speculation about Leon Slater's involvement during his appearance on Post Wrestling, directly contradicting fan theories. "A lot of people think, 'They didn't want Leon to lose.' Guys, I'm going to be honest, Leon wasn't losing. So, come up with a different theory on why they pulled him because that had nothing to do with it. The match was cleared, the finish were cleared," he explained. Despite this clarification, Bochicchio hinted that inter-promotional tensions may have been the actual cause, particularly given that AEW was running events around the same time as TNA. This wasn't an isolated incident either, as a match between MJF and Bear Bronson at another event had reportedly been canceled under similar circumstances, suggesting TNA was implementing stricter guidelines about its wrestlers appearing opposite AEW-affiliated talent.

The cancellation forced WrestleCon to completely restructure its main event. Instead of the advertised Slater versus Ricochet singles match, organizers scrambled to create a new trios bout pairing Ricochet with Bishop Kaun and Tonga Loa of The Gates of Agony against Kevin Knight, Mike Bailey, and Michael Oku. However, the last-minute restructuring proved to be only the beginning of the night's chaos.

As fans entered the arena knowing their promised match had been altered, the atmosphere grew tense. When the new main event trios match was announced, the crowd's frustration boiled over. Loud chants of "F*** TNA" erupted throughout the venue before the match even began, with the anti-company sentiment clearly audible across the building. The chants reflected broader fan disappointment about the loss of cross-promotional matchups and the apparent prioritization of company politics over fan interest.

Behind the scenes, organizers faced an entirely different crisis that threatened to make the situation even worse. The Gates of Agony, scheduled as part of the main event, had their flight into Las Vegas severely delayed, putting their arrival at the venue in serious doubt. When the plane finally touched down, it did so well beyond the scheduled time, creating a race against the clock to get Tonga Loa and Bishop Kaun to the WrestleCon venue at the Horseshoe.

In response to the time crunch, the team demonstrated remarkable commitment to the event. Upon landing, Tonga Loa and Bishop Kaun bolted from their plane without even retrieving their luggage from baggage claim, immediately heading to the venue to make the main event on time. Meanwhile, WrestleCon officials worked frantically behind the scenes to buy time for their arrival. The promotion added extra time to undercard matches and even inserted an additional match between Man Like DeReiss and Ethan Allen into the card specifically to give The Gates of Agony time to arrive without disrupting the scheduled main event.

The contingency planning ultimately paid off. By the time the main event trios match was ready to begin, both members of The Gates of Agony had arrived and were ready to perform. The main event proceeded as the newly revised card intended, delivering on what WrestleCon had promised despite the organizational chaos behind the scenes.

Bochicchio later reflected on how close the event had come to complete disaster. "In the end, it worked out, which was a big relief to all involved, but there was worry during the show to make sure they delivered what was advertised." His comment captured the tension that existed throughout the evening as organizers navigated multiple crises simultaneously—first the sudden talent withdrawal and main event restructuring, then the flight delays that threatened to eliminate their replacement participants entirely.

The evening wasn't entirely consumed by the main event saga. Another ongoing rivalry carried over into WrestleCon when The Righteous crashed The Hardys' autograph signing on April 16. Following their intense tables match on the April 9 TNA iMPACT episode where Matt and Jeff Hardy successfully defended the TNA World Tag Team Championship against Vincent and Dutch, the feud remained active. During the signing, Vincent and Dutch approached the Hardy merchandise table for a tense face-to-face encounter. Matt Hardy responded to the interruption by saying, "We are trying to take care of the Die Hardys," before pivoting to the possibility of continuing their rivalry outside of TNA. He mentioned being House of Glory Tag Team Champions and suggested another possible showdown, which Dutch quickly indicated interest in pursuing.

The WrestleCon experience ultimately illustrated the complex relationship between independent wrestling events and major promotions. While WrestleCon managed to execute its main event despite mounting obstacles and fan disappointment, the event became a flashpoint for larger industry conversations about cross-promotional collaboration, talent control, and the fan interest in seeing wrestlers from different companies compete against one another. The loud anti-TNA chants served as a clear indication that audience members felt strongly about these issues and were willing to voice their frustration when their expected matchups failed to materialize.

ODB lobbies for WWE/NXT appearance through TNA partnership

ODB has been pushing for a WWE or NXT appearance as part of TNA's ongoing partnership with the promotion before she retires from professional wrestling. The veteran wrestler recently discussed her desire to make at least one appearance on WWE or NXT programming while speaking on The Velvet Ropes with SoCal Val, noting that she has been actively campaigning for this opportunity.

Despite her over two decades in the wrestling business, ODB remains a notable exception among wrestlers in her generation. "I'm pretty much probably one of the only wrestlers that's only been in TNA. You don't really hear that a lot. A lot of people did TNA, then did WWE, or WWE then TNA, and AEW. And I've just been a TNA Knockout. I've never been anywhere else," she explained. While she has made brief appearances for multiple other promotions including AEW and ROH, TNA remained her primary home throughout her career.

ODB expressed her enthusiasm about the possibility of a WWE appearance while acknowledging that she will continue to advocate for this opportunity. "It would be cool. And that's something I've brought up, and I'm gonna keep bringing it up. Hey, just one appearance or something. Even if it's NXT or just something. I mean, come on." She emphasized that even a brief NXT appearance would be meaningful to her at this stage of her career.

The wrestling veteran also opened up about a significant missed opportunity from her early career. She revealed that she had tried out for the original WWF Tough Enough in 2000 and was among the top 25 candidates selected. Al Snow, who was in charge of the competition, personally remembered her from the auditions that saw over 250 people. "He's like, I remember you. Out of every single person we saw that day, they saw over 250 people. He's like, you had it. You had something. But unfortunately, MTV, I had no story behind me. I didn't have a sad story." This means that despite showing promise, she was not selected for the televised competition.

During her interview, ODB also addressed frustrations from her time in TNA under former management. She recalled a photo shoot where she was not invited, which she later learned was due to Dixie Carter's preference. "There was one photo shoot I didn't get invited to, and it pissed me off. This is what I heard, where Dixie Carter would always say, like, ODB is not, I don't want her to represent the Knockout division." This occurred during a period when the Knockouts division was being repositioned with a focus on sexiness, featuring car washes, mud wrestling, and other similar segments. However, ODB remained true to her character and continued to be herself when she returned to the promotion.

ODB was recently announced for induction into the TNA Hall of Fame during her current run with the company, which has been described as her final run in professional wrestling. She has also been critical of the current generation of female wrestlers in TNA, expressing concerns about their lack of personality and excessive focus on social media content.

A particular point of frustration for ODB involved the treatment of veteran Traci Brooks following her Hall of Fame induction. ODB was upset that many of the current TNA Knockouts were not present backstage to show support for Brooks. "I was really mad that not a lot of the girls were watching Traci Brooks get inducted. And they should have been standing backstage and clapping for her. I asked her, did any of the Knockouts take pictures of you? She's like, no. And I'm like, what? No one took a goddamn picture with you." She emphasized the importance of respecting those who paved the way for the division.

ODB stressed the historical significance of veterans like Traci Brooks and other women who came before her. "She opened the door for a lot of us. She opened the door for me. She was there in the beginning days. She took every guy's finisher. People don't realize that. Just because you didn't win a Knockouts championship, just because you weren't main eventing, doesn't mean you didn't do s*** in this business." She also highlighted the contributions of women like Jazz, Lisa, Luna, and Sherry Martel who opened doors for her and other wrestlers to be different and find their own paths in professional wrestling.

The veteran expressed disappointment with the current locker room's lack of knowledge about wrestling history and the contributions of those who came before them. "A lot of us women that came before me, like a Jazz, Lisa, Luna, Sherry Martel, those chicks opened the doors for me to be different. People just think, oh, whoever's popular, that's who I want to take a picture with. But I'm like, nah, know your history, bitches, let's go." She believes that younger wrestlers should understand and respect the sacrifices and hard work of previous generations.

ODB was also critical of how the current generation compares to wrestlers from her era. "There was a lot more personality back in my day. We didn't have social media. These girls are constantly doing TikTok content. I haven't heard the word content so many times. I'm like, can you guys just worry about your matches right now?" She suggested that the current focus on building social media presence has detracted from the development of in-ring talent and character development.

Commenting on the modern era's attitude toward veteran wrestlers, ODB addressed criticism that established wrestlers returning to the company are considered "old timers." "When we come back, people are like, oh, the old timers coming back. I'm like, bitch, you want to have a ring to stand in? If it wasn't for chicks like us that paved the way, that paid the dues, you guys, what, they found you on TikTok or something? Come on, you and all your Only Fans. None of these girls would be able to handle it back in our day. They wouldn't have been able to hang. Hell no." This comment reflected her belief that the younger generation lacks the toughness and dedication required during her era of professional wrestling.

Despite her criticisms, ODB has found at least one young wrestler she respects in the current Knockouts roster. She named Jody Threat as her favorite current Knockout, indicating that she recognizes talent and potential in the new generation when it exists. ODB recently returned to TNA, and this current run has been described as her final stint in professional wrestling. Her induction into the TNA Hall of Fame represents a recognition of her significant contributions to the company and the women's wrestling division throughout her career.

Wrestling Hall of Fame inductions and tributes

Lou Thesz: Wrestling's Foundational Legend

Throughout wrestling history, there are very few wrestlers that you can honestly say are unmovable from the Mount Rushmore of wrestling. Many debate more contemporary wrestlers, like Steve Austin, Hulk Hogan, Ric Flair, and the Rock. But many forget the "George Washingtons" of Mount Rushmore, the ones who laid the foundation of the business we all enjoy watching. In wrestling, two names come to mind for that George Washington spot: Ed "the Strangler" Lewis and Lou Thesz.

If you truly are a wrestling fan, and I mean a deep wrestling fan, you'll know that in 1963, Bruno Sammartino defeated Buddy Rogers for the WWWF World Heavyweight title. But what people often overlook is how Buddy Rogers having the title caused the split between the NWA and the WWWF at the time. In 1963, Buddy Rogers was the biggest attraction in the Northeast. Capital Wrestling Corporation was the largest territory in the nation, and because of this, whoever they wanted as champion was usually the person with the best draw. CWC at the time was mainly a tag team territory, but one person continued to draw large attendance month after month, and that person was Buddy Rogers.

From 1961 to 1963, Rogers was not just the NWA champion; he was basically the Northeast territory champion, losing and winning the title often in Toronto, New York City, and Montreal. The NWA didn't recognize any of these title changes, which is a shame because we would have had our first Black champion then in Bobo Brazil. Instead, they realized that the CWC and Buddy Rogers were ignoring the rest of the country. Rogers was not touring; he was staying close to his home base, and much of the country was unaware of the multiple title changes that were happening. So the NWA had to act.

In 1963, the NWA set their number one challengers to retrieve the title. Enter Lou Thesz. Thesz might have been the greatest shooter of all time, but at this point, he was enjoying his retirement. However, with the wrestling business in trouble, the NWA called on Lou to ask a favor: get back the NWA title. The NWA sent Thesz to Toronto, and he would wrestle Buddy Rogers for the NWA title.

The CWC did maybe the only thing it could do. On January 23, 1963, Lou Thesz beat Buddy Rogers in a one-fall match for the NWA title. CWC claimed the title change was illegitimate because they only recognized two out of three falls matches at the time. Officially, CWC would still recognize Buddy Rogers as their NEW World Wide Wrestling Federation Heavyweight title, and rename themselves the WWWF. That part will often get mentioned. But on the other side, Lou Thesz would take back the NWA title and defend it for the next three years, touring the nation and helping spread the wealth.

The Beginning

At the age of 14, Lou Thesz started high school and started training in folkstyle and freestyle wrestling. This sounds like a great beginning, except that for six years previously, Lou Thesz's father had already been training him in Greco-Roman wrestling. Thesz was being built to be a true wrestling machine. Also at 14 years old, Thesz would train in boxing, and at 16, he'd train under the legendary John Zastro in St. Louis.

Quickly becoming one of the most dominant wrestlers in the country, Thesz was recruited by pro-wrestling promoter Tom Parks. Thesz would then train under George Tragos for Catch as Catch Can wrestling. He would train for six months under another Catch wrestler, Ad Santel, and then finally, he'd get the eye of pro-wrestling legend Ed Lewis. It was Lewis who would then become his manager in the ring. The majority of this training occurred before Thesz turned 18 years old, as he dropped out of school to continue his training.

The Attraction

By the age of 21, Lou Thesz was the top attraction in St. Louis. He built his resume by beating both top amateur wrestlers and carnival wrestlers. Had he not found pro-wrestling, Thesz said he would have gone the amateur route.

The IInspiration retain TJPW Princess Tag Team Championship

The IInspiration are fighting princess champions, retain TJPW tag titles

Jessie McKay and Cassie Lee continue to make their mark around the world. The iconic duo traveled to Japan where they currently hold the Princess Tag Team Championship. When Tokyo Joshi Pro-Wrestling ventured to Las Vegas for Slam Fest during WrestleMania week, The IInspiration were present to defend their gold. At TJPW Live on April 16, 2026, they successfully retained the Princess Tag Team Championship over Shoko Nakajima and Hyper Misao.

The match began with characteristic playfulness as the challengers mocked the champions with their iconic pose. The IInspiration responded in kind, executing cartwheels into the pose to show their own flair. This opening display of personality set the tone before the match transitioned into serious wrestling action.

Hedonistic Resonance, the team of Nakajima and Misao, mounted genuine offensive pressure during the contest. The challengers showcased impressive teamwork, with the Big Kaiju executing a double 619 and the masked superhero landing a double flying crossbody. Shoko Nakajima came close to victory with a bridging Northern Lights suplex on Cassie Lee, but Jessie McKay was able to break the pin before the three count.

The IInspiration regrouped and began their comeback. Cassie Lee escaped a combination maneuver by shoving Misao directly into her partner Shoko. The momentum shift continued as Cassie struck Shoko with a spin kick and followed up with a Codebreaker to Misao. With their challengers reeling, The IInspiration seized the opportunity to hit the IIdolizer on Shoko Nakajima to secure the victory and retain their championship.

Following their successful title defense, The IInspiration gloated backstage about their status as fighting champions. The celebration was interrupted when Miu Watanabe stepped up to challenge them. Watanabe announced that she and partner Rika Tatsumi would be coming for the titles, setting up their next championship bout. The IInspiration confirmed they would be at Korakuen Hall on May 4 to defend the Princess Tag Team Championship against this new challenge.

The TJPW Live event also featured other notable title defenses. In the main event, Yuki Arai retained the Princess of Princess Championship over American Gladiator J-Rod. The powerhouse displayed her strength throughout the match, including an innovative pin attempt where she flexed and performed push-ups. After J-Rod charged for a spear, Arai delivered a boot to turn the tide. When J-Rod went for a second spear, Arai ole'd and J-Rod crashed into the ring post. Arai finished the match with a full nelson slam followed by an axe kick to the skull for the victory.

In the third title bout of the evening, Suzame retained the International Princess Championship over Sakura Hattori. Hattori initially had momentum but missed on a springboard senton, allowing the champion to rally with a cutter. The two competitors continued to tango for position until Suzame struck with a springboard facebuster. With Hattori unable to continue, Suzame finished her with a cutter to retain the title.

WWE WrestleMania X-Seven: Retro 3 Things We Hated & 3 Things We Loved

Welcome to another edition of retro reviews, where notable wrestling shows from the past are examined through a loved and hated format. This is a significant retrospective as we celebrate WrestleMania week and mark the 25th anniversary of what remains one of the best, if not the best, WrestleManias of all time. It has been a quarter of a century since TLC 2, the McMahon street fight, and the heel turn that shocked the wrestling world. Now it's time to look back and reflect on this historic event.

The full show cannot be covered with the standard format since the entire first two hours are being overlooked, ranging from William Regal versus Chris Jericho to Kurt Angle versus another competitor. However, there is still plenty to discuss, including Chyna, WCW, and the Gimmick Battle Royal. This article examines three things hated and three things loved about WrestleMania X-Seven as we turn the clock back to the full glory and final days of the Attitude Era.

Hated: Chyna Gets Her Moment and It's Fine

When retrospectives were done for the 2001 Royal Rumble pay-per-view, the injury angle that ended Ivory's match with Chyna made the hated list. While the WrestleMania X-Seven match wasn't as bad as what happened at the Rumble, primarily because there was no storyline of sudden paralysis from a Handspring Back Elbow, it still wasn't great.

Watching WWE Attitude Era matches through modern eyes is always interesting when examining the women's division. Nowadays, fans are fortunate to have access to high-quality female athletes across every major company in the world. However, back in 2001, while a WWE Women's Championship existed, it was treated with about as much prestige as a doorstop by those in power. To be direct, the WWE Divas were there to look attractive, and if they could wrestle, that was considered a bonus.

This point makes the match even more disappointing because both Chyna and Ivory, especially Ivory, could really perform, and given the opportunity, they could have delivered a good-to-very-good match. However, the storyline called for a squash match. Ivory needed to get what was coming to her, and that's exactly what happened. Still, it wasn't even a good squash. It consisted of Chyna moving around at a pace that if she slowed any further she would be going backwards, while Ivory did everything she could to make Chyna look like an unstoppable monster. The execution simply didn't work.

The Gorilla Press finish provided a good photo opportunity but nothing more. Even the way Ivory landed was messy. Chyna lay on her like she was sitting on a couch, and that was it. The heel got what was coming to her. Yes, Chyna had beaten men numerous times, so having her squash the champion made sense. However, it was so anticlimactic that viewers were left thinking "...that's it?"

No one walked into this expecting fireworks, though Chyna's entrance did deliver them. It wasn't supposed to be a five-star classic, nor did anyone expect it to be. With that said, they could have done more with it because after this match, the show goes into overdrive quality-wise, making this one even more forgettable. Blink and you'll miss it, and even if you do miss it, you're not missing much.

Hated: McMahon Street Fight Lacks WCW Stars to Kick Off Invasion

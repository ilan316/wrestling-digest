Jim Johnston, the legendary composer behind some of wrestling's most iconic entrance themes, has launched a pointed critique at both WWE and AEW, arguing that their modern approach to entrance music is preventing either company from creating the megastars that defined previous eras of professional wrestling.

During an appearance on The False Face podcast, Johnston explained that while both companies have talented wrestlers on their rosters, the generic nature of contemporary entrance themes is failing to elevate performers into star status. He drew a stark contrast between the current landscape and the era in which he composed music for WWE, emphasizing that entrance music serves a critical function beyond mere entertainment.

"I felt a great responsibility, like part of these guys' careers and successes were in my hands," Johnston said. "The music now in WWE and in AEW—and I'm sorry if this is mean—it's all really homogeneous and really mediocre, and it doesn't have anything to do with the characters. I don't think there are no potential big stars on the roster hiding there. Before Steve Austin was Steve Austin, he was The Ringmaster, and there were lots of stories like that. These people need the right storyline, the right costuming, and definitely the right music to lift them up."

Johnston's core argument centers on the fundamental purpose of entrance music. According to his philosophy, a truly effective theme should instantly communicate a wrestler's identity and character to the audience, regardless of whether they're paying full attention.

"One of the things I always wanted was that if somebody was in the kitchen getting a beer and they heard the first second of the music, they knew exactly who was coming out," he explained. "You didn't have to wait until they walked onto the stage. The music should tell you who that person is. That's the job of the music. Music is one of the most powerful emotional communicators there is. It should reinforce the character. It should make you feel who this person is before they even appear. If the music doesn't do that, it's missing its purpose."

Johnston described how this principle worked in practice during his tenure with WWE. "It used to be, whether it was my music or a Jimmy Hart theme, or an outside band thing, you used to be able to be making dinner in the kitchen with the TV in the other room, and you'd know who was coming out. You could almost follow the program from the other room."

That era, Johnston contends, has fundamentally changed for the worse. "Now, I just don't think it's like that at all. It just sounds like noise to me and all the themes just sound generic."

The legendary composer connects this creative decline directly to the shortage of mainstream wrestling superstars. "For some reason, the powers that be have forgotten their roots of how powerful music is in creating stars. I further believe that's why they're not creating—either WWE or AEW—they're not creating big stars anymore. There are no new Steve Austins. There are no Rocks around now. It's because everyone's sort of in this layer of generic, I suppose. I don't understand it."

Johnston was careful to note that his criticism doesn't stem from a belief that the talent simply isn't there. Rather, he views the current state of entrance music as a systematic failure to properly develop and present available talent. In essence, he sees generic music as part of a broader package that fails to distinguish wrestlers from one another in meaningful ways.

Interestingly, Johnston expressed appreciation for Vince McMahon's approach to innovation and willingness to evolve the product. "I give credit there to Vince because he was always trying to push the envelope forward. Sometimes he pushed it places that I certainly didn't agree with, but at least the guy was embracing progress and change and asking, 'How can we make the product even better?' That was a compatibility that he and I really had because I always wanted to try different things and make the music better."

The broader implication of Johnston's critique is that modern wrestling leadership may have lost sight of a crucial element in star-making: the power of distinctive, character-driven entrance music. By allowing entrance themes to become homogeneous and forgettable, both WWE and AEW may be inadvertently undermining their own ability to create the kinds of larger-than-life personalities that captured mainstream attention in previous generations.

Jim Johnston WWE Iconic Themes Stories

Legendary WWE composer Jim Johnston, the creative force behind many of professional wrestling's most iconic entrance themes from 1985 until his departure in 2017, recently appeared on The False Face podcast for an extensive interview covering his career, the origins of legendary entrance music, and his thoughts on the current wrestling landscape.

During the nearly two-hour conversation with host Paul McNamee, Johnston challenged Steve Austin's long-standing account of how the "Stone Cold" theme was created, revealed that Brock Lesnar's entrance music was originally composed for the XFL, discussed his creative philosophy, and shared why he believes neither WWE nor AEW is producing stars on the level of previous generations.

For years, Steve Austin has recalled bringing Rage Against the Machine's Evil Empire album to Johnston as inspiration for his entrance music, specifically citing "Bulls on Parade." Johnston, however, said he doesn't remember that happening. "I'm not sure that ever happened," he stated.

According to Johnston, the music was actually created before he had even met Austin, originating as part of a series of vignettes designed to introduce the emerging "Stone Cold" character after his Ringmaster persona. "The creation of that theme happened way before I ever met Steve, because it started with his promos. There were a series of promos when they transitioned him from Ringmaster, and these two producers, friends of mine, David Sahadi and Chris Chambers, who did some unbelievably creative stuff, they had this idea for a series of things that they shot up in this really beat up, dark, destroyed warehouse in upstate Connecticut, and they were almost poetic. I remember one final line was Steve saying something like, 'You want forgiveness? Go to church.'"

He explained that the now-famous musical hook was originally intended as the dramatic payoff to those promo packages. "At the time, I saw the vibe of the promos, and what I came up with, the first version of Steve's theme, was a score for those promos. I did sort of orchestral type stuff for 90 percent of the promo, and each one of them had a tagline, and boom. It needed something that kind of kicked you in the ass, and that's when I came up with it."

Johnston also noted that the iconic glass-shattering sound effect was already part of those original promos. While he acknowledged Austin may have later suggested tweaks to the music's energy, Johnston believes the foundation had already been established. "Maybe he came to me later, either directly or indirectly, and said, this is the energy I want. But so maybe he didn't like the first version or something."

When McNamee joked that Austin should weigh in, Johnston laughed. "Yeah, Steve, you pain in the ass, trying to bury me."

Johnston didn't hold back when discussing modern wrestling entrance themes, arguing that today's music lacks individuality and has contributed to a decline in genuine superstar creation. "It just sounds like noise to me, and all the themes just sound generic. It used to be, whether it was my music or a Jimmy Hart theme or an outside band thing, you used to be able to be making dinner in the kitchen with the TV in the other room, and you'd know who was coming out. You could almost follow the program from the other room. Now, I just don't think it's like that at all. And for some reason, the powers that be have forgotten their roots or something, of how powerful music is in creating stars. And I further believe that's why they're not creating, either WWE or AEW. They're not creating big stars anymore. There are no new Steve Austins. There are no Rocks around now. And it's because everyone's sort of in this layer of generic."

He added that large companies often become complacent after finding financial success. "I think that happens to a lot of corporations. They find what works. They're making a lot of money. And, oh well, we must be doing it right. So don't change anything."

Vince McMahon Impact on Pro Wrestling Debate

Debate: Did Vince McMahon destroy Pro Wrestling?

A live debate hosted by Wrestling With The 80's brought together two of the channel's most prolific guests to discuss one of professional wrestling's most contentious questions: whether Vince McMahon destroyed the industry he helped popularize. The discussion stems from recent episodes examining the World Wrestling Federation before the Hulkamania phenomenon, which prompted nostalgic reflections among longtime fans but also raised important questions about what was lost in wrestling's evolution.

The core question at the heart of the debate is whether Hulk Hogan and Vince McMahon, despite achieving unprecedented mainstream success, ultimately transformed wrestling into something fundamentally different from what it had been. The transition from classic wrestling in the pre-1984 era to the glitzy, entertainment-focused product that followed represents a dramatic shift in the industry's philosophy and presentation. Some argue that while McMahon and Hogan propelled professional wrestling to the pinnacle of popularity, they may have sacrificed the sport's soul and integrity in the process, converting a beloved athletic competition into a money-making sports entertainment machine.

Taking the affirmative position is Evan Ginzburg, an author and associate producer of The Wrestler and 350 Days, who has been a wrestling enthusiast since the 1960s. Ginzburg's credentials as a witness to wrestling's transformation are particularly strong, having attended regular events at Madison Square Garden from 1974 to 1985. During this period, he observed some of wrestling's greatest performers, including the legendary dominance of Bruno Sammartino, the flashy style of Superstar Billy Graham, the steadfast technical excellence of Bob Backlund, and the meteoric rise of Hulk Hogan that would reshape the entire industry.

Arguing against the proposition is Steve Gennarelli, a podcaster and wrestling fan since 1976, whose experience with professional wrestling spans the East Coast of the United States. Gennarelli witnessed the climactic end of Bruno Sammartino's epic championship reign, lived through the Bob Backlund era that followed, and experienced the industry's recharged energy with the arrival of Hulkamania. His perspective provides crucial counterpoint to the narrative of decline, suggesting that McMahon's evolution of wrestling may not have destroyed the industry but rather transformed and expanded it.

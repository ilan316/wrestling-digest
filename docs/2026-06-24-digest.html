🔥 AEW New Four Pillars 2026

The concept of the Four Pillars of All Elite Wrestling has been a central topic of discussion since AEW's inception in 2019. Originally, MJF, Jack Perry, Darby Allin, and Sammy Guevara were recognized as the company's foundational young talents who developed alongside the promotion and helped establish its identity. MJF and Darby Allin have achieved the most success among the original group, with both becoming AEW World Champions and main eventing multiple pay-per-views. MJF is currently on his third world championship reign. While Perry and Guevara have also enjoyed notable careers in AEW, their career trajectories have differed, with Guevara's best years coming in the company's early years and Perry's peak performance occurring over the last two to three years. The original Four Pillars concept eventually evolved into a major storyline that culminated at Double or Nothing 2023, where all four men competed for the AEW World Championship.

Paul Heyman Roman Reigns Heel Turn

During an appearance on INSIGHT with Chris Van Vliet, Paul Heyman provided extensive behind-the-scenes details about the creation of Roman Reigns' iconic Tribal Chief character and the transformation that would ultimately become one of the most successful character pivots in modern wrestling history. Heyman reflected on the summer of 2020 when Reigns returned to WWE television and aligned himself with Heyman in a shocking twist that fundamentally changed the trajectory of his career.

According to Heyman, Vince McMahon expressed concern about the presentation of their initial reveal, particularly regarding the aesthetic choices. "Vince thought the couch was not opulent enough for a character that was then going to end up being called The Tribal Chief," Heyman explained. He acknowledged that McMahon may have had a valid point but noted that time constraints were a factor in the decision. "I can't disagree with him, but we were in a rush to get that done."

Heyman then detailed the original creative vision for Reigns' return during the pandemic era. The plan was far more elaborate than what ultimately aired. "The original idea was for us both to return at SummerSlam, and then be on TV that Friday and lead into Payback that weekend." This timing was specifically calculated to maximize storytelling impact. "I thought let Roman return, give that moment to The Big Dog, and then that Friday on television, he's not cooperative in the triple threat, he's not cooperative with Adam Pearce, you know something's up, but you can't put your finger on it."

The creative vision called for a dramatic and carefully timed reveal. WWE official Adam Pearce would serve as the conduit for audience suspicion. "Go to him sitting on the couch, and Pearce is looking over going, 'What the f*ck is Heyman doing there?!'" The moment was designed to create a sense of confusion and intrigue, with viewers unable to immediately comprehend what they were witnessing. The reveal would culminate with Reigns delivering what would become one of his signature lines. "At the last second pull out because Roman says, 'That's not a prediction, that's a spoiler.'"

Heyman envisioned the audience experiencing a moment of genuine shock and disbelief. "So for five seconds you see us together. Did I just see that? "That weekend get the title on Roman, and then the next Friday is our first appearance, truly together in front of the cameras as a unit." This pacing would allow WWE to build maximum intrigue before formally introducing the partnership to the world.

The discussions also revealed that McMahon initially had reservations about the "Tribal Chief" nomenclature itself, which would become central to Reigns' entire persona going forward. "The first time I came up with Tribal Chief, [Vince said] 'Really? You want to call him the Tribal Chief?'" McMahon wanted "The Big Dog, Roman Reigns, with his advocate, Paul Heyman."

Heyman pushed back firmly on that idea, believing a complete reinvention was absolutely necessary for the angle to succeed. "I said, 'Well, I can't be his advocate as Brock Lesnar's advocate. We have to have something different to Roman Reigns, and Roman Reigns has to be different.'" He argued that simply recycling the advocate role would undermine the entire purpose of the partnership and character evolution. "You got to pull the trigger on this. Heyman stressed that the Tribal Chief wasn't merely a slight adjustment to Reigns' existing persona but rather a fundamental reconstruction. "This is a whole new character. "He could not have played this persona earlier, because he was too young to do it." Instead, Heyman believed Reigns needed years of experience, maturation, and exposure to adversity before he could authentically embody the role. Shane McMahon and ECW History

Shane McMahon Wanted To Buy ECW Before Vince Chose A Different Path

Paul Heyman recently shared a significant piece of ECW history during an interview with Chris Van Vliet, revealing that Shane McMahon had pursued the acquisition of ECW as an independent operation.

According to Heyman, there were only three legitimate payments from WWE connected to ECW: a weekly payment, a secured loan, and a late 2000 payroll-related payment made while WWE examined the company's financial records. The December 2000 situation arose during an especially dire period for ECW. The company had lost its television deal with TNN, revenue from In Demand was minimal, and In Demand was demanding eight pay-per-views in 2001 despite ECW lacking national television coverage. WWE expressed interest in reviewing ECW's books because Shane McMahon wanted to purchase the company and operate it independently.

"At the very end, Christmas week 2000, when everything was dire. We had lost TNN. The money is now trickling in from In Demand. Now In Demand wants eight pay-per-views in 2001. We don't even have national television. WWE says let's look at the books because Shane wanted to buy it," Heyman explained. "So Shane wanted to buy ECW and run it on its own."

However, Vince McMahon envisioned a different strategic value. Rather than allowing Shane to acquire ECW as a separate entity, Vince decided it would be more advantageous to let the company enter bankruptcy and acquire Heyman in the process. Heyman noted that Shane continued to advocate for the purchase despite his father's opposition. "Vince said it's easier just to let it go into bankruptcy and we get Heyman. Shane still pushed and pushed and pushed."

The $83,500 loan WWE provided during that critical week served to help ECW make payroll and avoid involuntary bankruptcy proceedings while WWE conducted their financial review. Heyman was emphatic about clarifying the extent of WWE's involvement, dismissing longstanding rumors of secret ongoing funding. "So for the accountants to take a look at all the books and to buy us the time not to be pulled into an involuntary bankruptcy by somebody, we were loaned $83,500 to make payroll that week so that we can survive the week for WWE to look at the books. There was no other payments made by WWE. There was no other secret funding. And this is all a matter of public record because it was a publicly traded company by this time. And it's all there and it's all in the bankruptcy papers. But never let the truth get in the way of a good story."

When asked why Shane ultimately did not purchase ECW, Heyman revealed that Vince prioritized having him work within WWE rather than outside the organization. Vince's philosophy, according to Heyman, was famously summarized as preferring to have talented individuals inside his company working for WWE's interests. Heyman drew a comparison to Apple's acquisition of Beats, noting that the technology company valued Jimmy Iovine as much as the product itself. Similarly, Vince was tired of Heyman operating as an independent entity and wanted him fully integrated into WWE's operations.

"Vince found it more valuable to have me in the, as Vince would say, 'I'd rather have Paul Heyman in the castle pissing out than out of the castle pissing in.' Apple didn't need Beats. Apple wanted Jimmy Iovine. Vince McMahon was tired of me being out on my own, being an independent, doing my own thing. Decided we need Heyman and wanted Heyman involved."

According to Heyman, while WWE needed to secure ECW's library and assets for the bankruptcy court, the real objective was acquiring Heyman himself. The acquisition of ECW's assets essentially came as part of the larger deal to bring Heyman into WWE's fold. "And to get Heyman, you had to at least secure the assets of ECW or promise the bankruptcy court to secure the assets of ECW so that there's a curator for our library. And along with that comes Paulie."

This revelation presents a fascinating alternate history scenario. If Shane McMahon had successfully purchased ECW and operated it as a separate company under his ownership, the landscape of professional wrestling during the early 2000s might have developed entirely differently. Instead, Vince McMahon's strategic decision to acquire Paul Heyman as a talent and secure ECW's assets fundamentally altered the company's fate and Heyman's career trajectory within the wrestling industry.

AJ Styles Claire Lynch TNA Storyline

AJ Styles has opened up about the original creative direction behind one of TNA's most divisive storylines. Speaking on the Phenomenally Retro Podcast, the wrestling legend explained that the Claire Lynch angle was dramatically different from what ultimately aired on television.

According to Styles, the original concept centered around a member of the Carter family rather than the character that became Claire Lynch. The storyline was designed to keep potential shame away from the Carter family name while still giving AJ a logical reason to be involved in the situation.

"She was supposed to be a niece. Because we didn't want some shame brought on the family of the Carters. I would go as AJ Styles and check on the niece and report back to Carter so that nobody would know this was going on," Styles explained.

The original direction would have evolved in a specific way, according to Styles. "So then it would be this hot, unbelievably smoking hot niece to the point we go, listen, you know what? AJ wasn't cheating with Dixie, but he may have been cheating with her niece because she's hot."

Styles stressed that having a family member involved in the storyline was essential to making it work narratively. "It was essential that it was a family member that was in rehab and not a friend because a friend doesn't matter. Who cares, right? If the Carter family were helping out a friend, did that make them look like a million bucks? But if someone of the family was going through this, then it may not have been as great as the Carters would want to look."

Instead of proceeding with this concept, TNA moved forward with what became the Claire Lynch storyline, which Styles critiqued with a colorful comparison. "But then we have what we had. The original concept was developed collaboratively among Styles, Christopher Daniels, and Frankie Kazarian. "If you want to know the truth, me, Christopher Daniels and Frankie Kazarian, we thought of the storyline," Styles revealed.

Styles explained his reasoning for why having a Carter family member involved made the angle more compelling. "There was a reason why AJ Styles was helping out and not them." This justification would have provided logical grounding for his character's involvement in the storyline.

Looking back on the finished product, Styles acknowledged that it may not have achieved its intended impact, but he maintained that the goal was to create meaningful storytelling beyond the wrestling matches themselves. "I immediately think of when the top leadership — and that is what everybody's coming out saying — there's all kind of chatter about it. The vacuum gets filled immediately," he noted. "The vacuum was started being filled the minute that decision was made."

According to Jarrett, running a wrestling company involves far more than simply writing television. "Leadership is what this industry has always and always will thrive off of. There is a single leader that is — you can call him the author of the book, you can call him the head coach, you can call him the head babysitter — but whoever is leading the company," he explained.

The veteran executive emphasized that the job requires balancing multiple areas at once. "You have a creative lane, you have a talent lane, and you have a production lane. And you better have somebody in that position that is highly skilled in all three," he said. He added that when a leader departs, those departments naturally begin competing for influence. "When that said leader is removed, those three lanes are jockeying for position."

The conversation then shifted to comments made by Sammy Callahan, who recently suggested that the same people have been running wrestling behind the scenes for decades. Jarrett surprisingly agreed with part of the criticism. "The business, the format of professional wrestling has not changed. I think it is in desperate need of change," he stated. Jarrett said he has felt that way for years, noting that he has made this argument since 2011 and 2012.

However, he acknowledged that changing wrestling is easier said than done because those in charge often prefer proven formulas. "The people who control not just the purse strings, excuse me, the decisions, they haven't changed. Experience sitting in the seat will always trump out. Most often we're overruled because they're going to say, 'Hey man, I got to protect and stick with quote-unquote what's working,'" Jarrett explained.

He also noted that WWE's financial success makes it difficult to argue for major changes to the industry's presentation. "Nick Khan could make a hell of a convincing argument and say, 'No, Jeff, look at the revenues. Format doesn't need changing at all,'" Jarrett pointed out. Still, he believes the wrestling landscape is evolving rapidly. "The business is changing right before our very eyes. I just think so many of us don't know really where it's going," he said.

As the discussion turned to reports regarding TNA potentially being sold, Jarrett questioned many of the claims currently circulating online. "I don't buy the report at all. I don't buy the report," he stated firmly. Jarrett also wondered why more attention isn't being paid to the specifics of TNA's relationship with WWE. "What's the real relationship between WWE and TNA? Everything else is reported on. Where's that report at?" he asked.

Despite all the uncertainty surrounding TNA, Jarrett remains interested in seeing who ultimately takes over the creative leadership role and what direction the company chooses moving forward. "What are they bringing to the table? What innovation? What newness? Freshness, however it may be. That's what most excites me about the evolution of the industry," Jarrett concluded.

🔥 CMLL Champion Hechicero AEW Crossover Plans

CMLL World Heavyweight Champion Hechicero is looking to elevate his championship to the highest levels possible. During an exclusive interview with The Takedown on SI, the recently crowned champion discussed his goals and ambitions following his March 20 victory over Claudio Castagnoli at Homenaje a Dos Leyendas in the Arena México, where he won his second CMLL World Championship reign.

Hechicero made it clear that his aspirations extend beyond CMLL and into the international wrestling landscape. The champion named several dream opponents he wants to face, with AEW World Champion MJF topping his list. "Well I aspire to the highest level as CMLL World Heavyweight Champion. First of all, MJF is the company's world champion, it would be really great to face him," Hechicero stated. A potential champion vs. champion match between Hechicero and MJF would represent a significant crossover moment between the two promotions, particularly given MJF's status as one of AEW's top draws.

Beyond MJF, Hechicero expressed interest in several other prominent AEW competitors. He voiced a desire for a rematch with Castagnoli, stating "Also, Claudio Castagnoli, it would be great to have a rematch against him because he did so well and I had two matches before with him in CMLL." Mark Briscoe also made Hechicero's list of desired opponents. "The last time I also face Mark Briscoe, so I really want to face the top of AEW because if we see the AEW roster, there are the greatest wrestlers that I really want to face for the CMLL World Heavyweight Champion," he explained.

Hechicero's ambitious vision for his title reign extends beyond just opponent selection. He laid out a comprehensive plan for his championship tenure in his interview, revealing his intention to defend the CMLL World Title frequently. "I really want to have title matches per month, if I can do it more, I'm open to do it not only, like I said before, in Mexico, but also in other companies with this relationship," Hechicero said. The wrestler explicitly stated his commitment to making a difference compared to his first championship reign. "But I really, really want to make a lot of defenses with this championship, that's something that I really want, and I also want to make a difference from the last time I was CMLL World Heavyweight Champion to this time. So I want this time to be different from the last one."

The "other companies with this relationship" that Hechicero referenced would likely include AEW, Ring of Honor (both owned by Tony Khan), and New Japan Pro-Wrestling, opening up a world of possibilities for championship defenses across multiple major wrestling promotions. This kind of cross-promotional opportunity is relatively unique in modern professional wrestling and represents a significant advantage of holding a CMLL title while simultaneously being signed to AEW.

Hechicero's dual contract arrangement has become a major part of his career trajectory. He signed with AEW around September of the previous year on a multi-year deal that specifically allows him to maintain his CMLL contract simultaneously. The schedule is demanding, as Hechicero acknowledged in his interview. "With this double contract with AEW and CMLL and also since that match with Bryan Danielson, for me has been very fun, tired, constantly traveling long distances in Mexico, United States, but I really enjoy being part of this dual contract with CMLL and AEW. It's something important for my career," the champion explained.

His match with Bryan Danielson in February 2024 on AEW Collision served as a career-defining moment that helped establish Hechicero on the American wrestling scene. That match, which took place when Hechicero was brought in to represent CMLL, proved to be a significant stepping stone in his career development and led to increased opportunities and visibility.

Hechicero's career in professional wrestling has been a long journey. He has been competing for CMLL since 2014 and made appearances on the Ring of Honor circuit in 2016. Nearly a decade after beginning his CMLL tenure, he made his AEW debut to face Bryan Danielson, representing his home promotion while expanding his international profile.

His first CMLL World Heavyweight Championship reign came in September 2021 when he defeated Ultimo Guerrero. However, that run was relatively brief, as he lost the title to Gran Guerrero in November 2022. Now, with his second reign beginning in March 2026, Hechicero is intent on proving that this championship run will be more prolific and impactful than his first.

The timing of Hechicero's statements about wanting to face MJF adds an interesting layer to AEW's championship picture. MJF is scheduled to defend the AEW World Championship against Kenny Omega at AEW Dynasty on April 12. Additionally, MJF is involved in a non-AEW feud with Nic Nemeth, with the two set to collide in May at a Create-a-Pro Wrestling event in Melville, New York. MJF appears to have another potential feud lined up following these engagements, with Hechicero now openly positioning himself as a challenger.

The crossover potential between CMLL and AEW has long been an intriguing possibility for wrestling fans. Recent years have seen increased interaction between the two promotions and their rosters, with various champions and top contenders crossing promotional lines. Hechicero's explicit statements about wanting to defend his CMLL World Championship across multiple companies, including against AEW's top star, suggests that such crossovers may become increasingly common.

AEW's partnership model, which allows wrestlers like Hechicero and Mercedes Moné to hold multiple championships and compete internationally, has proven to be a successful approach to talent management. Moné has particularly benefited from this arrangement, simultaneously holding multiple championships across various wrestling promotions on the international scene. Hechicero appears to be taking a similar approach, looking to maximize the value of his dual contract by pursuing high-profile matches across promotions.

Hechicero's commitment to making frequent title defenses and specifically pursuing matches against AEW's top talent demonstrates his understanding of how to leverage his unique position in professional wrestling. By holding the CMLL World Championship while maintaining an AEW contract, he has access to a broader pool of potential opponents and larger audiences than would typically be available to a single-promotion champion.

Moving forward, wrestling fans should keep an eye on developments regarding potential matches between Hechicero and the AEW roster members he mentioned, particularly MJF. Such matches could represent landmark moments in the evolution of the relationship between CMLL and AEW, showcasing the kind of cross-promotional dream matches that have long captured the imagination of international wrestling enthusiasts.

Eric Bischoff WCW Historical Commentary

Eric Bischoff Addresses Major nWo "What If" Scenarios: Why Ric Flair and Shawn Michaels Never Joined

Speaking on the 83 Weeks podcast, Eric Bischoff has put to rest two of wrestling's longest-running fantasy booking debates by explaining why neither Ric Flair nor Shawn Michaels were ever seriously considered to join the nWo during WCW's biggest era.

When asked about the possibility of Ric Flair joining the nWo alongside Hulk Hogan or even replacing him as the group's leader, Bischoff didn't hesitate to shut down the idea. The concept has been discussed by fans for years, especially when considering the massive success of the faction and imagining how different personalities could have changed its direction. However, Bischoff made it clear that from a storytelling perspective, the concept simply didn't fit what WCW was trying to accomplish.

"No… you know, bad casting — that actually is, you know, I've always heard that saying, 'There's no such thing as a bad idea.' Actually, there is. And this is one of them. Not that I'm not grateful for it — I am — because it's fun. But it would be an example of bad casting just… and I, you know, I know people can have fun with that kind of idea, just bouncing it around, you know, maybe over a beer or something, but from a storytelling perspective, it just wouldn't work. Rick would have hated it," Bischoff explained.

According to Bischoff, the dynamic between Flair's character and the rebellious nature of the nWo would have clashed in ways that likely wouldn't have worked on television. Beyond creative concerns, Bischoff believed that Flair himself would have rejected the concept outright if it had ever been suggested behind the scenes. The personalities and motivations involved would have made the pitch extremely difficult to sell to Flair.

"I don't think there's any scenario in the world where anybody could have pitched this idea to Rick and he would have gone, 'Wow, that's great. I can't wait to do that.' It would have been an uphill battle for a lot of reasons. So no, never got pitched," Bischoff stated.

Bischoff then addressed another major "what if" scenario regarding Shawn Michaels. When asked to imagine how things might have changed if Michaels had taken the role that ultimately went to Sean Waltman, also known as X-Pac, during the nWo's early days, Bischoff made it clear that he had no interest in pursuing that option at the time.

"Well, without, you know, I'll keep the snarky, you know, obvious comments out of the equation… and I'll say it because Sean has talked about it openly and I've said it before, but just to summarize it — I wouldn't have ever taken a risk on Shawn Michaels back then. His reputation — I mean, I had enough to deal with already and Shawn's reputation preceded him. So, I was not interested in hearing from Shawn Michaels in any way, shape, or form," Bischoff explained.

Bischoff emphasized that Michaels' reputation during that era made him someone he simply wasn't willing to gamble on, especially when WCW already had enough chaos to manage during such a crucial moment in the company's history. Even if he had entertained the idea in theory, Bischoff believed the role that Waltman filled in the nWo was uniquely suited to him and wasn't something Michaels could have improved upon, regardless of his undeniable in-ring talents.

"So, let's change that reality and think for a minute about me being open-minded to it and Sean being willing to jump over. What would that have looked like? I don't know, man… The thing about X-Pac is just like Ric Flair would be an example of bad casting in the nWo — X-Pac might have been one of the better examples of casting for the nWo," Bischoff said.

These comments reinforce the long-standing narrative about Shawn Michaels' backstage reputation during the mid-1990s, a period often discussed as one of the most turbulent stretches of his early career. At the same time, Bischoff's remarks serve as a reminder that the nWo's success wasn't just about acquiring big names — it was about finding the right personalities to make the group work and maintaining the specific chemistry that made the faction revolutionary.

Looking back, the nWo changed the wrestling business and helped redefine how factions were presented on television. While the idea of either Flair or Michaels joining the group has remained popular among fans who enjoy reimagining the Monday Night Wars era, Bischoff's comments make it clear that these concepts never moved beyond fan imagination and likely would have caused more problems than benefits if attempts had been made.

Pro Wrestling History and Archives

April 1986 represents one of the most extraordinarily busy periods in professional wrestling history, with the industry experiencing a convergence of major events that would define the era. During this single month, wrestling fans were treated to multiple giant supercards across the three major American promotions, creating an unprecedented level of competition and viewer interest.

The WWF's contribution to this historic month came in the form of WrestleMania 2, which introduced several significant innovations to the wrestling landscape. The event marked the debut of what would become one of wrestling's most iconic match stipulations: the big blue steel cage. Beyond the cage match itself, WrestleMania 2 made headlines by incorporating NFL players into a battle royal, bringing mainstream sports crossover appeal to professional wrestling in a way that had not been attempted before at such a high-profile level.

Simultaneously, the NWA hosted the inaugural Jim Crockett Sr. Memorial Tag Team Tournament, an event designed to honor the legacy of Jim Crockett Sr. while showcasing the promotion's deepest tag team talent. This tournament became a significant showcase for the NWA's tag team division and would become a recurring event in the promotion's calendar.

The AWA, not to be outdone, presented WrestleRock '86, adding yet another major supercard to an already crowded wrestling calendar. With three major promotions all running significant events within the same month, April 1986 created a perfect storm of wrestling activity that remains rarely matched in the industry's history.

Brian Cage Discusses Returning To The Ring, Botched Knee Surgery Delaying His Return

Brian Cage Discusses Returning To The Ring, Botched Knee Surgery Delaying His Return

Brian Cage has finally received medical clearance to return to professional wrestling after spending the last year sidelined with a serious injury that was further complicated by surgical complications.

Cage's injury timeline began with a torn quad that would have typically resulted in a nine-month recovery period. However, in an effort to address additional issues with his right knee, Cage elected to undergo knee surgery in June that included a special replacement procedure. The intention was to use the extra time to fully heal so that he could return to competition in November without any lingering problems.

Unfortunately, the June surgery did not go as planned. Speaking to Rob Van Dam on the 1 One a Kind podcast, Cage revealed the extent of the setback. "The whole point of getting what I got done on my right knee in June, was so that when November came around, I could be good to go instead of coming back and still having problems with my right knee. Long story short, they botched it. I should've come back in November, but I had to go back under the knife in November, and now I'm cleared from that surgery."

Cage admitted that discovering the surgical error in October was emotionally difficult. "When I found that out in October, I was pretty devastated," he said, reflecting on the difficult news that prolonged his recovery significantly beyond the original timeline.

Despite the extended layoff, Cage is optimistic about his return. He described his current physical condition as strong, though not quite at peak performance levels. "I feel great. I've been cleared for a while. I'm not 100% as far as performance-based. I'm not lifting as much weight on my leg lifts and I'm not jumping just as high just yet, but I can do everything I've done in the ring several times. I'm doing different dynamic jumping stuff on my off days. I'm feeling good and excited. I've been waiting to come back."

Cage has already begun his return to action with a significant moment. He participated in a battle royal match exactly one year after his initial quad injury, and notably eliminated the wrestler responsible for the original injury with the assistance of Chris Masters. This victory marked a symbolic return to competition for Cage.

Moving forward, Cage has an ambitious schedule in place. "I've got three matches on the same day Mania weekend. It just worked out that way," he explained, indicating that his return will be far from a slow, gradual process.

Cage remains under contract with AEW as a member of the Don Callis Family stable. However, the exact timing of his return to AEW television programming has not yet been announced. The company will determine the best moment to reintegrate the powerhouse into its roster as he continues his rehabilitation and rebuilding process.

Martinez described feeling underutilized and shelved by AEW, particularly during the latter portion of her employment. She elaborated on how the lack of utilization affected her confidence and mindset as a performer. According to Martinez, being contracted but not actively used created a psychological toll that forced her to question her own abilities and relevance in the wrestling industry.

"I think it happened last year when being contracted, especially with AEW and not being utilized to my full potential, kind of brought me into that weird space headspace of like, am I really, like, can I still hang with the females? Can I still hang with the top tier? Do I still have a lot to prove? Do I have anything to prove left? I'm blessed for the opportunity that Tony gave me for the last four years, but it felt like the last year, year and a half, almost two years, I kind of just sat on the shelf not doing anything for the company. And it kind of messes with your mind a lot. It kind of just makes you think, am I worth it. Does he really, you know, want to utilize my talent and my expertise?"

Martinez acknowledged several potential factors that may have contributed to her limited usage. She recognized that her wrestling style, which emphasizes technical proficiency and stiff striking, may not translate as effectively to a television format. She also speculated that some wrestlers in the locker room may have been reluctant to work with her, possibly due to the physical nature of her in-ring style.

During her time with AEW, Martinez pitched creative ideas for storylines and angles that she believed would showcase her abilities. Rather than continue in a diminished capacity or seek opportunities elsewhere, Martinez chose to take control of her narrative by retiring on her own terms.

When it became clear that AEW would not renew her contract, Martinez made the deliberate choice to step away from professional wrestling entirely. Will Vince McMahon Return To WWE?

For a few decades, it would've been unthinkable that Vince McMahon wouldn't be involved in WWE. The emperor of sports entertainment had taken his father's well-respected northeastern territory and within a handful of years had broken the majority of the rules of the old guard to propel it into a national promotion. He changed the perception of not only what professional wrestling was, but also what it could be. Granted, it's debatable if that was an overall positive or negative, but that's a different discussion for another time.

A few decades after Vince's expansion, his sports entertainment juggernaut was so massive that those from the prior generation that might've scoffed at his attempts to cross the regional lines of the territory system of a bygone era wouldn't have thought success on such a level was possible in the industry. As incredible as the old school business was, when kayfabe invoked legitimate emotion from the audience, the modern WWE was on a completely different scale. The promoters of their specific territories looked to draw a good house, whereas Vince became a billionaire by putting his wrestling company on the stock market.

So it wasn't a stretch to think that the grim reaper might be the only one that could've taken Vince McMahon away from WWE, and given Vince's sheer defiance of anything that opposed him, even the reaper's odds were questionable. That's why it was so shocking when the Wall Street Journal story initially broken in June of 2022 revealed that Vince had paid off several women over the course of several years to keep quiet about affairs and misconduct allegations. These accusations ranged from consensual affairs to claims of assault. The story has been well-documented and discussed at length so there's no need to rehash some of the details of the allegations, but for a guy that was so meticulous with every aspect of his sports entertainment production, it was almost unbelievable that he threw around an estimated 19 million dollars to cover up his affairs with the notion that money was all that he needed to essentially buy his way out of any responsibility.

If proven to be true, and there's evidence to support the claims against him, Vince's behavior was horrendous and careless, which is the exact opposite of how he carefully crafted his sports entertainment product. This was a guy that beat Uncle Sam in court in 1994, partially because he was smart enough to never put himself in a position where he could be directly or indirectly tied to his roster taking steroids. It just didn't make sense that he would leave a paper trail of non-disclosure agreements, but it appears that being a billionaire made him think that the almighty dollar could cover up almost anything.

The lengthy story about his retirement before his return and the way that he strong-armed his way back onto the WWE board to set up the eventual sale to Endeavor that created the TKO corporation after the merger with the UFC is well known. More recently, a lawsuit about the merger went into the discovery phase last month, claiming that there were text messages exchanged that prove that McMahon had prearranged the sale to Endeavor because the conglomerate agreed to keep him in the position as the head of WWE. Of course, this would be a violation of the regulations of the Securities and Exchange Commission since it would be evidence that McMahon didn't act in the best interest of the stockholders if he didn't legitimately take offers from other potential buyers.

This will probably be nothing more than a relatively small payout to investors, given the massive amount of money that has been made for those same shareholders since the merger with the value of the stock, especially after the WWE and UFC media rights deals respectively. However, the bigger point is, Vince could've been working hand-in-hand with Endeavor long before the merger was officially on the table. Furthermore, there were reports that Vince was orchestrating events behind the scenes during this entire period, suggesting his influence never truly diminished despite his formal departure from the company.

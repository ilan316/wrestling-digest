🔥 Hikaru Shida wins AEW TBS Championship

Hikaru Shida is the new AEW TBS Champion after emerging victorious from the first-ever Survival of the Fittest match held on the July 1 episode of AEW Dynamite in San Diego, California. The championship had been vacated by Willow Nightingale at the end of May due to a shoulder injury, leading AEW to book a series of qualifying matches to determine who would compete for the vacant title.

Six competitors qualified for the Survival of the Fittest elimination match: Harley Cameron, Hikaru Shida, Kris Statlander, Maika, Persephone, and Queen Aminata. The match was structured as an elimination contest where wrestlers had to score pinfalls or submissions to advance, with the last woman standing becoming the new TBS Champion.

The action started immediately with all six women fighting across the ring. Harley Cameron was the first competitor eliminated when Persephone hit her with a Razor's Edge, though some reports described it as a powerbomb from the top rope. Right after that elimination, Kris Statlander attacked Persephone with a powerful Olympic Slam, establishing herself as a dominant force early in the match.

As the match progressed, Statlander and Maika traded heavy shots while Hikaru Shida utilized a kendo stick against Statlander to gain an advantage. Statlander interfered when Shida was using the weapon against other competitors. After trading suplexes with Maika, Shida caught her with a jackknife pin to eliminate the Stardom competitor, cutting the field down to four women.

Queen Aminata then took control of the match, hitting Shida with several hard shots. During this sequence, Statlander interfered once again. Aminata countered a suplex attempt from Shida with a big headbutt and tried to capitalize on the moment. However, Persephone took advantage of the chaos and rolled up Aminata with a folding press to score a surprise elimination.

As the match reached its final stages with only Statlander, Shida, and Persephone remaining, the three women exchanged near falls and big moves. Statlander relied on her power while Shida used striking and technical skills. Persephone continued looking for another surprise pin throughout this sequence. Statlander eventually eliminated Persephone with a victory roll, setting up a one-on-one contest between Statlander and Shida.

The final two competitors traded counters, submissions, and signature moves. Shida hit a Falcon Arrow that nearly ended the match and even attempted to use the championship belt as a weapon, but Statlander avoided the shot and fought back. Both women continued exchanging offense with multiple pin reversal attempts.

The finish came when Shida locked Statlander in a tight ankle lock submission hold. Statlander tried crawling toward the ropes to escape, but rope breaks were actually illegal in this particular match format. As Statlander reached for the ring apron trying to escape the submission, Persephone returned to the ringside area and smashed Statlander in the head with the TBS Championship belt, knocking her out. With Statlander unable to defend herself after the belt shot, the referee stopped the match and declared Shida victorious.

The controversial finish, aided by the outside interference from Persephone, crowned Hikaru Shida as the new AEW TBS Champion. Shida locked in another submission hold on the already incapacitated Statlander after the belt attack, but the match had already effectively been decided by the chairshot. This is Shida's first TBS Championship and her first title reign in AEW since November 2023, when her second AEW Women's World Championship run ended after just 39 days.

The TBS Championship lineage now includes Jade Cargill, Kris Statlander, Julia Hart, Willow Nightingale, Mercedes Mone, Willow Nightingale again, and now Hikaru Shida. Shida, who is a three-time AEW Women's World Champion, adds another accolade to her impressive resume with the company.

Kenny Omega Makes a Deal with the Devil to Get an AEW World Title Match

AEW Dynamite Beach Break is shaping up to be one of the most consequential events in company history, with multiple title matches and high-stakes storylines set for July 8. The biggest announcement came on the July 1 episode of Dynamite when AEW World Champion MJF proposed a potentially career-altering world title opportunity to Kenny Omega.

The stipulation was offered immediately following MJF's successful title defense against Mark Briscoe, which came three days after Briscoe's team defeated MJF's team at Sunday's Forbidden Door PPV in a new Devil's Door steel cage match. The match between MJF and Briscoe was a bloody affair with both men taking crazy bumps. Briscoe managed to hit Jay Driller, his late brother's finisher and the move that had beaten Friedman multiple times in the past. This time, however, Big Hebrew kicked out. After a rollup exchange and a counter slam, MJF retained the championship with a Heatseeker.

Immediately after retaining his title, MJF attacked the fallen Briscoe with the Dynamite Diamond Ring until Kenny Omega rushed to the ring to make the save. Omega grabbed a microphone and issued an immediate challenge to MJF for the AEW World Championship. MJF declined the request, reminding Omega that he had already defeated him twice and even kicked out of the One-Winged Angel. However, instead of walking away, the champion revealed what he called an "exploding offer."

MJF proposed a title match for the Beach Break edition of Dynamite the following week, but only under one major condition. If Omega lost, he would never be allowed to challenge for the AEW World Championship again. This stipulation mirrors the career-altering agreement that Hangman Adam Page accepted earlier in the year. At Revolution 2026, Hangman put his championship future on the line in a Texas Death Match against MJF and lost, permanently removing himself from future AEW World Title contention. The same stipulation had also been used previously when Cody Rhodes challenged Chris Jericho for the AEW World Championship at Full Gear 2019, losing that match and being permanently barred from competing for the company's top prize.

Throughout the remainder of the show, Omega received warnings not to accept the stipulation. Both The Young Bucks and Will Ospreay, who earned a title shot at August's All In, tried to talk Omega out of it. In backstage segments, MJF continued to mock Omega, insisting he had already beaten him twice and questioning whether "The Best Bout Machine" would have the courage to accept the terms. Earlier in the show, Omega had questioned Ospreay's allegiance to the Death Riders in a backstage segment and said he was coming for the title if MJF defeated Briscoe.

Despite the warnings and stakes involved, Omega eventually came to the ring for the main event spot and delivered a big babyface promo. At WWE Night of Champions 2026, Sami Zayn achieved a major career milestone by defeating Cody Rhodes and GUNTHER to win the Undisputed WWE Championship for the first time in his career. Vince Russo, former WWE writer, has been particularly vocal in his criticism of the title change. Russo emphasized that many wrestlers have spent similar or longer periods in the business without winning world titles, citing examples like Mr. Perfect and Scott Hall. He questioned whether WWE genuinely believed the decision would increase viewership, suggesting instead that it was driven by emotion rather than business logic. Russo argued that a world champion should be someone who immediately looks like a larger-than-life superstar, comparing the role to elite athletes and movie stars like Brad Pitt, George Clooney, and Arnold Schwarzenegger.

In his criticism, Russo described Zayn as "every man" who could be anyone's neighbor, Uber driver, or cook at a fast food restaurant, stating there is nothing special about him in appearance. While acknowledging that Zayn excelled in comedy segments with The Bloodline, Russo contended that transitioning from comedy to world champion is a problematic booking decision. He predicted that the move would not draw new fans and would instead push casual viewers further away from the product.

Russo made a controversial comparison between Zayn's title win and David Arquette's infamous WCW World Title victory, suggesting they were equally ridiculous booking decisions. "You can have a wrestling company, but if you don't have television, what do you have? Television was everything. Once they decided they didn't want wrestling on their networks anymore, that was it. Rather than dismissing Russo's work, Nash acknowledged the extraordinary difficulty of the job Russo was asked to perform. "I have to defend Vince a little bit here. He'd be the first one to tell you not everything he did worked. But think about what you're asking somebody to do. You've got all these Hall of Famers. Nash further defended Russo on a personal level, describing him as "straight as an arrow" and pushing back against the longstanding perceptions that have surrounded the former WCW head writer. While acknowledging that creative decisions sometimes fail—pointing out that ideas that sound good on a Wednesday morning don't always translate to successful television—Nash emphasized that the creative process in a company of WCW's size was inherently difficult regardless of who was making those decisions.

Where Would a New Fan Best Start With Wrestling? The Answer Surprised Even Me

I was recently posed with the eternal question from a new wrestling fan: Where do I start? With AEW, WWE, AAA, TNA and NXT all having readily accessible shows on streaming and TV, it's actually kind of a difficult choice. What's great about wrestling is there is pretty much a new entry point every week. If done right, new viewers can be caught up to speed on the immediate stories and plot lines right in the shows themselves via commentary and recap packages. That doesn't however always translate to the best experience for a potential new fan who is just getting into wrestling.

I thought long and hard about what I would show someone to get them into the wild ride we call professional wrestling. It's a much more nuanced decision than "what match would I show them" or "what promo would do it." After I'd considered the variables, I was quite surprised with where I landed.

WWE is the best known choice, but…

The obvious instinct would be to show a newcomer some WWE. It's the standard bearer and the company most people think of when they think of wrestling with big names that are well known in pop culture circles. The problem is that WWE's programming has been so flat lately and everything moves so slowly that I just don't think someone new to wrestling would have a good experience with it.

NXT unfortunately is currently a bad TV show. Segments bleed into other segments, commentary barely talks about the matches, and we spend more time in Robert Stone's office than we do in the ring. It's a far cry from the classic Black & Gold days. It oddly seems both overbooked and not well thought out at the same time. The talent is good and the matches can be fun—Tony D'Angelo vs. Naraku at Great American Bash was great—but it's almost impossible to know what's going on, and I say that knowing what's going on.

The best possibility of the WWE brands for a newcomer would be AAA as it's a fast-paced, fun and exciting show. The mask vs. mask match is still a frontrunner for Match of the Year in my mind, but the show itself is a lot to take in for new viewers. I think they would be amazed at what they saw, but wouldn't really know what's going on without taking in a few episodes. The shorter length works in its favor, but I think that I would be more likely to show them select matches from AAA as opposed to full shows.

AEW is where the best wrestle...a lot

Showing someone interested in wrestling an episode of Dynamite or Collision might stir up some interest, but I think that AEW has two factors that make it less accessible to a new fan. The first issue is that AEW programming tends to be very inside baseball. There are so many callbacks, nods and references that veterans of the Wrestling Wars appreciate, but a new person probably wouldn't get. Guys like Chris Jericho or even MJF might bring some people to the table, but even though they tore the house down at Forbidden Door, I doubt a new wrestling fan would be as invested in a Will Osprey or Swerve Strickland.

The other aspect is that AEW programming is so jam packed with matches back-to-back-to-back that even though they are usually really good matches, I think that someone not used to that might just gloss over and lose interest. It's kind of like trying to watch the Lord of The Rings movies all in a row. By the start of The Return of the King, you're so exhausted as a viewer that you don't really care who makes it to Mount Doom.

The AEW-run Ring of Honor has all these issues in spades. Even though it's got some fantastic talent putting on great matches week after week, there's no hook for a person who's just coming into it. It lacks consistency in its programming and I think that would turn people off.

Maybe they need to go where the action is Total and Non-Stop…

In thinking about all this, even I was surprised to find myself in the Impact Zone. But bear with me here because there's some reasoning behind what I'm going to say. I think TNA would be my choice. TNA has been through it all and has a storied history.

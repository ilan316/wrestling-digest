🔥 Will Ospreay AEW neck surgery recovery

Will Ospreay has opened up about his recovery process following major neck surgery that threatened to end his professional wrestling career. The AEW star recently returned to competition in New Japan Pro Wrestling, where he became a new NEVER Openweight 6-Man Tag Team Champion with his United Empire faction.

In an interview with Forbes, Ospreay detailed how his neck condition deteriorated over more than a year before becoming severe in May of last year. He described the moment when his condition "started fucking destroying" him, forcing difficult conversations with his medical team that ultimately led to surgery and rehabilitation. Ospreay emphasized that this was his first surgery ever, and it turned out to be one of the most major surgical procedures he could have undergone.

The financial burden of the surgery was substantial, and Ospreay credited AEW president Tony Khan for making his return possible. Ospreay explained that when he saw the medical bill, it was more expensive than his own house. He expressed deep gratitude to Khan, noting that the procedure itself was only feasible due to advances in medical science. Ten years ago, such a neck injury would have medically disqualified someone from wrestling entirely. Ospreay acknowledged that there were moments during his recovery when he seriously contemplated stepping away from professional wrestling altogether.

Throughout his rehabilitation, Ospreay turned to several wrestlers who had experienced similar neck injuries. He specifically credited his surgeon, Dr. Wallace, for giving him a second chance at his career. Beyond his medical team, Ospreay leaned heavily on support from his peers in AEW. Adam Copeland became a crucial mentor figure, having undergone three of the same surgeries that Ospreay had. Copeland provided Ospreay with his personal number and made himself available to talk whenever Ospreay needed to vent or discuss his concerns, even making accommodations for time zone differences when Copeland traveled to London.

Ospreay also spoke with Kyle O'Reilly, Roderick Strong, and Bryan Danielson, all of whom had suffered severe neck injuries throughout their careers. He expressed appreciation for how quickly news of his injury spread backstage and how his peers rallied around him with genuine support and well wishes during his recovery period. The wrestler described the wrestling community as "such a good group of people backstage."

The return to in-ring competition, however, has not been without its challenges. Ospreay admitted that even seemingly simple aspects of professional wrestling now cause him anxiety. Running the ropes, a fundamental aspect of wrestling that he had done thousands of times throughout his career, became an anxiety-inducing experience. He questioned whether he had been running the ropes incorrectly his entire career or whether his neck muscles simply hadn't yet developed sufficient strength after surgery. Basic movements like forward rolls and back rolls, which previously came with great flexibility and range of motion, now feel unfamiliar and restricted.

The surgery has necessitated significant modifications to Ospreay's wrestling style. He explained that he's uncomfortable doing certain moves and has eliminated them from his arsenal entirely. Most notably, he will no longer perform the Shooting Star Press, a high-flying signature move that requires extensive head rotation and arch that his surgically reconstructed neck cannot safely accommodate. This adjustment represents a substantial change for a wrestler known for his aerial offense and innovative offense.

Despite these challenges, Ospreay has returned to competition with nearly ten matches completed since his return in March following his six-month absence. He continues to adapt his approach and work within the limitations imposed by his surgery while maintaining his position as an active competitor.

When asked about other wrestling topics, Ospreay expressed enthusiasm about the prospect of Xavier Woods and Kofi Kingston potentially joining AEW following their recent departure from WWE. He stated he would love to see them sign with AEW and face them in competition. Ospreay noted that he had heard nothing but wonderful things about both men and expressed admiration for Kingston's WWE Championship win and what it represented for people. While Ospreay has never met Woods in person, they have communicated through direct messages, with their conversations focusing primarily on wrestling attire. Woods has apparently messaged Ospreay multiple times expressing his appreciation for Ospreay's jackets. Ospreay emphasized that both Woods and Kingston have been described by everyone he's spoken to as wonderful human beings, and he would enthusiastically welcome the opportunity to wrestle them if the professional circumstances aligned.

Sting's surprise AEW Dynamite appearance

AEW Dynamite opened on an emotional note Wednesday night with a tribute to the late Ted Turner, and new details have surfaced regarding how the segment came together behind the scenes. The week's episode was dedicated to the legendary WCW owner and TBS founder, who passed away earlier in the week at the age of 87. The show began with a surprise appearance from Sting, who joined Tony Schiavone in the ring for a heartfelt tribute honoring Turner's impact on professional wrestling and television.

The sudden appearance of Sting had many believing that AEW had flown in the wrestling legend on short notice in order to pull the tribute off. However, Dave Meltzer revealed on Wrestling Observer Radio that while Tony Khan did rewrite portions of the show in order to include the Turner tribute, Sting was already going to be in the North Charleston, South Carolina area for the show anyway.

According to Meltzer, Sting had not been brought in specifically to appear on the broadcast itself. The plan was for Sting to come in and do promotional work in Charleston because AEW felt that, given they were in the Carolinas and Sting started early in his career working that territory, his presence would be valuable for media appearances and related promotional duties. So they flew Sting in to do media and other promotional work. Notably, Sting himself asked when he got there, or even before arriving, that he did not want to be on the show because it was not his time anymore and he did not want to take time away from anybody else on the card.

That position quickly changed once news broke about Turner's passing. Tony Khan reportedly made major changes to the show shortly before airtime in order to properly honor the man many credit with helping transform wrestling on cable television during the WCW era. Khan basically rewrote much of the show, because he felt they needed to open the show with a proper tribute. He changed things around and wanted Tony Schiavone and Sting to do the tribute to Ted Turner, which Sting was happy to do given Turner's significance to his career.

The segment itself was completely unscripted in nature. As observers noted while watching it, Schiavone and Sting simply went out there and winged it. The spontaneous nature of the tribute was evident and the segment was very well done. All Elite Wrestling honored a man who changed professional wrestling forever, with Tony Schiavone and Sting paying tribute to Turner on behalf of the entire company. The wrestling community's thoughts went out to Ted Turner's family, friends and everyone whose lives he touched.

AEW Redemption in Montreal: Why it might be a big gamble for the company

This column is based on a rumor that was reported by Dave Meltzer, and it might not even happen, but the topic warrants discussion mostly because understanding the Montreal market requires local knowledge and perspective as to why bringing the Redemption pay-per-view to the area at the end of July could be a significant gamble for All Elite Wrestling.

First, let's examine the history. Montreal has been a WWE hotspot since the late 1980s, after Vince McMahon was able to eliminate the remaining territorial companies in the area, and that stronghold is not going to change. One thing that has helped WWE throughout the years is ensuring major local talent on the roster to bring in the local French community and sell tickets for shows. Having local talent who are big enough stars is also a plus because they can do promotion in both French-language and English-language media, which always makes a significant difference. AEW doesn't really have that advantage. Sure, they have Mike Bailey, but he's not really well known outside of internet wrestling community circles, and local media might not be willing to give him a lot of time to promote an AEW show like they would for Kevin Owens or Sami Zayn.

You also have to look at how poorly AEW performed the last time they came in December 2023. Attendance for those shows was 5,043 for Dynamite and 2,852 for Collision, for a total of 7,895 tickets sold over two nights in a building that can easily hold between 15,000 and 18,000 people, depending on how the stage is set up. So right away, they have a huge mountain to climb. In comparison, Total Nonstop Action Wrestling did 4,500 for Slammiversary and around 2,500 for each of their television tapings seven months later, and that was mostly thanks to PCO, who did a lot of advertising for the show. They also ran a much smaller building, which made the event feel a lot bigger.

Then you have the fact that July, especially the last two weeks of July, is a huge time for entertainment in Montreal. You already have to compete with the Just for Laughs festival and, to some extent, the Fantasia International Film Festival, but you also have to compete for available venues, especially at the Bell Centre, as the arena is usually booked solid for a good portion of the month. Here's just a small sample of what's coming to Montreal around the time AEW wants to bring in their pay-per-view: Lionel Richie on July 5, a Women's National Basketball Association game on July 12, Billy Talent and Alexisonfire on July 17, J. Cole on July 25, and Ariana Grande on July 28, 30, and 31.

The Knee-Jerk Counter Argument: TKO is OK (It's Just Business)

Professional wrestling has always been profit-driven, even though the scale and structure of TKO represent a major shift from most of wrestling history. In earlier eras of wrestling, territorial promotions in the 1970s and 1980s, and even the national expansion of WWE under Vince McMahon, were still centrally controlled entertainment and wrestling businesses. From Gagne managing the AWA to Heyman with ECW to even Bischoff with WCW, these promotions were driven by the instincts of the promoters rather than by large-scale corporate shareholder expectations.

TKO operates more like a modern media conglomerate, emphasizing quarterly growth, global licensing, rights fees, sponsorship integration, and operational efficiency at a scale wrestling historically never experienced. What is Enshittification?

The term "enshittification", coined by Cory Doctorow, refers to the gradual degradation of a service as it shifts from serving users to extracting maximum profit from them. According to Doctorow, this occurs in stages.

Early stage (user-focused): The service is high-quality, useful, and often generous to attract users.

Growth stage (balance): The platform begins monetizing but still tries to keep users satisfied.

Late stage (profit-maximizing): The company shifts heavily toward extracting value—often at the expense of users and partners.

Examples of enshittification are abundant in modern business. Facebook evolved from a clean, user-focused feed into one saturated with ads and algorithmically promoted content designed to maximize engagement and revenue. Uber initially attracted users with low fares and generous driver incentives, but later raised prices and reduced driver compensation as it pushed toward profitability. Amazon shifted from a straightforward retail experience to a marketplace where search results are often dominated by sponsored listings and lower-quality products. Streaming services such as Netflix similarly shifted from offering broad, relatively stable catalogs at low cost to fragmented content libraries, rising subscription prices, and frequent account restrictions. Fast food chains have followed similar trajectories, compromising ingredient quality and portion sizes as they pursued greater operational efficiency and shareholder returns.

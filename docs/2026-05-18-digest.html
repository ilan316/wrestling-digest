🔥 Sidney Akeem AEW/ROH Deal

Sidney Akeem says the next chapter of his wrestling career could soon involve a full-time role with All Elite Wrestling and Ring of Honor. Many fans will remember Akeem from his time in WWE under multiple identities, including Reggie, the acrobatic former 24/7 Champion, as well as the masked Scrypts persona and later as part of O.T.M. in NXT before his WWE contract expired in 2024.

Speaking on the Rewind Recap Relive podcast with R3 Jonah, Akeem reflected on his unconventional path into professional wrestling, which began after his work with Cirque du Soleil. He discussed his journey from humble beginnings in St. Louis, where his options as an adult were very limited, to achieving success in entertainment and wrestling.

"I never thought that I'd be here. I never envisioned or imagined the life that I'm living. It's true because of where I come from. As a kid from St. Louis, my options as an adult were very limited. Now, to think that I made it to the mountaintop in the circus world. I made it to WWE. I wrestled for TNA, and now I am in the midst of getting a deal done with AEW/Ring of Honor. I'm just like, what is this world? What is this life that I'm living?"

Akeem revealed that active discussions are currently taking place regarding a possible long-term agreement with AEW and ROH. He emphasized the emotional significance of his work with the promotion, particularly regarding his Soleil persona, which carries deep personal meaning connected to his real-life background and artistic identity.

"It felt so great to not only debut on Ring of Honor, because I've done that years ago, but to put something that was so close and personal so late on Ring of Honor, then to see the reception that it got… It's better than my WWE debut. Because when you pour so much of your heart into something, you get to showcase that, and you see people, 'Oh, that is damn good.' That feels so good."

According to Akeem, bringing the Soleil persona to ROH carried deep emotional significance because of its connection to his artistic identity. "To put something that was so close and personal, Soleil, on Ring of Honor, and then to see the reception that it got… in some ways it's better than my WWE debut."

Since departing WWE, Akeem has already made several appearances for ROH under both the Sidney Akeem name and his more personal Soleil character. Most recently, he teamed with Gringo Loco against Top Flight, featuring Dante Martin and Darius Martin, in the promotion.

Akeem originally entered WWE television in 2020 as Reginald Thomas, the sommelier aligned with Carmella on WWE SmackDown. His acrobatic athleticism quickly made him stand out, including a memorable early match against Sasha Banks in 2021. He was later repackaged into the character Scrypts, a cryptic masked wrestler, and finally as a member of O.T.M in NXT until his contract ran out in 2024.

Since leaving WWE, Akeem has continued rebuilding his identity across the independent wrestling scene while leaning more heavily into the high-level athletic and performance elements that originally made him unique. If a deal with AEW and ROH is finalized, it would mark another major evolution in one of wrestling's most unconventional modern career journeys.

Karrion Kross and Scarlett WWE Return/AEW Debut

Karrion Kross and Scarlett Departure From WWE And Current Success

Since August 2025, Karrion Kross and Scarlett have carved out a successful path outside of WWE, enjoying considerable opportunities across the independent wrestling circuit and Major League Wrestling, where Kross currently holds the MLW World Heavyweight Championship. Despite their flourishing career away from WWE, the couple has not completely ruled out a return to the company that initially elevated them to mainstream prominence.

When discussing the possibility of returning to WWE, Scarlett expressed openness to the idea while acknowledging the circumstances of their departure. "Never say never," she told D-Von Dudley. "We love that locker room. We miss the writers. We miss the producers. We miss them. We blame TKO for our deal not getting done. So if they decide to fix things, maybe. If they decide to call one day, maybe."

Kross echoed similar sentiments about maintaining dialogue with WWE. "We never closed the door on dialogue," he said, referring to his attempts to inquire about his contract. "We never had a bad relationship with WWE. We've stayed in contact with everybody. Is it possible? Of course it is. There were still things that needed to be discussed. They closed the door on those discussions. We leave the door open for all discussions with everybody."

The circumstances surrounding their WWE departure highlight a breakdown in negotiations. Days before their contracts expired in summer 2025, WWE placed a new offer on the table for Kross with a 24-hour acceptance deadline. When Kross requested additional information about the metrics used to determine the financial terms, WWE declined to provide clarification and rescinded the offer entirely. Scarlett never received an offer at all during this negotiation period.

AEW As A Potential Landing Spot

All Elite Wrestling, widely recognized as the second largest professional wrestling promotion globally, represents another potential destination for the couple. However, Kross and Scarlett have made clear that company size or financial compensation alone would not drive their decision-making process.

When asked about the possibility of an AEW debut, Kross articulated his priorities clearly. "Wherever we go, whether it's an independent or it's a televised company or streaming, whatever have it, we want to be out there performing at our best," he explained. "We don't want to show up, collect a paycheck, and just leave. We're making an excellent living. We're in a very unique situation, man. It doesn't always work out this way for everybody independently, but we're in an excellent situation where we're able to work all over the world. To put it as plainly as possible, it's not about clearing a particular amount of money. It's about the purpose of the performance."

Scarlett reinforced this philosophy, emphasizing that meaningful long-term creative plans would be necessary to make AEW a serious contender for their next opportunity. "We want to do meaningful performances with purpose and have fun," she stated. "Since we've been independent, we've been able to do that. It's feeding our soul."

Both Kross and Scarlett emphasized that their commitment to excellence remains constant regardless of audience size. Whether performing before 40,000 fans or 2,000, they intend to deliver their highest-level performances. An AEW opportunity would place them alongside a roster featuring numerous former WWE stars including Jon Moxley, Toni Storm, Chris Jericho, and Adam "Edge" Copeland.

Scarlett's Comments On Kross' Post-WWE Success

Adding another layer to the narrative of their WWE departure, Scarlett has posted messages that appear to indirectly critique how Kross was handled during his time with the company. Following Kross winning the WSW World Heavyweight Championship while using the Killer Kross name, Scarlett shared a series of posts that fans quickly interpreted as shots at WWE's management of her partner.

"There's something really powerful about watching someone who bet on themselves after people tried to define what his ceiling was," she posted online. In follow-up messages, she continued with broader commentary on personal value and self-determination. "Don't ever let someone's opinion of your value become your reality… because your current position is not your final destination."

Scarlett went further in articulating her perspective on moving beyond perceived limitations. "Sometimes the best thing you can do is walk away from the limits people place on you and build something else for yourself instead. Bet on yourself. Even when nobody else does."

Context For Kross' Career Trajectory

Kross had achieved considerable success during his time in WWE's NXT brand, establishing himself as a dominant force with strong presentation and booking. However, his transition to WWE's main roster did not maintain the same momentum, with many fans and observers feeling his presentation never fully matched the momentum he previously carried. The gap between his NXT run and main roster utilization became a frequent topic of discussion within the wrestling community.

Since departing WWE, Kross has successfully rebuilt his professional profile on the independent scene and in international wrestling promotions. His current championship reigns and strong performances have positioned him once again as a top attraction in professional wrestling. His post-WWE resurgence has only intensified conversations among fans who believe he possessed far greater potential than WWE ultimately allowed him to demonstrate during his tenure with the company.

With championship gold now around his waist and momentum building through strong independent performances, the discussion surrounding Kross's career renaissance outside WWE continues to grow, with Scarlett's social media commentary adding credence to the narrative that both he and his partner felt constrained by WWE's creative decisions and booking strategies.

Jeff Jarrett on TKO and AEW

Jeff Jarrett, WWE Hall of Famer and veteran wrestling booker from his TNA years, has weighed in on both AEW's creative direction and TKO's business approach in recent comments made on his podcast.

Regarding AEW's experimentation with non-traditional venues, Jarrett expressed admiration for the company's willingness to take creative risks. This past Saturday, AEW presented the one-hour TV special "Collision: Fairway To Hell" from an indoor pro golf arena at the SoFi Center in Palm Beach Gardens, Florida. The event featured the wrestling ring set up directly in the middle of the golf course, with talent utilizing the green and sand traps throughout the show. The main event saw Darby Allin retain the AEW World Title against PAC in this unique setting.

"I just think there is a lot of tailwind that you can get on trying new venues and this is one of them. I just think that it helps the product. I think it helps the industry," Jarrett stated. WWE House Shows Return

WWE House Shows Are Back, But Not For the Reason They Should Be

WWE is adding 10 house show stops to its schedule for July and August this summer. According to reports, the reason WWE is adding these shows to its schedule is because the company wants to get younger talent more experience in the ring. But there is another reason house shows can be a good financial move for the company, even if the individual shows themselves lose money.

The Corporate Short-Term Thinking Problem

There used to be a saying in business that went something along the lines of, "Don't be penny wise, but pound foolish." Basically, what this saying conveyed was: don't do anything stupid that makes you money short-term, but costs you money long-term. Nobody says this anymore. It's not considered good advice because in modern corporate culture, it does pay to be penny wise and pound foolish, so long as it makes your next quarter's results look good.

In a world where companies buy other companies daily, sell companies just as fast, and are owned by investment portfolios, nobody has a vision or a dream for what their product looks like. They just have a dream of numbers going up in their bank account. Cutting WWE house shows might have been a penny wise move, but it's very pound-foolish if you care about where the industry will be in 20 years.

House Shows Create Lifelong Fans

House shows are money losers, so TKO got rid of them. Why would you do anything that loses money? Well, the idea is that it would grow your fan base in the long term. Wrestling, like baseball, like other sports, and like many other things, is something that is passed down to someone from an older generation.

A parent, an older sibling, or somebody else takes them to wrestling, and at a young age, that person forms a connection with it that is countless times deeper than the connection they form with it through a television screen. They're surrounded by thousands of people who also like this same ridiculous thing that they like. The performers from the television screen are right in front of them. It becomes a much bigger deal.

That young fan is far more likely, in theory, to become a diehard fan throughout their life and, even more importantly, pass down their wrestling fandom to their kids when the time comes. When you cut out house shows, you cut out the creation of fans through this method. It's a poor long-term strategy for your business.

Why Vince McMahon Kept House Shows Running

When Vince McMahon continued running house shows, even though they lost money, there was a reason for it. It wasn't just that Vince liked to throw away money. It was that he saw house shows as a pivotal part of growing the audience. And he was right.

But in 2026, what's the point in doing something that will be good for your business 20 years from now if there's a chance you're not even going to hold any stock in that company 20 years from now? The only people within an industry trying to improve that industry are people who intend on passing down a business to their kids. When it came time to do that, Vince wasn't willing, and now TKO is running the company. And we got what we got: a make-money-now, who-cares-about-tomorrow approach.

House Shows Are About Fan Development, Not Just Talent Development

WWE bringing back house shows because they are good for talent development is part of the equation, but not the full story. This is not just talent development. It is also fan development. But does TKO Group Holdings care about the state the wrestling industry will be in the 2040s or 2050s? They don't. Because everyone involved with that company right now will have cashed out by then.

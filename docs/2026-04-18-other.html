🔥 GCW Spring Break X Results

Joey Janela's Spring Break X took place on April 17 in Las Vegas and featured several career-defining and franchise-altering moments that will be remembered as pivotal points in GCW history.

In one of the evening's most emotional encounters, Allie Katch faced her former partner and friend EFFY in a loser leaves GCW match. The bout showcased the complexity of their relationship, as Allie ultimately could not bring herself to use every tool available to win. When presented with an opportunity to strike EFFY with a wrench, Allie hesitated and instead hugged EFFY. The moment of vulnerability proved costly as EFFY capitalized immediately, striking Allie with a headbutt and jabbing her with a screwdriver before securing the pinfall victory. The match also saw interference from Dark Sheik, who attempted to aid Allie, and Manders, who was struck by EFFY during the chaos. With this loss, Allie Katch must exit GCW after joining the promotion as a full-time competitor in 2019. She leaves behind a legacy that includes a GCW Tag Team Championship reign alongside EFFY.

The Sandman's final professional wrestling match served as an emotional centerpiece of the evening. The ECW legend came out to "Enter Sandman" by Metallica one last time, accompanied by Kendra Lust. However, Lust's presence in the match would prove duplicitous, as she eventually turned on Sandman and aligned with his opponent, the Invisible Man, striking the icon with a Singapore cane. The match descended into absolute chaos from the opening bell, featuring weapons, brawling, and pure mayhem befitting a send-off tied to Sandman's legendary career. The bout included numerous surprise appearances and cameos that made it an unforgettable spectacle. Missy Hyatt appeared to save Sandman after Lust's attack. Additional appearances came from Bill Alfonso, who managed the Invisible Man, ECW Zombie, Richard Holliday, ICP, Vampiro, 2 Tuff Tony, and Early Morning Guy Steele. The match featured approximately 20 referee bumps, adding to the bedlam.

Mick Foley made an unannounced appearance that stole significant attention during Sandman's farewell. The Hardcore Legend jumped into the ring and went straight after the Invisible Man, unloading with punches in the corner before dropping him with his signature double arm DDT. Foley then pulled out Mr. Socko and locked in the Mandible Claw, sending the crowd into a frenzy with this show of support for his fellow hardcore icon. Despite this unexpected assistance, the moment was not enough to change the match's outcome. The Invisible Man, who was rejoined by his formerly dead brother Invisible Stan, recovered and executed a low blow on both Sandman and Foley. The Invisible Man then rolled up Sandman for the three count, bringing the ECW legend's career to a close in defeat. As the match concluded, Sandman left one last beer in the ring while the crowd chanted "Thank you, Sandman," creating a poignant farewell moment that transcended the in-ring result.

In a different highlight of the evening, San Francisco 49ers tight end George Kittle made a surprise appearance at Spring Break X. Kittle appeared after the Grab the Brass Ring ladder match, which notably was not a traditional ladder match, celebrating with 1 Called Manders. The two share a history as college roommates at the University of Iowa. Kittle, who is a noted wrestling fan and friends with WWE Intercontinental Champion Penta, has previously involved himself at WrestleMania events. During his time at the event, Kittle was also seen hanging out with NBA legend Dennis Rodman. By winning the Grab the Brass Ring ladder match, Manders earned the right to challenge for the GCW Title at any point of his choosing, establishing himself as a major contender in the promotion's future.

🔥 Brooke Hogan Hulk Hogan Will Documentary

Brooke Hogan has opened up about deeply personal decisions she made regarding her late father Hulk Hogan's legacy, revealing regrets about removing herself from his will while simultaneously choosing not to participate in an upcoming Netflix documentary about his life.

Speaking on the Hollywood Raw Podcast with Dax Holt and Adam Glyn, Brooke explained that she removed herself from her father's will because of safety concerns, not financial motivations. She described situations that frightened her and made her worry about her own well-being and that of her family. "There were situations that had me so scared. I really took myself off the will because I was actually scared for myself, my family, my safety," she said. She emphasized that no amount of money would have been worth the risk she felt she was facing. "There's not enough money in the world that would matter with what I felt I was facing."

However, Brooke admitted that she now regrets this decision, though her regret stems from emotional rather than financial concerns. "I regret that decision. Not because of the money — I've never been about the money — but because now I don't have a seat at the table," she explained. The loss of that symbolic seat at the table following her father's death has carried lasting emotional weight for her.

Hulk Hogan passed away in 2025 at the age of 71 after suffering cardiac arrest at his home in Florida. His death became worldwide news given his integral role in professional wrestling and entertainment history. WWE honored his legacy by unveiling a bronze statue celebrating his contributions to the sport. Additionally, Netflix is preparing to release a documentary titled "Hulk Hogan: Real American" that will celebrate his life and career.

Regarding the Netflix documentary, Brooke revealed that she was asked to participate in the project but chose not to respond to those requests. Her decision was based on patterns she has observed throughout her life and skepticism about how family narratives are portrayed. "I was asked, I just didn't answer. If you know the patterns of people that you've known since you were born, I knew this was going to be something that everybody jumped on," she said.

She elaborated on her concerns about motivations and authenticity, suggesting that the documentary might become an opportunity for others to seek attention or financial benefit. "Another chance to be on TV, another chance to be in the pocket. It's crazy how quick people can switch up. One day you're making a video talking about how terrible this person is, and the next, 'I love him so much,'" she said. Brooke expressed confidence that audiences would be able to discern between genuine contributions and those motivated by self-interest. "It'll be interesting to see how everybody else saw it or are saying they saw it. I feel like people aren't stupid and the audience will know who is full of shit and who is being genuine."

She explained that she did not want to be part of a narrative that has changed repeatedly over the years. "I didn't know if people were going to be honest or how it was going to be painted. I didn't want to be part of a narrative that I've seen change 8 million times," Brooke stated.

The documentary is scheduled to premiere on Netflix on April 22, 2026, timing that places it just days after WrestleMania 42 weekend and the WWE Hall of Fame ceremony, where Hogan will receive the Immortal Moment Award for his iconic WrestleMania III match against Andre the Giant. The full episode of the podcast is set to be released on Monday, April 20, with preview clips already circulating online.

Bloodsport XV Event

Bloodsport XV concluded with significant developments for both the event's featured performers and its future as a promotion. The hard-hitting showcase delivered memorable moments and performances that left an impact on wrestling fans.

Nattie Neidhart had a standout night at the event, emerging victorious from a brutal encounter with Shayna Baszler. Following her win, Neidhart shared backstage photos with Steve Austin, who was in attendance for the show. One particular image quickly caught the attention of fans and media alike, capturing both wrestlers raising their middle fingers in the kind of pose that became iconic during Austin's legendary Attitude Era run. The photo showcased Neidhart matching Austin's energy as they struck the signature salute together, giving the moment an unmistakable classic wrestling feel. The appearance of Austin at Bloodsport already generated headlines, but the iconic pose alongside Neidhart elevated the moment further, especially coming on the heels of Neidhart's undefeated performance at the event.

In the main event, Josh Barnett secured a victory over Yuji Nagata. However, Barnett used his post-match platform to make a surprising announcement regarding Bloodsport's immediate future. He declared that Bloodsport XV would be the last installment "for the foreseeable future," effectively putting the event series on hiatus.

Originally launched in 2018 by Matt Riddle, Bloodsport quickly established itself as a unique offering during WrestleMania weekend. The promotion distinguished itself through its shoot-style presentation, eliminating ropes and emphasizing a more realistic, fight-based approach to professional wrestling. When Riddle signed with WWE, Josh Barnett assumed control of the event in 2019 and guided its evolution into a can't-miss attraction for fans seeking something different from traditional wrestling programming. Throughout its run, Bloodsport featured talent from across multiple promotions and combat sports backgrounds, including wrestlers from WWE, AEW, and NJPW, solidifying its reputation as a cross-promotional showcase. Barnett's announcement marks a significant shift for the event, which had become a staple of the WrestleMania weekend calendar for years.

Jon Lajoie Hulk Hogan Interview

Jon Lajoie on Hulk Hogan Slamming Andre the Giant and the Power of Nostalgia

You had to be there. The 80s were a special time. The innocence of the 50s were long gone. The civil unrest of the 60s had just reached a boiling point. The less said about the 70s, the better. For so many people, the 1980s were a decade that defined a generation. The music, the movies, the fashion – it was a decade of taking chances, of making art, of creating memories. And for musician-cum-actor-cum-comedian Jon Lajoie, one of his most important memories happened in the World Wrestling Federation.

The date was March 29, 1987. The location was Pontiac, Michigan, at the Pontiac Silverdome. The event was WrestleMania III and in the world of professional wrestling, this was the event that defined the WWF. Depending on to whom you speak, WrestleMania III took place in front of 85,000 or 93,000, or maybe even 100,000 fans – it doesn't really matter to wrestling fans who, for the most part, came for one reason and one reason only. They wanted to see Hulk Hogan slam Andre the Giant.

Hulk Hogan was the star of the 1980s. Andre the Giant, for all intents and purposes, was the star of the 1970s. They were the two biggest attractions in the WWF, so it only seemed natural that the two would meet in a matchup at the biggest wrestling event of the year. Story-wise, Andre was jealous that Hulk Hogan was more popular than him, and he was mad that Hogan never gave him a championship title match. So instead of asking for a match, Andre demanded one – ripping off Hogan's iconic t-shirt and his cross necklace, Hulk Hogan being a big Jesus fan, don't ya know. The table was set for the "The Irresistible Force vs The Immovable Object."

The matchup itself was nothing special in terms of athleticism. Hulk and Andre had to follow "Macho Man" Randy Savage vs. Ricky "The Dragon" Steamboat, which is still talked about as one of the greatest WrestleMania matches of all time. But Hulk vs Andre was never about athleticism. It was about spectacle. And oh, what a spectacle it was. The majority of the match was plodding, albeit entertaining. The 150,000 people in attendance cheered every punch, every clothesline, every headbutt. But everybody knew what was coming. They waited, with bated breath. There was the shoulder block. There was the Hulking Up. And then. Finally. Hulk Hogan slammed Andre the Giant.

For hundreds of thousands of professional wrestling fans, this was the moment. It was the moment that really, truly made Hulk Hogan a megastar. It was the moment that launched professional wrestling into the pop culture pantheon. And, for many fans, it was the first moment in pro wrestling that actually made them feel. Jon Lajoie was one of those fans.

Lajoie, known for his role as "Nacho" in the FX television series The League, is also an incredible musician. At first, he performed various comedic rap and hip hop songs, several of which went viral before "going viral" was even a thing; but eventually, he ventured into more serious music under the moniker of Wolfie's Just Fine, a reference to T2: Judgment Day. Wolfie's Just Fine has released three albums, all of which feature songs that refer to and revel in various pop culture figures and moments.

There's A New Beginning, which details Lajoie's first time watching Friday the 13th Part 5: A New Beginning. There's Pigeon Lady which, as the title suggests, refers to the Pigeon Lady in Home Alone 2: Lost in New York. And then, there's his most recent wrestling-related song: Hulk Hogan Slammed Andre the Giant. It's a song that, like A New Beginning, encapsulates a time for Lajoie and people his age when they were children, just on the verge of adolescence.

While A New Beginning is about growing up – it's about death and sex and fear of death and sex – Hulk Hogan Slammed Andre the Giant is about holding onto childhood as long as we can and, as adults, remembering those moments and remembering how we felt when those moments happened. Both songs are nostalgic and bittersweet, and they cause the listener to feel the weight of time passing and the power of memory to transport us back to simpler moments.

Wrestling Headlines/LOP Hall of Fame 2026: Midnight Express

Southeastern Championship Wrestling might not be a place many people think would be the birthplace of one of the greatest tag teams in wrestling history, but in 1980, three wrestlers came together to form a faction that would dominate the wrestling world for the next decade. Tag Team Dennis Condrey and Randy Rose would feud with the team of "The Shadow" (Norvell Austin) and Brad Armstrong. After losing the territories tag team titles to Condrey and Rose, "The Shadow" turned on Armstrong, joining the newly crowned champions and starting a faction known as the Midnight Express. Using the "Freebird Rule," Norvell Austin, Condrey, and Rose would defend that title. They legitimately loved dressing in black, driving fast black cars, and staying out all night long, and as they toured the territories, they won a lot of belts and earned a lot of money.

But by 1983, it seemed the faction's run was at its end. Each wrestler went their separate way, and eventually "Loverboy" Dennis Condrey would go to Mid-South Wrestling with Bill Watts. Not one to let a good gimmick expire, though, Watts asked Condrey to continue the Midnight Express, this time teaming him with "Beautiful" Bobby Eaton, and adding manager Jim Cornette. History was about to change forever.

From 1983 to 1987, the Midnight Express consisted of Eaton and Condrey, and managed by Jim Cornette. This was the peak Midnight Express. They quickly went into their legendary feud with the Rock N Roll Express, then followed up by going to World Class Championship Wrestling to feud with the Fantastics, and finally in 1985 they went to Jim Crockett Promotions, where they settled the rest of the decade. The Midnight Express were put back into their feud with the Rock N Roll Express, selling out house shows across the south. This feud helped boost them as the top heel tag team in the territory. This is why at Starrcade 1986, the Midnight Express were facing the Road Warriors in a scaffold match. This match would be legendary when Jim Cornette fell off the scaffold and busted his knees.

In March 1987, Dennis Condrey would leave JCP suddenly, leaving Bobby Eaton without a partner. Wanting to keep the Midnight Express momentum, new partners were suggested. Among the names, Tom Prichard was suggested, remember that name. Eventually Stan Lane, formerly of the Fabulous Ones was chosen, and he'd adopt the name "Sweet" Stan Lane, as well as talk in a radio voice. Lane replacing Condrey changed the team and created a different dynamic which the audience could connect to.

While Bobby Eaton was popular with the audience, Condrey usually attracted the heat. Eaton was a quiet guy, so he needed a mouthpiece like Cornette to get the audience to get the reaction that was desired. But with Lane, you now had a person who could play off Cornette on the mic, someone who was seen as cool by the audience. One year into their run, they were not only a top tag team, they were battling the top acts in JCP. Placed as tweeners, the Midnight Express were matched against the Road Warriors, the Steiners, and the Four Horsemen. By the end of 1987, the Midnight Express were now full blown faces, marked by when Randy Rose and Dennis Condrey did a run in to force the Midnight Express to confront the Original Midnight Express.

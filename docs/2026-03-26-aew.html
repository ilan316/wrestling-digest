🔥 AEW MJF Criticism of Indie Wrestlers

TL;DR: AEW World Champion MJF criticized independent wrestlers for lacking work ethic, saying many are lazy and don't deserve major promotions without putting in the necessary grind.

AEW World Champion MJF recently spoke with The Takedown on Sports Illustrated about the current state of independent wrestling, and he wasn't mincing words about what he sees as a troubling trend among up-and-coming talent.

MJF opened with a blunt assessment of the indie scene. "I think a lot of these guys suck," he said, immediately qualifying his statement by acknowledging that the wrestlers he's faced during recent independent appearances—such as those at House of Glory and Limitless Wrestling—are the exceptions. According to MJF, these hardworking performers are "making the towns and doing everything that they need to do to become better talents." The distinction he's drawing is clear: some indie wrestlers are pulling their weight, while many others simply aren't.

The heart of MJF's criticism centers on what he perceives as a widespread lack of dedication among today's younger wrestlers. "I think a lot of these new guys are lazy, they're not getting in the car, they're not traveling to do ring crew," he stated. To illustrate his point, MJF drew from his own experience grinding through the independent circuit, contrasting his work ethic with that of modern talent.

"Brother, when I was on the independents, I would get in my car, and I would drive to, name a state in North America, I drove there. Name a place in Canada, I drove there. Name a place in Mexico, I drove there," MJF explained. "And I did it for $20 and a can of Coke and a hot dog and a handshake. I did it because I wanted to get better, and I needed reps." This recounting of his early career emphasizes the sacrifices he made—minimal compensation, constant travel, and relentless pursuit of improvement through repetition and experience.

MJF also identified a problematic mindset that has apparently taken hold among some indie wrestlers. With multiple major promotions now visible and accessible—AEW, WWE, TNA, and MLW among them—some rising talent seem to believe they should be signed without having earned their stripes. MJF's response to this expectation is characteristically unforgiving.

"A lot of these guys that are coming into the industry, they're thinking, 'Oh, there's AEW, there's WWE, there's TNA, there's MLW, why aren't I signed yet?' You're not signed yet because you f---ing suck. Get better. Apply yourself. Go to training three to four times a week. Find a ring. Roll around. Make yourself better. Do tape study. Become a student of the sport."

Rather than simply attacking the state of indie wrestling, MJF offered concrete advice on how wrestlers can improve their standing. He emphasized the importance of consistent training, deliberate practice, self-education through watching and analyzing matches, and a fundamental commitment to mastering the craft. His message is that no shortcut exists—the path to professional success requires deliberate effort and sacrifice.

MJF then made a specific pitch about AEW's standards and culture. He made clear that the promotion isn't a haven for those seeking easy rides or expecting opportunities without corresponding effort. "Stop resting on your laurels, just assuming you're gonna be handed something," he said. "If you want to be a part of AEW, you better be ready. You better be ready to work, and work hard, and want it. We're not the lazy promotion. Now if you wanna f--k around and j--k off, go somewhere else."

His final message to independent wrestlers was direct and unambiguous: "Nothing's going to be handed to you. If you want to come to AEW, be ready to work."

MJF's recent experiences on the independent circuit—including his work with promotions like House of Glory and Limitless Wrestling against opponents such as Zilla Fatu and Alec Price—have apparently given him a clear window into the current talent pool. Additionally, his significant run with Mistico across AEW and CMLL, which included winning the CMLL World Light Heavyweight Title against Averno, has kept him connected to the broader wrestling landscape. With his upcoming appearance at 1 Fall Wrestling's The Devil Returns To Georgia event, MJF continues to remain active in multiple wrestling ecosystems, giving him a vantage point from which to assess the work ethic and dedication of contemporary wrestlers.

The comments reflect a perspective rooted in MJF's own journey—a wrestler who built his reputation through relentless hustle before achieving major platform success. His criticism, while harsh, appears motivated by genuine concern about what he views as a deteriorating standard of commitment in a business that historically demanded total dedication from those seeking to succeed.

🔥 AEW Revolution and Ronda Rousey

TL;DR: Ronda Rousey revealed her surprise AEW Revolution appearance was intentionally a statement against TKO Group, the parent company of both WWE and UFC.

Ronda Rousey has opened up about her surprise appearance at AEW Revolution on March 15, 2026, revealing that the decision carried deeper significance than simply supporting her friend Marina Shafir. In a YouTube vlog documenting her experience, the UFC Hall of Famer and three-time WWE Women's Champion explained that her appearance at the Los Angeles-based pay-per-view was "a cool little bit of a 'f*ck you' to the TKO group," which owns both WWE and UFC.

Rousey appeared at Revolution after Marina Shafir's match against Toni Storm, confronting Storm in the ring before security intervened to separate the two. While her shocking debut received mixed reactions from fans, Tony Khan welcomed her to AEW, noting she is always welcome in his company. However, it was clarified that Rousey is not signed to AEW and that her appearance was a one-off booking to support Shafir and promote her upcoming MMA return fight against Gina Carano, scheduled for May 16 on Netflix.

In her vlog, Rousey praised her best friend Shafir, explaining their shared history: "Marina is my best friend in the whole wide world and we grew up doing judo together as little kids. We both quit judo and started doing MMA and then we both quit MMA and started doing pro wrestling. She's absolutely crushing it and I'm so proud of her. I'm so happy that everybody's finally starting to take notice of how incredibly special she is."

Rousey revealed that AEW went to extraordinary lengths to keep her involvement secret prior to the show. "I was brought into the arena in a very unique way — I thought this was going to be much more chill like whatever kind of day, but they're acting like it's freaking Royal Rumble super secret whatever," she said. "But they had me jump into a wheelchair and throw a tarp over me and wheel me in here."

This was actually Rousey's first full-on AEW pay-per-view appearance, despite previous involvement with Ring of Honor, AEW's sister company. "This is my first, I think, full-on AEW show because I only did Ring of Honor before. I think I've been at the shows, but never really performing them. And Ring of Honor was kind of like a pre-taped, that was a smaller crowd. So I've never been to an AEW pay-per-view, so I don't really know what to expect," she explained.

Rousey found the AEW experience refreshing compared to WWE. "It's a little bit weird, but also familiar, but also it's like AEW, so it's nice. It's a little bit more laid-back," she said. She went on to describe AEW as "a less restrictive, more adult version of WWE, which sounds like a good time. Those are all very good descriptions, I felt."

The most candid moment came when Rousey acknowledged her underlying motivation for the appearance. "And it's kind of a cool little bit of a 'f*ck you' to the TKO group, which is kind of funny because WWE is on Netflix. But I kind of figured it'd be easier to ask for forgiveness instead of permission on this one. Like, I'm promoting your show. It's fine. We didn't advertise it. It's not like we boosted the ratings of it, so it should be fine," she said with apparent confidence.

The appearance appears to reflect tensions between Rousey and TKO Group. Rousey has been open about how negotiations for her UFC comeback fell apart, and her sentiments toward TKO as the ownership entity have been notably less than favorable. Despite the complications—including WWE's partnership with Netflix, which is also streaming her MMA fight—Rousey seemed to believe her surprise AEW appearance fell within acceptable boundaries, particularly given that it wasn't heavily promoted and didn't artificially boost AEW's viewership.

🔥 AEW Unrestricted Podcast Ends

TL;DR: AEW Unrestricted, the promotion's official podcast that ran for six years with 316 episodes, has officially ended with hosts Aubrey Edwards and Will Washington confirming new AEW podcast content is in development.

All Elite Wrestling has ended one of its longest-running content series. AEW Unrestricted, the company's official weekly podcast that launched in 2020, has officially wrapped up following the release of its final episode on Thursday. The show, hosted by Aubrey Edwards and Will Washington, closes the book on a six-year run that produced more than 300 episodes—specifically 316—centered around talent interviews and pay-per-view previews.

Aubrey Edwards, a long-time AEW employee who has worn many hats for the promotion since joining shortly after its formation in 2019, announced the news on Bluesky. In addition to her work as a referee, Edwards has also been the one constant presence hosting the podcast, which previously featured Tony Schiavone as co-host before Washington took over that role. "Thank you for 316 episodes," Edwards said. "The last AEW Unrestricted is available now on all the podcast apps. I only cried once!"

The final installment, titled "The Final Bell: The Last AEW Unrestricted," saw Edwards and Washington reflect on the podcast's journey and impact. "AEW Unrestricted signs off with hosts Ref Aubrey Edwards and Will Washington reflecting on the moments, memories, and matches that defined the official podcast of All Elite Wrestling," read the description. "They share behind-the-scenes stories from the show's beginnings during the pandemic, how the podcast became a gateway for talent and fans alike, and what it meant to tell those stories week after week. Aubrey and Will shout out the people who made it all happen, including Tony Schiavone and Tony Khan, while revisiting favorite interviews, unforgettable episodes, and the impact Unrestricted had on AEW's growth." The final guest was Zayda Steel.

Importantly, both Edwards and Washington made it clear during the final episode that the decision to end Unrestricted had nothing to do with their status in AEW. Both will remain active in their respective roles within the company, with Edwards continuing her on-screen duties as a referee and Washington working as Wrestling Administration Coordinator. The podcast's 316-episode run largely featured Edwards and her co-hosts interviewing the stars of AEW, while occasionally producing preview shows for AEW pay-per-views with AEW owner Tony Khan.

While Unrestricted is ending, AEW is already planning additional podcast projects. Both Edwards and Washington emphasized that this is not the end of AEW's foray into podcasting, with new concepts in development that align with ideas fans have been requesting for years. Edwards described the upcoming content as "exciting stuff," though neither would elaborate on the specifics of what future AEW podcasting content will entail or where it will land.

Julia Hart on Alexa Bliss Influence

TL;DR: AEW's Julia Hart credits WWE's Alexa Bliss as a significant influence on her career while emphasizing she developed her own identity by transitioning from a cheerleader gimmick to a darker wrestling persona.

Julia Hart has credited WWE star Alexa Bliss as a major influence on her professional wrestling career. During a recent interview with ITR Wrestling, the AEW star discussed how she drew inspiration from Bliss while developing her own unique character and path in the company.

Hart grew up watching Bliss perform in WWE and developed a genuine appreciation for her work. However, Hart was careful to clarify that she didn't attempt to directly replicate Bliss's career trajectory. "I didn't look at her career and think, 'I want to do exactly that.' She definitely was a big influence and I definitely grew up loving her and watching her," Hart explained. "So, I would go back and use her as a reference, but I never directly went like, 'This is what I was going to do.'"

A significant turning point in Hart's AEW career came with her decision to abandon her cheerleader character. When Hart first joined AEW in 2021, she performed as a cheerleader with The Varsity Blondes, drawing from her real-life experience as a high school cheerleader. However, this initial direction proved limiting for her development as a performer. "I wanted to be a cheerleader because I was a cheerleader in real life in high school and I had no other character ideas," Hart said.

The cheerleader gimmick eventually became a constraint on Hart's growth. "I think it just needed to happen because I was so hung up on cheerleading. That's all I knew for six years so I was trying too hard to put a cheerleader in wrestling instead of being a wrestler and just happen to be a cheerleader kind of thing," Hart explained. "So I think this character shift really helped me become my full self as a wrestler and kind of leave cheer behind and really helped me get to the next level."

The transformation proved instrumental to Hart's confidence and professional evolution. "I started to feel more confident because I wasn't trying to be a cheerleader, 'A cheerleader wouldn't that, a cheerleader wouldn't do this.' Okay, but you're not a cheerleader, you're a wrestler. It definitely helped me, I needed that character shift."

Hart's darker character direction emerged following a storyline interaction with Malakai Black in late 2021, which eventually led to her alignment with House of Black alongside Black, Buddy Matthews, and Brody King. Hart actively pursued this opportunity as a means to increase her television time and elevate her status in the company. "We were then put in a program with Malakai (Black) and I was like I want to be on TV more and I wanted to ask like, 'How can I make this a story?' And he was fully on board with me and worked with me and helped me and Brody (King) and Buddy (Matthews) helped as well," she said. "And then Tony (Khan) brought it to life. It was just trying to figure out how can I get out of this loop and get to the next level."

The collaborative effort paid off significantly. Hart's character transformation helped establish her as a more serious competitor in AEW's women's division. She has since achieved notable success, eventually forming the faction Triangle of Madness with Thekla and Skye Blue.

AEW All In Texas - Kyle Fletcher

TL;DR: Kyle Fletcher has opened up about AEW All In: Texas 2025 being one of the worst days of his life after a last-minute plan change left him in emotional turmoil just hours before his match.

Kyle Fletcher, the current AEW TNT Champion, has finally spoken publicly about the traumatic experience surrounding last year's AEW All In: Texas event. In an interview with Soundsphere Magazine, Fletcher reflected candidly on why that day remains one of the most difficult of his life.

Fletcher explained the extensive preparation he had undertaken leading into the event. "It was one of the worst days of my whole life. When you have so much built-up anxiety and you have put that pressure on yourself…You know, I'd been dieting for two, three months leading up to that show. Every single day, I'd been thinking about this moment, this match, all of it," he said, describing the mental and physical toll of his preparation.

The wrestler's carefully laid plans were dramatically disrupted when AEW was forced to pivot its card mere hours before the event. Fletcher had been scheduled to face then-TNT Champion Adam Cole, but Cole suffered a concussion days prior during a match against Fletcher on Collision, forcing him to vacate the title. This created a chaotic situation that left Fletcher scrambling to understand what would happen next.

"And then, the rug gets pulled five hours before. You can't really put into words what that kind of feels like. I was just in this weird limbo. I had no idea what was gonna happen, and I had flown my mum out for that show. It was obviously a huge day for me, and then it just all went to shit," Fletcher expressed, emphasizing both the personal and emotional weight of the situation.

The match was hastily restructured into a multi-man affair, which Dustin Rhodes ultimately won to capture the TNT title. Fletcher acknowledged the difficulty of processing his disappointment while recognizing the broader circumstances.

"Obviously, this is my perspective. It's very hard for me to complain about it, because I don't feel like I have the right to complain about it. But yeah, it was very emotional in a lot of different ways, and not how you want a big day like that to go," Fletcher noted, balancing his personal frustration with understanding of the situation's circumstances.

Despite the setback at All In, Fletcher's perseverance eventually paid dividends. He later captured the TNT Championship from Rhodes in a Chicago Street Fight match on the July 31, 2025, episode of AEW Collision, redeeming himself in the process.

Looking forward, Fletcher expressed his determination to create a more positive experience. "So I think, especially this year, I just want to make sure it's a day to remember and a much more positive one," he concluded, signaling his intent to put the disappointment behind him and move forward with renewed focus.

MJF vs Hangman Aftermath

TL;DR: With Hangman Page defeated and removed from title contention following their Revolution match, MJF's AEW championship is now open to a crowded field of challengers.

---

If you look at any story in any form of media, there are protagonists and antagonists. Their moral compasses and ethical codes might not always be cut and dried, and they may very well operate in the moral grey. Regardless of whether they live in the grey area or the much more pronounced black and white, we identify with the stories of those main characters and what they represent.

Many wrestlers have stepped through the ring ropes to work for All Elite Wrestling. A good number of those you could argue should be in the conversation for the greatest of all time. Some are stalwart fixtures in pro wrestling that lasted decades like Sting or Chris Jericho. Others are known for their exceptional in-ring work, whether that's Kenny Omega, Bryan Danielson, Okada, or anyone else on AEW's roster. Among them, it would be hesitant to call any "main characters" of AEW. They're legends and greatness personified in their own way, but if you consider for a moment what the heartbeat of AEW is at its core—what it's founded upon as an upstart promotion, off a hedged bet—it's on the backs of the passionate, opposite characters that Hangman Page and MJF have grown into since the company's inception in 2019.

**The Philosophical Divide**

Regardless of Revolution's outcome, which thankfully was what it should have been, the intertwined story of Page and MJF is not over. It might not be for the AEW championship for a while, if ever again, but Page excluding himself from the world title picture hardly means they'll never wrestle again. MJF will not always be world champion; not only are there other titles to fight over, but what they represent to each other transcends the need for championships. Their feud isn't just a matter of wins and losses, accolades and championships. It's a philosophical one.

This isn't a matter of MJF and Adam Cole becoming buddies, or Swerve Strickland and Page learning to see eye to eye for a common goal. Fundamentally, Page and MJF can't be allies. They're oil and water. If we accept Page is AEW's hero—the Superman to MJF's Lex Luthor—then Superman is dead, like it's 1993. MJF's post-match Revolution promo said as much.

**The Door Remains Open**

While it might seem far-flung to be thinking potentially a year or more ahead, realistically, if needed, AEW can use Page again and have him hedge his entire career on one more chance. An example would be to do it at Double or Nothing 2027. Whether MJF's reign lasts that long is uncertain; if he is champion around that time next year, he may have already traded the championship back and forth with one of the other contenders in the picture.

It was a smart move, and there's plenty AEW can do with Hangman Page outside the world title picture. However, it would be shocking if he never challenges again. As was pointed out at the press conference, it was only, after all, a verbal agreement.

**Open Season on the Champion**

With MJF appearing on Dynamite for the first time since his defence at Revolution, and with Page out of the way, it's open season on MJF and his championship. The gauntlet of challengers has begun to form, and while most have already declared their intent, some have not and probably won't until summertime.

Although MJF's first reign probably didn't end as strongly as it could have, the first part of that reign was littered with good first-time matchups that pushed him beyond his comfort zone. His current second reign has already rekindled this spirit, succeeding the best parts of his first reign. The Texas Deathmatch with Page set the tone for what to expect going forward—a championship reign defined by challenging competition and elevated stakes.

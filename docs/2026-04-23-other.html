🔥 TNA Steve Maclin Injury Recovery

Steve Maclin is officially cleared to return to wrestling after suffering a frightening in-ring injury at TNA Sacrifice 2026. The incident took place early in the main event World Championship match between Maclin and challenger Mike Santana. What started as a routine exchange quickly turned serious when Santana connected with a superkick that caught Maclin flush, appearing to knock him out within minutes of the match beginning.

Referee Alice Lane immediately recognized something was wrong and threw up the "X" sign, which signals a legitimate injury. Medical staff rushed to aid Maclin, who struggled to stand as officials tended to him. The match was immediately stopped and declared a no-contest out of concern for Maclin's health. To keep the storyline moving, Eddie Edwards ran out and engaged in an impromptu brawl with Santana, giving the show some sense of conclusion while the focus remained on Maclin's wellbeing.

Following the incident, Maclin was taken to a hospital for further evaluation. Initial concerns centered on whether he had suffered a concussion, but fortunately those fears did not materialize. Maclin later detailed exactly what happened during the impact, explaining that a dangerous blow to his neck caused his body to temporarily shut down, even though he remained conscious throughout the ordeal. The situation was serious enough that weeks of uncertainty followed regarding when and if he would be able to return to competition.

TNA President Carlos Silva confirmed that Maclin passed all medical testing and did not suffer any major long-term injury. The road to recovery began shortly after, with Maclin resuming training in early April 2026, a sign that his recuperation was progressing well. On April 22, 2026, Maclin made it official by announcing his medical clearance through social media.

"AIN'T FOUND A WAY TO KILL ME YET. Cleared & ready to get back to work," Maclin wrote on X, along with his booking email. His message demonstrated a defiant attitude and eagerness to return to action without hesitation, showing that the close call had not diminished his determination to compete.

The timing of Maclin's clearance opens interesting possibilities for TNA's storyline direction. Since the incident at Sacrifice, Mike Santana has remained actively involved in the company and has continued a feud with Eddie Edwards, picking up a victory at TNA Rebellion 2026. Since Maclin never received a proper finish in his World Championship match against Santana, there is speculation that the rivalry between the two competitors may not be finished. However, no official date has yet been announced for Maclin's in-ring return.

Alberto Del Rio LM52 Status

LM52, a new Mexican lucha libre promotion, has taken decisive action to clarify its relationship with Alberto Del Rio in the wake of his domestic violence arrest. The promotion issued an official statement emphasizing that Del Rio is not part of the corporate or shareholder structure of the company and will have no involvement with LM52 going forward.

Del Rio was arrested on March 6 for domestic violence in the State of San Luis Potosí, Mexico, after being caught in the act of verbally and physically assaulting his wife. He was initially held at La Pila prison and faced potentially several years in prison until reaching a financial settlement with his wife that led to his conditional release.

The clarification became necessary because LM52's debut press conference, which occurred prior to Del Rio's arrest, heavily featured El Patron front-and-center. The promotion's website tagline stated "This time he's going it alone," which created the widespread perception that Del Rio was a founder and owner of the new promotion, particularly after he had been ousted by AAA, the last major wrestling promotion that regularly worked with him.

LM52's official statement reads: "LM52 considers it pertinent to clarify that Alberto del Rio is not part of the corporate or shareholder structure of the project and will not have any involvement in LM52 going forward. We know that, due to its high public profile in the initial stages of the launch, a broad association of its image with the league may have developed. However, LM52 will continue under its current structure."

The statement concludes by affirming the company's values: "LM52 rejects any form of violence and any conduct contrary to the principles of respect, integrity and professionalism that govern the project."

The clarification was first issued two days after Del Rio's arrest and was reiterated at LM52's press conference held on April 22nd as the company finalized preparations for its debut show.

LM52 has restructured its promotional vision to position Dr. Wagner Jr and Pirata Morgan as the main stars of the company following the announcement that Del Rio would not be part of the roster. The promotion has secured a significant broadcast deal with Warner Bros, with the show available live on Space and Discovery channels as well as on the HBO Max platform, which has exclusive rights in Mexico.

The debut show will take place on May 7th at the Foro Interlomas in Huixquilucan, Estado de Mexico, and will feature seven matches. The card includes a four-way match featuring Big Mami versus Diosa Quetzal versus Candy Swing versus Valery Moreno, a tag team match between Bendito and Wisin el Perro against the Mexa Boys, a singles match between Mamba and Súper Nova, a trios match featuring Legado Wagner against Emperador Azteca and his partners, an Australian trios match between Burgey Boy's team and Puerkiza Extrema, a special international singles match between Demonio Infernal and Jack Morris, and a legends tag team match featuring Rey Wagner and Texano Jr against Cibernético and Pirata Morgan. The card remains subject to change.

Ryback Mother Passing Tribute

Ryback Mourns the Loss of His Mother Following Cancer Battle

Former WWE star Ryback is grieving the loss of his mother after she passed away following a battle with cancer. The wrestler, whose real name is Ryan Reeves, took to social media to share the news publicly, posting a lengthy and deeply personal tribute about the final five months he spent caring for her as the disease progressed.

In his emotional message, Ryback reflected on how his understanding of cancer fundamentally changed after witnessing its effects firsthand. He described the illness in stark terms, emphasizing just how devastating the experience was for him and his family.

"What I witnessed over the last five months taking care of my mother is something I will never forget. It is vicious. It is ugly. It destroys you from the inside out," Ryback wrote, capturing the physical and emotional toll that cancer took on his mother and on him as her caregiver.

Ryback shared a particularly touching memory from his mother's final days that he said perfectly captured her spirit and personality. After spending hours without speaking, he told her that it was okay to let go and that the pain could finally end. His mother's response was both unexpected and deeply moving.

"She got this sudden burst of strength, opened her eyes, and said, 'I'm sleeping.' Me and my sister laughed, because that was so her," Ryback recalled, finding comfort in this moment of humor and connection during such a dark time.

The final weeks were especially difficult for Ryback as he watched his mother's health decline day by day. However, he found some peace in knowing that her suffering had finally ended. Rather than being consumed by sadness, he has chosen to focus on gratitude for the relief her passing brought.

"When I feel anxiety, sadness, or that emptiness from her not being here, I just say thank you. I'm thankful her suffering is over. The final weeks were incredibly hard," he expressed.

Throughout his tribute, Ryback highlighted the remarkable strength and resilience his mother showed even as her body was failing her. Despite her declining condition, she refused to let cancer completely steal her ability to enjoy life's simple pleasures.

"She still went outside and sat in the sun every day. She still found the strength to go on nightly walks, even with her body failing her. Those are moments I will cherish forever," Ryback said, celebrating the small victories and daily acts of courage his mother demonstrated.

In processing his grief, Ryback has found solace in a spiritual perspective on his mother's passing. He expressed a belief that her presence remains with him in a different form.

"I believe energy never dies. It just changes form. And I believe she's still with me, just in a different way now. That gives me peace," he wrote.

Ryback credited his mother with passing on her resilience and strength to him, qualities he said he will carry forward throughout his own life. He emphasized that her impact on him extends far beyond her physical presence.

To honor her memory and continue processing his loss, Ryback announced his intention to share more stories and memories about his mother moving forward.

"I'm going to continue sharing things about my mom, because it makes me feel good and keeps her with me. I love you, Mom. Always," he concluded his tribute, making clear that while his mother's physical battle with cancer has ended, her influence and memory will remain an integral part of his life.

The loss marks a deeply personal moment for Ryback, who opened up publicly about the emotional and physical toll of caring for his mother through her final months. His willingness to share such intimate details of his grief has resonated with many who have experienced similar losses.

Victoria Crawford on TNA vs WWE

Victoria Crawford, formerly known as Alicia Fox, has opened up about wrestling in TNA compared to WWE. Crawford was with WWE from 2006 all the way up until May 2023, though her last match for the company prior to that was January 2022 in the Royal Rumble. She has since signed with TNA Wrestling and actually wrestled on NXT in October 2025 via the companies' partnership.

In a new interview, Crawford spoke about how she wants to wrestle more frequently to get to the same point in the ring she was at when she was on the road regularly in WWE. She explained that she still needs to get warmed up again after her time away from full-time competition.

Crawford reflected on her WWE experience, noting the significant volume of matches that wrestlers performed. She said: "Back at WWE we had 280 shows a year, so we were always rolling around with each other. Nattie and I could probably have a match now and not even talk, not even look at each other, and it'd be a total banger match."

The veteran wrestler emphasized the importance of developing consistent in-ring chemistry with her opponents. She continued: "That's what I wanna build at TNA – a consistent arsenal of things that work with each of the girls. Because then it becomes like origami. Sometimes you get two minutes and you've gotta cut it short, but you know how to do the business."

Crawford highlighted how this familiarity also serves a safety function in the ring. She noted: "Even when someone gets hurt, you know how to get safe quickly. So I wanna try and bring that psychology to TNA. The girls are all trained well and they can protect themselves and stuff, but when they work with me, I'd love to just keep a flowing arsenal with all the girls and see where it takes us."

Regarding her overall goals in TNA, Crawford expressed a desire to see her character develop more through in-ring performances. She stated: "I do wanna see my character do more in-ring stuff, I'm still trying to figure out where my feet are there. And just honestly speaking because I need to get warmed up again."

The topic of match frequency in professional wrestling has become increasingly relevant in recent years. WWE has had significantly fewer live events under TKO ownership than in previous years, which presents both advantages and disadvantages for talent development. While wrestlers benefit from more time at home with less risk of injury, there are drawbacks to having fewer opportunities for in-ring work.

Fewer matches can negatively impact a wrestler's ability to hone their craft and improve performance in front of diverse audiences. Additionally, regular in-ring work is crucial for developing the safety awareness and muscle memory that prevents injuries during matches. This issue has even affected WWE's developmental brand, as Shawn Michaels recently informed Triple H and Nick Khan that one of the reasons NXT was developing talent slower than in the past was because there were no NXT live events. This led to the company running NXT live events more regularly in recent times to address the problem.

Crawford's comments underscore the value that veteran wrestlers place on consistent match schedules and the psychological aspects of professional wrestling that can only be developed through regular in-ring work with diverse opponents.

Ric Flair Road Stories

Ric Flair is telling one of the wildest road stories yet from wrestling's party-heavy era, claiming a night involving Curt Hennig ended with him waking up in Baltimore missing multiple Rolex watches.

During an appearance on The Ariel Helwani Show, Flair went deep into stories from the 1980s and 1990s, describing just how out of control life on the road could get. He compared the fame and chaos of that era to rock stars at their peak. While reflecting on those days, Flair explained just how big the Four Horsemen lifestyle had become at the time.

"I didn't go to bed in the 80s. I'm not sure I've slept yet. Brother, we were the Beatles. Man, Four Horsemen coming in town, ladies and gentlemen, the Marriott Hotel Tonight, 18 to 28, no boyfriends, no husbands. LFG," Flair said.

Flair then admitted that nights out with The Undertaker often led to expensive consequences, revealing that he lost several luxury watches during those outings. "I've lost four Rolexes hanging out with The Undertaker, that son of a b––," he stated.

But the wildest story came when Flair claimed Curt Hennig once drugged him during a night out — a situation he says ended with him waking up in Baltimore missing three Rolex watches and trying to piece together what happened.

"Because I used to get drunk with him and then Curt Hennig had roofied me. I woke up one night in Baltimore with two girls. My wallet was gone, my rental was gone, so I'm married, right. Then I go, 'Okay, girls, what the f––? Where's my s––?' They said, 'You threw your three Rolexes into a bowl of spaghetti last night at Sabatino's.' I had 13 of them. Well, what am I gonna do? Call the cops? I'm married," Flair recounted.

Host Ariel Helwani pressed Flair further on the claim that Hennig drugged him, leading Flair to double down and say a similar incident happened during the infamous Plane Ride From Hell in 2002.

"Yeah, he roofied me in the plane ride from hell too, brother," Flair said.

Flair even joked that he'd be willing to share more details about the situation but only if the price was right. "Hey and Ariel, if you can find enough money, I'll tell you the whole story," he offered.

Flair also referenced the legal fallout from that infamous flight, which later became the subject of lawsuits and documentaries examining the alleged behavior that took place onboard. The Plane Ride From Hell happened on May 5, 2002, during a WWE-chartered flight returning from a European tour. The trip became one of the most controversial moments in wrestling history, involving multiple incidents among talent and crew, including a backstage fight between Curt Hennig and Brock Lesnar that nearly ended in disaster mid-flight.

Curt Hennig, best known to fans as Mr. Perfect, passed away on February 10, 2003, at the age of 44. He was released from WWE following the fallout from the Plane Ride From Hell incident.

Flair's claims add to the long list of stories tied to wrestling's wild road era, especially involving high-profile names and infamous moments that fans still talk about decades later.

Berwyn Eagles Club Wrestling Venue Tribute

Berwyn Eagles Club: What It All Meant in the End

Across the street from a Mexican restaurant and an ice cream parlor, next door to a car wash, sits a simple place. Nothing crazy about it that screams for more attention than most. A small one with a pronounced sign outside. Much of it is hugged by verdant greenery en route to a dark wooden door; the rectangular pavement in front of it is cracked. Once you glance at the blue sign, "Berwyn Eagles" with the titular bird breaching containment atop it, you know you're there at Chicago's local independent wrestling haven, Berwyn Eagles Club.

Immediately, the clammy stench of history envelopes you. The arena is compact; the Club's breadth is intimate, the type of place where people are packed like sardines, within intimate proximity to each other. Hardwood flooring and walls house chandeliers, embodying an atmosphere of Reagan-era structure, a time capsule of the 1980s. As you walk through the halls, 8x10 posters invite you further, making it clear that you're going to be in the midst of an art unrestrained by the global and corporately controlled capitalism outside. These posters continue into the side and main bar. The world is on fire, but inside, there's the comfort of great vibes and storytelling in the guise of sport.

This main bar is the entrance where fiction struts through the reality that cheers and jeers it. It has two doors, but only one is used for the performer's entrances. Fans can be seen dipping in and out of the bar or visiting their ATM, hopefully having enough in their account to withdraw. Wrestlers hang out backstage, sharing drinks and laughs at the bar. Every so often, if your eye strays enough from the bombastic theater unfurling before you, it might catch sight of wrestlers high and low on the card watching from the entrance and backstage. The smell of alcohol and paraphernalia permeates the air. A scent of perspiration soon wafts through. One does not go to independent wrestling shows to be in the presence of aesthetics and sweet aromas. No, this was a place where you forget your troubles and get in the pit of sweat and rage and the masquerade of sport, where heartache, comedy, hopes, and dreams come to a modest square.

The Berwyn Eagles Club has hosted wrestling for over 20 years. In the American Midwest, it was the place to be. A place where, if wrestlers were positioned to be a big player in the independent wrestling scene, they went here. Seth Rollins, Kevin Owens, Bryan Danielson, Beth Phoenix, Sami Zayn, Claudio Castagnoli, Becky Lynch, Nattie Neidhart, Athena, and Asuka are just a few of the major names who performed at the venue before achieving worldwide prominence.

For nostalgic CM Punk fans, this is where he made his first wrestling appearance since winning the WWE Championship from John Cena at Money in the Bank in 2011. Yes, where he praised the courage of Gregory Iron, a wrestler living with Cerebral Palsy. It was there, in Berwyn, at AAW's "Scars and Stripes" event. During Cody Rhodes, Drew McIntyre, and Matt Cardona's indie reinvention runs, they too circled through its wooden palace. From residential lucha promotions to Wrestle League and Squared Circle Superstars, attendees could expect all the charm of indie wrestling. Yet, chief among these were AAW and the all-female SHIMMER wrestling promotion.

That's the impact of the Berwyn Eagles Club. It doesn't have a history solely in independent wrestling, but in the overall industry as a whole. Whether it was the loyal talents or the ones who compete under blinding lights, wrestling came through here. Just as Reseda was to PWG, Berwyn Eagles Club served as an essential venue in the wrestling landscape. Since the Club is set to lose a main source of income, a GoFundMe campaign has been established to help venue owner Chuck Marose Sr. in the aftermath of this financial hardship, ensuring this historic wrestling institution can continue to provide a home for independent wrestling in Chicago.

Lucha Libre Mexican Wrestling Culture

Lucha Libre: A Culture Beyond A Sport

Since 1933, Mexico has had the privilege of being the home country of what is currently the oldest wrestling company in the world: El Consejo Mundial de Lucha Libre (CMLL), guided by the vision of the man known as "El Padre de la Lucha Libre" (The Father of Lucha Libre), Salvador Lutteroth, whose inspiration and memories are still remembered every year in La Catedral de la Lucha Libre in Arena Mexico with the event Homenaje a Dos Leyendas. Then, in 1992, during one of the biggest booms that the sport has ever seen in the country, former CMLL writer and booker Antonio Peña founded what would become known as Lucha Libre AAA WorldWide with his own vision, giving an alternative promotion for Mexico and bringing some talents with him on a road to create a new legacy. While this was disliked by many individuals who felt it was a betrayal, the reality was different—it was an opportunity to share a beloved sport with even more people around the country.

Both companies entered into a rivalry that created a gigantic clash between the two promotions. They brought some of the most iconic luchadores to shows to see which promotion could present the biggest spectacles, and both companies worked evenly, featuring talents like Mil Mascaras, El Canek, El Hijo del Santo, Eddie Guerrero, Blue Demon, and Blue Demon Jr. But the rivalry also created new icons and characters that people could relate to in the form of La Parka (also known as L.A Park), Rey Mysterio Jr, Psicosis, Atlantis, Ultimo Guerrero, El Felino, Negro Casas, and many more. Lucha Libre was here to stay, not just as an occasional sport, but as a way of living and an escape from reality that would be embraced in several environments and become part of Mexico's culture.

Jackie Joe, a luchador from Sonora, was the first Mexican to wrestle in the United States in 1933, but it was not until 1972 when Mil Mascaras made the first jump to a major company when he wrestled for WWE (then WWWF) at Madison Square Garden. Mil Mascaras went on to challenge for the WWWF World Championship, Intercontinental Championship, and the WWF Tag Team Championship alongside SD Jones. Thanks to the creation of AAA, the idea of being conservative and traditional with Lucha Libre started becoming an old idea as Antonio Peña saw some of his contracted luchadores make the jump to American soil, regardless of whether he agreed with their decision or not. He was recognized as one of the principal people that brought Lucha Libre to the United States.

Some luchadores worked at the 1997 Royal Rumble with Mil Mascaras himself, Cibernetico, Latin Lover, and Pierroth Jr as participants, then worked for several promotions, but most remarkably in Extreme Championship Wrestling (ECW) and World Championship Wrestling (WCW). These appearances delivered infamous moments such as the Mexican Death Match between Tajiri and Super Crazy, La Parka running around with his chair and being nicknamed "The Chairman," and Rey Mysterio's legendary battles against Eddie Guerrero and Psicosis. All of this were the first steps for American fans to explore a new style of wrestling, a style that would become more noticeable in WCW's Cruiserweight Division and in matches featuring luchadores who were part of the original Latino World Order (LWO). All these main elements and legacy would allow for several eras of luchadores to participate in companies such as TNA, WWE, and most recently, AEW.

In wrestling, a gimmick is usually the character that identifies a career for maybe a short period or the rest of a career, but in Lucha Libre, it determines your life forever if you decide to pursue the dream of entering the four corners of survival. The most important figures of the industry that have left this world adopted a life tied with the mask, going beyond the sport and becoming icons, one with the name. El Santo and Blue Demon were buried with their masks, cementing the eternal bond between luchador and identity that transcends the wrestling ring itself.

Doctor’s Stoppage Forces Sudden End to AEW Dynamite Match

AEW Dynamite on April 22 delivered a hard-hitting contest that took an unexpected turn. A highly competitive bout between Will Ospreay and Mark Davis was brought to a sudden halt, not by a pinfall, but by medical intervention that shifted the outcome in an instant. What began as a fast-paced clash quickly escalated into something far more serious as momentum swung wildly with both men trading heavy offense inside and outside the ring.

The match reached a critical point when Davis connected with a brutal piledriver on the apron, leaving Ospreay in visible distress. Referee Paul Turner immediately called for ringside physician Dr. Michael Sampson to evaluate the situation. The referee signaled that this wasn't part of the usual near-fall drama that fans are accustomed to counting along with.

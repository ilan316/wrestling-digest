🔥 AAA General Manager Announcement

Lucha Libre AAA is preparing for a major leadership shake-up with the announcement of a brand new General Manager. The revelation of who will hold this position is scheduled for Saturday, May 23, 2026, during an AAA event in Mexico City, Mexico.

The storyline began on the April 18, 2026, episode of AAA on FOX when Dorian Roldán, son of AAA owner Marisela Peña Roldán, approached his mother with a proposal. He asked to be officially named as the new General Manager of AAA. However, Marisela stated that she needed time to think about the significant decision before committing to naming her son to the role.

One week later, on the April 25 episode of AAA on FOX, Marisela provided an update that changed the direction of the storyline entirely. Rather than immediately naming Dorian to the position, she announced that she would be conducting a search for the right person to assume the General Manager role over the coming weeks. This decision opened the door for other potential candidates to be considered for the powerful position.

During her announcement, Marisela made a bold statement about the impact the new General Manager would have on the company. She declared that nothing in AAA will remain the same once the new General Manager is unveiled, teasing major changes and a new direction for the promotion moving forward. She also acknowledged the significance of the moment, referencing that it had been exactly a year since AAA made history at WrestleMania, and that this new General Manager announcement would mark another historic moment for the company.

Marisela further explained her vision, stating: "For the next few weeks, I will be looking for the best candidate to assume this super important and powerful role, and take lucha libre to heights never seen before." She thanked the fanbase for their support and urged them to prepare themselves for the changes coming to AAA with the new General Manager.

This move brings AAA in line with WWE's organizational structure, which already features General Managers across multiple brands. Currently, Adam Pearce serves as the General Manager of WWE Monday Night RAW, Nick Aldis holds the position on Friday Night SmackDown, Robert Stone is the General Manager of NXT, and Timothy Thatcher serves as the General Manager of EVOLVE.

Speculation about who might assume the General Manager role has already begun among fans and analysts. Dorian Roldán remains a logical candidate given his direct pitch to his mother and his stated goal of improving the positions of AAA wrestlers like El Hijo Del Vikingo and Omos. However, other names have been floated as potential candidates for the role.

Rey Mysterio, the WWE and AAA Hall of Famer, has been mentioned as a possible choice. However, he has been actively working on commentary alongside Corey Graves and JBL in recent months, so accepting the General Manager position might require him to step away from that role. Another name that has been discussed is Chavo Guerrero, who was reportedly back in the WWE and AAA fold at one point but has not appeared in recent programming. The General Manager role could potentially mark his return to a visible position in the company.

As the May 23 reveal date approaches, anticipation continues to build about who will ultimately take control of AAA and what transformative changes they will bring to the promotion once they step into power.

Richard Holliday NXT MLW

Richard Holliday has emerged as one of the most compelling figures in professional wrestling today, commanding attention from major promotions while maintaining a strong independent presence. His journey represents a unique intersection of athletic excellence, personal resilience, and savvy self-promotion that has made him arguably the most marketable performer in the current landscape.

Holliday first gained widespread recognition through his work in Major League Wrestling, where he became a cornerstone of the promotion's roster. He served as a founding member of The Dynasty alongside MJF, developing a persona centered on arrogance and calculated precision. That character work, paired with a fundamentally sound wrestling style, helped him stand out in a crowded independent scene. However, when discussing The Dynasty, Holliday doesn't seem to romanticize the experience. "The Dynasty was cool for what it was, but really didn't last that long," he said. MJF would go on to leave MLW for the bright lights and television, creating an unfinished narrative that still lingers with fans who view it as a case of Max leaving behind friends and partners for the big time.

Since their split, the two have taken very different paths. While MJF became a centerpiece on a national stage, Holliday doubled down on building his own name and brand. When asked if he reflects on how things have played out, Holliday made it clear where his focus lies: "No... I'm far too focused on my career as the current top independent wrestler in the world today." The difference in mindset is vast—one is traveling the world, proving his worth against top talent every single week, often multiple nights a week, while the other seems to wrestle for a fat paycheck.

The idea of Holliday and MJF eventually meeting in a high-profile singles match refuses to die among fans, and Holliday isn't shutting the door on it either. "In today's world, it seems like any match can happen. If a promoter wants to step up and book it, then so be it. Tony Khan can book it. An independent can book it. Or fans can continue to fantasy book it. Or it can just never happen—I am fine with any of that." Holliday's response creates a gray area where momentum builds naturally rather than through explicit callouts.

However, Holliday's journey hasn't been defined solely by unfinished business in the ring. Before any hypothetical career-defining payday or long-awaited showdown could materialize, real life forced him into a far more serious fight. In 2022, Holliday was diagnosed with Hodgkin lymphoma. He stepped away from competition to undergo treatment, documenting parts of his journey publicly. His openness resonated beyond wrestling, drawing support from fans and peers across multiple promotions. His determination to overcome the disease was, and still is, inspiring to those who followed his story.

Holliday returned to the ring in 2023, marking a significant personal and professional milestone. Rather than easing back into competition, he quickly reestablished himself as a featured performer on the independent circuit. Promotions across the United States have since utilized Holliday in prominent roles, often highlighting both his technical skill and the real-life perseverance that now defines his story. His return has been nothing short of remarkable, with recognition coming from top figures in the industry.

Fast forward to today, and Holliday's name sits at the top of the independent circuit mountain. He has earned recognition on major podcasts, including Cody Rhodes' show, where industry figures have immediately named him among the top talents on the independent scene. When asked about top independent wrestlers, industry insiders like Matt Cardona have immediately cited Richard Holliday alongside other elite performers.

Beyond his in-ring work, Holliday has demonstrated an understanding of how to leverage social media and public moments to build his brand. A recent incident exemplifies this perfectly. Holliday had a face-to-face moment with an online critic known as Bad Ass Construction Worker after weeks of trash talk on social media. The confrontation took place at a wrestling event and ended up documented on Instagram. Bad Ass Construction Worker had been posting negative commentary about the wrestling scene, calling performers losers and directing specific insults at Holliday, referring to him as a dork and dismissing his mustache.

When Holliday spotted the critic filming at the event, he approached directly to address the comments. "Hey, dude, you're the guy. You're the guy. You. You go on the Internet, you call wrestlers losers. You've even called me one. No. Yeah. Remember you said Richard Holliday — dork. Remember you said the mustache guy you don't want me to collaborate with? Bro, I'm here right now, man. I'm right here. Come on. You call me a dork now? Dude?" The moment looked like it was about to turn physical when Holliday teased throwing a low blow, stopping just short before completely changing the tone of the interaction.

What happened next revealed Holliday's understanding of entertainment and human psychology. "Bro, I'm just kidding, dude. I like your content, man. I respect it. Seriously, I like it. Keep doing it, but just so you know I can strike on your bearded ass whenever I want. But keep making the content, bro. It's good stuff, man. Thanks, dude." The exchange shifted from confrontation to humor, with Holliday making it clear he was aware of the online insults but wasn't actually looking to escalate the situation. Instead, he turned a potential negative interaction into something that generated interest and demonstrated his ability to work with personalities outside traditional wrestling.

This moment encapsulates what makes Holliday such a marketable commodity in today's wrestling landscape. He understands that online trash talk can spill into real-life moments at wrestling events, and he knows how to navigate those moments in ways that build his brand rather than damage it. The clip, posted by Bad Ass Construction Worker himself, became another piece of content that showcased Holliday's charisma and awareness.

As NXT, TNA, and other major promotions express interest in acquiring Holliday's services, his value continues to rise. He represents the complete package: a technically sound wrestler with a compelling personal story of perseverance, someone who understands modern promotion through social media, and a performer capable of drawing attention through both in-ring work and out-of-ring content creation. Whether he eventually signs with a major promotion, continues to dominate the independent scene, or pursues a high-profile match against MJF, Richard Holliday has positioned himself as one of wrestling's most essential performers in the current era.

Lewis had built a fearsome reputation as the most real wrestler in America, a credential that carried immense weight during a time when the line between legitimate wrestling and professional entertainment was still being defined.

The anticipation surrounding a championship match between these two titans was substantial. When Burns and Lewis finally met for the American heavyweight championship, industry observers expected the contest to usher in a significant new era in professional wrestling history. The match promised to be a defining moment that would establish which wrestler would lead the sport into the next chapter of its evolution.

However, the outcome defied expectations in a way that nobody had anticipated. The encounter between Burns and Lewis did indeed change professional wrestling forever, but not in the manner that was expected. The match had profound consequences, most notably for Lewis himself. His reputation as the most real wrestler in America, a distinction he had carefully cultivated and maintained, took a considerable tumble as a result of his performance and the match's outcome. This defeat marked a turning point not only in Lewis' career but in the broader trajectory of professional wrestling as an industry.

Wrestling Observer Podcast

On the latest edition of Saturday Wrestling Observer Live, host Jim Valley addressed an interesting debate surrounding the terminology used to describe modern wrestling matches. A viewer had commented on the Cody Rhodes versus Randy Orton main event from WrestleMania 42, calling it "old school" based on the match's deliberate pacing and the absence of significant variation throughout its duration.

Valley used this observation as a jumping-off point to explore what the term "old school" actually means in the context of professional wrestling. Rather than accepting the commenter's assessment at face value, he questioned the accuracy of the characterization and delved into the nuances of wrestling terminology that fans often use without fully considering their implications.

The central question Valley examined was whether the description of that particular match as "old school" genuinely reflects how wrestlers performed during the territorial era of professional wrestling or during the PG Era of WWE programming. He considered whether the commenter was accurately applying historical wrestling knowledge or simply using the term as a relative descriptor based on their own experience as a wrestling fan.

Valley's broader inquiry touched on the subjective nature of how wrestling fans perceive and categorize wrestling styles. He explored whether "old school" is a meaningful descriptor with concrete historical roots or whether it primarily reflects the generational perspective of individual viewers. What one person considers old school might differ significantly based on when that person began watching professional wrestling.

The episode also included Valley's personal thoughts on WrestleMania itself, as well as commentary regarding Sid's family. Listeners interested in Valley's full analysis and perspective on these wrestling topics can access the complete podcast episode through the subscription service.

Brooke Hogan Netflix Documentary

Brooke Hogan Addresses Netflix Docu-Series On Hulk Hogan: What They Got Wrong, Left Out & More

Brooke Hogan appeared as a guest on the Bubba The Love Sponge Show for an in-depth interview covering all things related to Hulk Hogan. During the discussion, the daughter of the late pro wrestling legend spoke at length about the new Hulk Hogan: Real American documentary series on Netflix, offering her perspective on what the production accomplished and what it failed to deliver.

On the documentary's coverage of her father's wrestling career, Brooke acknowledged that it did provide comprehensive coverage. "For a wrestling fan, it captured every single moment of his career from beginning to end. It was definitely a showcase piece for sure," she stated. However, she was critical of what the documentary omitted from a personal and factual standpoint. "But it was, in my opinion, missing about 98% of critical, real-life, factual information. I feel like I was living in an alternate universe. I was like, 'I didn't realize this is how it went down.' … I was just shocked that they acted like everything that was in there was news to everybody. Like we knew about steroids and we knew about drugs. But there were many key things, things that tie in. There's many causes for the reaction."

Brooke expressed particular frustration about how she was portrayed in the documentary, describing her role as essentially nonexistent. "Literally buried. Oh, she doesn't exist," she said, highlighting the emotional weight of this omission. She explained that prior to watching the documentary, she had held onto some hope regarding her relationship with her father. "What's really sad is there was some sort of, before I watched this, there was some sort of, for lack of a better term, magic or hope or something that I held onto. You know, I asked to be taken out of the will and my dad did it. I think I imagined that there would be some sort of why or 'Hey, you're the only person I trust.' Like, let's talk about it. But we had already talked about it."

Brooke revealed that she had reached out to her father about his inner circle, attempting to protect his interests. "I was, again, looking through my text messages at things that I was texting him saying, and it's funny because in the text messages, I'm listing what he said to me about the people around him. I'm like, you've told me this, this, this, and this dad, I'm only watching out for you. When I was pigeonholed is what it felt like and had to make this decision that I felt I had to at the time."

The interview also touched on Brooke's absence from WWE's ten bell salute tribute to Hulk Hogan. She clarified the circumstances surrounding her non-attendance, explaining that she had addressed the matter on Instagram. "I said something on Instagram where I was like, 'For everybody giving me crap for not being there, I wasn't invited.' Nick Khan called me personally and said that he extended the invite through my brother. That was kind of it. So I would imagine that if I were to be able to go, I would probably have to be invited by Nick, my brother," she explained.

Brooke expressed deep disappointment about the broader situation, particularly regarding Andre the Giant's daughter attending the tribute. "It really hurt to see Andre the Giant's daughter there because this is the thing… Right now, at the point in life I'm at, I could not be more disappointed and hurt. I'm just flabbergasted. I have no more tears to cry. I have no more gusto in me. I fought for my dad. The truth is, I fought so hard for my dad until I literally was about to lose myself."

Hulk Hogan: Real American is available now in full for on demand viewing for subscribers of Netflix.

Shawn Michaels Begged To Keep This WWE Main Roster Star In NXT For Six More Months

Je'Von Evans' departure from NXT came sooner than expected, and now fans understand exactly why. After capturing the Iron Survivor match at NXT Deadline in December, Evans was positioned to challenge then-NXT Champion Oba Femi at New Year's Evil. His momentum on the brand appeared undeniable, yet his time on the Tuesday night show came to an abrupt end following a loss to Ricky Saints the following week, before he made his official debut on WWE Raw.

Shawn Michaels, the Hall of Famer heading creative direction for NXT, disclosed his reluctance to lose Evans so quickly. Speaking to Ringer Wrestling, Michaels explained the thought process behind attempting to keep Evans in developmental.

"I was begging, 'Can we just keep him for another six months?'" Michaels said. "From an 'NXT' standpoint, we were, in our minds, going to obviously build around Je'Von for the next year. That was kind of the model, and they're like, 'Oh, no, no. We're taking him, too.' I pushed back a little bit and was like, 'Six months?' 'No.' 'Three?' 'No.' I'm like, 'Alright. Fair enough.'"

The creative vision for Evans in NXT involved a sustained, long-term investment in the young star. Michaels and his team had structured plans to center their programming around Evans' development and growth. When main roster management decided to promote Evans, Michaels negotiated for a compromise, first requesting half a year in NXT before scaling back his request to three months. Both proposals were rejected.

Despite his disappointment at losing Evans prematurely, Michaels maintains perspective on what such callups represent for NXT's mission and effectiveness. He drew a comparison between NXT and college athletics while acknowledging the fundamental purpose of the developmental brand.

"In many respects, yes, we're like the NCAA," Michaels said. "But, at the same time, they are not meant to stay here. We're not going to build around these people for years and years. It's just not the reality of 'NXT.'"

Michaels emphasized that if NXT's biggest problem is losing too many talented performers to main roster promotions, it signals the brand is fulfilling its core function successfully. He acknowledged this is an important reminder for himself and others within the organization.

"If the worst news they get in 'NXT' is that the main roster is taking too many people, it means they're doing a good job. He said that's something he always has to remember, and it's part of his job to remind others that, as well."

WWE WrestleMania 42 Exposed How Hokey Pro Wrestling Has Become

As I'm writing this, last night was the first night of the NFL Draft. Because it is in Pittsburgh, a special guest of honor was "Big" Ben Roethlisberger, an all time great Pittsburgh Steelers quarterback who won the franchise a Super Bowl in 2009. "Big" Ben took the stage with a few Terrible Towels in hand and threw them to the live crowd. One recipient was absent AEW star Dr. Britt Baker DMD. "Big Ben" did not take the stage dressed as an elf wielding a useless wooden sword and mutter into a microphone, "It's dangerous to go alone! Take this!" Why do you suppose that is? Because "Big" Ben is a superstar and he knows that this grand stage is his to own.

Since WWE WrestleMania 42, the legitimate sports media has been having a belly laugh at the expense of pro wrestling. That belly laugh is deserved. I know this is not an article the Internet Wrestling Community is going to pin to their fridge with a gold star on it. But it is a harsh reality that a fan base far too close to the situation needs to read. WrestleMania 42 exposed how hokey pro wrestling has become.

I'm not a black and white kind of guy. I grew up on the golden age of WWF Wrestling, the Rock 'N' Wrestling Connection era when the dank, dark production of pro wrestling became super colorful and quite literally cartoonish. I loved that era and still do. However, that era is blamed for starting what we have today. Well, if that is the case, maybe all the purists were right back then. Because I could not stand what I saw on both Saturday and Sunday night. I said I'm not a black and white guy. I'm also not a statistics guy. Usually it is the intangible that makes things great and you can't explain it, you can't predict it. But you know when something is special.

However, I'll forgo that "feeling" element and give you some statistics that should bother you. WrestleMania was 2 nights with 8 total hours of programming. The actual wrestling time over two nights was slightly over 3 hours. The average ring entrance time over 8 hours was just under 3 hours. The average commercial and self-gratification and self-indulgent video packages was just over 2 hours. Finally, and this is a fact, the most featured person shown on screen most during WWE WrestleMania 42 over 2 nights is someone who died 9 months ago in "The Immortal" Hulk Hogan who racked up over 40 minutes of on screen time via video packages. Do those stats make you happy as a wrestling fan? If they do, I'd like some of what you're smoking.

We all could feel that this was going to be a bad WrestleMania. The build up to the matches made zero sense. For example, the AI slop Pat McAfee angle was put on our screens 2 weeks before WrestleMania because the company is willing to forgo logic to get cheap plugs on Pat McAfee's weekly ESPN show.

Let's follow the bouncing ball of that story. Cody Rhodes used to be a member of Randy Orton's "Legacy" faction as an underling. Cody even mentioned back then that he hoped to never end up the way Triple H and Randy Orton ended up once upon a time. That was over 15 years ago. Now they have ended up exactly like Triple H and Orton where Cody eclipsed Orton the same way Orton eclipsed Triple H over 20 years ago. Pretty good story, right? Wrong.

Instead of that being the story told, we had a hasty Randy Orton heel turn between the Royal Rumble and WrestleMania that the fans didn't truly buy into. Who caused the heel turn? Why, the voices in Randy Orton's head that he was talking to on the phone. Who could possibly want Orton to embrace his former heel self? Perhaps "Cowboy" Bob Orton who is still alive and could have offered a reason for Orton turning heel and also poured salt in the wounds that Cody's father Dusty is no longer with us? Nope. How about Aliester Black who had a spooky dooky dopey feud with Orton earlier this year and could have played the role, Kevin Sullivan style, of the bad "voices"? That would have made logical sense and built off existing storylines. Instead, we got lazy booking that prioritizes flash over substance and celebrity appearances over compelling narratives.

AEW World Championship and Double Or Nothing 2026

Darby Allin's reign as the new AEW Men's World Champion continues to generate positive momentum for the company three weeks after his shocking victory over MJF. The title change itself has been widely celebrated as an exciting moment, with Allin successfully defending his championship twice since winning it, defeating both Tommaso Ciampa and Brody King in competitive matches.

Jeff Jarrett, who serves as both an occasional on-air talent and behind-the-scenes member of AEW's creative team, recently expressed his admiration for how the entire title change was executed. Speaking on his podcast "My World with Jeff Jarrett," Jarrett praised the technical aspects of Allin's victory, highlighting the specific wrestling sequences that made the moment memorable.

"I just thought the timing of it...I just think it was a lot of things done right," Jarrett said. "The tackle, headlock takeover and kind of have Darby bridged on that. There were little nuances that made it a gamble, it's a risk in so many ways, but hats off to all involved, cause I don't want to pick out any individual talent. I thought the announcers were right on point, I thought the crowd...anyway, all good man."

Jarrett reserved his highest praise for AEW owner Tony Khan, who either made the call directly or approved the decision to have Allin dethrone MJF. Jarrett noted the significance of the moment, emphasizing that Allin had come a long way since Jarrett's arrival at AEW when Allin was one of the company's Four Pillars of the promotion but had never captured the world title.

"Obviously, Tony is the one who either made the call specifically or approved of the call," Jarrett said. "But man oh man, what a way to drop it in there...I am very happy for Darby. And yes, he was the four pillars two years ago when I arrived. And he never won the title. But it finally happened, in his hometown, the week of WrestleMania. Hats off."

Paul Wight AEW Return Plans

Paul Wight, best known to WWE fans as The Big Show, has not competed in a ring for more than two years, but he has confirmed that his wrestling career is not over just yet. Wight has been under contract with All Elite Wrestling since 2021 after spending more than two decades with WWE.

While appearing on The Chris Evans Breakfast Show on Virgin Radio UK to promote AEW's All In London event at Wembley Stadium on August 30, Wight was asked directly if fans will ever see him wrestle again. "Yes! Do you know the absolute H-E-double hockey sticks I've gone through to get back in the ring?" the seven-foot giant replied.

The veteran emphasized that he is not looking to take on a demanding schedule at this stage of his career. "I don't have to do much at this stage of the game. I'm not competing for championships, I'm not pulling the wagon, working every week," Wight said. He highlighted the multiple roles he has taken on with AEW, explaining that his primary focus is on helping the next generation of talent. "I have a great opportunity with All Elite Wrestling, which was very important to me, to be able to help mentor some of the younger talent when they want it, tell them some stories to help them avoid some troubles in life, do the commentating where I can help explain what they're doing and help develop their characters, and then get in the ring."

Wight described his current approach with humor, comparing himself to Snake Plissken from the film Escape from New York. "I make the joke I'm like Snake Plissken from that film. You know, everybody says, 'Hey, man. I heard you were dead.' No, I'm still alive. I show up once in a while, everybody's happy. I come over here, get a nice little pop from the crowd, 'Oh, we remember him back when he had long hair and a bright future. We're glad he're still doing well.'"

While Wight has been focusing on his mentoring and commentary work, he has also been closely observing the current AEW roster and has identified specific opponents he would like to face before retiring. Speaking about today's wrestling landscape, Wight expressed high praise for the current generation of talent. "Super positive. Super hard work ethic. Gets the business, the industry, incredible love for wrestling and for going out there and giving everything for the fans," he said.

Drawing from his extensive experience working with legends from the 1970s and 1980s like Ric Flair, Hulk Hogan, Randy Savage, and Arn Anderson, Wight reflected on how the industry has evolved. "I've worked with some of the greatest talents that have ever set foot in this business or been around them from legends from the 70s and 80s like Flair and Hogan and Savage and Arn Anderson, Terry Taylor. I got to sit, talk with Harley Race. I mean, there were so many amazing talents. Brett Hart, Kevin, Scott," he said.

Despite the industry's transformation, Wight believes the talent pool may be deeper than ever. "Overall, there's never been, in my opinion, an influx of such incredible talent in men's and women's wrestling. The talent is unreal. Sure, there's a different business. There's different ways of psychology. There's different match approaches, but there's a lot of incredibly athletic talent, diversified talents that have personalities that's just when you think it's all been done, there's something new that comes along," he explained.

When looking at AEW's roster specifically, Wight identified Kenny Omega as one opponent he has tremendous respect for and would love to work with in some capacity. "So, when I look at our AEW roster, even though he's not a younger cat, he's still somebody that I've had respect for a long time – Kenny Omega. I think Kenny is one I would think 'man, I would love to either tag with Kenny or to compete against Kenny'," Wight said.

Darby Allin is another name on Wight's potential opponent list, and he became particularly animated when discussing a potential match with the high-flying competitor. "I would love to have a great heel run and work Darby. I mean, that would be ridiculous. Cause I could sling Darby's guts all over the place. Even the old school one where I slammed Rey into the ring post when he was tied to the stretcher. I can only imagine the carnage I could do with Darby," Wight said, referencing one of his most memorable moments with Rey Mysterio.

Recently, Wight shared an update on his physical transformation, indicating that he is actively preparing for a return to in-ring competition. With his sights set on a final run in AEW before retirement, Wight appears motivated to add a few more chapters to his legendary career while continuing to support the next generation of wrestling talent.

CONCERNING IMPLICATIONS FOR WWE

The messaging from WWE presents a fundamental contradiction that observers find troubling. WWE has spent years presenting itself as a booming global brand with massive television deals, packed arenas, and record-setting revenue. This narrative is promoted heavily during every Premium Live Event and especially during WrestleMania. However, quietly asking roster members to accept pay cuts directly contradicts this image of growth and success.

The impact extends beyond mere optics. Wrestlers are already covering travel, gear, training, and often medical costs as independent contractors. Their pay isn't just income—it's what keeps the job sustainable and what helps them maintain the presentation expected of WWE superstars. Slashing pay by 50% or significant amounts is not merely a business decision; it carries personal implications that can affect morale and performance.

This approach comes at a particularly disadvantageous time for WWE. Talent now has genuine options that didn't exist during previous eras of cost-cutting. All Elite Wrestling is established as a viable alternative. International promotions have become increasingly accessible. The independent wrestling scene remains active and lucrative. Unlike the Monday Night Wars era, when competition could drive salaries upward, WWE is now handing leverage back to talent by signaling they can be replaced or pressured into accepting less.

Industry observers worry about the long-term damage to company morale and product quality. If performers begin to feel they are being treated as replaceable, it inevitably affects their energy and commitment. Morale sinks. Performances suffer. The philosophy of maximizing profits with minimal focus on loyalty to talent is manifesting both on screen during the post-WrestleMania period and behind the scenes with the contract restructuring discussions.

Whether talent will respond with the same resolve demonstrated by Kingston and Woods—developing what one observer called "a spine"—remains to be seen.

The historical pattern in WWE has been cyclical, oscillating between periods of aggressive profit maximization and phases of aggressively signing talent to big money deals. 🔥 Chelsea Green Heart Procedure

Chelsea Green recently shared a significant health update with fans via Instagram, announcing that she had undergone a heart procedure to address a serious medical condition that had gone undiagnosed for a decade. The WWE Superstar revealed that doctors finally caught her SVT after years of dealing with symptoms without proper diagnosis.

SVT, or supraventricular tachycardia, is a type of irregular heartbeat also called an arrhythmia. It's characterized by a very fast or erratic heartbeat that affects the heart's upper chambers. According to medical sources, SVT can cause the heart to beat very fast, sometimes exceeding 150 beats per minute, and episodes can happen suddenly and last anywhere from a few seconds to hours. While often benign on its own, the condition can yield further symptoms and obscure more severe conditions if left untreated.

In her Instagram post, Green wrote: "After 10 years, doctors finally caught my SVT and I was able to get a heart procedure done! Three hours later and I'm on the mend." The post included a photo of Green resting in a hospital bed alongside her husband and fellow WWE Superstar Matt Cardona, who was there offering support during her recovery.

The procedure Green underwent was likely an SVT ablation, a treatment that uses cold or heat energy to create tiny scars in the heart to block faulty electrical signals and restore the heart rhythm. Such procedures typically take three to six hours, which aligns with Green's account of the three-hour operation.

This health issue comes on top of what has already been a challenging period for Green in 2026. WWE 45th Anniversary Celebration

WWE took time to commemorate the 45th anniversary of one of its most influential pre-Hulkamania era matches by uploading the complete video of Sgt. The match took place at Madison Square Garden on May 4, 1981, and stands as one of the company's best-remembered bouts from that era, holding particular significance as an early example of WWE presenting hardcore wrestling.

The upload featured an introduction with Slaughter recounting the story behind the match. Slaughter explained how his rivalry with Patterson began when Patterson was working as both an announcer and wrestler. Slaughter and his manager The Grand Wizard bullied Patterson into accepting a $10,000 Cobra Clutch challenge. During that challenge, when Patterson was about to break free from the hold, Slaughter struck him with a knee strike and bloodied him during a subsequent beatdown. These events set the stage for their ultimate confrontation in the Alley Fight.

The Alley Fight itself was contested without a referee and had no rules, representing a radical departure from standard wrestling presentation of the time. In the match, the heel Slaughter lost when Grand Wizard threw in the towel, though Slaughter made clear he wanted to continue fighting. The bout was recognized as the 1981 Match of the Year by the Wrestling Observer Newsletter, cementing its legacy in professional wrestling history.

Slaughter credited the match with establishing his toughness with the audience. He also noted that the bout helped inspire future wrestling icon Mick Foley to pursue a career in professional wrestling. Foley later told Slaughter directly that he had been in the third row during the match and that witnessing it excited him so much that he wanted to become a professional wrestler.

The impact of the Alley Fight extended far beyond its initial presentation. WWE has held it up as a prime example of how to tell a compelling story through in-ring work. The company made it required viewing for wrestlers, using it as an instructional tool about what professional wrestling should be. As Slaughter explained, telling a story without a referee present was particularly challenging, making their accomplishment all the more noteworthy.

When reflecting on the match's legacy, Slaughter expressed surprise that WWE never held another Alley Fight in the subsequent 45 years. He speculated that the decision to never repeat the match format may have been made out of respect for Patterson and himself. Slaughter said he continues to wait for an opportunity when two wrestlers unable to settle their differences through conventional matches might be booked in an Alley Fight, though the company has never again utilized the stipulation. Regardless, Slaughter firmly views the original match as a classic that deserves its place in wrestling history.

WWE's Jade Cargill 'Would've Loved To Wrestle Way More' As Champion

Jade Cargill's first WWE Women's Championship reign came to an end at WrestleMania 42 in April when she lost the title to Rhea Ripley, but the champion herself has acknowledged that her nearly six-month run with the gold was not everything she hoped it would be.

Cargill won the title last November at Saturday Night's Main Event when she defeated Tiffany Stratton. However, her reign was marked by limited television time and minimal title defenses. Throughout her championship run, Cargill only successfully defended the WWE Women's Title once and competed in just six matches on television. Most notably, three of those six televised matches lasted five minutes or less, providing limited opportunity to showcase her abilities as champion.

Following her loss to Ripley, Cargill opened up about her disappointment during an interview with MuscleManMalcolm. When discussing her title reign, she stated: "I thought it was good. I felt like I would have loved to wrestle way more. I felt like I would have liked to really sink my teeth into a juicier storylines. But overall I think I delivered when I was given the chance to do that and I think that Rhea and I's match last night went great."

This marks the second time Cargill has publicly expressed her frustrations about limited opportunities as champion. Back in March, she had previously shared her aspirations for more impactful title defenses, specifically mentioning desires to face Charlotte Flair and Alexa Bliss during her reign.

Since dropping the title to Ripley, Cargill has not yet returned to in-ring competition. However, she has been moving forward creatively, as she formed a new faction with fellow wrestlers Michin and B-Fab ahead of WrestleMania 42. This alliance suggests that WWE may have new plans for Cargill moving forward, which could potentially include the more substantial storylines and wrestling opportunities she expressed desiring during her championship reign.

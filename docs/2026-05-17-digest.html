🔥 Paul Wight AEW contract status

Paul Wight, the legendary wrestler known as "The Big Show," has agreed to a new deal with All Elite Wrestling to remain with the company moving forward. Sources within AEW confirmed that Wight's original contract was coming up for expiration earlier in 2026, but the two sides successfully negotiated a fresh agreement to keep him under their banner. While the exact length of the new contract has not been publicly disclosed, internal sources believe Wight remains firmly locked in with AEW at this time.

In a recent interview with Virgin Radio UK, Wight explained his approach to his current role in the wrestling business. "I don't have to do much at this stage of the game. I'm not competing for championships. I'm not pulling the wagon and working every week," Wight stated. "I have a great opportunity with All Elite Wrestling, which has been very important to me, to help mentor some of the younger talent when they want it, tell them stories that might help them avoid some of life's troubles, and do commentary where I can help explain what they're doing while also helping develop their characters. Then I can get in the ring, because there are things I know I can do while working with them that will help them. And it's fun for me, because I don't have to do much."

Since joining AEW in 2021, Wight has worked in multiple capacities for the promotion. His roles have included commentary work, backstage appearances, interviews, special appearances, and occasional wrestling matches. The veteran has primarily operated in a backstage and ambassador-type capacity in recent years, with limited high-profile on-screen visibility. However, he has made it clear that he views these positions as valuable contributions to the company and its roster development.

Wight has previously stated during interviews that he plans to spend the final years of his wrestling career with All Elite Wrestling, signaling his long-term commitment to the promotion. While he has never officially ruled out stepping back into the ring for future matches, there have been few recent updates regarding any potential in-ring return plans. His current focus appears to be on using his extensive wrestling knowledge and experience to help develop the next generation of talent within AEW.

Darby Allin AEW World Championship reign

Darby Allin's wheels were about to come off in his latest AEW World Championship defense during Collision on May 16, 2026. The reigning champion faced fellow daredevil Sammy Guevara in the main event, and things got off to a rough start before the bell even rang.

Allin entered the ring, laid the title on the mat, and knelt in a prayer position. Sammy seized the opportunity to strike with a curb stomp directly onto the title, leaving Allin loopy for much of the match. This smart pre-bell tactic set the tone for what would become one of Allin's toughest title defenses yet.

The match was officially underway under a no count out stipulation, which Allin had requested earlier. Sammy took full advantage by setting up a ladder. He climbed to the top and leaped down onto Allin to crash through a table with a massive high-impact maneuver. The momentum appeared firmly in Guevara's favor as he controlled the pace of the match.

Allin found himself in serious trouble, with the effects of the early curb stomp continuing to take their toll. During one exchange, Allin briefly passed out while applying the Scorpion Deathlock. After a quick referee check, the match was allowed to continue, though questions arose about whether Allin had finally pushed his body past the breaking point.

Despite being light-headed and dealing with residual effects from the head trauma, Allin still showed flashes of his technical wrestling prowess. When Guevara attempted a springboard, Allin caught him mid-air and transitioned into a sleeper hold. Guevara was fading from the submission but managed to reach the ropes to break the hold.

As the match progressed, Sammy mounted a serious offensive surge. He hit two straight GTH finishers with his bare knee, each one close enough to finish the match. Rather than go for a pin following the second knee strike, Guevara instead climbed to the top rope to attempt a frog splash. Allin showed his resilience by kicking out of the cover.

Guevara decided to get loco once more, setting up another table dive. However, Allin dodged away, and Sammy crashed through a table on a 630 senton, leaving him vulnerable. With the window of opportunity finally opening, Allin showed urgency and desperation to hit his finishing move, the Coffin Drop, in the ring to secure the victory.

This turned out to be an exceptionally tough title defense for Allin. He never fully recovered from the initial curb stomp and the subsequent impacts from Sammy's high-flying offense. Allin showed remarkable resilience to survive two back-to-back knee strikes like the GTH, let alone while already suffering from head trauma. If Allin was a cat, he may have used up some of his nine lives in this match.

The championship picture remains incredibly busy for the AEW World Champion. Allin has a date with MJF in a Title versus Hair match at Double or Nothing on Sunday, May 24, but he is not taking any time off to recover or prepare. Allin's next title defense is already set against "Speedball" Mike Bailey on Dynamite. Bailey's feet are lethal weapons, and the challenger will look to tenderize Allin before his high-stakes match against MJF.

The intrigue surrounding Allin's title reign was further heightened when MJF took to social media before the Collision event with a direct message aimed at Sammy Guevara. MJF didn't hide his intentions, posting: "Hey shithead, sammyguevara. If you win the match tonight I'll give you 500k. Get the job done." The post immediately garnered attention due to MJF's current issues with Darby Allin heading into Double or Nothing.

As things currently stand, MJF is scheduled to challenge Darby Allin in the Hair vs. Title match at the pay-per-view event, adding massive personal stakes to their rivalry. Darby has been one of AEW's busiest world champions since defeating MJF for the title at AEW Dynasty. Since winning the championship, Allin has already racked up five successful title defenses while continuing his trademark fighting champion approach. That makes Sammy Guevara directly relevant to one of AEW's biggest rivalries.

Whether MJF was seriously offering Guevara half a million dollars or simply trying to stir chaos before the match remains unclear, but the timing certainly raised eyebrows with Double or Nothing quickly approaching. The offer adds another layer of complexity to Allin's championship reign and raises questions about what alliances might form in the coming weeks leading to the pay-per-view event.

Swerve Strickland ROH and AEW World Championship obsession

On Friday at the Supercard of Honor pay-per-view event, AEW's Swerve Strickland descended upon Ring of Honor and launched a brutal assault on Bandido immediately following the ROH World Champion's successful title retention against Blake Christian. The attack came post-match as Bandido was celebrating his victory, with Strickland blindsiding him with a House Call kick to the head.

The violence did not end there. Strickland delivered a second House Call before setting up a chair and executing a vertebreaker onto it, sending a clear message to the champion. This assault was characteristic of heel work, but it was Strickland's backstage promo that truly revealed the depths of his desperation.

When questioned about the necessity of the cheap attack, Strickland made his intentions crystal clear. He stated that any means is necessary at this point in his career. He expressed indifference toward whether Bandido might be injured and unable to compete in the Owen Hart Tournament, prioritizing his own goals above all else. With a steely determination, he declared: "My actions will be seen and my voice will be heard."

Strickland's singular obsession centers on recapturing the AEW World Championship, a goal that has driven him to embrace a desperation that strips away his previously natural swagger and cool demeanor. His last televised match occurred on the March 25 edition of AEW Dynamite, where he fell short against Kenny Omega, eliminating his opportunity to become the next number one contender to former world champion MJF. Since that loss, Strickland has remained off AEW programming while turning his attention toward Bandido, viewing the ROH World Champion as a bounty worth collecting.

The timing of Strickland's assault is significant because the brackets for the 2026 Owen Hart Cup Tournament had just been revealed earlier in the week, pitting the two men against one another in the first round at AEW Double or Nothing. The tournament structure carries immense importance, as the overall winner receives a world title shot at All In London from Wembley Stadium in August. This tournament opportunity represents Strickland's clearest path back to the world championship picture.

Strickland's character arc has become one of professional wrestling's most compelling heels in recent times. While he still carries inherent natural talent and charisma, his world title obsession has transformed him into a character devoid of redeemable qualities worth cheering for. The desperation that now defines him has made him a worthy target for audience disdain, which represents skillful heel work in an era where drawing genuine heat is increasingly difficult.

The question is no longer whether Strickland will capture the AEW World Championship again, but rather when he will achieve that goal. His willingness to employ any means necessary and his relentless focus on the prize suggest that his path to a second world title reign remains inevitable, pending the outcome of his Owen Hart Tournament encounter with the man he attacked so viciously at Supercard of Honor.

"I've been trying to be respectful to them and try to conduct myself as a gentleman and then for them to go on background and spread these things...I'll just turn the other cheek, and maybe a couple of years ago I wouldn't have done it like that," Khan explained.

He also praised several performers, including Lio Rush for his continued growth and fantastic work, as well as Zayda Steel for stepping up when needed. WWE'S WRESTLING STYLE AND PERFORMER DEVELOPMENT

Rob Van Dam has pushed back against long-running criticism of WWE's in-ring style, explaining his perspective that the company's approach actually makes wrestlers better performers in the long run. RVD noted that he once warned fellow wrestler Sammy that WWE would change his style and force him to rethink how he worked. According to RVD, WWE teaches wrestlers to slow down, make fewer moves count, and connect more deeply with the crowd rather than simply executing a high volume of moves. RVD explained that every wrestler goes through this adjustment when entering WWE's system, and that while there is an appropriate pace for competition, slowing down what you're doing and making it mean more creates more explosive and impactful moments. He pointed out that doing more moves does not automatically make a match better, and that one strong, explosive moment can mean more to an audience than several weak-looking moves stacked together. RVD acknowledged that wrestlers often resist this lesson initially because the artist inside them believes they're being restricted, but he contends that over time they realize WWE's style is designed to help them connect with a bigger audience and ultimately make more money. He emphasized that the mainstream audience will typically respond more to this style than to faster, more self-indulgent approaches, even if independent or smaller show audiences might prefer the latter. RVD concluded that performers owe it to themselves to learn WWE's style and adapt when necessary, as timing, selling, crowd connection, and making moments feel bigger are skills that make performers more effective on the biggest stages.

He explained that television networks and corporate executives often make such decisions without fully understanding the product or audience, reasoning that since talent is already on the roster, adding an extra hour doesn't significantly increase production costs while allowing the sale of additional advertising. The extended format inevitably fatigues the audience, dilutes the value of characters, and dilutes the impact of storylines. Three-hour shows require keeping a tremendous amount of extra talent on the roster just to fulfill the additional hour with meaningful content, creating a situation where executives essentially exploit the format as a revenue stream at the expense of creative quality. Bischoff noted that this cycle has been happening for a long time and represents a recurring problem within the television industry where executives prioritize short-term revenue gains over long-term audience satisfaction.

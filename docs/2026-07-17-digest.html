PROGRAM AND ROSTER MOMENTUM

AEW is garnering significant praise for its current creative direction. Tommy Dreamer, the ECW legend, reviewed the most recent AEW Dynamite episode from Boston and declared that AEW is currently "very, very hot." Speaking on Busted Open After Dark, Dreamer highlighted how the company has leaned heavily into character development and storytelling progression rather than relying solely on in-ring action.

Dreamer's assessment was based largely on the audience's reactions throughout the show. He noted that when wrestlers walk out, some are getting very big reactions that he described as "superstar reactions," a term typically associated with WWE. He explained that the AEW audience really loves many of their performers, and when next-level people show up, the crowd responds with genuine enthusiasm for their performers.

One of the night's biggest highlights for Dreamer was the opening AEW World Tag Team Championship match featuring champions Adam Copeland and Christian Cage against the Death Riders. He particularly praised AEW for allowing the story to unfold naturally through the performers' actions rather than relying on lengthy promos. "They have done an amazing job telling stories without really telling the story on a microphone," Dreamer said. "It's storytelling. It's body language. It's the fans chanting, hug it out. We all want to see you all together, and Christian not trusting the guy when, by the way, none of us ever want to trust Christian."

Dreamer also applauded AEW for resisting the temptation to immediately resolve storylines. "They're telling a wonderful story through the chaos. The bad guys beat up the good guys, and then here come the good guys to even the odds. And now we have turmoil, and we're waiting to see what happens next," Dreamer said. "And when nothing happens next, we're gonna wait till next week, and that's how you tell stories."

Another segment that stood out to Dreamer was Kenny Omega's championship celebration, where the newly crowned AEW World Champion attempted to convince Kevin Knight to distance himself from Don Callis by reflecting on his own past. "Kenny being the voice of reason, the guy who's been there pretty much the longest. He's also your champion. He's also overcome so, so much to be there, and wants to do good, because when he won his title, he wasn't in that same mindset, and he's trying to tell people don't live the way I lived, which is beautiful theater," Dreamer said.

According to Dreamer, what made the episode stand out was that every segment felt meaningful and helped move stories forward. "They didn't feel like backstage vignettes. They didn't feel like backstage interviews. They didn't feel like another run-in. It felt like different chapters of a book that I have to continue to watch," Dreamer said. "I feel like tonight AEW is in the movie making business."

Dreamer believes AEW has found a strong balance between high-level wrestling and compelling storytelling, something he feels will help sustain the promotion's momentum. "This is a great mix for AEW, because this is what they need to continue this momentum and to continue to have people wanting to see this product," Dreamer said. "You know you're going to see great wrestling, but when you have all these intertwined things, and they also gave them time." He concluded by saying AEW has matured significantly since its early years. "I feel like all the growing pains are gone. Yes, you're going to have some. No show is ever perfect, but tonight's show is really, really interesting for a whole other different reasons than your normal AEW shows," Dreamer said.

BUSINESS AND INFRASTRUCTURE DEVELOPMENTS

Dave Meltzer explained what AEW stands to gain from the potential WBD-Paramount deal. While the United States Justice Department approved the Paramount Skydance takeover of AEW media partner Warner Bros. Discovery in June, the companies' merger faces several roadblocks, including a lawsuit from a dozen states alleging a monopoly. The California Attorney General, alongside multiple other attorneys general, have requested a temporary restraining order, meaning the sale would be paused as the legal process continues.

From a pro wrestling standpoint, Meltzer explained that AEW wants the deal to go through as quickly as possible. The idea would be that HBO Max and Paramount+ would merge, creating a more powerful entity. AEW and UFC, which streams exclusively on Paramount+, would be in far more homes with the merger of the two streaming services, especially on a worldwide basis. Paramount's CEO David Ellison intends to merge both streaming services into one, which would give the merged companies a little over 200 million direct-to-consumer subscribers. The initial timeframe for the 110 billion dollar sale was quarter three of this year, with Ellison hoping to finalize things by late September. 🔥 WWE Salary and Contract Negotiations

Nick LoPiccolo has delivered a stark assessment of professional wrestling's labor landscape, arguing that the competitive era between WWE and AEW has effectively ended and that wrestlers must fundamentally rethink their approach to career leverage.

In a series of posts on July 15, 2026, LoPiccolo analyzed TKO Group Holdings' $7.5 billion decline in market value from its March peak, arguing that the stock drop is not a sign of underlying business failure but rather a valuation correction after investors placed an enormous premium on TKO's dominant position. However, he contends that this correction has created an opportunity for management to reset WWE's expenses before the next major media-rights cycle begins in two to three years.

The talent agent emphasized that WWE's recent decisions—roster cuts, salary compression, and allowing established wrestlers to depart—reveal what he sees as management's true confidence level. "Roster cuts, salary compression and allowing established talent to walk aren't the moves of a company worried that AEW, or anyone else, is about to gain meaningful ground," LoPiccolo stated. He argues that TKO is operating without fear that released wrestlers will strengthen AEW, suggesting the company no longer views its competitor as capable of punishing cost-cutting decisions.

LoPiccolo pointed to the absence of major media companies capable of creating another bidding war for wrestling rights as fundamental to the market shift. According to his analysis, all major buyers that could plausibly create real wrestling rights competition are already committed elsewhere, cutting costs, or strategically unavailable. This absence means the institutional competition that once drove salary increases no longer exists, despite what he calls "television competition" between the two companies.

The broader implication of LoPiccolo's argument is that wrestler leverage has fundamentally changed. He contends that if TKO believed AEW possessed durable media economics, meaningful distribution leverage, or the ability to absorb expensive talent at scale, every salary cut would create competitive risk. Instead, he claims TKO is behaving as though AEW cannot change the outcome of the wrestling market's competitive landscape.

LoPiccolo distinguished between temporary television competition and lasting institutional competition, arguing that wrestlers were never correct to assume two billionaires bidding against each other would create sustainable leverage. "I told you television competition wasn't institutional competition. I told you another billionaire signing wrestlers wasn't a sustainable labor market. I told you your leverage couldn't depend on two wrestling companies bidding against each other forever," he wrote.

For wrestlers still banking on continued competition between WWE and AEW, LoPiccolo's message was direct: "To every wrestler reading this, stop waiting for a wrestling war to create leverage for you. It's over. TKO knows it. Wall Street knows it. The media companies know it."

LoPiccolo argues that when one employer controls salary, television exposure, creative opportunities, outside approvals, and perceived market value, talent becomes dangerously dependent. Jarrett explained that TNA's unique identity was built on several distinctive elements that set it apart from WWE. Beyond the ring itself, the company featured the X Division, the Knockouts division, and four distinct wrestling divisions that together created TNA's DNA. Jarrett noted that while the six-sided ring was not the only thing that defined TNA, it was an integral part of the company's brand and identity. The ring also mattered from a business standpoint, as it led to merchandise opportunities and toy visibility. Jarrett recalled how the unique ring design helped TNA secure shelf space at major retailers like Toys R Us, something WWE did not have at that time.

When Hogan took over creative influence alongside Eric Bischoff, Jarrett said the company immediately began abandoning these core identity markers. Both Hogan and Bischoff went on record stating that they hated the six-sided ring and hated the X Division. Jarrett said he knew at the time that if Hogan had focused on better creative storytelling and leaned into what made TNA special, such as the X Division, the outcome might have been different. Instead, Hogan and Bischoff brought in older wrestlers from WWE and WCW, such as the Nasty Boys, essentially sweeping out anything associated with Jeff Jarrett's version of the company.

The financial impact was swift and devastating. Jarrett stated that from the day Hogan came in, it took less than twelve months for TNA to go from profitability to a financial death spiral. During Hogan's tenure, TNA made the aggressive decision to move Impact! to Monday nights to directly compete with WWE's Raw. UNSOLICITED ADVICE FOR PEOPLE PRODUCING WRESTLING TV SHOWS

I've been fortunate enough in my life to watch a lot of wrestling. Like, a lot. I've also been fortunate enough to spend some of my professional life in storytelling mediums including publishing, comic books and TV/film. When I look at wrestling through those lenses I see a lot of things happening across the board from WWE, AEW, TNA and more that make it a lesser storytelling medium and less accessible to new audiences.

The best way to tell stories in wrestling is obviously in the ring. Two or more talented workers taking us on a ride like no other medium can. However, what's put out around all that, the vehicle that takes us there and draws in new fans, is the TV show or streamer in today's day and age. Whether it's because streamed shows don't have to worry about being as tight as broadcast shows in the past, or just the way consumer media has changed, or even just people are getting lazy, there are things happening in wrestling shows that were once slight annoyances but have now become full-blown habits, devoid of what any story needs: logic and the ability to make sense.

A six-point checklist has been assembled for anyone producing a wrestling show on behalf of the audience. These are things that shows need to think about, because better television makes for better wrestling, which makes for bigger audiences and so on.

TELL US WHO EVERYONE IS, PLEASE

This shouldn't have to be said, but in every show being watched, at least once or twice per episode, someone is prominently featured in a segment, promo or match but no one says their name. Not even a chyron or graphic just to tell the audience who Noam Dar's friend is. This is basic homework for any storytelling. If the audience doesn't know who someone is and if they haven't watched the show regularly, then how are they supposed to get behind them or understand their story? Complete bios and histories aren't needed every time someone comes on screen, but their name would be nice.

PROMO PARADES ARE OUT OF CONTROL

A good promo or backstage segment that adds passion and fire to a storyline or feud is valuable. What is not valuable is a promo that starts with one person, goes through four interruptions, takes twenty minutes and results in a match between them all that could have just been booked and promoted. This is lazy storytelling and frankly annoying to watch. Not every match needs to be set up in twenty minute opening segments with person after person coming out to interrupt. Promos are meant to promote something that's going to happen, is happening, or is pushing the story forward based on what just happened. They aren't supposed to "set up our main event" or frustrate a GM into making a tag or multi-person match for "right here in" the town where the show airs.

REMEMBER, THIS IS SUPPOSED TO BE A SPORTING EVENT

Wrestling is fiction. Everyone knows this, accepts it and loves it. However, wrestling works best when treated and presented like a legitimate sporting event. An audience should be living and dying with wrestlers the way they do with their favorite football or hockey teams. This is where simple storytelling logic comes into play. No UFC card goes to air with matches, or at least a rundown, undecided and presented to the audience. No boxing show does this. No spelling bee does this. Why does wrestling do it? All it does is make it look like those in charge have no idea what they are doing and just show up to the arena hoping something happens to fill time. Wrestling tends to forget this and shows feature promo parades that set up main events or matches "right now." All that does is make an audience wonder "what were you going to do if that hadn't happened?" Did they just have no opening match planned? Was there no previously decided main event? Yes, the card is subject to change, but the fundamental presentation of a wrestling show should respect its own fiction by presenting scheduled programming the way a sporting event would.

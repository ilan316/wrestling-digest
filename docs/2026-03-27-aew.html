🔥 Toni Storm AEW Absence and Creative Impact

**AEW Forced to Abandon Major Toni Storm Title Program After Sudden Creative Pivot**

A mystery continues to surround the sudden absence of "Timeless" Toni Storm in All Elite Wrestling, with the situation appearing far more complex than initially expected. The circumstances surrounding her removal from programming have forced AEW to completely reshape its women's division plans at the last minute, scrapping what was positioned to be a significant championship storyline.

On the Dynamite episode following AEW Revolution, Storm had been scheduled to compete in a No Holds Barred match against Marina Shafir. However, plans abruptly changed when Storm was found unconscious backstage in her dressing room, with Mina Shirakawa discovering her before stepping in to attack Shafir in her place. According to reports, Storm's hiatus is not believed to be injury-related, and the true reason behind her absence has remained tightly guarded by AEW management.

The last-minute nature of the creative change reveals just how significant the original plans were for Storm's immediate future. According to the Wrestling Observer Newsletter, all plans until the afternoon of the show were for Storm to defeat Marina Shafir to lead to the start of a championship program against AEW Women's Champion Thekla. This title feud would have immediately repositioned Storm back into the championship picture and reshaped the direction of the women's division.

"Toni Storm has been reported as being out of action until 2027. There are a number of stories floating around but it is obviously something that they want to keep under wraps. It is not an injury situation and it came up on 3/18 the day of the show as all plans until the afternoon were for Storm to beat Marina Shafir to lead to the start of a Thekla vs. Toni Storm program for the title."

When AEW learned that Storm could not compete, the company immediately pivoted toward a different creative direction. Rather than simply postponing the match, AEW laid out an injury angle on television and began developing what would become a mystery-based storyline designed to keep fans engaged while Storm remains off the roster.

"When they found out she could not wrestle, they laid out the injury angle and will be doing a tease akin to Who Shot JR? with clues and such and multiple potential assailants."

This pivot inserted Mina Shirakawa into a more prominent role within the women's division, as AEW needed to shift momentum immediately while maintaining interest in the women's championship picture. Instead of beginning a direct title chase, the company is now building toward an eventual revelation about who attacked Storm, keeping the storyline alive despite her extended absence.

An intriguing detail has emerged regarding the contrast between Storm's on-screen presentation and her real-life appearances. Despite performing an injury angle on television, Storm appeared at an autograph signing over the weekend and was not selling any injury at all. This contradiction further reinforces that the situation is not related to any legitimate physical setback but rather something that AEW and Storm have decided to keep confidential.

The impact on AEW's creative planning extends beyond just the immediate women's division storylines. A title program between Toni Storm and Thekla would have immediately reshaped the championship scene and provided Storm with a high-profile return program. With those plans now abandoned, the division has been forced to move in a different direction while Storm's future remains uncertain.

Adding to the intrigue surrounding the situation is Tony Khan's public silence on the matter. According to reports, Khan has outright refused to address the topic publicly and has made it clear that Storm's status is off-limits for discussion. In fact, Storm's absence is reportedly one of only two subjects that Khan refuses to discuss publicly, with the other being AEW's alleged bid to purchase WWE in 2023.

"I was told outright before even asking about the subject that the two subjects Tony Khan will not talk about are Toni Storm and the story about his company putting in a bid to purchase WWE in 2023."

The level of secrecy surrounding Storm's situation suggests that the true reason for her absence involves sensitive personal or professional matters that AEW has determined should remain private. As AEW continues to develop the mystery attacker storyline, fans and observers will be watching closely for any clues or developments that might shed light on Storm's eventual return. For now, her extended absence until 2027 stands as one of the wrestling industry's most closely guarded secrets, with even industry insiders left to speculate about the true reasons behind her sudden removal from AEW programming.

🔥 Anthony Bowens Posturing Seizure Incident

---

Anthony Bowens Reflects on Frightening Posturing Seizure During Early WWE Match

AEW's Anthony Bowens has opened up about a harrowing moment from early in his wrestling career that left a lasting impression. The incident occurred during an enhancement talent appearance on the December 21, 2016 episode of WWE NXT (taped November 30), where Bowens teamed with Jonathan Ortagun to face The Authors of Pain (Akam & Rezar).

The match was short and dominant in favor of AOP, who quickly finished their opponents with their signature Super Collider maneuver. What followed, however, was the truly disturbing moment. After hitting the Super Collider, AOP performed a brutal follow-up spot in which Ortagun was powerbombed directly onto Bowens' head. The impact was severe, and Bowens appeared visibly shaken and concussed in the aftermath of the sequence.

In the ring, Bowens began seizing, prompting the referee to stop the match early. At the time, many observers speculated that he had sustained a serious concussion or traumatic brain injury from the hard-hitting encounter. However, it wasn't until years later that Bowens publicly revealed the true nature of what happened.

Responding to a social media post looking back at the incident on March 27, 2026, Bowens confirmed his condition with a chilling simplicity. "I believe that's called a posturing seizure," he wrote. A posturing seizure can occur after sustaining any sudden brain injuries, such as traumatic brain injuries (TBI) or concussions, making it a serious medical concern in professional wrestling.

The severity of the incident is underscored by the timeline of Bowens' return to action. He did not compete in another match until January 21, 2017, when he faced Drew McIntyre at a Battle Club Pro event. That nearly two-month absence further fuels speculation about the serious nature of his injury and the recovery process required.

Interestingly, that December 2016 NXT appearance turned out to be Bowens' first and last match in WWE. Despite the traumatic nature of the incident, he continued to perform on the independent scene and eventually signed with AEW in November 2020. Since joining AEW, Bowens has become one of the promotion's most prominent stars. He formed The Acclaimed with Max Caster and has held both the AEW World Trios Championship and AEW World Tag Team Championship, establishing himself as a major force in the company.

AOP, Bowens' opponents that fateful night, have since gone their separate ways from WWE. Rezar has transitioned into bare-knuckle boxing competition. Bowens, meanwhile, has become a testament to resilience in professional wrestling, overcoming that early career setback to achieve significant success and recognition in AEW.

**Eddie Kingston Returns to the Ring: Finding Peace After Lengthy Injury Hiatus**

Eddie Kingston is back where he says he belongs—in the wrestling ring. After suffering a broken leg and torn ACL in 2024 that sidelined him for more than a year, the AEW star made his return in September 2025. Though he recently missed some shows last month due to severe migraines, Kingston is now active again and competing on major cards.

Kingston is currently teaming with Ortiz for upcoming matches, with their next high-profile bout taking place at Ring of Honor and Maple Leaf Pro Wrestling's Global Wars event in Windsor, Ontario, Canada. There, Kingston and Ortiz will face Adam Priest and "The Dynamite Kid" Tommy Billington. Beyond their on-screen partnership, Kingston and Ortiz are planning to open a wrestling school together, signaling their commitment to developing the next generation of talent.

When discussing his return from injury, Kingston emphasized the emotional and psychological benefits of being back in action. "Oh I love it. I finally have a little peace in my life, believe it or not," he told The Takedown on SI. "Being in the ring brings me peace and all that chaos in the ring and fighting in the ring brings me peace. It is what it is, you know what I mean. So having that in my life is great, but nah it's normal, it's the same old stuff."

Kingston also reflected on his financial perspective regarding wrestling, noting that his passion for the sport transcends monetary compensation. "I don't get paid to wrestle, I would wrestle for free. I get paid for everything else before the ring. But that's all the same, that's all the same, but I'm back where I'm meant to be in the ring."

**Humility and Unconventional Advice for Young Wrestlers**

Despite his accomplishments and growing role as a mentor through his wrestling school with Ortiz, Kingston has difficulty viewing himself as an inspiration to younger talent. When asked about his role as someone younger wrestlers might look up to, Kingston's response was characteristically blunt and self-deprecating.

"I tell them, 'Don't look up to me. It took me 18 years, what are you nuts?' Don't do what I do, let me crash out, let me yell, let me scream, let me do all that, let me be a pain in the ass," he explained. "You do the opposite of me and you'll be okay."

Kingston acknowledged the difficulty of his own path while emphasizing the importance of moral conviction in wrestling. "Look, you can do it my way, it's just gonna be hard. And you're gonna make it harder on yourself, but you're gonna have your morals, you're gonna take your stand and you're gonna die on your hill or hills. I'm learning how to take that 's' out of the equation and just have a hill. It's like I wake up in the morning and say, 'Which hill is it today?'"

His comments reveal a wrestler who has achieved success through perseverance and principle, but who understands the toll that unconventional path has taken. Rather than encouraging others to emulate him, Kingston is advising younger talent to find their own way while maintaining their integrity.

**Future Goals**

Looking ahead, Kingston has stated that his remaining goals in wrestling include winning the AEW World Championship and tag team titles, along with the dream of wrestling at Budokan Hall in Japan. These objectives demonstrate that despite his lengthy hiatus and current status as a veteran, Kingston remains hungry for major accomplishments.

MVP on AEW Outrunners Corniness

**TL;DR:** MVP discussed how modern wrestling has shifted away from colorful, over-the-top characters toward more "legitimate" wrestlers, but acknowledged that corniness can work when executed authentically. He cited The Outrunners as a prime example of a campy, 1980s-style gimmick that resonates with audiences despite—or because of—its cheesiness.

---

Pro wrestling's character landscape has undergone a significant transformation in recent years. While the industry has long been a haven for larger-than-life, over-the-top, colorful superstars, the pendulum has begun to swing in the opposite direction. Modern audiences have increasingly gravitated toward wrestlers who project legitimacy and realness, harking back to wrestling's earliest days when legitimate tough guys dominated the ring. In contrast, the colorful characters that defined the 1980s and 1990s have begun to be viewed by contemporary fans as goofy or corny.

MVP recently weighed in on this evolving dynamic during an appearance on "Marking Out with MVP and Dwayne Swayze." While discussing corny wrestling characters, MVP initially stated his belief that such gimmicks don't generally work in today's wrestling landscape. However, he quickly amended that stance, acknowledging that notable exceptions exist.

According to MVP, the biggest exceptions to this rule are The Outrunners, a tag team duo who bill themselves as "The Youngest Men Alive" and have previously challenged MVP's Hurt Syndicate stablemates Bobby Lashley and Shelton Benjamin for the AEW World Tag Team Championships.

"I'll give you a great example of somebody, two guys that go over huge, The Outrunners," MVP said. "They're caricatures of 80s wrestlers. You know, 'Tell 'em Turb!' Dude, it is so campy, it is so, like, cheesy 80s wrestling. But you love it. They're so good at it. You know, it's like 'Yeah, I like that.' **Kyle Fletcher Faces Backlash Over "Woe-Is-Me" Remarks Regarding Adam Cole's Career-Ending Injury**

The saddest moment of last July's AEW All In pay-per-view came when Adam Cole vacated the TNT Championship after discussing "health problems" that would keep him sidelined for an extended period. Cole's words made it evident that this could be a career-ending injury for the veteran wrestler.

The originally scheduled match at All In was supposed to feature Cole defending his title against heel Kyle Fletcher, who was widely expected to become the new TNT Champion. After Cole's unexpected vacancy of the belt, the match was restructured into a 4-way contest with a feel-good outcome, as underdog babyface Dustin Rhodes captured the title. Fletcher subsequently won the championship from Rhodes a few weeks later in a Chicago Street Fight.

In a recent interview with Soundsphere Magazine, Fletcher discussed how All In 2025 represented one of the most difficult days of his career. He explained the emotional toll of the cancelled opportunity: "It was one of the worst days of my whole life. When you have so much built up anxiety and you've put that pressure on yourself…You know, I'd been dieting for two, three months leading up to that show. Every single day, I'd been thinking about this moment, this match, all of it. And then the rug gets pulled five hours before. You can't really put into words what that kind of feels like. …I was just in this weird limbo. I had no idea what was gonna happen, and I had flown my mum out for that show. It was obviously a huge day for me, and then it just all went to shit."

Fletcher expressed optimism for future opportunities, stating: "Obviously, this is my perspective. It's very hard for me to complain about it, because I don't feel like I have the right to complain about it. But yeah, it was very emotional in a lot of different ways, and not how you want a big day like that to go. So I think, especially this year, I just want to make sure it's a day to remember and a much more positive one."

However, Fletcher's remarks have sparked significant controversy. Wrestling Observer Live's Bryan Alvarez revealed receiving numerous complaints from people who view Fletcher's comments as tone-deaf and self-centered. The core issue centers on the circumstances surrounding Cole's injury: Fletcher allegedly delivered the lariat that caused Cole's concussion just days before All In, directly leading to Cole's title vacation.

According to Alvarez, Cole's injury appears to have ended his wrestling career entirely. "I was pretty much furious reading this, and trust me, I was not the only one. I heard from a lot of people today furious reading this," Alvarez stated. "The reason that the 'rug got pulled out' from under Fletcher…is because he lariated Adam Cole's head off, and concussed him, and that's why Cole had to vacate the title. And I'm not sure if you guys remember or not, but that's pretty much the end of Adam Cole's career. As far as I know, it is the end of his career. He is still not 100 percent. He probably, for all I know, may never be 100 percent."

Alvarez further emphasized the severity of Cole's situation, noting that the wrestler nearly announced his retirement due to depression stemming from the injury. "I know if he's ever wrestling again…he was so depressed that he was gonna go out and just announce his retirement, and people talked him out of it… There's a very good chance that Adam Cole is never wrestling again…the fact is, the thing that ended Adam Cole's career is Kyle Fletcher lariated his head off. That's what happened. And then I had to read this interview about how it was the worst day of Kyle's life. He didn't win the belt, because he lariated the guy's head off and concussed him, and the guy had to vacate the title."

The timing and context of Fletcher's interview comments have drawn considerable criticism. Making the cancellation of his title opportunity the focal point of discussion while Cole faces potential career termination strikes many observers as remarkably self-centered and insensitive to the gravity of the situation. Fletcher's reference to the event as the moment "the rug gets pulled" particularly resonates poorly given that Cole's career may have been permanently ended by the very incident that led to the match cancellation.

Steve Mongo McMichael CTE Diagnosis

Steve "Mongo" McMichael, the Chicago Bears legend who later became a professional wrestler and commentator for WCW, has been posthumously diagnosed with stage 3 chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE). The Concussion & CTE Foundation announced the diagnosis nearly one year after McMichael's death in April 2025 at age 67, following a long battle with ALS, also known as Lou Gehrig's Disease.

McMichael had a distinguished dual career as both an NFL player and professional wrestler. On the football field, he played defensive tackle for the legendary 1985 Chicago Bears team. Later in his career, he transitioned to professional wrestling and became known as a member of the Four Horsemen in WCW, where he also served as a United States Champion and commentator during the promotion's heyday in the 1990s. During his battle with ALS, McMichael achieved a long-awaited dream of being enshrined into the Pro Football Hall of Fame.

The diagnosis of CTE was made by researchers at the Boston University CTE Center. Dr. Ann McKee, director of the center and chief of neuropathology for the VA Boston Healthcare System, explained the severity of McMichael's condition: "Steve McMichael had severe CTE as well as ALS with TDP-43 inclusions typical for ALS in his brainstem and spinal cord. There is strong evidence linking repetitive brain trauma and ALS; in our CTE brain bank, about 6% of individuals with CTE also have ALS."

McMichael's wife, Misty, made the decision to donate her late husband's brain to research and has publicly shared details of his diagnosis to help raise awareness about the long-term effects of repetitive head impacts. Misty McMichael, who was married to Steve for 25 years, issued a statement highlighting the urgent need for further research into the connection between football, ALS, and CTE.

"By sharing Steve's diagnosis, we want to raise awareness of the clear connection between CTE and ALS," Misty McMichael said. "Too many NFL players are developing ALS during life and diagnosed with CTE after death. I donated Steve's brain to inspire new research into the link between them."

Research has established a significant correlation between professional football and ALS development. According to a 2021 study, NFL players are more than four times more likely to develop ALS than the general male population. Several former NFL players who died of ALS and whose brains were studied at Boston University were all diagnosed with both ALS and CTE, suggesting a potential link between repetitive head trauma and the neurodegenerative disease.

McMichael was aware of this research while he was alive and made the conscious decision to participate in efforts to advance scientific understanding of these conditions. His contribution to medical research has been recognized by the Concussion & CTE Foundation, which is working to create better outcomes for athletes across all sports.

Dr. Chris Nowinski, co-founder and CEO of the Concussion & CTE Foundation, praised McMichael's final act of contribution to the medical community. "Steve McMichael was known for his strength, toughness, and larger-than-life presence, but his final act was to give a piece of himself back to the sports community so we might have a chance to save ourselves," Nowinski said. "I appreciate all the former athletes, including many of Steve's '85 Bears teammates, who are raising funds and volunteering to participate in CTE research so we can create a brighter future for athletes everywhere."

To honor McMichael's memory and contributions to research, the Concussion & CTE Foundation will host the Race to End CTE event on April 23 in Chicago, marking the first anniversary of his death. The event will provide opportunities for those who wish to attend and make donations in McMichael's name to support ongoing CTE research initiatives.

Lady Frost Wrestling Career

Lady Frost is built for live television. Despite having only eight years of experience under her belt, Frost is very seasoned when it comes to television wrestling, as she has spent most of her career with promotions like AEW and ROH, TNA, and the NWA. In a recent interview, Frost spoke about production values being different in each company and how wrestlers must adapt to each promotion's style.

"There are always, always differences everywhere you go and you have to adapt to what they want, what they want to put out there. It's their product. They have a vision and you just have to fit in to that as best as you can. TV is TV. If the red light's on, I'm on. Even if I'm in the back, something happens. I want to be someone that you can call at any moment, say, 'Go out, game time.' Can you cut a promo? Can you entertain the audience? That's what it's honestly about. Can you do it at a high level where it can translate to television?" Frost explained.

Frost went on to stress how important it is to have independent experience before wrestling on television. "But I think a lot of people are on television nowadays that have never worked in indie crowd and it's very, very important because you have to read the emotion of the audience. It will translate on TV if you do it well enough. But I think television product has become so prevalent. It's a different style, which is very fun to go back to the indies because it's like, well, there's not really repercussions here. It's just, I'll do whatever and you don't have a ref scream at you like, 'We're going to commercial break.' It's very different pressure wise."

Frost recalled her first match being on live television, which took place against Asuka on an episode of Raw in 2018. "But some people are built for it and I think I definitely am. I broke in, literally first match was on live television. I don't think there's any more pressure bigger than that. I get butterflies still. There are nerves because you want to perform at your best level but it's worked for me and I think that I'm very good at it. So you show up to the next place. What's the job and I'm gonna kill it," Frost said.

Elsewhere in the interview, Frost also recalled interviewing for the American Gladiators reboot.

NJPW G1 Climax 36 Schedule

NJPW has unveiled the full schedule for its upcoming G1 Climax 36 tournament. This prestigious annual round-robin tournament will begin on Saturday, July 11th, at the NOW Arena in Chicago, Illinois, and conclude on Sunday, August 16th, at the Ryogoku Sumo Hall in Tokyo, Japan.

The G1 Climax remains one of the most prestigious tournaments in professional wrestling. The winner of this grueling round-robin competition earns the right to challenge for the IWGP Heavyweight Championship at Wrestle Kingdom in January, one of NJPW's flagship events.

Last year, the tournament was won by Konosuke Takeshita, who made the most of his opportunity by successfully cashing in his title shot early. Takeshita defeated Zack Sabre Jr. to win the IWGP Heavyweight Championship at King of Pro-Wrestling in October, demonstrating the value of the tournament's main prize.

The tournament spans over a month with competition taking place across Japan and the United States. After the opening day in Chicago on July 11, the action moves to Japanese venues including stops in Sapporo, Sendai, Niigata, Tokyo, Osaka, Kagawa, Hiroshima, Fukuoka, Kanagawa, Gunma, Mie, and Shizuoka. The tournament features 19 days of competition, with the finals weekend scheduled for August 15 and 16 at the iconic Ryogoku Sumo Hall in Tokyo, bringing the tournament to its climactic conclusion.

NWA Powerrr New TV Deal

NWA Powerrr has found a new television home with the Sinclair Broadcasting Group. Broadcaster Joe Galli announced the deal during the recent Crockett Cup event, revealing that the flagship NWA show will air on Comet, the sci-fi broadcast network under the Sinclair banner.

The new television partnership marks an important development for the National Wrestling Alliance as it continues to expand its media presence and reach new audiences. Comet joins a growing list of platforms that have hosted NWA content over the years, following the promotion's previous homes on various streaming services and broadcast networks.

Prior to this deal, NWA Powerrr aired on the Roku Sports Channel and has previously been streamed on YouTube, Twitter, The CW App, and FITE TV. This new arrangement with Sinclair Broadcasting represents a return to traditional broadcast television for the promotion.

The partnership with Sinclair is particularly notable given the company's history in professional wrestling. ROH programming previously aired on CW and MyNetworkTV affiliates during its time with Sinclair.

In addition to the television deal announcement, the NWA is aiming for a return to Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, in July, signaling the promotion's commitment to rebuilding its presence in major markets and continuing its growth trajectory.

Can't Knock The Hustle: My Pro Wrestling "I Did Not Care For The Godfather" Unpopular Opinions (Part Two)

ROBERT DUVALL!!! Long-time viewers of Family Guy will already get the reference in the title of this column, as well as the name drop of the late, great Robert Duvall to kick things off. Even if you aren't a fan of Family Guy, there's a chance you understand the reference, anyway, as "I Did Not Care For The Godfather" has been a meme and social media engagement template for a while. For everyone else, the line is from an episode of Family Guy that saw the entire Griffin family on the verge of death. In a situation like that, what better time for Peter Griffin to reveal to his family that he has been carrying a secret… he has never been a fan of the 1972 film, "The Godfather." The revelation stuns everyone, as the movie is widely considered to be one of the greatest and most influential pieces of cinema ever created.

I've been writing pro wrestling columns for about 18-and-a-half years now. At various points in that 18-and-a-half year span, I've revealed some of my own "I Did Not Care For The Godfather" unpopular opinions, and frankly, I think it's time to do so once again. It's time to get my unpopular opinion on. As always, these are in no particular order. Let's have some fun, shall we?

Stephanie McMahon's Podcast Is Entertaining: While I wouldn't say that "What's Your Story?" is one of the best podcasts I've ever listened to, I will gladly admit that I find myself being entertained far more often than not when I catch episodes, largely dependent on who the guests are. It hasn't been "cool" to say anything positive about Stephanie or anyone involved with the McMahon family in any way for a long time. Steph and her friend and co-host, Elyse Dudzinski, come across as very warm, genuine, and motherly with their guests. That helps them to stand out, especially in the largely testosterone-driven landscape that is pro wrestling podcasts.

One example I really like is the recent episode that saw Oba Femi as their guest. Oba made multiple mentions of the close relationship he has always had with his mother, and you could hear how much that meant to both Steph and Elyse, who are mothers themselves. Like I said, the show stands out in a land of podcasts hosted by people like Cody Rhodes, Jim Cornette, Eric Bischoff, Dutch Mantell, Bruce Prichard, Jeff Jarrett, Kevin Nash, and on and on and on and on and on.

Logan Paul Should Win A World Title Before Bron Breakker, Oba Femi, Kyle Fletcher, or Anyone Else People Feel Is "Up Next": Now, this doesn't mean that I feel Logan is "better" than any of the other names I mentioned there. I just feel that Logan Paul has positioned himself in such a way that he would make for quite the story if he reaches the top of the mountain. From the time he started wrestling, I made my thoughts very clear… he was a future World Champion if he ever decided to dedicate himself to wrestling and become a full-time performer. Slowly, but surely, he started working more and more, and now, he is absolutely, positively a full-time performer. He continues getting better and better in the ring, and he remains a heat magnet. He's ready.

This isn't a knock on Oba Femi. I'm just waiting to see how he does on a huge stage against a marquee name before I truly call for him to be THE guy. In a couple weeks, I could be standing outside of Paul Levesque's house with a Fathead of Oba's face in one hand and a boombox blasting "The Man" by Aloe Blacc in the other hand. For now, though, I'm in full "wait and see" mode.

This isn't a knock on Bron Breakker. My past columns are littered with instances of me saying that he's going to be a World Champion and a WrestleMania main event player moving forward. I just don't see him as a complete package like Logan Paul is. As physically gifted and as intense as Bron is, he continues to have problems harnessing his energy properly at times. He'll still have moments where he's moving at 100 miles per hour when perhaps 92 miles per hour would do, and he'll slip up somewhere because of it.

Former WWE Star Wants To Start Wrestling Stable Of Ex-NFL Players

Former WWE star Bishop Dyer recently shared his thoughts on the growing debate between WWE stars and NFL players. The discussion started after Tom Brady made comments on Logan Paul's podcast, calling WWE more of a "cute" show, which sparked reactions quickly. Paul fired back by offering a $1 million boxing challenge and calling out NFL names like Myles Garrett. Soon after, former players like Le'Veon Bell showed interest, but WWE reportedly stepped in and shut things down. Now, Dyer has added his voice to the conversation by looking at the debate from both sides.

Speaking with Denise Salcedo, Dyer explained his thoughts in detail and even suggested forming a group of NFL players in wrestling. "We have NFL, we're better than wrestlers all over the world, and I think it would be a cool kind of group that you could form, because it would irritate everybody. As you see, it's done, like the controversial statements of athletes and non-athletes, and it's all scripted, and there's some truth to all of it on both sides," Dyer stated.

Dyer acknowledged that NFL players are extremely athletic but emphasized that professional football operates on a different level than professional wrestling. "In the wrestling world, the power of the pin controls the outcome, where in NFL it's the actual ability of each athlete to keep their job. You have to be the best, and that's not the way it is in wrestling. You just have to either have the right person in your corner or you have to be the best. There are multiple things," he explained.

He went further in his critique, pointing out that professional wrestling allows for less meritocratic advancement than the NFL. "There's been plenty of people that we've seen through WWE who are not the best in-ring performers, who are not good at promos, and they're on TV every week. That wouldn't be the case if that was the NFL. They would be out of there in a blink of an eye because they're not the best. They just have a writer who loves them, or Hunter who loves them, or Tony who likes them. It doesn't matter. There is some legitimate favoritism, where in NFL, billions of dollars are at stake for wins and losses. So you have your best players playing," Dyer said.

Dyer then made his most striking claim, stating that the top 10 wrestlers in the world would "get smashed" if they faced the top 10 NFL players in a legitimate physical confrontation. These comments align with what Dyer previously stated during an earlier interview, where he said that "95 percent of NFL players would run over most wrestlers" in a real fight.

Smoke Break Psychology: The Art of the Story

Smoke Break Psychology: The Art of the Story

By That Donnie Back From the Break

Yeah, I've been gone. And not just from wrestling — from everything. I didn't take a break; I took a blackout. Socials? Deleted the apps. Notifications? Off. Group chats? Muted like they owed me money. I didn't even doom-scroll, which in 2026 basically qualifies as a spiritual retreat. I didn't disappear because of wrestling burnout. I disappeared because my brain hit the eject button and forgot to warn me first. Sometimes you don't need a break — you need a full system reboot. And the only thing that made sense during that blackout was stepping away from the noise, lighting up, and letting the world shrink down to something I could actually hold.

And when I finally came back to wrestling, it wasn't because I missed the moves. It was because I missed the stories. The long arcs. The psychology. The stuff you only notice when you're quiet enough — or high enough — to actually see it. That's what this column is. Not a recap. Not a rant. A return.

The High Road to the Long Game

There's a funny thing that happens when you watch wrestling high: you stop seeing the moves and start seeing the motives. You stop seeing the spots and start seeing the structure. You stop seeing the "this week on NXT" and start seeing the "oh, this is chapter seven of a book they didn't tell anyone they were writing."

And if you've been paying attention — really paying attention — the NXT women's division has quietly become the best long-form story in wrestling. Not because they're trying to be prestige TV, but because they accidentally stumbled into it by doing something revolutionary: They let things take time. I know. Shocking. In 2026, where fans want a heel turn every Tuesday and a title change every Thursday, NXT had the audacity to say, "What if… we didn't?" And somehow, in the middle of a wrestling landscape addicted to instant gratification, they built a division that rewards patience, investment, and — dare I say it — emotional intelligence. And yes, I'm saying that about a show where a woman once tried to murder another woman with a parking lot door. Growth is real.

The Era That Was Never About the Champion

Let's start with the funniest irony in the whole division: Jaycee Jane's title reign was never actually about Jaycee Jane. Don't get me wrong — she played her part. She strutted, she sneered, she talked like she was auditioning for the role of "Mean Girl #2" in a CW reboot. But the real purpose of her reign wasn't dominance. It was infrastructure.

Jaycee was the scaffolding holding up the division while everyone else found their roles. Lola Vice was sharpening the backfist heard 'round the Performance Center. Kendall Grey was becoming the division's emotional anchor without anyone noticing. Sol Ruca was the athletic cheat code keeping the midcard from collapsing into chaos. Tatum Paxley was quietly becoming the most unsettling character in the company. Zaria was building a résumé like she was applying for a job in HR instead of wrestling. Jaycee's job wasn't to be the star. Her job was to be the center of gravity. And she did it well — even if half the time she looked like she was one bad promo away from blaming the crowd for not understanding her "art."

The Fall That Wasn't a Fall

Stand & Deliver wasn't a title match. It was a controlled demolition. Everything that happened was intentional: Kendall hits the moonsault. Jaycee hits the Rolling Encore. Lola hits the backfist. Jaycee gets pinned. Kendall isn't involved. Lola becomes champion. The division resets instantly. That's not chaos. That's choreography.

Lola Vice: The Champion Who Earned It

Lola didn't get the rocket. She got the grind. She didn't get the "you're the chosen one" treatment. She got the "prove it every week" treatment. And she did. The strikes got sharper. The presence got bigger. The confidence stopped being a gimmick and became a truth. By the time she won the title, it didn't feel like a surprise. It felt like inevitability.

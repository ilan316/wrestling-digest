🔥 AEW Double or Nothing 2026 Fallout

AEW Double or Nothing 2026 Recap and Analysis

All Elite Wrestling presented Double or Nothing on pay-per-view this past weekend, an event that serves as a milestone on the AEW calendar, marking the anniversary of the first official event that took place seven years ago. The 2026 edition was held at Louis Armstrong Stadium in New York and sold out with more than 15,000 fans in attendance. The event reflected both the consistent positives and negatives of the AEW product, though the effectiveness of the main event angle avoided some of the usual pitfalls that have plagued the promotion.

The opening match featured Adam Copeland and Christian Cage versus FTR for the AEW World Tag Team Championships in an I Quit match. This contest served as the payoff to an extended feud between the teams. In a vacuum, this was a very good contest structured with solid ring psychology and logical progression. However, the match was heavily laden with extreme elements including barbed wire, tables, ladders, chairs, a spiked two by four, and a flaming table spot toward the conclusion that saw Cash Wheeler and Stokely Cutler crash through it. FTR, still probably the best tag team in the business, and Copeland and Christian, who maximize everything they do in the ring despite their age, delivered quality performances. After a pair of submissions and an assist from the spiked two by four, Dax Harwood said I Quit, allowing the Copeland and Christian team to win the titles.

While the match itself was a tremendous payoff, a significant concern existed with its placement on the card. The match opened a four-hour broadcast and featured nearly every extreme stipulation and high-risk element available in the opening twenty minutes. The ring was partially disassembled, and the audience witnessed barbed wire, fire, tables, ladders, and chairs immediately. This created a problem for the remaining card, as there was very little escalation available for subsequent matches. A table was used in the main event more than three and a half hours later, but without the fire element, making it difficult for the crowd to react with the same intensity. Historically, ECW, despite pushing the envelope with extreme content, strategically placed the most dangerous spots like flaming tables in the middle of shows to allow for a natural reset or intermission before more mayhem continued. By opening with such a high-risk match, AEW inadvertently diluted the legitimacy of the risks other performers took on the rest of the card and made subsequent matches less effective by comparison.

Adam Copeland reflected on his tag team title win on Instagram following the event, noting that he and Christian Cage accomplished something they hadn't done in 25 years. In his post, Copeland mentioned that Christian had been retired for seven years while he had been retired for nine years, but they fought to get their careers back. He stated that last night made it all worth it, praising the New York crowd for coming out in full voice and supplying one of his favorite nights of his career. He also mentioned winning the title against FTR, one of the best teams ever, and expressed appreciation for having nights like this so deep into his career with his best friend. Copeland humorously noted that he blew his pants out during the match and encouraged people who missed the PPV to watch it on AEW.

The Konosuke Takeshita versus Kazuchika Okada match was absolutely tremendous. This was a modern match done right, featuring the fast-paced, hard-hitting style expected from two New Japan alumni, but structured in a fashion that allowed the audience to digest what they were watching with deliberate moments of storytelling throughout.

In the AEW Women's World Championship match, Thekla retained her title in a four-way contest that also included Jamie Hayter and Kris Statlander. During the match, Statlander appeared to have the match won until she was hit over the back with a kendo stick by Hikaru Shida. The two then got into a brawl, and Thekla took advantage of the chaos to pin Statlander and retain her championship. The attack set up a new singles rivalry between Statlander and Shida.

Following the event, Kris Statlander addressed Hikaru Shida's attack in a digital exclusive, challenging her to a Philly Street Fight on AEW Dynamite. Statlander said that when she got cracked from behind with that kendo stick, she knew it was Shida. She stated that the hit didn't hurt her but that she liked it and wanted to do it all over again, noting that they would be in the perfect place to do so on Wednesday night in Philadelphia with a street fight stipulation.

Hikaru Shida had previously formed an alliance with Kris Statlander following her return to AEW television on April 1. The two had recently begun to develop tension following their loss to Divine Dominion on Dynamite. The dynamic shift between the former allies into active competitors set up an interesting new direction for both performers heading forward.

MJF made headlines ahead of the Double or Nothing event by confirming that he owns the trademarks to both "Maxwell Jacob Friedman" and "MJF" during an interview with Tony Maglio of The Hollywood Reporter. When asked who owns the rights to his character, MJF stated that he has it fully trademarked with both options. This ownership matters significantly in professional wrestling, where performers can spend years building a name, catchphrase, or character only to find out the company controls the rights once the relationship ends. By securing these trademarks, MJF has ensured that no matter where his career takes him, the name attached to his success remains his.

According to AEW, Early Access Premium Seating began on May 26, with general public ticket sales officially opening on June 1 through AEWTix.com. AEW President Tony Khan stated that "Montreal's one of the greatest wrestling cities in the world. There's such a rich history of professional wrestling. This will be a special event. It's the first-ever AEW Redemption and it's also the first-ever AEW pay-per-view in Montreal." The announcement marks AEW's continued expansion of its Canadian footprint over the last two years, as the company has been running television tapings and special events across multiple provinces while building its international touring presence.

However, the manner in which the announcement was made has drawn criticism from observers. The announcement was made through Toronto media outlets rather than through local Montreal media, which some view as a significant marketing misstep. This approach is particularly problematic given that Montreal and Toronto maintain one of the biggest rivalries in sports history and in Canada in general. Announcing an AEW event in Montreal while making the announcement through Toronto media may have inadvertently alienated local fans and signaled a lack of confidence in the Montreal market.

The criticism extends further regarding awareness and reach. For fans who follow AEW but don't actively monitor wrestling social media, there is a possibility they may not even know that AEW is coming to town if no media outlets in their local market covered the announcement. Given AEW's previous struggles drawing crowds in Montreal, Khan may have been hoping that the Toronto announcement would encourage Toronto fans to make the six-hour trip to Montreal to watch the show, using the familiar market as a launching point for ticket sales in the adjacent region.

The company has a significant challenge ahead. This is a strong WWE town, and it will be hard to convince fans to attend a show from another company, especially when that company doesn't have the same exposure that WWE or, for that matter, TNA has in the area. AEW will have to do considerable advertising in Montreal to move tickets for this event.

Ultimately, this represents a major gamble for Tony Khan by returning to Montreal with a brand-new PPV. There is hope that he will be able to fill the Bell Centre, because if the crowd turns out to be as small as it was the last time AEW came to Montreal, it will look really bad on television, and that's the last thing the company wants when launching a new PPV event.

Kenny Omega AEW World Championship Victory

Kenny Omega's return to championship glory represents one of professional wrestling's most inspiring comeback stories. On July 8 at AEW Dynamite: Beach Break, Omega captured the AEW World Championship for the second time in his career, defeating MJF to begin a new reign. This victory marks his first world title run since 2020-2021 and completes a remarkable journey back to the top following a lengthy absence caused by diverticulitis, a serious health condition that threatened both his life and wrestling career.

For The Young Bucks, Omega's championship victory carried profound significance beyond just another title change. Matt and Nick Jackson have been close friends with Omega throughout his AEW tenure, and his triumph represented validation of his perseverance through an incredibly difficult period. Despite the emotional importance of the moment, neither Young Buck was physically present at Beach Break. Matt Jackson was enjoying a family vacation in Hawaii while Nick Jackson was spending time with his wife and children in Nevada. However, their absence from the venue did not diminish their engagement with the event.

Matt Jackson made certain preparations to ensure he could witness the match live, fully charging his phone and securing cell service while in Hawaii so he could watch the championship bout. Following Omega's victory, Matt spoke about his longtime friend's performance and what it meant given everything Omega had overcome. "It was great to see Big Match Kenny do what he does best," Matt said. "He creates an atmosphere that no other current wrestler can. When he's in his element, there's this inevitable feeling that you're about to watch something that'll be remembered forever."

Beyond praising Omega's in-ring abilities, Matt reflected on the emotional weight of the moment. "I was just super happy for him. He's been through so much and to see him come back from a near-death experience to be at the top of the mountain again, it was truly inspiring." This sentiment encapsulates how Omega's peers view his achievement, not merely as a professional accomplishment but as a personal triumph over adversity.

Omega's championship reign is already taking shape with several high-profile challengers lined up. He is set to make his first title defense against TNT Champion Kevin Knight in the main event of AEW Redemption, the inaugural pay-per-view of that name, scheduled for July 26 in Montreal, Quebec, Canada. Looking further ahead, Omega is expected to defend his championship against Will Ospreay in a headline match at All In 2026 on August 30.

The Young Bucks themselves are positioned prominently in the storyline surrounding Omega's championship reign. At the Redemption pay-per-view, The Young Bucks are scheduled to compete in a tag team match against Will Ospreay and Jon Moxley. The narrative suggests that while Omega and Ospreay maintain a friendship, The Young Bucks are working to convince Omega that Ospreay cannot be trusted following his association with the Death Riders faction.

Young Bucks Reflect on Leaving TNA

Former AEW EVPs The Young Bucks (Matt & Nick Jackson) opened up and reflected on parting ways with TNA Wrestling over a decade ago. After signing with TNA in 2009, The Young Bucks were renamed Generation Me. Following a brief stint at the promotion, Matt & Nick Jackson parted ways with it in 2011 over creative and financial differences. After their match against Eric Young and Shark Boy at TNA Destination X on July 10, 2011, the duo requested their release from the promotion.

"Call it blind confidence, or young arrogance, we just saw something in ourselves that they did not, and we had the courage to make a change," Matt Jackson told Justin Barrasso of Undisputed while reflecting on their departure from the promotion 15 years ago. The brothers' decision to leave was rooted in their belief in their own potential, even when the company did not see the same vision for their careers.

However, their departure did not mark the end of their relationship with the company. The Young Bucks returned to TNA in 2013 at One Night Only, where they faced off against Christopher Daniels and Frankie Kazarian. They again made an appearance at TNA Against All Odds 2021 during the AEW-TNA partnership years, demonstrating that despite their initial split, both parties remained open to future collaborations.

MAIN EVENT DELIVERS AS D'ANGELO RETAINS NXT TITLE IN STREET FIGHT

Tony D'Angelo successfully defended the NXT Championship against Naraku in a brutal Street Fight that served as the main event of WWE NXT on July 21, 2026. The match started dramatically as Naraku hit D'Angelo with red mist from the opening bell, leaving the champion temporarily blinded. This early advantage allowed Naraku to assault D'Angelo with a series of chair shots, steel step slams, and a toss into the nearby LED board. Naraku continued his offense with a suplex onto a stack of chairs, building momentum in the early portion of the contest.

D'Angelo eventually regained his footing and rinsed his eyes out with water, beginning his comeback when Naraku followed him to the outside. The champion sent his challenger crashing into the barricade and the match spilled into the backstage area before both competitors made their way back to the ring. The action remained intense and physical throughout, with both men utilizing various weapons including ladders, tables, kendo sticks, crowbars, and trash cans to gain an advantage.

In the closing moments, D'Angelo set up a table in the ring and placed a second table on the entrance. After Naraku mounted another comeback assault, D'Angelo fought back and blinded Naraku with the red mist, turning the tables on his challenger. The champion then unloaded several kendo stick shots to the blinded Naraku before lifting him and driving him through a table with a chokeslam, securing the pinfall victory and retaining his title. REINA VOLCAN IMPRESSES IN NXT TELEVISION DEBUT

Reina Volcan, the former Nikki Blackheart, made her NXT television in-ring debut on the July 21 episode against WWE LFG Season 2 winner Skylar Raye and immediately made an impression with a dominant performance. From the opening bell, Volcan's strength and athleticism were evident, as she controlled much of the contest against her opponent.

The match began with a lock-up that Volcan won decisively, shoving Raye to the mat with ease. Volcan followed up with a scoop slam and a powerful shoulder tackle, maintaining her control of the match. She transitioned into a quick scoop powerslam for a near fall, continuing to dictate the pace of the bout. Despite Raye's efforts to create openings and mount comeback attempts, Volcan's power proved to be too much for the LFG competitor.

🔥 AJ Styles Retirement and Post-Wrestling Comments

AJ Styles Reflects on Retirement, Legacy, and Life After In-Ring Competition

Six months have passed since AJ Styles stepped away from professional wrestling following his loss to GUNTHER at the 2026 Royal Rumble event held in Saudi Arabia. During an extensive interview with Chris Van Vliet for his Insight podcast, the 49-year-old wrestling icon discussed his retirement, his post-wrestling endeavors, his family's involvement in wrestling, and various moments from his legendary career.

Regarding the possibility of wrestling again, Styles has maintained a measured stance. When asked directly if he would ever step back into the ring, he responded by acknowledging the commonplace nature of wrestling comebacks. "That's right, I know how wrestling retirements are, but I have said this before, and I'll say it again, usually we're granted one comeback for one match. I'm not saying it's gonna happen. I'm just saying usually it's granted. Never say never." Styles indicated that one scenario that could potentially lure him back would be the opportunity to wrestle his own sons in a tag team capacity. "Listen, the opportunity to wrestle with my son or sons, that'd be pretty cool," he said. However, he emphasized that any such comeback would likely be limited to a single match, as is customary for retirement returns in professional wrestling.

Styles chose to retire at the Royal Rumble rather than waiting for WrestleMania, a decision rooted in personal significance. He explained that while he had originally planned to retire at WrestleMania 41, his foot injury recovery pushed the timeline back. After recovering and renegotiating his contract to extend through 2026, he decided to end his career at the Royal Rumble because it was the event where he debuted with WWE in 2016. "That wasn't my pay-per-view or PLE. That's not where I started. For me to be able to debut and then end it at the Royal Rumble—and I don't care that it was in Saudi Arabia—it's still the Royal Rumble. That meant something to me. I know there are people who have WrestleMania as their big PLE, but mine was the Royal Rumble."

The decision to have GUNTHER be his final opponent was not Styles' choice but rather part of WWE's creative direction. Styles explained the logic behind the booking, noting that GUNTHER had previously retired both Goldberg and John Cena. "That wasn't up to me. It was up to the story. He had retired Goldberg, he had retired John Cena. Why not retire AJ Styles?" Styles initially questioned some aspects of his retirement arrangement, particularly when he discovered that GUNTHER would also compete in the Royal Rumble match itself later that evening. However, he later acknowledged that his concerns were unfounded and attributed his emotional state to the weight of knowing his wrestling career was ending. "I just was questioning everything. A normal, sane AJ Styles would not question this; it would just be what it is. When reality is, nobody thought about that; nobody did, nobody cared, even when GUNTHER came out at the Royal Rumble and got beat."

The aftermath of his final match proved deeply emotional for Styles. As he made his way backstage, he discovered that most of the roster had gathered to greet him. Abyss, his former TNA colleague, pulled him in for a warm embrace along with numerous other wrestlers and staff members. The overwhelming emotional response prompted Styles to seek a moment of solitude. He found an empty storage room away from the main activity where luggage was being kept, intending to change clothes in quiet. However, the door did not shut properly. "I kind of got back there. I ran in there, and I thought I closed the door behind me, but apparently it didn't. It just bounced right wide open. I had to stand there for a second and just gather my thoughts and shed a couple tears because AJ Styles is no longer wrestling again. That was tough, but it was okay."

Shane Helms, known as The Hurricane during his WWE career, was the first person to enter the storage room and found Styles in this vulnerable state. Rather than adding to the overwhelming congratulations, Helms simply offered a knowing hug, having experienced a similar career trajectory himself. "I just needed Shane Helms being the first one to come in there and he knew what was up. I think he's been through that somewhat before, kind of gave me a hug. I just needed to get that out, and I needed that hug from Shane, and that was okay. It was emotional."

Regarding his unexpected match against John Cena during Cena's retirement tour, Styles expressed doubt that he was originally scheduled for that bout. "I'm pretty sure I wasn't supposed to wrestle him. I don't care what anybody says. I don't know who called it, but I know that I wasn't in the picture. Something else needed to happen. I don't know what that was, someone's gonna have to tell that story because I don't know." Styles acknowledged feeling like an afterthought in WWE despite his ten-year tenure with the company. "Nothing, never a talk of me having a match with John. Listen, I just assume I'm not that guy. I'm not that guy again. Sometimes I feel like even though I've been in WWE for 10 years, I'm not a WWE guy. That was just one of those times."

WWE presented Styles with a meaningful parting gift following his final match. After losing the WWE Tag Team Championship with Dragon Lee, Styles asked for the personalized side plates that had been attached to the title belt. He was initially intending to request only the plates, but WWE officials surprised him by giving him the entire championship belt with his plates still attached. "After we lost the titles, I went to one of the guys who takes off the plates because when you win, you have your own plates on there. I had the P1 plates. I knew that I wasn't winning another championship, and I was like, 'Hey, can I have those plates since you're not going to use them anymore?' They went and asked, and they came back to me and go, 'Here. They gave me the title.' Styles emphasized that this gesture held special significance because he is not typically someone who requests championship belts as keepsakes. "I have the title with the plates on at home. I just thought that was a really cool gift to give me, even though I'm not a guy who asks for belts. I don't do that. They were willing to hand that over to me. It was a really cool moment."

Since retiring, Styles has taken on a new role with WWE that keeps him actively involved with the company. He serves as a talent scout and contributes to the WWE ID program, which identifies and develops international wrestling prospects. Beyond his WWE responsibilities, Styles has launched an independent podcast called "Phenomenally Retro" that operates outside the WWE umbrella, allowing him creative freedom in his projects. He emphasized the importance of staying active post-retirement. "It's kind of just part of the lifestyle. I mean, it's a good thing to be busy. I think when you retire, you die early. A lot of people, I've seen that. Something happens. You stop moving. So getting in there with my son and wrestling around and doing things like that at the Performance Center is a big deal to me."

Regarding his legacy, Styles offered an assessment that may surprise some observers. He stated that his accomplishments in TNA Wrestling ultimately shaped his career more significantly than his WWE tenure. "The legacy in WWE is just not as great as the one in TNA. It was just because of the time in which I was in TNA. Being at the company that you helped build up a reputation of really great stuff, I'll take that with me. I'll take that legacy." Styles credited his TNA and New Japan Pro-Wrestling experiences with developing his wrestling identity, which he then brought to WWE. "At the end of TNA is where I really found out who AJ Styles was and what that looked like. He credited his independent streak and resilience, noting that he would not have thrived in an environment where backstage bullying occurred. "I don't think I would have made it. I'm not good with getting bullied. I don't do well. I fight back. It's just a redneck thing. I don't think I would have done well." He also acknowledged that the wrestling landscape changed significantly when Daniel Bryan proved that average-sized wrestlers could be main event draws. "Daniel Bryan was the first guy to make smaller, not small guys, but just average guys look good. He had the opportunity of doing what most didn't, especially at that time."

If you could see my first match and his first match, holy moly, he's day one better than me. But I didn't have a guy coaching me that had done all the things that I have done."

His focus as a coach is on teaching Avery the fundamentals and the subtle details of professional wrestling that most wrestlers overlook. "I hope that he does at some point, but he's not ready yet. My job is to make him ready and teach him the little things that we want to see as coaches. Styles explained that WWE scouts look beyond flashy high-flying moves and focus on how wrestlers execute the transitions and basic movements between spots. "There's been plenty of guys that even myself have went for tryouts, and they say, 'Hey, we don't care about the high-flying moves, we just want to see what you can do.' The pressure of carrying the Styles name in wrestling is something Styles has discussed with his son. It's finding out what's best for him at the time and where he's at because it's like take the things that we know and let's perfect them so that we can move on after perfection."

Styles' approach to coaching his son emphasizes perfecting the fundamentals before moving on to more advanced techniques. He uses the analogy of test scores, indicating that he wants Avery to score above 90 on every fundamental before progressing. "I don't want you to make a 70. At 19 years old, Styles believes his son has time to develop physically while gaining valuable in-ring experience. "He's 19. He's not even a man yet. Looking forward, Styles appears content with his decision to retire while he could still perform at a high level. He emphasized that stepping away before his physical abilities severely declined was important to him. His post-retirement life is proving fulfilling through his work with WWE's talent development, his podcast, and his involvement with his family members pursuing wrestling careers.

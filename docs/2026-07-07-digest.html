🔥 Jake Roberts AEW Departure

Jake "The Snake" Roberts, one of professional wrestling's most legendary and influential figures, has announced that his time with AEW has come to an end. During an interview with legendary wrestling journalist Bill Apter that was recorded in May at 80s Wrestling Con in Parsippany, New Jersey, Roberts confirmed he is no longer under contract with Tony Khan's promotion, bringing his six-year association with the company to a close.

Roberts first appeared in AEW during the company's early years in 2019 before officially joining in 2020. He quickly became the on-screen manager for Lance Archer and remained a recurring presence on AEW television in various roles. Beyond his television responsibilities, Roberts also became involved with AEW Together, the promotion's community outreach initiative, starting in 2023. He also managed La Faccion Ingobernable as his responsibilities evolved. However, his television appearances became increasingly less frequent in recent years before confirming his departure.

Despite his exit, there is no indication of any bad blood between Roberts and the promotion. When asked about his experience working for Tony Khan, Roberts had nothing but praise. "Loved it," Roberts said of his time with AEW. "I just finished up working for him. I'm no longer employed by AEW." He was equally complimentary when reflecting on Khan specifically, stating "[Tony Khan] is awesome, really is — breath of fresh air."

Roberts, who recently celebrated 14 years of sobriety, acknowledged that his current focus has shifted outside the professional wrestling world. The 71-year-old explained that much of his attention these days is devoted to spending time with his grandchildren. He also noted his passion for his 1965 Cadillac convertible. Additionally, Roberts admitted that he hasn't been keeping up with professional wrestling closely over the past year, choosing instead to focus on personal interests and family.

Health-wise, Roberts has experienced significant improvements in recent years. At the start of 2026, he underwent successful hip surgery. While he has COPD and emphysema, his condition has improved substantially over the past few years to the point where he no longer requires supplemental oxygen. His remarkable personal comeback has been well-documented, particularly in the 2015 documentary "The Resurrection of Jake The Snake," which chronicled his journey to sobriety and recovery.

A member of the WWE Hall of Fame's Class of 2014, Roberts remains one of wrestling's most respected and influential figures. His legendary promos, signature DDT maneuver, and extraordinary ability to captivate audiences established him as a cornerstone of multiple major promotions throughout his career. His most notable work came during his time in the then-WWF, where he engaged in legendary feuds with Ricky "The Dragon" Steamboat, Randy "Macho Man" Savage, The Honky Tonk Man, and Andre the Giant, among numerous other wrestling icons from wrestling's golden era in the 1980s.

While discussing the current state of professional wrestling, Roberts offered some critical observations about the modern product. He criticized the industry's "lack of characters" and noted that talent is "wasting so much" in the ring. Roberts advocated for a return to fundamentals, stating "They need to go back to simple wrestling. Not so much high flying. If you're going to do something high flying, they need to make it count." These comments reflect his perspective as a veteran who built his legendary career on psychology, character work, and storytelling rather than acrobatic displays.

With Roberts officially exiting AEW, another chapter in the company's early history comes to a close. Unlike his famous DDT maneuver, this wrestling ending did not land with a thud but rather with mutual respect and positive regard. The Hall of Famer leaves on excellent terms with Khan and the promotion, proving that professional relationships in wrestling can end amicably. The news of Roberts' departure was officially revealed when Bill Apter uploaded the interview clip to his YouTube channel on June 5, though the interview itself had been conducted several weeks prior in May.

🔥 AEW All Out 2026 Chicago Announcement

AEW is bringing one of its signature pay-per-views back to familiar territory in 2026. AEW President Tony Khan announced on Monday that AEW All Out 2026 will be held at NOW Arena in Hoffman Estates, Illinois, on Saturday, September 26. Tickets will go on sale to the general public on Monday, July 13, with a VIP pre-sale beginning on July 7 and the standard pre-sale launching on July 9. Early Access Premium Seating begins on Tuesday, July 7, with fans able to sign up as an AEW Insider through AEWTix.com to receive presale access and additional ticket information.

The announcement marks a return to the Chicago area for All Out after the event broke tradition in 2025 by emanating from Toronto, Ontario, Canada. Prior to last year's show, every edition of All Out had taken place in the Chicago/Hoffman Estates market dating back to AEW's inaugural year. The only previous exception was 2020 during the pandemic era. The NOW Arena has hosted All Out four times previously and recently hosted Worlds End 2025. The venue will also host NJPW's G1 Climax 36 on Saturday, July 11.

While the Chicago-area tradition is returning, the event will once again be held later than its original Labor Day weekend slot. The last time All Out was held over the Labor Day weekend was in 2024. Speaking with Q101 Radio, Khan explained that the scheduling change allows AEW more time to build toward the event following All In in London, which takes place at the end of August during a holiday weekend. "I want to make sure that the fans get the best possible experience at All Out," Khan said. "So that means giving four weeks between these pay-per-views to fully build up and have about a month between AEW All In and AEW All Out. So we're going to have a huge All Out. It's one of our biggest events of the year, and the fans who have been to the All Out shows around Chicago know this pay-per-view has an incredible track record of delivering great results."

This announcement comes as AEW has confirmed a busy schedule of pay-per-views throughout the summer and fall. The updated AEW PPV schedule now includes Redemption on Sunday, July 26 at Bell Centre in Montreal, Quebec, Canada; All In on Sunday, August 30 at Wembley Stadium in London, England; and All Out on Saturday, September 26 at NOW Arena in Hoffman Estates, Illinois.

The announcement has generated significant fan discussion online, with some wrestling fans questioning why AEW continues to run the same markets repeatedly, given All Out's exclusive history in the Chicago area. Additionally, the logo design for All Out 2026, which is based on the Chicago flag featuring blue bars and red stars, has sparked speculation among wrestling observers. The imagery is closely associated with CM Punk, the Second City Saint who built much of his legacy in Chicago. There have been whispers that Punk may not be entirely satisfied at WWE, with some speculation about a possible return to AEW. The timing of the announcement and the CM Punk-coded graphic have led some to believe AEW may be working toward such a storyline, though Khan's official statements have focused purely on the event's logistics and the territory's strong track record for producing memorable wrestling shows.

🔥 AEW Dynamite Viewership

AEW Dynamite on July 1, 2026 posted mixed viewership results as the company followed up on Forbidden Door programming. The show drew 634,000 total viewers on TBS, representing an increase of 18,000 viewers or 2.9% from the previous week's 616,000 viewers. This marked the second largest audience for the show since June 3rd. However, the demographic performance told a different story, as Dynamite averaged a 0.09 rating in the 18-49 demo, down 0.01 points or 10% from the prior week's 0.10 rating. This tied the second lowest rating the show has achieved in that category since April 29th.

The episode faced significant external competition that likely contributed to the mixed results. Dynamite went head-to-head with a US World Cup Soccer match between Team USA and Bosnia & Herzegovina that averaged between 24.4 and over 36 million viewers across FOX and Telemundo, making it a formidable ratings competitor. Despite this competition, Dynamite tied for sixth on cable charts in terms of 18-49 rating and placed 13th in total viewers on cable for the night. The show was simulcast on HBO Max, though viewership information for that platform was not available.

Week-over-week comparisons showed Dynamite performed slightly above its recent averages in total viewers. The 634,000 viewers represented a 2.8% increase compared to the 10-week average of 616,600 viewers. However, the 0.09 demo rating represented an 18.2% decline from that same 10-week average of 0.11. Looking at the most recent 11 weeks of data on TBS, viewership has ranged from a low of 542,000 on June 10th to a high of 704,000 on May 27th, while the 18-49 demo has fluctuated between 0.08 and 0.15.

Year-over-year comparisons painted a starkly different picture. Compared to July 2nd, 2025, when Dynamite drew 584,000 viewers with a 0.16 demo rating, the July 1st, 2026 episode showed a substantial year-over-year change of up 8.6% in total viewership but down 43.8% in the crucial 18-49 demographic. This significant demographic decline represents a concerning trend despite the viewership growth, suggesting that while more total people are watching, the younger core audience that advertisers prioritize has substantially shrunk in the year-over-year comparison.

The episode featured several high-profile segments that drove engagement on YouTube. The most viewed video clip, featuring Andrade El Idolo being destroyed by his former family, accumulated 142,000 views. Title Changes Historical

July 6th and 7th stand out as historically significant dates in professional wrestling when it comes to championship title changes. According to wrestlingdata.com, approximately 229 major and notable title changes have occurred on July 6th throughout wrestling history, while July 7th has seen around 246 such changes, making both dates exceptionally active for the sport.

The earliest documented July 6th title change dates back to 1985 when Dusty Rhodes won the NWA World Television Title and Tito Santana captured the WWF Intercontinental Title. That same year began a tradition of significant title changes on this date. In 1995, Blue Panther, Fuerza Guerrera, and Psicosis won the Mexican National Trios Titles, adding another layer to the date's historical significance.

One of the most remarkable occurrences in wrestling history happened on July 6th, 1997, when El Samurai won four different titles on the same day. He captured the NWA World Welterweight Title, the WWF World Light Heavyweight Title, the J-CROWN Octuple Unified Title, and the IWGP Junior Heavyweight Title, an extraordinary achievement that stands as a testament to his dominance that evening.

The year 1998 produced one of the biggest moments in WCW history when Bill Goldberg won the WCW World Heavyweight Title on July 6th. Goldberg defeated Hollywood Hogan in front of 41,412 fans at the Georgia Dome in Atlanta, Georgia during a WCW Nitro broadcast. This victory remains etched as one of the most significant moments in WCW's legacy and captured the imagination of wrestling fans nationwide.

The WWE Hardcore Championship became a notable fixture on both July 6th and 7th in 2002, due to its unique 24/7 rule that allowed for constant title changes. On July 6th, 2002, the title changed hands four times, with Christopher Nowinski, Steven Richards, Bradshaw, and Crash Holly each holding the championship on that single date. This frenetic activity repeated on July 7th, 2002, when the title changed hands five times between Bradshaw, Crash Holly, Christopher Nowinski, Steven Richards, and other competitors. Additionally on July 7th, 2002, Ricky Marvin won the NWA World Welterweight Title.

July 6th continued to produce significant championship moments in subsequent years. In 2004, Billy Kidman and Paul London won the WWE Tag Team Titles. Seven years later in 2007, Ryusuke Taguchi captured the IWGP Junior Heavyweight Title. More recent history on this date includes 2021, when Io Shirai and Zoey Stark won the NXT Women's Tag Team Titles and La Silueta won the Occidente Women's Title. In 2022, the NXT United Kingdom Title was vacated on July 6th.

July 7th has its own distinct collection of championship moments that have shaped wrestling history. The earliest entry on this date comes from 1977 when Bobo Brazil won the NWA United States Heavyweight Title (Mid-Atlantic Version). Moving forward to 1990, Sting captured the NWA World Heavyweight Title, a victory that represented a significant moment in professional wrestling history. In 1995, the IWGP Tag Team Titles were vacated, and a year later in 1996, Ric Flair won the WCW United States Heavyweight Title.

Beyond the notable 2002 Hardcore Championship activity, July 7th has hosted several other significant title changes. In 2003, Booker T won the WWE Intercontinental Title, demonstrating his rising prominence in WWE during that era. The year 2013 saw Mikey Nicholls and Shane Haste win the GHC Heavyweight Tag Team Titles, while Black Metal captured the Occidente Middleweight Title on the same date. In 2017, AJ Styles won the WWE United States Title, adding another major championship to his collection. The following year in 2018, Juice Robinson won the IWGP United States Heavyweight Title.

The most recent entries on July 7th include 2022 activity when Tyler Bate won the NXT United Kingdom Title and Noam Dar won the NXT UK Heritage Cup, representing the continued significance of this date in modern professional wrestling. The most recent title change on this date occurred in 2024 when Ethan Page won the NXT Title, showing that July 7th remains an active date for championship changes in contemporary wrestling.

These two consecutive dates in early July represent peaks of championship activity throughout wrestling history, reflecting the consistent importance of title changes in storytelling and the sport's ongoing evolution across multiple promotions and eras.

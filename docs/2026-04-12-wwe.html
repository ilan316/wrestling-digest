🔥 Charlotte Flair on WWE NXT Women's Division

Charlotte Flair Weighs In on NXT Talent and Future Plans

During a recent interview with Jeremy Jacobowitz ahead of WrestleMania 42, 14-time WWE world champion Charlotte Flair discussed the rising talent in WWE NXT's women's division and her current place in the company at age 40. Having graduated from NXT herself in the mid-to-late 2010s, Flair remains a frequent visitor to the developmental brand and has maintained interest in emerging wrestlers who could follow in her footsteps to the main roster.

When asked about standout performers in NXT, Flair highlighted three names that have caught her attention. She spoke positively about Sol Ruca and Zaria, two wrestlers she has already competed against in the ring. "I've had the opportunity to be in the ring with Sol Ruca and Zaria. I think they're killing it," Flair said. However, it was Blake Monroe who received the most specific praise from the veteran competitor. "Blake Monroe, her character, I think, is spunky and stands out. She's very, talks, I mean, yeah, I think it's great," Flair explained. Monroe, a 27-year-old who recently signed from AEW, has yet to compete on the WWE main roster but is currently pursuing the NXT Women's North American Championship against Tatum Paxley.

Sol Ruca, in particular, appears to be on the radar for a main roster call-up in the near future. The 26-year-old finished third in the 2026 Women's Royal Rumble and defeated Bayley at John Cena's final WWE show on December 13, signaling that WWE has significant plans for her career trajectory. Ruca and Zaria are currently set to face off in a Last Woman Standing Match at NXT Revenge, which will run across two dates on April 14 and 21.

Meanwhile, Flair herself is focused on her current project at WrestleMania 42. She will team with Alexa Bliss to challenge for the WWE Women's Tag Team Championships in a fatal four-way match that also features The Irresistible Forces (Lash Legend and Nia Jax), The Bella Twins, and Bayley with Lyra Valkyria. The match will take place on Night One of WrestleMania 42 at Allegiant Stadium in Las Vegas on April 18-19.

Discussing her upcoming match, Flair emphasized how special this moment feels in her career. "This will be my ninth WrestleMania. I have wrestled for almost every other title there is. Something feels very special about competing for the Tag Team Women's Championship with my tag partner, allies of convenience, Lexi. I'm ready. We're night one and I can't wait," she stated. This marks a significant moment as it is the first time in Flair's career that she will not be competing for a world title at WrestleMania.

The Flair-Bliss team has proven to have strong on-screen chemistry, with their partnership helping Flair rehabilitate her public image after a tense build to WrestleMania 41 last year. Flair has reflected positively on the contrast between the two years, noting that the past year has been one of her favorites. "It was a little tense. It's probably been one of my favorite years. From the in-ring moments with Lexi, to the styles and gears, to life in general. I've had more fun, a lot of growth, and I'm looking forward to seeing what 2026 brings," she said.

At 40 Years Old, Flair Rejects Retirement Talk

Just days after reaching her 40th birthday, Flair was asked about potential retirement plans during an appearance with Katie Nolan. She made her position abundantly clear: retirement is not something she is considering. "None of that," Flair said definitively when the topic was raised. Instead, she took aim at what she perceives as a double standard in the wrestling industry and beyond. "It's unfortunate that those sentences happen because I feel like in our industry, men don't reach their prime until 40. I started late. I didn't win my first title until I was 30. Where the girls are now, which is awesome, a lot of people are in their younger 20s," she explained.

Flair's path into professional wrestling differed from many of today's younger performers. She graduated from college, experienced her "awkward 20s of not knowing what to do," and then entered wrestling later in life compared to many contemporary wrestlers. This unconventional trajectory informed her perspective on age and career longevity. "But saying that the men don't hit their prime until 40, why am I not hitting my prime now? I don't look at it like that. Yes, I realize it would be easier for people to label me in that box, but I'm not going to let anyone put me in that box. And I hope women in any profession who are about to turn 40 don't think that it's a slowdown or an endgame," she stated.

Flair drew comparisons to athletes in other fields who have successfully extended their careers well into their 40s. "If anything, in whatever industry you're in—and in sports, like Lindsey Vonn did—it's about what you're capable of, what you're willing to do, and the time you're willing to put in," she said. Flair acknowledged her fortunate starting position in WWE, beginning her career near the top of the card. "I'm very fortunate. I started out on top. It might seem like my career is longer, but I've just had the opportunity to be at the top of the game," she noted.

Most importantly, Flair made clear that she will not apologize for her continued success or allow age to dictate her career timeline. "Now I'm not going to say sorry anymore for winning. I'm not going to age out of my career. I'm going to quit when I want to quit. I'm going to lace it up when I want," she declared. This statement represents a firm rejection of the perceived double standard that exists in professional wrestling regarding male and female performers as they age.

Flair on the Pat McAfee Controversy

When asked about the Pat McAfee controversy involving Cody Rhodes and Randy Orton at WrestleMania 42, Flair took a measured approach. Pat McAfee was revealed as Randy Orton's mystery ally on the April 3 episode of WWE SmackDown, with the pair subsequently attacking Rhodes on multiple occasions. The decision sparked significant backlash from some members of the WWE locker room, with CM Punk delivering a fiery promo criticizing McAfee's involvement, and WWE Tag Team Champion Damian Priest revealing that numerous wrestlers were unhappy with the angle.

However, Flair maintained a diplomatic stance on the matter. "I'm so into what I'm doing right now. I'm not in that storyline, so it's not my place whether I like it or don't like it," she said. Flair further explained that she maintains respect for both Rhodes and Orton due to their shared status as "legacy kids" in the industry. "I'm just a fan of Cody and Randy because I'm a legacy kid and they're legacy kids. You know the three favorite nepo babies," she added with a laugh. Her response differed markedly from the more vocal criticism that other wrestlers had expressed regarding McAfee's role.

🔥 Shawn Michaels Documentary and WWE Appearances

Shawn Michaels continues to make the media rounds promoting his upcoming documentary, "The Heartbreak Kid: Becoming Shawn Michaels," which will premiere on Peacock on Monday, April 13, 2026. During interviews, the WWE Hall of Famer and top executive behind-the-scenes in WWE NXT has provided detailed insight into the creative direction of the project and his role in bringing it to life.

When discussing his involvement in creating the documentary, Michaels explained that he maintains a "very hands off" approach to these types of projects. However, he was initially curious about what would make this documentary different from previous ones that had already covered his story extensively. He expressed concern about being dismissive of his own narrative, but wanted to understand what new angle the filmmakers would take.

The key focus that ultimately impressed Michaels was the documentary's heavy emphasis on his work in NXT and its importance to his life and family. Rather than rehashing his extensive in-ring career, the project centers on his current role developing the next generation of WWE talent. Michaels stated that this was exactly the direction he was pleased to see the documentary take, as his work in NXT represents the biggest part of his life right now and allows him to give back to an industry that has treated him well.

When asked about the conspicuous absence of his 2018 Crown Jewel return match—one of the most talked-about matches of his later career—Michaels was forthright about the reasoning. He explained that he was not aware of any discussions about including the bout and clarified that the documentary was intentionally designed to shift focus away from his in-ring legacy. Instead, the project concentrates on the personal side of his journey, particularly the relationships he rebuilt after difficult years in the business.

Michaels emphasized that the documentary tells a story of redemption and reconciliation rather than reliving specific matches. He reflected on the importance of being able to return to the industry after personal struggles and repair damaged relationships along the way. His emphasis was on his salvation and being able to come back to a line of work that is notoriously tough, allowing him the opportunity to apologize to people he had wronged and ask for their forgiveness.

The former Heartbreak Kid explained that getting a second chance in wrestling and turning that opportunity into something meaningful was a major part of the documentary's message. Rather than retelling matches that fans already know about, the project leans into how his career evolved after personal change. He acknowledged that while it might sound sentimental, the documentary is more about personal growth, forgiveness, and finding new purpose than about his actual wrestling career, since that aspect has already been well documented through his various matches and performances that defined him as a showstopper and performer.

Beyond the documentary itself, Michaels has been part of unexpected crossover moments in WWE recently, including appearances alongside rapper Sexy Red. When asked whether these collaborations were carefully manufactured or whether he was planning future celebrity partnerships, Michaels made clear that such moments are not the result of behind-the-scenes orchestration. He explained that those opportunities have all come about organically and reflected on lessons he has learned throughout his life about the dangers of trying too hard to force things to happen.

Michaels stated that when he tries to push for too many things to make them happen, he ends up sabotaging himself. Instead, his current philosophy is to do his best to walk through doors that are already open rather than trying to kick open ones that do not appear to be available. This approach applies both to his personal life and to his professional responsibilities.

His primary focus remains on NXT and the goals of continuing to build stars for Raw and SmackDown and for the future of WWE. Michaels made it clear that he prefers to allow good things to happen naturally and to take advantage of them when they present themselves, while keeping his focus on his core responsibility of developing wrestling talent. Celebrity crossovers have become increasingly common in WWE programming, but Michaels' comments suggest that not every moment fans see is part of a long-term strategic plan, and many of the biggest moments happen when the timing is right rather than when someone attempts to force them into existence.

Royce Keys WWE SmackDown Debut

Royce Keys' long-awaited arrival on WWE SmackDown is already generating considerable buzz in the wrestling community. After months away from WWE television following his Royal Rumble debut, Keys officially stepped into the spotlight on the April 10 episode of SmackDown, where he delivered an emphatic and dominant victory over Berto. The impressive showing immediately caught the attention of veteran observers and industry figures alike.

WWE Hall of Famer Mark Henry appeared on Busted Open Radio to share his thoughts on Keys' performance, offering both genuine praise and constructive feedback as the rising powerhouse begins to establish himself on the blue brand. Henry expressed enthusiasm about what he witnessed, stating "I enjoyed the Royce Keys segments. With the match, he stayed within himself; he stayed powerful." However, the legendary wrestler didn't shy away from pointing out areas where Keys can improve his craft. "He made some mistakes in the sense that, if you're going to hit somebody with something, follow it up with something. Don't get caught up in the crowd, wanting to wave at your friends in the front row and all of that, because you kicked him in the a**."

Henry emphasized that Keys has already demonstrated a willingness to learn from those around him. "He hasn't had a problem with mentorship; There's a few guys that I know are in his ear that I've watched talk to him. He's going to be a good one, I can see it already." This early endorsement from a Hall of Famer speaks volumes about the trajectory Keys appears to be on within the company.

During the same SmackDown episode, Keys was featured in a backstage segment alongside Solo Sikoa, who extended an open invitation and made it clear that the door is always open if Keys ever wants assistance or alignment. This could represent a tempting offer for a rising star seeking to build momentum, but it also presents a classic crossroads scenario that requires careful navigation.

Henry weighed in on that possibility, offering strategic advice about Keys' path forward. "I don't think that Royce Keys should focus his attention on joining a clique, I think that he should try to be his own man right now. But when you're your own man, you rub people that want you to be muscle for them the wrong way." The underlying message suggests that maintaining independence early in one's WWE career can establish credibility and prevent being pigeonholed into secondary roles within larger groups.

Keys, who longtime fans will remember from his time as Powerhouse Hobbs in AEW, had previously been rumored for a high-profile WrestleMania 42 program involving Jelly Roll. However, with the premium live event just days away at the time of this report, current WWE storylines do not appear to be heading in that direction. Instead, all eyes remain on how Keys builds momentum from this impressive debut, with both fans and established veterans in the industry watching closely to see how he develops his character and in-ring capabilities on the main WWE roster.

Ethan Page in NXT Under Shawn Michaels

Ethan Page Reflects on Perfect Timing of Shawn Michaels Connection in NXT

Ethan Page has opened up about his journey in NXT under Shawn Michaels' leadership, explaining how the timing of his arrival proved to be exactly what he needed at that stage of his career. After his stint with AEW came to an end in 2024, Page made the transition to NXT, where he has since become a significant player on the brand, even capturing the NXT North American Championship during his tenure.

In an interview with Big Gold Belt Media, Page was asked about his experience working under Michaels and whether he anticipated things would develop the way they have. Page acknowledged that neither he nor Michaels likely expected the trajectory that unfolded. However, he emphasized his deep respect for how Michaels approached their initial conversation.

Page explained that when he first arrived at NXT, Michaels made no grandiose promises. "He promised me nothing. I respected it so much because I had just come from a place that promised me everything," Page recalled. He referenced his previous experience in AEW, where promises had been made but circumstances changed, ultimately leading to his departure and the need to reassess his career path.

What stood out most to Page was Michaels' honesty and understanding of what Page genuinely needed. "He flat out was like, 'Look, I'm going to give you the opportunity, and I'm not promising you anything. The only thing I'm promising you is that you'll be creatively satisfied. So, you will constantly be doing something,'" Page shared. This promise of consistent creative involvement carried significant weight for an athlete who had recently felt sidelined.

Page reflected on his mindset coming into NXT, noting that he had been sitting in catering at his previous position, desperately seeking work. At that point in his life, he recognized the urgency of his situation. "I was at an age in my life where I was like, if I'm not working now, when am I going to be working?" Page stated. This realization fueled his hunger and determination to make the most of whatever opportunity presented itself.

The wrestler attributed his success in NXT to a combination of perfect timing and his own mental and emotional state. "I think preparation meant opportunity. Shawn Michaels ran into me at the perfect time in my life, at the perfect stage of my career, at the perfect maturity level, hunger level, passion level, desperation level. Everything that was supposed to happen happened," Page concluded.

His comments paint a picture of a professional who was ready to seize an opportunity when it arrived, and a leader in Michaels who recognized what Page needed and provided exactly that without overselling what could not be guaranteed.

Jade Cargill WrestleMania 42 Match

Jade Cargill is preparing to defend her WWE Women's Championship against Rhea Ripley at WrestleMania 42, the company's biggest annual spectacle. However, "The Storm" recently revealed that the challenger wasn't her first choice for the showcase of the immortals.

In an interview on The Joe Budden Podcast, Cargill discussed how Charlotte Flair was initially expected to be her opponent at this year's WrestleMania. According to Cargill, the teasing of a Flair matchup has been present since day one of her WWE tenure, and the two superstars have generated substantial fan reactions whenever they've had face-to-face confrontations.

"We've always teased Charlotte Flair since day one. And every time I had a face-to-face with this girl, we got a huge reaction," Cargill said. "I've had, maybe, two face offs with Rhea. The reaction? It was a total difference in reaction here. I would think that we'll build more to Rhea 'cause we don't have history. Not saying Charlotte and I have history, but there's been more of a reaction with Charlotte and I having attitude and yelling at each other... I think that would have been an easier route. But I believe the reason we went this route is because it's more money that's going to be made going against Rhea."

Despite acknowledging that a program with Flair might have been the simpler path, Cargill respects the decision to pursue the Ripley matchup, recognizing the financial upside of facing an already-established immortal like "Mami." The two competitors have only had approximately two face-to-face interactions, lacking the built-up tension that exists between Cargill and Flair.

Cargill took the opportunity to fire shots at her WrestleMania opponent, referring to Ripley as a "backed and built talent by WWE." The champion has been critical of how the company has constructed Ripley's career trajectory as a Grand Slam Champion.

Looking ahead, Cargill has made her intentions clear regarding Charlotte Flair. Should she successfully retain her championship at WrestleMania 42, the champion would like to pick up where she and Flair last left off, with a preferred destination of SummerSlam in August. Cargill and Flair have been circling one another with tense staredowns since Cargill joined WWE on October 13, 2023, creating anticipation for a future collision between the two competitors.

Dave Meltzer SmackDown Appearance Fallout

The fallout from Dave Meltzer's appearance at SmackDown has escalated significantly after Vince Russo launched a pointed critique of the veteran journalist on social media. The situation began when a video emerged showing a fan harassing Meltzer at ringside during the April 10 episode of SmackDown, but quickly evolved beyond that initial incident.

On April 12, Russo took to Twitter to respond to the situation, using it as an opportunity to address past criticism from Meltzer. Russo referenced an earlier comment in which Meltzer had questioned his career achievements, specifically citing a quote that stated: "The guy hasn't been successful at anything on a major scale since 1999, and wasn't at the end of that year either."

Russo directly challenged this assessment by emphasizing his professional longevity. He pointed out that 27 years have passed since 1999, and he continues to maintain an active presence in the wrestling industry. To support his argument, Russo listed his current endeavors, including his podcast network, media appearances, and an upcoming book scheduled for release on July 14th. He also highlighted his work with an independent promotion, noting specific achievements in that role.

In his Twitter thread, Russo stated: "1999. It's now 2026. Do the math—27 years later and I am STILL making a living in the wrestling business. Podcasting Network is now over a decade strong, The Coach and Bro is just coming off a wildly successful interview with AEW Chief Tony Khan, I have a third book coming out on July 14th and I am working for the hottest Indy Promotion out there right now, where we have increased our numbers 400% in 6 months."

The confrontation took a more personal turn when Russo shifted focus to Meltzer's presence at the SmackDown event. He questioned what Meltzer's definition of success truly encompasses, suggesting that simply purchasing a ticket to attend wrestling events and writing reviews for a newsletter does not constitute meaningful achievement. Russo further contended that Meltzer's newsletter has experienced declining subscriber numbers over the years, implying this represents a failure in the journalism space.

Russo asked pointedly: "So what is success to Dave Meltzer? Buying a ticket to WWE Smackdown and sitting with the Marks so he can write a review for a newsletter that has lost thousands of subscribers over the years? That is your definition of success, Dave?"

The criticism surrounding Pat McAfee's involvement in WWE programming continues to grow, but one wrestling veteran sees the negative reaction as a sign of success rather than failure. John Bradshaw Layfield recently addressed the mounting backlash on the Something to Wrestle podcast, explaining that the fan response to McAfee actually validates his effectiveness as a heel performer.

Following two consecutive weeks of low ratings and increasing complaints about McAfee's presence on the show, JBL took to the airwaves to defend the controversial broadcaster. According to JBL, the fact that fans are actively expressing their dislike proves McAfee is performing his role correctly.

"He is a bad guy. And the idea that fans online are hating that Pat McAfee is involved and don't want him near the main event—that's the desired result as a heel. Don't you think?" JBL stated during his appearance on the podcast.

JBL went further in his defense, pushing back strongly against the widespread suggestion that McAfee's heat constitutes "go-away heat," the wrestling term for a reaction so negative that fans simply stop caring and tuning in. Instead, JBL argued that the level of discussion surrounding McAfee actually proves the opposite is occurring. He emphasized that the sheer volume of conversation demonstrates genuine engagement rather than apathy.

The veteran commentator then delved into the psychology of effective heel work, explaining that the best villainous characters succeed when they blur the line between performance and authentic dislike. JBL suggested that McAfee has tapped into this dynamic, where fans forget they are watching an act and instead react with genuine emotional responses to the person on screen.

"You think it's just—it blows me away when a guy gets heat like this and people go, 'Oh, no, no, no—it's go-away heat.' No, it's not. You're talking about it. If it was go-away heat, you wouldn't be talking about it—you wouldn't even mention it. It's real heat," JBL explained during the podcast segment.

Breaking down the mechanics of successful heel performance, JBL referenced old-school wrestling wisdom from Killer Tim Brooks to illustrate his point. He explained that true heel effectiveness is achieved when audiences can no longer distinguish between the character and the performer, leading them to genuinely dislike the individual rather than simply recognizing a staged performance.

"When you come out there and you're able to strip away the veneer of that person playing a heel to that person just being an asshole and you don't like them—that's when you're winning. The key to being a heel is when they take away the fact that you're playing a character and they don't just say, 'It has nothing to do with Killer Tim Brooks as a wrestler—I don't like that man. I hate him. I think Pat's doing an incredible job,'" JBL stated.

This perspective shifts the narrative surrounding the recent controversy away from simple metrics like viewership numbers and audience complaints, instead positioning the backlash as a calculated element of storytelling strategy. If JBL's assessment is accurate, WWE may be deliberately cultivating this negative reaction as part of a larger narrative leading into WrestleMania 42, using the polarizing response to build anticipation for major matchups.

The question remains whether WWE shares JBL's optimistic view of the situation or whether the ratings decline and fan complaints represent a genuine problem requiring adjustment.

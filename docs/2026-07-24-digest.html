🔥 AEW Redemption PPV 2026

During Thursday's media call for AEW Redemption, Tony Khan addressed the absence of former AEW World Champion MJF from the pay-per-view event scheduled for July 26 at the Bell Centre in Montreal, Quebec, Canada. Khan explained that MJF is currently dealing with significant injuries sustained throughout 2026 while working an active schedule as champion and across AEW programming including house shows and pay-per-view events.

MJF last competed on July 8 at AEW Dynamite Beach Break in Clearwater, Florida, where he lost the AEW World Championship to Kenny Omega. His third title reign lasted 45 days and included successful title defenses against Rush and Mark Briscoe. Since that loss, MJF has been absent from television as he recovers from the injuries he accumulated during his time as fighting champion.

Khan was careful to distinguish between MJF's on-screen character and the person himself during his explanation. "MJF is not a nice guy, not a nice person. But he is a great wrestler, and we love having him as a part of AEW when he's healthy," Khan stated. He further emphasized MJF's contributions during his latest championship run, noting that the former champion had done something different compared to his previous reigns. "MJF has been an incredible fighting champion in 2026, doing something very different than in his previous time as the champion before 2026. MJF has been out on TV, on pay-per-view, even on house shows fighting in big matches."

Khan took time to praise MJF for continuing to compete despite dealing with physical ailments throughout the year. "He's had some major injuries this year that he's been fighting through, as a credit to him. I think it speaks highly of MJF," Khan said. He reiterated that MJF deserves recognition for his efforts during a demanding stretch of wrestling. "I give MJF a lot of credit for fighting through some major injuries this year. I wouldn't be out here praising him and saying all these nice things if they weren't true. I absolutely think MJF has been one of the great things about AEW in 2026."

The most encouraging news for AEW fans was Khan's insistence that MJF's injuries are not career-threatening or expected to sideline him for an extended period. "I'm grateful that the injuries he sustained are nothing that would keep him out really, really long term. As you know, there are times in this sport when injuries can alter careers sometimes. So I'm glad he's going to be okay because he's a very important wrestler in AEW. We've had other great stars suffer injuries that have kept them out for years or even indefinitely. This isn't anything like that. He was definitely banged up, though, and he went through some very hard-hitting matches."

Khan emphasized that AEW's deep roster allows the company the luxury of giving injured talent proper recovery time without compromising the quality of programming. "That's what's tremendous about having such a strong roster of wrestlers, being able to not have to pressure them, because we want to extend these wrestlers' careers. And in the case of an MJF, that's somebody that I hope is wrestling for decades and decades at a very top level. He's a very young man, and he's had some major injuries this year that he's been fighting through, as a credit to him."

Khan confirmed that MJF will return to AEW in the near future. "I'm looking forward to MJF's return to AEW when he's back, which he will be back very soon, I'm sure," Khan stated. He expressed optimism that MJF would be available for AEW All In London at Wembley Stadium, one of the company's biggest annual events.

When asked directly whether MJF versus Kenny Omega had originally been planned for AEW Redemption, Khan declined to confirm the details. A TSN promo had aired suggesting Omega would be challenging for the title rather than defending it, prompting questions about potential last-minute card changes. Khan responded cryptically: "I have to keep some sleight of hand in these things." He did not directly address whether the Kenny Omega versus MJF match had been the original plan, instead saying, "I feel we have made the right moves," without elaborating on what those moves entailed.

Khan also used the media call to address other talent-related matters that had generated speculation among wrestling media. Regarding Tay Melo, Khan clarified that she had recently requested her release from AEW, contradicting earlier reports that the issue had been ongoing for months or dating back to 2025. According to Khan, Tay came to him after wrestling against The Divine Dominion at a recent Collision episode and asked for her release. Khan stated that he has not booked her since that conversation and intends to grant her the requested relief. Khan also noted that Tay had expressed displeasure with inaccurate reports being circulated about the situation.

Regarding Anna Jay, Khan emphasized that she remains in good standing with AEW and has actually extended her contract with the company. Khan indicated that Anna Jay is currently dealing with an injury and had recently undergone an MRI. Despite wrestling media reports suggesting otherwise, Khan made clear that Anna Jay is not leaving AEW and has committed to remaining with the promotion for the long term.

Khan also offered cryptic hints about other talent absences from Redemption. When asked about Swerve Strickland, Khan revealed that Strickland would be with him at Comic-Con in San Diego this weekend but said he would answer more questions about Strickland's status after Redemption concludes. Khan did note that Strickland is in very good standing with AEW. Regarding Hangman Page, Khan suggested that a return seems imminent following Page's recent promo on Collision, though he was less definitive about Page appearing specifically at Redemption.

🔥 WWE creative commentary and criticism

WRESTLING CREATIVE FACES SCRUTINY OVER STORYTELLING AND CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT

WWE's creative approach has come under intense scrutiny from industry veterans who argue the company's massive content output is preventing proper character development and story execution.

Vince Russo has emerged as a leading voice in this criticism, proposing a significant structural overhaul. Speaking on The Brand, Russo suggested WWE should eliminate its individual brands to simplify the creative system. He attributed recent booking problems—including Nick Aldis costing GUNTHER his match at Saturday Night's Main Event while serving as a babyface authority figure—to the company's overwhelming workload. Russo explained that producing more content than the creative process can support inevitably leads to shortcuts and poor decision-making.

"There's no time for detail. That's what it is," Russo stated. "Detail takes time. Writing stories takes time. Developing characters takes time. And that's one thing they don't have—time." He further elaborated on his solution, arguing that simplification is necessary given the volume of content WWE produces. "Because of all the content you are turning out, the system has to be simple. And bro, I think you do that—eliminate the freaking brands."

Bin Hamin has similarly highlighted specific creative failures that demonstrate broader systemic issues. He criticized WWE for overusing Danhausen during recent storylines, explaining that while comedy characters can successfully interact with serious wrestlers when properly balanced, oversaturation transforms meaningful segments into Saturday Night Live sketches. Hamin pointed out that the show's mystique gets lost when the character appears too frequently or in inappropriate contexts.

A more significant missed opportunity emerged during a Raw episode at Madison Square Garden involving Danhausen and The Judgment Day. After Danhausen was attacked, Joe Hendry made a save. However, Hamin noted that WWE completely failed to leverage Hendry's established gimmick. Hendry's character concept involves appearing when his name is mentioned, yet WWE never gave Danhausen the opportunity to say his name before Hendry's entrance music hit. Instead, Hendry simply ran down to the ring as a conventional run-in, undermining months of character establishment.

"They should have had him like, 'You're going to tell me where my money is.' Either you drop it then, or you give him the mic to give him the answer," Hamin explained. "What's Joe Hendry's gimmick? Say his name and he appears. They never gave him the chance to say his name."

Hamin also criticized continuity issues surrounding Dominik Mysterio's involvement with the storyline. After previously claiming he didn't care about money that Danhausen had stolen from The Judgment Day, the faction later demanded its return. This contradiction undermined the storyline's credibility and created logical inconsistencies that distracted viewers from the narrative.

Vince Russo has additionally taken issue with WWE's reliance on hometown celebrations and "easy cheap pops" that lack broader appeal. He pointed to three recent instances: Sami Zayn winning the Undisputed WWE Championship in Saudi Arabia, CM Punk claiming the title in Chicago, and a New York Knicks celebration at Madison Square Garden. While acknowledging that these moments generated enormous reactions in their respective venues, Russo questioned their value to viewers nationwide.

"That's going to be huge in the building. There's no question about that. The house is electric. But again, the guy watching in Washington State doesn't give a [expletive] about the Knicks. The guy in Idaho doesn't give a [expletive] about the Knicks. The guy in New Hampshire doesn't give a [expletive] about the Knicks. It was a New York thing," Russo stated.

Hamin expanded on this critique by noting a missed opportunity for character work. Rather than having everyone in the crowd celebrate, WWE could have positioned a heel to oppose the hometown affection, creating heat for a performer representing the rest of the country. "Where is the guy going, 'Screw New York?' Where's the Southerner? Where is the guy for everybody else? The opportunity is there to get heat because everybody's Mr. New York," Hamin argued.

Character protection emerged as another significant concern when Russo criticized Dominik Mysterio's booking at Saturday Night's Main Event. NBA star Karl-Anthony Towns delivered a chokeslam to Mysterio after the celebrity successfully handled JD McDonagh. While Russo acknowledged that Towns performed admirably and wrestling-trained celebrities traditionally need babyface assistance against heels, he argued that WWE should have protected Mysterio from the same fate as McDonagh.

Russo pointed out that Mysterio had already endured multiple embarrassing losses and encounters with the Minihausens before facing Towns. "Dominik just gets beat up by the little people. Then he goes out there again. Now Dominik gets chokeslammed, and Dominik was on the short end again last night. It's like, my God, bro. Are you guys going to have any freaking heels with heat whatsoever?" A simple adjustment—having Mysterio escape at the last second—would have preserved his credibility while still allowing Towns his moment.

Jim Ross weighed in from an outside perspective, suggesting that Charlotte Flair represents another victim of WWE's creative struggles. The Hall of Famer and AEW commentator argued that despite remaining one of WWE's biggest stars, Flair appears underbooked relative to her accomplishments and drawing power.

"Charlotte's probably been underbooked, in my opinion. Largely, she's very over. But I just think that they could probably do a little bit more with her, quite frankly," Ross stated. He emphasized that success requires both personal commitment and genuine company support, suggesting that Flair deserves a greater platform and investment in her storylines.

Other talent has also acknowledged the challenging environment created by WWE's current approach. Tiffany Stratton, the WWE Women's United States Champion, discussed how she manages criticism while maintaining her sense of self. Though her remarks were more philosophical than directly addressing creative direction, her comments reflected the broader reality that performers must navigate both legitimate feedback and dismissive criticism.

"I'm a public figure, so I have to expect the hate and the criticism. Sometimes, I do take into account what people say and see if I can apply it anywhere. Just having good discernment when it comes to hate and love, if you believe all of the good stuff about yourself, you have to believe all the bad stuff," Stratton explained. She credited strong self-awareness and personal conviction as tools for maintaining stability amid constant feedback.

The convergence of these criticisms suggests a pattern: WWE's content production has expanded beyond its creative infrastructure's capacity. The company produces material for multiple brands, numerous weekly television programs, and continuous storytelling demands that leave insufficient time for the kind of detailed character work and long-term plotting that professional wrestling thrives on. Quick fixes like hometown celebrations and celebrity guest appearances provide immediate reactions but do not compensate for weaknesses in fundamental narrative construction and character protection.

Russo's proposal to eliminate brand divisions represents one proposed solution, though implementation would require significant restructuring. More immediately, industry observers suggest WWE needs to prioritize storytelling consistency and character development over maximizing short-term crowd reactions. Without addressing these fundamental creative challenges, the pattern of missed opportunities and logical inconsistencies will likely continue.

WWE Hall of Famer Kevin Nash recently discussed the safety differences between harness systems used in WCW and WWF during the late 1990s, specifically addressing how Sting's rigging differed from the equipment that led to Owen Hart's fatal accident.

One of the biggest tragedies in professional wrestling history occurred in May 1999 at WWF's Over the Edge pay-per-view when Owen Hart, performing as his Blue Blazer character, fell to his death while being lowered from the rafters by harness and grapple line into the ring. During the same era in WCW, Sting was also regularly lowered from the rafters using a harness system for dramatic effect during his entrance and various other segments. Nash acknowledged that he was uncertain whether WCW continued using the harness after the tragedy, but he did clarify the critical differences in the safety mechanisms between the two companies.

According to Nash's explanation on "Kliq This," WCW's apparatus featured fundamental safety differences that prevented Hart's type of accident from occurring. "I think we still did it a couple of times. I might be wrong. We might have, out of respect for Owen, not done it again," Nash explained. "Different rigger. The distinction Nash highlighted was critical: WCW's harness system included a quick release mechanism that could only function when the performer's feet were in contact with the ground.

🔥 Sting and AEW pay tribute to Ted Turner

AEW HONORS TED TURNER WITH SPECIAL TRIBUTE EPISODE

Following the passing of Ted Turner, the pioneering television mogul and founder of TBS and TNT, All Elite Wrestling announced that Wednesday's special three-hour combined episode of AEW Dynamite and AEW Collision would be dedicated to his memory. Turner, who passed away at age 87, left behind a legacy that fundamentally shaped professional wrestling on television, dating back to the 1970s when he first brought wrestling to his networks.

AEW issued an official statement mourning Turner's loss: "AEW mourns the loss of Ted Turner and sends our condolences. A pioneer of TV and founder of TBS & TNT, Turner championed pro wrestling on his networks since the 1970s. AEW proudly continues that tradition, and we dedicate Wednesday Night Dynamite tonight on TBS to his memory."

The live broadcast from North Charleston, South Carolina opened with an emotional cold open featuring Tony Schiavone standing in the ring. Schiavone addressed the crowd about Turner's long-standing belief in professional wrestling and his conviction that the sport deserved a place on national television. He reflected on the history of WCW airing on TBS and later TNT, while praising AEW for continuing that legacy today. Multiple "In Memoriam" graphics were displayed throughout the arena on the big screen and surrounding LED boards as Schiavone spoke. He noted that everyone in the building was there because of Turner, as well as the leadership of Tony Khan and Warner Bros. Discovery.

In a genuine surprise moment, Schiavone introduced "The Icon" Sting, who made his first appearance since being in attendance for Darby Allin's AEW World Championship victory last month. Sting emerged wearing full face paint and ring gear, notably without entrance music, prompting the live crowd to erupt into loud chants. The two icons embraced in the ring as Schiavone noted that both of them knew Ted Turner well and that Turner played a major role in bringing them together for over four decades.

Sting then addressed the crowd himself, reflecting on Turner's passion for professional wrestling. He asked fans to imagine "an all-in billionaire who loved pro wrestling," before calling Turner exactly that kind of person. Sting praised "Big" Ted for everything he did for wrestlers over the years and recalled stories about executives at CNN wanting wrestling removed from television, only for Turner to continue fighting for it behind the scenes.

In his tribute, Sting shared a particularly poignant anecdote about Turner's commitment to wrestling: "I can't believe what he did for us. He was completely committed to us, devoted to us in every conceivable way. So much so that when some of the top brass up there in CNN Towers would have their meetings with Ted, they'd say, 'Ted, we don't know about this whole pro wrestling thing. I think it's time to cut 'em off because we're always in the red.' Ted would tell us that story and say, 'I'd look at all of them and say, You wrestlers just keep on doing what you're doing because I got some deep pockets.'"

Sting emphasized the far-reaching consequences of Turner's support, stating: "There wouldn't be a TNT title, there wouldn't be a Sting. There wouldn't be a Darby Allin, there wouldn't be a Tony Schiavone here tonight. You wouldn't be here tonight. So, thank you, once again, Ted." He also acknowledged the current ownership, mentioning "a second all-in billionaire keeping wrestling going" in reference to Tony Khan.

AEW President Tony Khan had previously revealed during an X livestream ahead of the show that the company would be making special tributes throughout the evening. Khan expressed the gravity of the moment: "The man who gave us this platform, the man who created TBS and TNT and so many great television channels and so many great concepts in the field of television, the late, great Mr. Ted Turner has passed. Very fittingly, we will have some words from Mr. Tony Schiavone, some heartfelt thoughts to start the show, as well as a 10-bell salute to the man who gave us this platform to have Wednesday night Dynamite, Saturday night Collision and all the AEW television shows from the very beginning."

As a further gesture of respect, Schiavone mentioned to fans to look at the side plates on the TNT and TBS Championships, which were redesigned in honor of Ted Turner per Tony Khan's request. The tribute segment concluded with a ten-bell salute as the camera zoomed in on the Ted Turner graphic, mixed with shots of the live crowd bowing their heads in silence. This served as a powerful way to begin the special tribute edition of Dynamite.

Turner's legacy in professional wrestling cannot be overstated. He signed off on Eric Bischoff's 1995 pitch to bring WCW Monday Nitro to television, which kicked off the legendary "Monday Night Wars" against WWE. With stars like Sting, Hulk Hogan, and the NWO storyline, WCW famously beat WWE in television ratings for many weeks, fundamentally changing the landscape of professional wrestling. Turner's unwavering financial support for wrestling, despite mounting losses and pressure from other executives, kept WCW alive and competitive during some of the most pivotal moments in wrestling history.

With Turner's death on May 6, 2026, the wrestling world lost one of its greatest champions. However, his influence continues through AEW's continued presence on the TBS and TNT networks that Turner founded, proving that his vision of professional wrestling on national television remains alive and thriving.

🔥 Will Ospreay reflects on WWE career and neck surgery

Will Ospreay Recently Opened Up About His Career Path and Recovery From Neck Surgery

In a comprehensive interview with Forbes, Will Ospreay reflected extensively on his professional wrestling journey and the serious medical situation that forced him out of action. The AEW star discussed multiple aspects of his career, from his pride in never being signed by WWE to the financial and physical toll of his recent neck surgery.

When discussing his career trajectory, Ospreay emphasized his satisfaction with the path he chose. He explained that going to the United States, competing in Japan, and eventually becoming a weekly TV star meant something precisely because he never joined WWE. "I was never made by WWE. I was never touched by them. I would hold such pride and honor in knowing that someone from England who never moved away from his country was able to do that. I really hope that inspires so many of the other UK lads and lasses over there that they don't need to compromise on their beliefs or what they want," Ospreay stated. He further emphasized his commitment to his principles, saying "I'm proud that I stuck to my guns. This is the last thing I want. I want the responsibility of being the guy — the world champion, the main event and the guy that can be depended on."

Ospreay's return to in-ring competition came after a serious neck injury that required surgical intervention. Currently involved in a storyline with The Death Riders, the very faction responsible for his kayfabe injury, Ospreay had to undergo a significant medical procedure. The wrestler revealed the shocking cost of his recovery when he discussed the financial aspects of his surgery with interviewers.

One of the most significant revelations from Ospreay's recent interviews involved AEW President Tony Khan's personal intervention in covering the costs of his neck surgery. Ospreay was visibly grateful for Khan's financial support, stating "I mean, 10 years ago, this was the type of thing that medically disqualified you from wrestling. So the fact that medical science has come so far, man, I'm just grateful and so blessed. And I must give the biggest shoutout to Tony [Khan] for paying for that surgery because the moment I saw that fucking bill — bro, it was more expensive than my fucking house. It was crazy."

Ospreay also gave substantial credit to his surgeon, Dr. Wallace, for enabling his return to professional wrestling. He acknowledged that modern medical science has advanced dramatically, as a neck surgery of this severity would have ended a wrestling career a decade earlier. The procedure gave him what Ospreay described as "a second chance at having a career doing this."

During his recovery process, Ospreay reached out to several veteran wrestlers who had undergone similar procedures. He spoke extensively with Adam Copeland, who has had three neck surgeries including a triple fusion. Copeland provided Ospreay with personal guidance and emotional support throughout the recovery process. Ospreay recalled: "I remember when I got told how severe it was because I didn't realize how bad it actually was. I'd been wrestling on it for like 10 months. It only really started getting bad around May, and then it started fucking destroying me. But I spoke to him, and he gave me the whole backstory of when he found out about his surgery and what was going to happen. He gave me his number to keep in contact because he knows it can get really depressing at times."

Ospreay also communicated with Kyle O'Reilly, Bryan Danielson, and Roddy Strong during his recovery period. He emphasized how the wrestling community rallied around him, noting that news of his injury spread quickly backstage and he received support from his peers throughout the process. "Everybody's just been super cool because it really is such a good group of people backstage. I feel like news got around very quickly, and it was one of those things where it was really lovely having the support of my peers and everybody wishing for me to come back," Ospreay explained.

Following his return at the AEW Revolution pay-per-view in March, Ospreay had to make significant adjustments to his in-ring wrestling style. The surgery and subsequent recovery period affected his physical capabilities in ways that required him to eliminate certain high-risk maneuvers from his arsenal. Ospreay explained the specific technical challenges he faced: "It's mainly my range of motion right now. There are so many things where I just don't feel comfortable doing certain tricks anymore, so I've kind of knocked them on the head. For example, I won't do a shooting star press anymore because that required me to really put my head back and get a good arch, and I just can't do it anymore."

The adjustment period extended beyond just removing moves from his repertoire. Ospreay admitted that the physical sensation of performing had changed. In discussing his mental state during his return matches, he noted feeling discomfort with his new physical reality, stating "It's super scary because it still doesn't feel like my head." Despite these challenges, Ospreay remained committed to demonstrating that he could still perform at the highest level. "I wanted to come back and still be able to reassure people that pay-per-view Billy Goat is still one of the best wrestlers in the world," he said.

Looking toward the future, Ospreay expressed interest in competing in international wrestling promotions while maintaining his AEW commitment. He discussed the possibility of participating in New Japan Pro Wrestling's G1 Climax tournament, which he has never won despite previous attempts. During an interview with Tokyo Sports, Ospreay acknowledged the difficulty of requesting time off from his current AEW obligations. "It's hard to ask Tony this year if I can take about six weeks off, but I've talked to my wife about it, and I do want to participate in the G1 Climax again someday. I've never won it, so I want to try again," Ospreay explained.

Beyond the G1 Climax, Ospreay also expressed desire to pursue New Japan's newly revived IWGP Heavyweight Championship, which was reintroduced as the fourth-generation version of the historic belt. He noted the prestige associated with the championship, which has been held by distinguished wrestlers including Hiroshi Tanahashi, Kazuchika Okada, AJ Styles, and Kenny Omega. "Also, the fourth-generation IWGP Heavyweight belt, which was revived this year, has been worn by Hiroshi Tanahashi, Kazuchika Okada, AJ Styles, Kenny Omega, and others, and I've always admired them. So I also want that belt," Ospreay stated.

Ospreay's journey back from neck surgery represents both a personal triumph and a testament to advances in modern sports medicine. His willingness to openly discuss the emotional and physical challenges of recovery, combined with his gratitude for the support he received from AEW management and fellow wrestlers, painted a comprehensive picture of his current situation in professional wrestling.

This high-stakes stipulation marks a significant development in the storyline, with the outcome potentially resulting in a dramatic physical change for one of AEW's top stars. The contract signing scheduled for next week's Dynamite will determine whether MJF accepts the terms and whether the match will officially be added to the Double or Nothing card.

🔥 Ted Turner death and wrestling world reactions

Ted Turner, the legendary media pioneer and former owner of World Championship Wrestling, has passed away at the age of 87. The news was reported by CNN, the groundbreaking 24-hour news channel that Turner founded. In 2018, Turner publicly revealed that he had been diagnosed with Lewy body dementia, a progressive neurological condition. Earlier in 2025, he was hospitalized due to a mild case of pneumonia but recovered while staying at a rehabilitation facility.

A statement from Turner Enterprises confirmed his passing on May 6, 2026. According to the family release, Turner died peacefully at age 87, surrounded by his family. The statement highlighted his legacy as a philanthropist, environmentalist, and cable pioneer. Beyond his business achievements, Turner was remembered for his no-holds-barred delivery, endearing sense of humor, and undying loyalty to those around him. He was known for his warmth and lack of conceit despite his many successes, often insisting that people call him simply "Ted" rather than "Mr. Turner." He is survived by his five children, fourteen grandchildren, and two great-grandchildren. A private family service is planned, with a public memorial to be held at a later date.

Turner's influence on media and television cannot be overstated. He founded CNN and created the "superstation" model through WTBS, which later became the TBS Superstation. His empire expanded to include TNT, Cartoon Network, and TCM. Beyond television, Turner donated over a billion dollars to various causes, saved imperiled and endangered species, preserved more than two million acres of land, and served bison in his Ted's Montana Grill restaurants. He also won the America's Cup in sailing, cementing his status as a visionary entrepreneur willing to take calculated risks on groundbreaking ideas.

However, in the wrestling world, Turner's impact is particularly profound. Long before wrestling became a global billion-dollar business, Turner recognized its value as television content. Through WTBS, he gave wrestling national exposure at a time when it was still largely regional, bringing promotions like Georgia Championship Wrestling into living rooms across the country. This decision helped set the stage for everything that followed in professional wrestling's evolution.

One pivotal moment came during the Black Saturday incident, when Vince McMahon briefly took over the TBS wrestling slot. The move failed to connect with Turner's audience, ultimately pushing him to go all-in on wrestling himself. In 1988, Turner purchased Jim Crockett Promotions and transformed it into World Championship Wrestling, keeping a national competitor alive and giving wrestling something it desperately needed at the time: real competition.

Turner's biggest gamble came in 1995 with the launch of WCW Monday Nitro, created and pitched by Eric Bischoff, which went head-to-head with WWE programming. Backed by Turner's resources and networks, WCW signed top stars like Hulk Hogan, Randy Savage, Kevin Nash, and Scott Hall, turning the company into a powerhouse. This era became known as the Monday Night Wars, with WCW dominating the ratings for 83 straight weeks and helping push wrestling into mainstream culture at a level it had never reached before. "I get them on, I can see everything crystal clear and I fire up and kick his ass," he explained.

Boogs noted that WWE officials thought the idea was funny, but they viewed it as more of a short-term comedy spot rather than a full character. "They're like, 'Yeah, it's funny but that's like a one-off thing,'" Boogs recalled.

The character never made it to WWE television, with Boogs eventually becoming known for his over-the-top guitar entrance and energetic performances instead.

🔥 Maria Kanellis AEW Run Reflection

Maria Kanellis recently spoke with Wrestling Life Online for an in-depth interview covering her wrestling career, including her time with AEW and her previous championship history. The veteran women's wrestler reflected on her three-year run with the company and discussed earlier career achievements, offering candid insights into her professional journey.

Regarding her AEW tenure from 2022 to 2025, Kanellis expressed a sense of unfulfilled potential. "I feel like I didn't really get to accomplish everything that I wanted to," she stated. "I appreciate the opportunity. I appreciate the fact that I got to work with such tremendous talent. But it just—I got my tumor right around the time my contract was ending, and so it just kind of ended."

The timing of her departure proved particularly frustrating for Kanellis given her extensive background in the wrestling industry. "It's sad for me because, you know, I had so many years in the wrestling industry and so many fantastic memories, and for it to end that way was really disheartening. It was just bad timing," she explained. She did acknowledge that Tony Khan handled the situation with consideration and grace, noting that he was supportive and kind when wishing her well regarding her health situation.

Despite her appreciation for Khan's approach, Kanellis's professional aspirations remained unfulfilled. "For me, professionally, I just felt like I could do more," she said, emphasizing that her health challenges arriving at such a critical juncture in her contract negotiations left her unable to fully realize her goals with the promotion.

In addition to discussing her AEW experience, Kanellis also reflected on her time in TNA Impact Wrestling, specifically addressing her TNA Knockouts Championship victory in September 2016. She offered an honest assessment of that achievement, acknowledging that while the win worked for the storyline and the division at that particular time, she didn't believe she truly deserved it from an in-ring perspective.

"For the story, it was awesome. For where the division was at, it worked," Kanellis explained. "Because if you take something away, then people want it more. It's that old saying of playing hard to get, and I think at the time, it worked perfectly." She emphasized the strength of the women's division during that era, noting that she was fortunate to work alongside exceptional talent including Chelsea Green, Allysin Kay, Gail Kim, Mia Yim, and Rosemary.

"I was so blessed to work with such amazing women," she said. "But I didn't really deserve the title because I wasn't really 'wrestling' wrestling. But as far as for the championship itself and for the story, I think it worked really well." This candid self-assessment demonstrated Kanellis's awareness of her own role within the broader narrative structure of professional wrestling, showing her understanding of how storyline placement can serve a division's long-term interests even when individual achievement might be questioned.

🔥 Road Dogg WWE Departure

Road Dogg Explains His WWE Departure: Lack of Fun, Financial Fears, and Shifting Company Values

In March 2026, Brian "Road Dogg" James departed WWE after holding a backstage creative position with the company since 2014. At the time of his exit, he served as co-lead writer of WWE SmackDown, a position he had recently assumed in February 2025. Since then, James has opened up extensively about the reasons behind his decision during appearances on Busted Open Radio, providing candid insight into his mindset and the circumstances that led to his resignation.

The primary reason James cited for leaving was straightforward: he stopped having fun. "I wasn't having any fun. It got to be so much work, that it stopped being fun," he explained. The job had become increasingly burdensome, and rather than providing creative fulfillment, it felt like an exhausting obligation. James acknowledged that he had wanted to leave sooner but remained with the company due to fear of the unknown and unemployment concerns.

Financial insecurity played a significant role in keeping James at WWE longer than he preferred. He was candid about his personal struggles, stating: "I actually stayed longer than I wanted to just out of fear of unemployment. I didn't do well with my money. I'm a drug addict and recovering alcoholic. I didn't manage my money well. That fear of the unknown kept me for a long time." These admissions revealed the internal conflict James experienced between his deteriorating job satisfaction and his anxiety about losing steady income.

The breaking point came during a conversation with his wife. According to James, she called him while he was about to enter a meeting and told him, "I lived with you in a tent. I'll live with you in a tent." This moment of perspective shifted everything for him. He responded immediately: "I said, 'I'm coming home right now.' If you lived with me in a tent, I don't need to pay the mortgage." The conversation illustrated that his wife's support and their relationship mattered more than maintaining financial status quo, and that realization prompted his immediate resignation.

Beyond the lack of fun and personal circumstances, James also felt that he was no longer needed at WWE. The company was performing exceptionally well—setting records, breaking them, and firing on all cylinders financially. This success, paradoxically, made James feel superfluous. "They were firing on all cylinders and making money and setting records and breaking them. It was complacent. I felt like I wasn't earning it. I was getting a check, but I felt like I wasn't earning it," he said. He expressed that he still loves wrestling and wants to work in the industry, but specifically for an organization that genuinely wants and needs his contributions: "I still love wrestling, and I want to do wrestling. I want to do it for somebody that wants it and needs it. And I feel like WWE didn't want it or need it anymore."

James also discussed how the fundamental nature of WWE's booking philosophy had shifted or how his perception of it had changed. He observed that while booking may have always been influenced by financial considerations, the emphasis now felt overwhelmingly money-driven. "I think the booking used to be about wrestling, and I think the booking's maybe always been about the money, and I just thought it was about the wrestling. But it feels like it's just all about the money now," he stated. This shift in focus contributed to his dissatisfaction and desire to leave.

The competitive landscape, particularly the emergence of AEW as a rival promotion, influenced WWE's approach to business. When asked about how things changed since AEW's arrival, James acknowledged that the company had to make competitive financial offers to retain talent. However, he felt this focus on money had overshadowed the creative and wrestling-focused aspects of the business. "It feels like WWE, for me, was a money grab. It didn't feel right," he said bluntly.

James elaborated on how business decisions increasingly overrode creative considerations in his departure statement. "It seems like the later it got, the more decisions were made above the writing room's head and the inner circle's head. They are business people, and they do good business. Record-setting everything. The facts don't give an F about your feelings, and that's the facts." He acknowledged that from a purely business standpoint, WWE's approach made sense. However, the shift troubled him philosophically: "It felt like it turned into the business wrestling instead of the wrestling business, and in my mind, that's backward. In my wallet, it makes perfect sense. It felt weird and I wanted to get out there."

Regarding WWE's perception of AEW in its early years, James revealed that the company had significantly underestimated its competitor. Internally, AEW was viewed dismissively as "the southern little money-mark company," a perception that proved to be incorrect. Looking back, James acknowledged this miscalculation: "We didn't and we probably should have to be quite honest with you. I would argue however long we are now into their tenure that was wrong to be looking at it that way. Here they're still here. They're still strong. I would argue they're getting stronger."

James also praised recent additions to AEW's production team, specifically mentioning Mike Mansury, who oversees their broadcast production. He noted that such strategic hires demonstrate the importance of all the elements that go into producing a successful wrestling show: "There's more things that go into a wrestling show than a lot of people want to give credit for." This comment reflected his understanding of the business beyond just in-ring performance.

Throughout his candid discussions, James emphasized that his departure stemmed from a convergence of factors: loss of creative fulfillment, feeling unnecessary to the company's operations, the shift toward prioritizing profit over wrestling quality, and ultimately, a wake-up call from his wife about what truly mattered. While he remains under contract with WWE through a Legends deal, his active creative role with the company has ended, and he continues to process his departure through public interviews, using the platform as therapeutic reflection on his time with the organization.

Road Dogg, who served as co-lead writer of SmackDown until his departure from WWE in March 2025, has offered candid insight into how the company viewed rival promotion AEW during its rise to prominence. Speaking with Busted Open, the veteran wrestling personality explained that WWE never truly felt like they were in direct competition with Tony Khan's upstart company, a perspective he now believes was misguided.

When asked directly whether WWE felt embattled with AEW, Road Dogg did not hesitate to confirm they did not. "No, we didn't, and we probably should have," he stated during the interview. The comment references the famous moment when Triple H delivered what many perceived as a dismissive jab at AEW during his D-Generation X Hall of Fame induction speech in 2019, when he remarked that WWE would purchase "that pissant company" simply to fire Billy Gunn again.

Rather than viewing AEW as a legitimate threat to their market dominance, Road Dogg explained that WWE's internal perception was considerably more dismissive. "Instead, I think it was looked at like, 'Oh that's this other little money mark's company,'" he recalled of the prevailing attitude in WWE's executive circles. This characterization reflected a broader sentiment that AEW was merely a vanity project financed by a wealthy individual rather than a serious professional wrestling enterprise capable of sustained competition.

However, Road Dogg's retrospective assessment suggests this dismissive posture was strategically flawed. "I honestly think that's how it was looked at. I would argue that, however long we are into their tenure, that was wrong to be looking at it that way," he stated. He pointed to AEW's demonstrated longevity and competitive strength as evidence that WWE's initial perception underestimated the company's viability and market potential.

Road Dogg specifically highlighted AEW's recent organizational moves as signs of the promotion's continuing evolution and strengthening position. "They're still here, they're still strong, they're getting stronger. Adding Mike Masury was a huge step in the right direction. He's a smart guy with good instincts," he noted, referencing AEW's hiring decisions that he views as evidence of improved strategic direction.

The comments place Road Dogg among veteran wrestling professionals willing to acknowledge how WWE's initial underestimation of AEW may have allowed the younger promotion to establish itself more securely in the professional wrestling landscape. With over a decade of experience working behind the scenes within WWE, Road Dogg's observations carry particular weight regarding the internal culture and decision-making processes that shaped the company's response to AEW's emergence.

During the same interview, Road Dogg also discussed how AEW's presence in the market ultimately forced WWE to reconsider its own booking philosophy and creative direction, suggesting that the competitive pressure, whether acknowledged at the time or not, did eventually influence WWE's strategic decisions.

🔥 MJF comments on WWE vs AEW

MAJOR STORY: MJF PITCHES AEW AS WRESTLING'S BEST ALTERNATIVE TO WWE

During recent media appearances, Maxwell Jacob Friedman articulated a comprehensive case for AEW's superiority in the professional wrestling landscape, addressing both business metrics and creative quality while positioning himself as the face of the current wrestling era.

When asked whether AEW has seen increased interest from WWE fans due to rising ticket prices and talent treatment concerns, MJF confirmed the trend while maintaining professional respect for WWE and its parent company TKO. "Yeah, a lot of people who work there aren't particularly happy. People are taking pay cuts. The show right now, to each their own, but to a lot of people right now, pretty disenchanted with the product," MJF stated. He acknowledged WWE's talented roster and proven business track record but emphasized that wrestling fans are actively seeking alternatives.

"I think people are so ready for an alternative. I can't think of a better alternative than All Elite Wrestling, probably in the history of any sport," MJF declared. He directly contrasted this with AEW's current trajectory, noting that while the company faced significant backstage controversies in the past that created "a black eye" on the promotion, 2026 has represented a complete turnaround. "Every Dynamite has been incredible, Collision has been incredible, pay-per-view," he asserted.

Regarding wrestling quality specifically, MJF praised Darby Allin for consistently delivering exceptional matches across multiple weekly shows. "He is putting on better matches than any other place you watch professional wrestling. So do other guys on the roster." While MJF acknowledged that he personally prioritizes business fundamentals and drawing power over pure match quality, he refused to downplay AEW's in-ring product. "I'm a win guy. I'm a money guy. I'm a sell-tickets guy. I'm going to get people to buy a pay-per-view guy, but I'm not going to sit here and pretend that we don't have superior wrestling."

MJF stressed that AEW's growth is already occurring organically. "Year over year in most markets we've sold more tickets. This year we've done more pay-per-view buys. We're hot right now," he noted. When asked what could elevate AEW further, MJF argued that the company simply needs time to continue its trajectory, positioning AEW as a younger promotion with tremendous upside compared to WWE and TNA, which have operated for several decades.

AEW TALENT TREATMENT VERSUS WWE APPROACH

Addressing the specific issue of mid-contract pay cuts that have plagued WWE under TKO ownership, MJF confirmed that AEW handles contract negotiations fundamentally differently. He explained that several wrestlers, including Kofi Kingston and Xavier Woods of The New Day, recently departed WWE rather than accept significant pay reductions. MJF had high praise for both men, calling them "incredibly talented" and commending them for understanding their market value. "Whenever they are legally allowed to come over, I think that those are guys that would be incredible additions to the AEW roster," he stated.

When directly asked whether AEW has ever reduced wrestler pay during active contracts, MJF was unambiguous: "No, that's never happened." He explained that AEW operates under different philosophical and financial frameworks than WWE, though he acknowledged both companies are fundamentally trying to generate revenue. "It's a different WWE now. They're owned by TKO. So, they are trying to enhance money as much as humanly possible," MJF stated. He continued, "AEW is trying to make money too. But I just think we handle the business differently and we look at the business differently in our front office."

MJFS STANCE ON STAYING IN CHARACTER

MJF emphasized his commitment to maintaining his wrestling persona across all platforms, particularly on social media. He noted that when he originally adopted this full-time character approach, many wrestlers in the industry ostracized him for it, but he has since observed younger talent copying his blueprint. "I see people following what I started on social media now. I see younger guys doing it the way that they're tweeting or posting videos."

He sharply criticized wrestlers who portray intense rivalries on television while immediately praising those same opponents online afterward, viewing such behavior as detrimental to professional wrestling's authenticity. "When I see guys, and there are some of these guys I respect, but I see guys, they're on TV and then they go on Twitter, 'Oh my god, I just had such a great match with so and so.' It makes me want to vomit. It's disgusting. That's not why I got in this sport. I got in this sport to talk sh*t. I got in the sport to beat people up and I got in the sport to make a whole lot of money doing it."

MJF elaborated on his controversial approach, stating that offensive content drives engagement and business results. "We live in a society filled with weak people who are afraid of offending others. That's not something I'm afraid of. When I offend you, you buy a ticket. When I offend you, the ratings go up."

MJFS LEGACY AND GENERATIONAL TALENT STATUS

MJF positioned himself among wrestling's all-time greats, arguing that truly elite performers must excel in both promo delivery and in-ring performance simultaneously. "There aren't a lot of people that can talk and wrestle like me. And I'd imagine the last person that could at the level that I do was Flair," he stated, directly comparing himself to Ric Flair, Stone Cold Steve Austin, The Rock, and John Cena.

He doubled down on these claims heading into the AEW Double or Nothing pay-per-view, declaring that his legacy is already defining the current wrestling era. "I call myself a generational talent because I know when my run's over, when people talk about the 2020s, they're going to talk about MJF. They're not going to have a choice. Um, because I am the focal point of this era and I'm about to become on this Sunday a three-time world champion by 30."

COMPLEX TAKES ON INDUSTRY FIGURES

MJF maintained a nuanced perspective on CM Punk despite their history, acknowledging Punk's wrestling excellence while sharply criticizing his character. When asked about Punk's WWE success, MJF stated bluntly: "I'm not surprised at how good he's doing. Doesn't change the fact that he's a f*cking c*nt. He just sucks. He's a bad guy, but he's an incredible professional wrestler. He's a piece of sh*t, but he's a great wrestler."

Regarding Mick Foley's potential AEW signing, MJF expressed strong opposition, viewing Foley as the blueprint for Darby Allin and therefore resenting him on principle. "I think Mick Foley is fat, and he's ugly. And I think he was the blueprint for what Darby Allin is now, which makes me hate him even more," MJF said.

On TNA President Carlos Silva, MJF initially engaged in heated criticism over perceived slights but softened his stance after learning Silva was relatively new to the wrestling business and learning on the job. "I've been told that he's a good guy, so I've pumped the brakes a little bit on ol' Carlos Silva. He's learning on the job."

AEW LOCKER ROOM CULTURE

MJF highlighted changes in AEW's locker room mentality, noting that the company no longer attracts talent viewing it as a stepping stone to WWE. "There's no longer people in the locker room that look at my company as a stepping stone. They look at it as a legitimate company. We had some guys that came in and they were like, 'I'm going to show up, be the top guy, and go back to Vince.' Really motherf*cker? You're going to leapfrog me? Good luck p*ssy. Not happening on my watch," he stated forcefully.

This cultural shift, MJF argued, has been instrumental in AEW's 2026 resurgence and its current positioning as WWE's legitimate competition in the professional wrestling landscape.

🔥 Parker Boudreaux discusses WWE and AEW differences

Despite feeling that he was doing everything correctly and performing well, Boudreaux was blindsided by his release. The release came shortly after what he considered a strong workout at the Performance Center during the COVID era. John Laurinaitis made the call informing him of the decision, citing budget cuts as the reason.

Looking back, Boudreaux has made peace with his WWE departure, noting that Laurinaitis is no longer with the company and that he maintains solid relationships with various WWE superstars and executives. This transition marked a turning point in how Boudreaux approached his career. While his WWE tenure was shorter than expected due to the unexpected release, he has since traveled internationally, trained at dojos in Japan, and developed various wrestling techniques that have contributed to his overall growth as a performer.

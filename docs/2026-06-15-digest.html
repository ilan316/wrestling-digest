🔥 Jack Perry AEW contract near expiration

Jack Perry's AEW future is currently being worked out as his contract nears completion. Perry has been with AEW since the company started in 2019, but his current deal is approaching the finish line. Bryan Alvarez provided an update on the situation, noting that Perry is now negotiating a new contract with the company.

Perry last wrestled on the May 27 episode of AEW Dynamite, where he lost to Mark Davis in an Owen Hart Foundation tournament match. That came after Davis beat him for the AEW National Championship on May 9 during the Fairway to Hell special.

Throughout his tenure, Perry has had several different chapters in AEW. He first made his name as Jungle Boy and teamed with Luchasaurus as Jurassic Express. In 2023, he turned heel and later won the FTW Championship. His AEW run also included one of the company's most infamous backstage stories when he was suspended after a backstage altercation with CM Punk, which later led to Punk's termination from the promotion.

Perry returned in 2024 and joined a new version of The Elite. He later won the TNT Championship, but after losing that title, he stayed off television through the fall of 2025 before returning and reuniting with Luchasaurus.

As part of his character reinvention, Perry moved away from his iconic "Tarzan Boy" entrance theme that had become one of the most recognizable entrance themes in AEW due to crowd participation. He eventually debuted a Beethoven track to accompany his darker persona. However, it was revealed that Alice In Chains' "Them Bones" was strongly considered and at one point planned to serve as Perry's entrance music before a late decision was made to go in a different direction.

Perry's contract situation is something to watch moving forward. AEW has invested considerable television time into him over the years, and with negotiations happening, fans will have to wait and see whether he stays with the company long-term.

🔥 AEW Survival of the Fittest TBS Championship tournament

Hikaru Shida was the first to punch her ticket to the championship match as she defeated Zayda Steel. Chris Jericho reveals why he retired Y2J persona

Chris Jericho Reflects on His Greatest Career Reinvention and Advice for Modern Wrestlers

Former AEW World Champion Chris Jericho recently discussed two pivotal aspects of his wrestling career: his decision to retire the Y2J persona and his perspective on what young wrestlers misunderstand about connecting with audiences.

Over nearly four decades, Jericho has built a reputation as one of wrestling's most versatile performers, constantly reinventing himself with characters like The List, Le Champion, The Painmaker, The Wizard, and The Learning Tree. Each iteration has helped him remain relevant and connected with fans. However, he credits retiring the iconic Y2J character as his biggest career risk.

Speaking with Dan Marston, Jericho revealed that when he returned in 2007, he recognized he was drifting into nostalgia territory. He explained: "I think probably when I dropped the whole Y2J persona and the countdown. There was a time when I came back in 2007 where I was kind of not gonna say a diluted version of Chris Jericho, but it wasn't the same. I had shorter hair and times had changed, right?"

Rather than lean into the familiar, Jericho made a drastic decision. "So, I thought I don't want to do this anymore. I don't want to be a nostalgia act. So, I switched from, you know, I cut my hair, like I said, and I switched from long tights to trunks. Dropped the Y2J. I told the announcers, 'Don't ever call me Y2J ever again.' Did a whole new video package that had none of that. And then the countdown was so synonymous with Jericho. Get rid of the countdown. It's done."

The gamble initially felt daunting. Jericho compared the move to KISS removing their famous makeup: "And that was kind of the first time where I really took a shot because I was like, it's almost like Kiss when they took off the makeup." He continued, "You're doing something very drastic here and you want people to understand we're doing something different. Whether you like it or don't like it, that's not for us to decide, but here's where we're going. Here's the path that we're taking. Come on board if you want to. And that was kind of it. It was a big risk, but not for long. And it's something that I had to do."

The reinvention proved successful and taught Jericho a crucial lesson about his career trajectory. Former WWE Star Raj Dhesi (AKA Jinder Mahal) Reflects On Legacy Of 3MB

Former WWE star Raj Dhesi, known professionally as Jinder Mahal, recently shared his thoughts on the legacy of Three Man Band, one of wrestling's more unique stables. The group, which formed in September 2012, featured Heath Slater, Drew McIntyre, and Dhesi, and became known for their air guitar performances as they made their way to the ring.

While 3MB was not particularly successful during its active run, with the faction rarely winning matches or titles, the group has experienced a significant shift in how it is remembered by fans. Speaking on Busted Open Radio, Dhesi reflected on this change in perception, noting that the stable is viewed far more favorably now than it was during its original tenure.

Dhesi shared an interesting story about the group's time performing in England, revealing that they experienced a level of popularity there that they had not encountered elsewhere. He explained that writers had plans to rebrand the trio as the Union Jacks, complete with corresponding attire, with the expectation that they would be squashed in their match.

"We got told by one of the writers that, 'You guys are dressing up today in the Union Jacks and you guys will be called the 'Union Jacks' going out there,' and we're going to get squashed by whoever," he explained. "We're in gorilla, the music hits. It's some generic music, and on the titantron, it says 'Union Jacks....' We see 'Union Jacks' come on the screen. Crowd is silent. Then, the screen changes to 'Three Man Band' and the 3MB music hits, and we're like, 'What is going on?' We've never heard a pop like that, a reception like that anywhere."

Dhesi attributed the current nostalgia and appreciation for 3MB to the fact that many fans grew up watching the faction during its run. He emphasized that the group represented something fun and lighthearted during an era when professional wrestling was less serious than it is today under current management. He contrasted this with the more serious tone of contemporary wrestling.

Despite the stable's lack of in-ring success, Dhesi expressed gratitude for the experience, recognizing its importance to his career trajectory. He credited his time in 3MB, combined with his subsequent WWE release, as the foundation that eventually led to his rise to prominence, culminating in his achievement of becoming WWE Champion.

"Without that experience, going through 3MB, getting released, I wouldn't have became WWE Champion," he said, according to Busted Open Radio.

KENNY OMEGA WINS SECOND AEW WORLD CHAMPIONSHIP, MAJOR WRESTLING MOMENTS

Kenny Omega has reclaimed the AEW World Championship, defeating MJF in the main event of AEW Dynamite Beach Break on July 8 in Clearwater, Florida. This victory marks Omega's second reign as AEW World Champion, coming 1,698 days after he last held the title, which he previously possessed from December 2020 through November 2021.

The match itself drew widespread critical acclaim from the professional wrestling community. Tommy Dreamer, speaking on Busted Open Radio, described the encounter as "a love letter to professional wrestling." Dreamer highlighted the dramatic near-fall moments, particularly noting how MJF low-blowed Kenny Omega and struck him with the AEW World Championship, only for Omega to kick out at the count of one. Dreamer recalled the crowd's energy reaching another level, with fans fully believing in Omega's comeback. The veteran wrestler emphasized that the match delivered on every level, with the adulation from the crowd palpable even through television.

Mick Foley, who appeared on the AEW Dynamite Beach Break card in an interview segment with Kyle Fletcher, took to Instagram to express his congratulations to Omega. Foley emphasized the significance of Omega's achievement, stating that Kenny has been great for a long time and deserves credit for overcoming his injuries and adversity to claim the title. Foley praised the atmosphere in Clearwater Beach, describing a feeling of electricity in the crowd and sense of community despite the heat and humidity. Foley concluded his message by expressing his fortune at working for AEW and its incredibly talented roster. Foley also encouraged fans to watch the MJF versus Omega match, calling MJF a "machine" while crediting Omega for the resilience he demonstrated.

In his post-match statements, Kenny Omega delivered an emotional promo to the crowd after the television broadcast ended. Omega reflected on the difficulty of his journey, noting that none of it came easy and that he could not have achieved this without the support of his friends, family, and the fans. Omega stated that while holding the belt might not seem proper, holding what it represents—the AEW World Championship—means the world to him. He promised to be the best champion he could be and pledged to keep the championship around his waist until someone proves they are good enough, willing to sacrifice enough, and willing to push hard enough to take it from him. Omega acknowledged his position nearer to the end than the beginning of his journey but emphasized the importance of finishing hard and finishing the race. He also gave credit to MJF for never having to take the match but doing so anyway, defending the belt regularly and showing up every week, setting a good example. Omega concluded by promising to be a champion that MJF himself would be jealous of.

On social media, Omega posted a message expressing gratitude to his supporters. He wrote: "After 1,698 days of waiting to get it back, we finished the race. Thank you for never giving up on me and always believing, one more time." This statement encapsulated the emotional weight of his return to the championship after such an extended absence, reflecting the injuries and health issues he has battled throughout his career.

The victory came with significant stipulations. Had Omega lost the match, he would never have been allowed to challenge for the AEW World Championship again, making this victory even more consequential for his career going forward.

🔥 Finn Balor WWE and NJPW Career Reflections

Finn Balor Reflects on Career Decisions, Injuries, and the Match That Never Was

In a comprehensive interview on the "Insight" podcast with Chris Van Vliet, Finn Balor opened up about several pivotal moments in his wrestling career, from his transition out of New Japan Pro Wrestling to his near-fatal injury during a championship match, and the dream matchups that almost happened but never materialized.

Before joining WWE in 2014, Balor was a dominant force in New Japan Pro Wrestling, where as Prince Devitt he ruled the Junior Heavyweight division, created the Bullet Club, and became one of the most recognizable foreign talents in the promotion. When WWE came calling multiple times during his eight-year NJPW tenure, Balor initially declined because he was content and wanted to finish his career in Japan. However, in 2014 when he was 34 years old, everything changed. Balor was attempting to negotiate a 10-year contract to stay with NJPW for the remainder of his career, but a significant obstacle emerged. Because New Japan was owned by another company at the time, they could only offer him a four-year deal. Given that Balor had already been there for eight years and had witnessed how the promotion phased out wrestlers once they reached their mid-to-late 30s, he realized that a four-year extension would leave him stranded in his late 30s without job security.

The decision became clearer when Balor realized WWE was aggressively signing younger talent and he understood it might be his final opportunity to make the jump. What ultimately pushed him toward WWE, however, was a conversation with his father. His dad reminded Balor that growing up, he had always dreamed of wrestling in WWE. While Balor was enjoying tremendous success in Japan, his father knew it was time for his son to take the chance. Following his father's advice proved to be one of the best decisions of Balor's career, as he has reflected fondly on the move ever since.

One of Balor's most significant moments in WWE came during his 2016 Universal Championship match against Seth Rollins at SummerSlam, an event that continues to haunt him. Despite winning the inaugural Universal Championship, Balor suffered a severe shoulder injury during the bout. He described the experience on the "Insight" podcast, explaining that while he was not in excruciating pain, he immediately knew something was seriously wrong. The injury dislocated multiple times throughout the match whenever he lifted his arm to hit the ropes or got whipped to the buckle.

During the match, Balor was fortunate to be working with Rollins, whom he praised as an absolute professional. The two were able to communicate constantly, calling the match on the fly and improvising changes to spots as the injury became increasingly apparent. The match they ultimately performed was significantly different from what had been planned. At one point outside the ring, Balor told Rollins that he believed he was hurt seriously and might not be able to wrestle for an extended period. Balor then asked if Rollins wanted to switch the finish, but Rollins responded with a firm "No, stick to the plan." Balor has often wondered what impact that decision had on his career trajectory, noting that if they had changed the finish, he might have been released before even returning from injury or potentially never become WWE Champion.

When asked to reflect on whether he had ever rewatched the match, Balor admitted he has not and actively avoids it. He explained that he gets tagged in tweets about the match regularly on social media and scrolls away before reaching the end, saying he dislikes seeing it. To this day, Balor addresses misconceptions about his injury history, noting that despite the incident, he has had only one major injury across his 24-year wrestling career, aside from an accidental broken jaw. He has torn muscles, but that is a regular part of the profession for all wrestlers.

Beyond the shoulder injury, Balor discussed other physical setbacks, including a calf tear he suffered just three weeks before WrestleMania 39. He was asked to make an impromptu appearance on SmackDown when he normally performed on Raw, and during a six-man tag match, he tore his calf while stepping backward to tag Damian. He initially thought Cruz Del Toro was messing with him by slapping his leg, but when he looked down, he realized the severity of the injury. Despite the damage, Balor finished the match, which he believes made the injury worse by adding additional stress to the tear. For three weeks leading up to WrestleMania, Balor was confined to a mobility scooter backstage. A promo shot on Raw just two weeks before WrestleMania was conducted carefully, with Balor limping out onto the stage while the camera angles were adjusted to hide his condition, after which he retreated backstage on his scooter.

One of the most intriguing revelations from Balor's interview was that WWE had seriously considered booking a dream match between The Demon and Bray Wyatt's The Fiend. The history of transitional champions in wrestling

The history of transitional champions in wrestling demonstrates that short title reigns serve a vital purpose in the sport. Last Monday, Sami Zayn lost the WWE Championship to CM Punk and became the latest wrestler to enter the transitional champion club. But something happened after he lost the title. Everybody who is anti-TKO/WWE, including The Coach and Vince Russo, got angry at the fact that WWE took the belt off Zayn after only nine days, mostly because of a report claiming that WWE doesn't see Zayn as a top guy. The funny thing is, those same people who were mad that WWE took the belt off Zayn were the same people who were mad nine days earlier when WWE decided to put the belt on him. It's hard to take them seriously when they complain about Zayn being champion.

Since all those people seem to have forgotten how wrestling works and that a transitional champion is just as important to wrestling as having a long title reign, it's worth looking at some of the most famous transitional champions in WWE/F history. Ivan Koloff was basically the first transitional champion in WWE history. WWE needed to take the title off Bruno Sammartino in 1971 and eventually get it to Pedro Morales, so Koloff was chosen to defeat Sammartino, only to lose the belt to Morales two weeks later. After losing the title, Koloff never won the WWE Championship or any other world title again, but he still had a legendary career and became a WWE Hall of Famer.

The Iron Sheik is probably the most famous transitional champion in wrestling history. In 1983, Vince McMahon Jr. had just bought the company from his father and wanted to take the company national, but he knew he couldn't do that with Bob Backlund as champion. So he signed Hulk Hogan away from the AWA and wanted to make him the face of the company. The problem was that, back then, you generally didn't do face-versus-face title matches. Vince decided to have The Iron Sheik beat Backlund for the championship so he could lose it to Hogan a few weeks later. After that, Sheik continued to have a strong career in the mid-card and eventually became a WWE Hall of Famer.

The Undertaker is one of the greatest legends in WWE history, but in 1991 he was still a relatively new character, and nobody knew if he would be a flash in the pan or have a long career. WWE decided to have him win the WWF Championship at Survivor Series 1991 as a test to see if fans would accept him as champion. That title reign lasted only six days before he dropped the championship the following week. After that, he wouldn't win another world title until 1997.

Kane won the WWE Championship only once, at King of the Ring 1998, when he defeated Steve Austin in a First Blood Match. He dropped the title the following night on Raw back to Austin and never won the WWE Championship again. He did go on to win the World Heavyweight Championship and the ECW Championship later in his career, and he still became a WWE Hall of Famer.

During the Ruthless Aggression Era, a lot of wrestlers had short title reigns. Edge, Jeff Hardy, Triple H, Randy Orton, Hulk Hogan, The Undertaker, CM Punk, and The Rock all had relatively short championship reigns at various points. They're all either WWE Hall of Famers or future Hall of Famers. Short title reigns have always been part of wrestling.

The Miz was the last wrestler to truly be a transitional WWE Champion. His second reign in 2021 lasted only eight days because WWE wanted to take the title off Drew McIntyre and put it on Bobby Lashley. As a great heel, Miz cashed in his Money in the Bank contract on McIntyre at Elimination Chamber and then lost the championship to Lashley on Raw. After that, he returned to the mid-card, continued being one of WWE's most reliable performers, and is widely considered a future Hall of Famer.

In the end, there are plenty of examples showing that being a transitional champion is an important role in wrestling. This outrage over Zayn becoming one is just that—outrage for the sake of outrage.

Was TNA actually good or were we just starved for a WWE alternative? | Opinion

Was TNA actually good or were we just starved for a WWE alternative? This is the question posed in a recent retrospective that examines one fan's journey with Total Nonstop Action Wrestling. The author prefaces his analysis by stating he was a big fan of TNA from around 2003 to 2009, specifically until the company removed the six-sided ring. After watching the first episode of the Dark Side of the Ring special focused on the company, he wanted to genuinely assess whether TNA was a quality product or whether he had latched onto it simply because there was a lack of major competition to WWE during that era.

The author's wrestling fandom journey began in the UK, where he had access to The Wrestling Channel on Sky. This was where he consumed most of his wrestling content during TNA's rise. He had previously been a fan of WWF through the end of the Invasion angle but lost interest when the Channel 4 deal ended and PPVs moved to Sky Sports, which he did not have access to. TNA filled that void and looked and felt like a proper wrestling show at the time. The author acknowledges he would go back and tell his teenage self to explore more independent wrestling like Ring of Honor, but he genuinely enjoyed seeing familiar performers like Rhyno, Raven, and Jeff Jarrett alongside newer talent like AJ Styles, Christopher Daniels, and Petey Williams presented in a format he already understood.

There is considerable revisionist history surrounding TNA, the author argues, similar to how many products from the 2000s through mid-2015 are now reexamined. A large group of wrestling fans didn't grow up with anything else and became accustomed to that particular product. The author cites CM Punk's 2011 pipebomb as an example of this revisionism. That promo was so different and fresh at the time partly because the WWE product was mostly awful. Some fans now claim they enjoyed WWE in the early 2010s, which is their right, but the author did not share that view apart from Punk, whose segments he watched weekly.

The author identifies several issues with TNA that harked back to WCW. While TNA and WCW comparisons can feel lazy, the parallels become more apparent when considering the structure: a great lower card consisting of the X-Division, the Cruiserweight Division equivalent, the Knockouts division, and strong tag team wrestling, but a main event held together mostly by older performers like Jarrett, Nash, and Steiner. These comparisons don't feel completely off-base when examined objectively.

The author pushes back against the notion that TNA was only watched because there was nothing else challenging WWE's international grip, but acknowledges that mistakes were made alongside genuine positives. He cites the Unbreakable 2005 triple threat match as his favorite match of that year and suggests it might still be his favorite match of all time. Having now watched most of the products he missed during that mid-2000s timeframe, including ROH, the author believes that TNA, as an alternative to WWE, didn't go far enough to lean into a truly alternative angle.

The term WWE-lite bothers the author, though he acknowledges there were attempts to recapture the feeling of the late 1990s with Vince Russo heading creative. He doesn't think Russo was a completely terrible writer, noting that he had a formula that worked for a time but struggled to recreate that success later. Additionally, many people in TNA had worked for WCW in its late stages or for WWE and WWF, so it's unsurprising that storytelling and production elements felt similar to the bigger, more established product. However, the author recognizes the irony in his position: the presentation with decent production and familiar names is exactly what drew him in originally and made him a fan. He enjoyed seeing performers he knew from the late 1990s and early 2000s.

The author identifies a contradiction in his own fandom. He initially complained about WWE-lite styling, yet that very approach worked on him as a viewer. TNA drew him in with names like Raven and kept him invested with the X-Division's innovative wrestling. This contradiction sits at the heart of the question about whether TNA was genuinely good or simply a good alternative that benefited from the hunger for something different during an era when WWE dominated the landscape. Ultimately, the author suggests this nuance matters more than a simple yes or no answer.

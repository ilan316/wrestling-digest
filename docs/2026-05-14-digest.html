🔥 Swerve Strickland on Darby Allin's Title Run

Swerve Strickland believes All Elite Wrestling could have done significantly more to prepare Darby Allin for his AEW World Championship victory over MJF. While Strickland praised Allin's performances since winning the title on the April 15 episode of AEW Dynamite, he suggested the original setup for the championship change may not have fully positioned Allin at the same level as previous top stars.

Speaking during an appearance on The Bootleg Kev Podcast, Strickland reflected on Allin's surprising title victory and the creative decisions surrounding it. "I feel like there could've been more done for Darby for that moment," Strickland said. "I feel like it anchored Darby a little too much."

Strickland compared Allin's presentation to the lengthy showcase matches AEW previously built around MJF, including a 46-minute Texas Death Match and a 38-minute match against Kenny Omega. These extended matches served to elevate MJF's credibility and establish him as a main event player worthy of top billing. "I'm not saying Darby's not qualified to compete with that, but you've got to make Darby qualified to compete like that," Strickland explained. "I feel like they didn't do him a favor with that."

Despite his criticism of the initial setup, Strickland made it clear he believes Allin's championship reign has quickly overcome those concerns through sheer consistency and performance. "But Darby is so talented that now his run is making up for that," Strickland said. Since defeating MJF for the title, Allin has already defended the AEW World Championship against Tommaso Ciampa, PAC, Brody King, and Kevin Knight in rapid succession.

Strickland praised Allin's demanding in-ring schedule and his willingness to consistently work high-profile matches on television. "He just killed it with Kevin Knight. Now he's about to go against PAC. Then Wednesday, possibly against Okada," Strickland said. "Who's setting their schedules up to go like that on TV?" He added, "He's just so undeniably talented that you can't — it's undeniable."

Strickland also briefly addressed the upcoming Hair vs. Title stipulation between Allin and MJF at Double or Nothing, although he admitted he was unsure how much fans truly cared about MJF's hair. The stipulation has raised eyebrows among wrestling fans and observers, though stranger stipulations have been debated throughout wrestling history.

For now, Strickland's comments paint a picture of a champion who may not have received the perfect creative runway, but has quickly forced audiences to believe in the reign through performance alone. Allin's willingness to take on top-tier competition on a frequent basis has helped establish his credibility as a world champion despite the lack of extensive groundwork leading into his title victory.

🔥 MJF vs. Darby Allin Hair vs. Title Match

MJF OFFICIALLY AGREES TO HAIR VS. TITLE STIPULATION BEFORE ATTACKING DARBY ALLIN

The May 13 episode of AEW Dynamite culminated in MJF finally putting his signature hair on the line against Darby Allin during the AEW World Championship match scheduled for Double or Nothing. However, the contract signing did not proceed without chaos, as Friedman blindsided the exhausted champion immediately after agreeing to the high-stakes stipulation.

The segment took place after Allin successfully retained the AEW World Championship against Konosuke Takeshita in the night's main event. Allin survived a grueling title defense and retained with back-to-back Coffin Drops before immediately calling MJF to the ring to finalize the contract for their upcoming match.

The unusual hair vs. title stipulation stemmed from Allin's demand for additional stakes in exchange for granting MJF another championship opportunity. The AEW World Champion told MJF the only way he would receive another title shot was by risking his hair, even referencing MJF's reported hair transplant trip to Turkey. Allin mocked Friedman by suggesting he cared more about his hair than the world title itself. This sparked what AEW would lean into heavily throughout the week leading up to Dynamite, with the company fully embracing the follicle feud angle.

Before the contract signing even took place, AEW ramped up the psychological warfare against MJF. During a backstage segment on Dynamite, Friedman was stopped by interviewer Renee Paquette and asked if he was really willing to risk his hair for another shot at the AEW World title. MJF remained silent and left her hanging. As he walked through the parking lot, bald people slowly started appearing around him in what was clearly orchestrated by AEW. The company gathered bald referees, wrestlers, and backstage staff specifically to tease Friedman. Tommaso Ciampa deliberately removed his cap to show his bald head to MJF and deliver the message. Ricochet removed his cap and teased the former AEW World Champion, saying, "Don't worry Max, you're gonna look great, just not good as me." Throughout these segments, fans continuously chanted "Bald!" at Friedman, adding to the mounting pressure heading into the contract signing.

When MJF finally made his way to the ring for the contract signing, the crowd showered him with "Bald!" chants as he repeatedly hesitated to put pen to paper. The former AEW World Champion launched a lengthy verbal tirade, dismissing Allin's legacy and claiming he would become a three-time world champion at Double or Nothing. He bragged about being destined to become the greatest wrestler in company history while Allin, who had just competed in a grueling main event match, grew increasingly impatient waiting for MJF to sign.

Allin fired back by accusing MJF of caring only about legacy and demanded he sign the contract so he could make him a "bald-headed bitch." With the crowd chanting along, MJF finally relented and signed the deal, making the match official.

However, the moment the contract was signed, Friedman's demeanor changed instantly. MJF blindsided the exhausted AEW World Champion with grounded punches before loading up the Dynamite Diamond Ring and dropping Allin with a knockout shot. The assault left the champion laid out in the ring while MJF stood over him screaming about becoming a three-time AEW World Champion. Friedman then got back in the ring and nearly delivered an avalanche Tombstone Piledriver in the corner before Kevin Knight sprinted to the ring to stop the assault.

This chaotic ending officially set the stage for one of AEW's most personal championship matches heading into Double or Nothing. Hair vs. title stipulations have historically created memorable moments in professional wrestling, and MJF's visible fear and reluctance throughout the week has added genuine emotional stakes to the feud. The storyline demonstrates AEW's commitment to building narrative tension through both serious and comedic elements, with the company managing to make the angle humorous while still maintaining real consequences for the match.

Allin has now successfully defended the AEW World Championship five times since winning the title in his hometown of Everett, Washington, in a shocking upset victory over MJF. MJF has been desperately seeking his rematch ever since his loss, even attempting to bribe Allin with money and his famous Dynamite Diamond Ring for another title shot before being rejected. With the hair stipulation now official, MJF will be looking for his third AEW World Championship reign at Double or Nothing, taking place in Queens, New York next Sunday.

🔥 Jack Perry CM Punk Fallout and Character Change

Jack Perry has opened up extensively about how the infamous backstage incident with CM Punk at AEW All In 2023 fundamentally changed his career trajectory and personal mindset. During the pre-show of that event, Perry defeated Darby Allin in a Coffin Match to retain the AEW TNT Championship, but a spot during the match angered CM Punk backstage, leading to Punk attacking Perry. This altercation ultimately resulted in Punk's firing from AEW, but left Perry immediately labeled as the villain in the eyes of a large portion of the wrestling fanbase.

Perry recently discussed the entire situation while appearing on Talk Is Jericho with Chris Jericho. He admitted that the initial period was brutal for him mentally. "During the time, it was not nice. It was uncomfortable and scary," Perry said. "That's why I think I ended up growing a lot because it forced me out of my comfort zone. I was out for a long time. At that time, it was not something I ever anticipated happening, obviously. I always just imagined what my road would look like. Work hard, do this. I never saw something like this."

What Perry found most transformative was the realization that came from being turned on by such a significant portion of the wrestling world. Throughout his career, he had always been driven by a need to make everyone like him, but the backlash forced him to confront an uncomfortable truth. "Now, I'm kind of grateful for it because a huge portion of the wrestling world turned on me, and I had always been wanting to make everybody like me," Perry explained. "It was kind of holding me back as well because it's not possible for everyone to like you. I was kind of playing it safe in a lot of ways."

The mental shift came when Perry accepted that some fans would never support him regardless of his actions. This realization became liberating rather than defeating. "I realized, at a certain point, 'Okay, now it's done.' There are people now where, they're never going to like me. When I came to terms with that and realized that it kind of frees me because I don't have to worry about trying to get this guy to like me, it's like the shackles were off. I just dove into the deep end at that point. It's not going to get worse."

The genesis of the Scapegoat character came directly from his conversation with Jericho during this turbulent period. Perry recalled texting Jericho while at the gym, expressing how things were not going well. Jericho's response proved pivotal. "You said, 'Yeah, you're the scapegoat.' As soon as I saw the word, I was like, 'That's interesting.' I didn't actually know what it was or where it came from," Perry said.

Curious about the term, Perry researched its origins and discovered it had deep religious roots. "I looked it up, and it's an ancient religious thing where once a year, people used to sacrifice a goat to the devil, 'please forgive us for our sins. Here is a goat.' I thought it was cool because I was thinking I was the goat, and now I'm sacrificed to pay for the sins of other people. I put the word and imagery and made it this thing."

The religious symbolism resonated even more deeply when Perry learned about the archetypal connection. "It says the main archetype of the goat is Jesus, and I thought it was cool," he explained. This became central to his character conception, though some fans drew comparisons to the late Raven's work. Perry was quick to clarify his inspiration. "People thought I was trying to be Raven. I was trying to be Jesus. I think a lot of that is what Raven was doing as well. Let me be Jesus in this form of wrestling with this dark thing around. I really got into this thing I was creating."

After disappearing from AEW television, Perry eventually resurfaced in New Japan Pro-Wrestling under the Scapegoat persona, and it was during his time in Japan that his performance style evolved significantly. He learned from watching wrestlers like Gabe Kidd, who demonstrated a more unscripted, authentic approach to promos. The Japanese environment, where performers could be more loose and natural, helped Perry shed his tendency to overthink his words.

"This allowed me to be me, but also get into this thematic stuff that was going on that felt like it could relate to my real life," Perry said about his evolution. "I got to go to Japan and they don't say anything, you come back from the match and there is a camera rolling. I watched Gabe Kidd, who is one of my favorite promo guys, he'd come back and be going insane. You can do that? It allowed me to come back and not be so in my head about what I was going to say. I just said what I felt. That started the whole thing of What do I want to do?"

Perry returned to AEW television in April 2024 as part of the new Elite faction alongside Kazuchika Okada and the Young Bucks. Though he recently lost the AEW National Championship to Mark Davis in May, Perry seems convinced that the CM Punk fallout, while initially devastating, ultimately unlocked a completely different and more authentic version of himself as a performer. What began as one of AEW's ugliest controversies transformed into the catalyst for Perry's greatest evolution as a wrestler and character performer.

The pivotal turning point in Fatu's life did not come from a counselor, a fellow inmate, or a community program. Instead, it came from a television set mounted on a dorm wall. While flipping through channels during one of the monotonous days that characterized jail life, he stopped on a WWE broadcast and watched his cousins Jimmy and Jey Uso, known professionally as The Usos, working a match. In that moment, something clicked. He has stated that watching his cousins perform was when his mind was made up about his future. But what truly broke him open was something far more simple and far more painful than any career epiphany. It was the image of his mother showing up to that jail and putting up her van to secure his release. Rikishi, his uncle and a WWE Hall of Famer, took him in and started him from scratch in 2012. What followed was years of paying dues with virtually no fan acknowledgment, a rite of passage that is common throughout professional wrestling. Fatu experienced all the obstacles that define the independent wrestling scene: the constant fatigue, performances in small venues, long drives between cities, dealings with shady promoters, and paychecks that barely covered gas expenses. These roadblocks are part of the standard path for independent talent, but Fatu refused to let them stop his progression. The grind continued relentlessly, night after night, performance after performance. Throughout his reign, Fatu was a fighting champion who defended his title everywhere against everyone. By the end of that title run, there was no serious argument left about whether he belonged at the top level of professional wrestling. As Fatu himself stated it, he had gone from looking out a jail cell window to looking out an airplane window. Before arriving in WWE, Fatu had wrestled for numerous independent promotions including DEFY Wrestling, GCW, HOG, and PCW Ultra, establishing himself as a formidable competitor across the entire landscape.

🔥 AEW-Ryan Nemeth Legal Battle

AEW has suffered a significant setback in its legal battle with former wrestler Ryan Nemeth after a federal judge rejected the company's bid to keep his wrestling contracts completely secret from public view. Magistrate Judge Patricia Barksdale of the United States District Court for the Middle District of Florida ruled on Tuesday that while AEW can refile redacted versions of the three contracts with a new motion to seal, the company cannot fully hide the agreements from public record.

The ruling came after Brandon Thurston of Wrestlenomics opposed AEW's original motion to seal the contracts, arguing that wrestler agreements should not be completely hidden from the public, particularly when they become part of court proceedings. Under the judge's decision, Thurston retains the right to file another opposition if he believes AEW's redacted versions black out more information than necessary, giving the public interest a continued voice in the proceedings.

AEW had argued that the contracts contain highly confidential information including pay details, business practices, and intellectual property language that should remain private. The company's push to keep the contracts sealed is tied to its larger legal strategy, as it filed a petition in June 2025 seeking to force Nemeth's dispute into private arbitration in Florida under a JAMS administrative office near Duval County, where AEW is headquartered.

Nemeth originally filed his lawsuit in February 2025 against AEW, Tony Khan, and CM Punk, alleging assault, breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and intentional interference with prospective advantage. The allegations stem from an altercation between Nemeth and CM Punk in 2023. AEW and Khan responded by countersuing and pushing to move the matter into private arbitration. While the two parties previously agreed to arbitration in April, they are currently disputing the location, with Nemeth arguing for California while AEW and Khan seek Florida.

The judge's ruling puts AEW in a difficult procedural position. If the company wants the court to consider the contracts as part of its arbitration enforcement effort, it must file redacted versions publicly along with a revised motion explaining and justifying the redactions. However, if AEW chooses to withdraw the contracts as exhibits entirely, the judge cannot use them when deciding whether the dispute should be handled in arbitration, potentially weakening AEW's case for moving the matter to Florida.

The court has established clear deadlines for the next phase of the dispute. If Nemeth files his first response to AEW's arbitration petition, AEW will have 21 days to submit the revised motion and redacted contracts. If Nemeth does not respond, the company must file the updated documents by September 24, which is 111 days after AEW's initial June 5 complaint. The court also denied a request for electronic filing access from the objecting party, meaning future submissions must be made by physical mail.

This ruling does not resolve the underlying dispute between Nemeth and AEW, nor does it determine the merits of either party's claims. It addresses only the procedural question of public access to the contracts and how much sensitive information AEW can shield while attempting to use those agreements as evidence in court. The decision reflects a broader tension between corporate confidentiality interests and the judicial system's presumption that legal proceedings and court records should generally remain accessible to the public.

WWE Trademark RKO Filing

WWE has made a new trademark filing for "RKO" after its most recent attempt to trademark the term was refused. On July 6, WWE submitted another trademark for Randy Orton's finishing move. The company already has active "RKO" trademarks for merchandising purposes, but WWE's 2024 application related to wrestling performances was refused over what the patent and trademark office determined to be potential confusion with marks owned by RKO Pictures, the classic Hollywood studio that had a hand in distributing movies like "Citizen Kane" and "It's A Wonderful Life."

It's been an ongoing process since the initial refusal, with the patent office ruling in January 2026 that WWE's trademark and the goods and services listed were "confusingly similar" to those owned by RKO Pictures. WWE's original 2024 application specified RKO in standard characters for "Wrestling exhibitions and performances by a professional wrestler rendered through broadcast media including television and radio, and via the internet or commercial online service; providing wrestling news and information via a global computer network" in International Class 041. The cited marks from RKO Pictures are RKO in standard characters (Registration No. 3318872) for "entertainment services, namely, producing, leasing and exhibiting motion picture films; producing and recording videotapes featuring stage performances and other entertainment performances" also in International Class 041.

In April, WWE filed a three-month extension to file a response, which explains this new application. The new application and the one from 2024 are both labeled as "live" and "pending" by the patent office. There is also another pending "RKO" filing, separate from WWE and RKO Pictures, from a Polish clothing company related to footwear.

WWE's new trademark application lists these goods and services: Wrestling exhibitions and performances by a professional wrestler and entertainer rendered through broadcast media including television and radio, and via the internet or commercial online service; providing wrestling news and information via a global computer network.

The RKO represents Randy Orton's initials, Randal Keith Orton, and has become one of the most famous finishing moves in professional wrestling. 🔥 Jeff Jarrett TNA and WWE Stories

MULTIPLE REVELATIONS FROM JARRETT AND JERICHO SHED LIGHT ON TNA AND WWE BUSINESS DEALINGS

During appearances at a Dark Side of the Ring Season 7 Q&A event in New York City on July 6, 2026, Jeff Jarrett and Chris Jericho opened up about several significant moments in wrestling history that had never been fully detailed publicly. The two legends provided insight into contract negotiations, business strategy, and creative decisions that shaped their careers and the landscape of professional wrestling during critical periods.

JERICHO'S STRATEGIC TNA MEETING USED AS WWE LEVERAGE

Chris Jericho revealed a calculated business move from 2007 when he was negotiating his return to WWE after being away from the company for a couple of years. According to Jericho, Vince McMahon had established what he called a "magic number" for salaries—a ceiling that represented the highest base pay any wrestler could receive regardless of their status. While main event performers could earn additional money through bonuses and other opportunities, McMahon had a minimum floor that he was unwilling to exceed.

Frustrated by WWE's unwillingness to meet his financial demands, Jericho decided to arrange a meeting with Jeff Jarrett and Dixie Carter at the Cheesecake Factory in Tampa, Florida. The meeting was ostensibly to discuss the possibility of Jericho joining TNA Wrestling. However, Jericho admitted that he orchestrated the meeting with a specific agenda in mind. He deliberately ensured that word of the meeting would reach the wrestling media by having his cousin sit nearby and observe the meeting, then providing contact information to someone in the wrestling dirt sheet world.

The strategy worked almost immediately. Jericho explained that just two days after the meeting became public knowledge, WWE came back with the salary figure he had been requesting. While Jericho acknowledged that TNA's product was genuinely appealing at the time—particularly praising the feud between Samoa Joe and Kurt Angle as some of the best wrestling being produced—his primary motivation for the meeting was to create the impression that another major company was interested in signing him.

Jericho emphasized the importance of competition between wrestling promotions, noting that having viable alternatives forced companies to make better financial offers. He stated that the entire situation demonstrated why wrestling thrives when multiple strong companies exist in the industry.

JARRETT'S PERSPECTIVE ON JERICHO'S NEGOTIATION TACTICS

Jeff Jarrett responded to Jericho's story by confirming that he understood the play being made. From Jarrett's perspective, Jericho was simply a talented performer using legitimate business strategy to increase his leverage and value. Jarrett recalled that he likely told Dixie Carter after the meeting that signing someone of Jericho's caliber would have been "a freaking grand slam" for TNA.

The key difference, according to Jarrett, was that he understood what Jericho was doing as a talent playing the business game, whereas Dixie Carter did not always grasp these negotiations in the same way. This distinction became relevant when discussing another potential signing that occurred during the Hogan years at TNA.

DIXIE CARTER'S MISPLACED CONFIDENCE ABOUT GOLDBERG

Jarrett revealed that Dixie Carter had her own meeting with Bill Goldberg, which she believed could result in signing the legendary WCW star. According to Jarrett, Carter was convinced that TNA could actually acquire Goldberg, but Jarrett himself was skeptical from the start. He believed that Goldberg was employing the same business tactic that Jericho had employed—using TNA's interest as leverage with another company, likely WCW or WWE depending on the timeframe.

When asked if Goldberg's meeting also took place at the Cheesecake Factory, Jarrett clarified that he was not even invited to that lunch, as it occurred during the Hogan era when Jarrett's influence at the company had diminished. Jarrett recounted that after the meeting, Carter told him she believed TNA could sign Goldberg. Jarrett's response was immediate skepticism, thinking to himself, "You don't have a clue."

Jarrett acknowledged that he knew Goldberg was playing both sides of the negotiation but appreciated the story nonetheless. The bottom line was that Goldberg never signed with TNA, and Jarrett's assessment that Goldberg was using the company as leverage rather than seriously considering joining proved accurate.

JARRETT CLARIFIES 1999 WWE DEPARTURE AND CONTRACT DISPUTE

In a separate appearance on The Ariel Helwani Show, Jarrett addressed one of wrestling's most enduring backstage stories—the narrative that he held Vince McMahon hostage for money before departing WWE in 1999. Jarrett strongly rejected this characterization, explaining that the situation involved legitimate contract negotiations that happened to occur at a crucial moment.

According to Jarrett's account, he was dealing with Jim Ross in contract negotiations while Jim Ross managed the roster. Ross had to keep talent happy, but according to Jarrett, the negotiations were not going favorably for him. As a red-hot heel at the time, Jarrett felt he deserved better terms, but Ross was playing hardball and refusing to meet his demands.

The situation became complicated because WWE's creative team and talent relations department were not communicating effectively. WWE had advertised Jarrett's Intercontinental Championship match against Chyna for the No Mercy pay-per-view on a Sunday, but Jarrett's contract was set to expire on Saturday night. This timing created leverage for Jarrett that WWE's internal miscommunication had inadvertently given him.

By September, Jarrett realized that Jim Ross was not going to budge on his contract offer. Business was booming in 1999, and Jarrett believed he deserved a pay increase. When it became clear that Ross would not negotiate further, Jarrett made a deal with WCW through J.J. Dillon. Once that deal was finalized, Jarrett strategically chose not to answer multiple calls from Vince McMahon on Thursday, Friday, and Saturday, knowing that his bargaining position had changed.

JARRETT'S STRATEGIC POSITIONING AT THE ARENA

When Jarrett arrived at the arena on Sunday, he immediately met with Jim Ross and Terry Taylor. Rather than threatening to not perform or holding anyone hostage, Jarrett laid out the business reality as he saw it. He explained that he had three pay-per-views already in the pipeline (wrestlers received payments on a 90-day delay), a UK tour scheduled, and several house show runs. He stated that he wanted to be paid that night in full because once he dropped the championship and left the arena, he would have zero leverage. Without payment in hand, WWE could theoretically delay payments or deny him payoffs, and he would have no legal recourse.

When Ross and Taylor asked what figure he needed, Jarrett provided a number. Ross and Taylor then went to Vince McMahon, who approved the payment. Jarrett locked the check in his car before returning to perform his match against Chyna.

However, Jarrett added another crucial element to the conversation. He reminded Jim Ross of what had happened in 1997 and 1998 when Jarrett's wife was diagnosed with breast cancer. Linda McMahon had personally directed that Jarrett not work any house shows so he could be home with his wife. Because Jarrett missed house show appearances due to Linda's directive, he did not meet his downside guarantee that year. According to Jarrett, Ross then cut his pay in year two of his contract, penalizing him for being unavailable due to his wife's illness.

Jarrett argued that this was unfair and used this history as part of his negotiation. He felt that WWE owed him additional compensation not just for his current performance but also for the financial penalty he had suffered when his wife was battling cancer and the company had directed him to stay home.

JARRETT'S PERSPECTIVE ON THE DEPARTURE

Looking back at the situation, Jarrett maintained that he never refused to lose the championship to Chyna. He respected Vince McMahon and the business, and he fulfilled his obligation by performing the match. From Jarrett's perspective, what happened was not a backstage screwjob or a case of him holding anyone up. Instead, it was a contract negotiation that happened to take place at the most convenient—or perhaps most difficult—possible time for WWE.

Jarrett emphasized that he wanted to make sure he was paid what he believed he was owed before his leverage disappeared. His contract had expired, he had already made a deal with WCW, and WWE had already promoted his title match. The only way WWE could have avoided this situation would have been to address his contract demands a month earlier, before the pay-per-view was scheduled and advertised.

JARRETT'S PITCH: BRINGING SUICIDE FROM VIDEO GAME TO WRESTLING

In another revelation during the Dark Side of the Ring Q&A, Jarrett discussed the development of TNA's first video game, TNA iMPACT!, which was created by Midway Games in 2008. Jarrett recalled that Midway Games approached TNA with genuine enthusiasm for the brand, visiting his home and diving deep into the company's creative process to ensure the game accurately represented TNA.

The project was especially meaningful to Jarrett personally, as he was managing the development process while his wife was seriously ill in the ICU. He was taking laps around the hospital and conducting calls about the game development simultaneously.

During the game development process, Midway Games introduced an original character created specifically for the video game called Suicide. When Jarrett first encountered this masked character, he immediately had an unconventional idea. Rather than leaving Suicide confined to the digital world of the video game, Jarrett wanted to bring the character into TNA Television as an actual in-ring performer.

According to Jarrett, the developers at Midway Games were stunned by this pitch. He recalled the moment vividly: "I'll never forget the look on their faces and I said, 'Hey man, I love this character. I want to make him into a real wrestler,' and they were like, 'What the…?'"

Despite the developers' initial shock, Jarrett's pitch became reality. Suicide transitioned from a video game character to an actual TNA Wrestling performer, creating a unique story of a fictional creation becoming part of professional wrestling history. Rather than simply respawning in a video game, the character had entered the squared circle.

MIDWAY GAMES BANKRUPTCY AND LOST SEQUEL OPPORTUNITY

Jarrett also revealed that while the first TNA video game was successful, a planned sequel never materialized. TNA 2 had been booked for development for years, but Midway Games eventually went bankrupt, which derailed the project. Jarrett noted that the planned sequel might have also been abandoned due to the later involvement of Hulk Hogan with TNA, which he suggested created complications that ultimately led to the sequel falling through.

Jarrett's reflections on the Midway Games partnership demonstrate that TNA iMPACT! left a lasting legacy beyond just being a video game. The game created a wrestling character whose impact and legacy extended far beyond the console and into professional wrestling itself.

Title Changes July 7 Wrestling History

July 7th stands out as one of the most active days in professional wrestling history for title changes. According to wrestling data, there have been 246 notable and major title changes on this calendar date spanning several decades, making it a significant milestone day for championship transitions across multiple promotions.

The earliest documented July 7th title change occurred in 1977 when Bobo Brazil won the NWA United States Heavyweight Title in its Mid-Atlantic version. This marked the beginning of a long tradition of championship activity on this particular date. The 1990s saw continued activity with Sting capturing the prestigious NWA World Heavyweight Title in 1990, establishing himself as one of wrestling's top stars. In 1995, the IWGP Tag Team Titles were vacated, and the following year in 1996, the legendary Ric Flair won the WCW United States Heavyweight Title.

The year 2002 proved to be exceptionally chaotic on July 7th, featuring an unprecedented five title changes on a single day. This remarkable occurrence was primarily driven by the WWE Hardcore Championship's unique 24/7 rule, which allowed for title changes to occur anytime and anywhere. Throughout July 7th, 2002, four different wrestlers held the Hardcore Title, with Bradshaw, Crash Holly, Christopher Nowinski, and Steven Richards each claiming the championship during the day's events. Beyond the Hardcore division, Ricky Marvin won the NWA World Welterweight Title on the same date, adding to the day's title change activity.

The period from 2003 to 2018 continued the tradition of July 7th championship success. In 2003, Booker T won the WWE Intercontinental Title, securing a mid-card championship. The year 2013 saw two additional title changes on this date, with Mikey Nicholls and Shane Haste capturing the GHC Heavyweight Tag Team Titles while Black Metal claimed the Occidente Middleweight Title. AJ Styles joined the list of July 7th title winners in 2017 by capturing the WWE United States Title. In 2018, Juice Robinson won the IWGP United States Heavyweight Title, further cementing July 7th as a day of championship opportunity.

The most recent years have continued this trend into the modern era. In 2022, two NXT UK-related title changes occurred when Tyler Bate won the NXT United Kingdom Title and Noam Dar won the NXT UK Heritage Cup. Most recently, in 2024, Ethan Page won the NXT Title, maintaining July 7th's status as a day when championship dreams are realized across the wrestling landscape.

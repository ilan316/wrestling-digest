Diana Hart defends Bret Hart memories regarding British Bulldog

Diana Hart continues to defend the legacy of her late brother-in-law British Bulldog with a series of passionate Facebook posts challenging Bret Hart's public statements about their iconic 1992 SummerSlam encounter. The widow of Davey Boy Smith has emerged as a vocal critic of how Bret has portrayed the match and the circumstances surrounding it, particularly in recent public appearances.

In her initial social media post, Diana Hart shared video documentation of medical records dated July 21, 1992, which she claims prove that Davey was dealing with a significant knee injury leading up to the match. She expressed frustration with Bret's dismissal of these facts, stating that despite explaining the situation repeatedly, Bret refuses to acknowledge her perspective and shuts down the discussion. Diana emphasized that Bret remains the only person she knows who has criticized the match or Davey's performance in it, especially given how supportive Davey was toward Bret throughout their careers.

The situation escalated in Diana's follow-up posts, where she addressed more serious health concerns. Diana revealed that Davey was actually suffering from necrotizing fasciitis, commonly known as flesh-eating disease, at the time of the SummerSlam match. She was particularly upset by Bret's recent characterization of this serious medical condition at a public presentation at his bar on Saturday evening, where he allegedly dismissed it casually as something comparable to a pimple.

Diana described attending this event with her boyfriend, sitting at a front-row table where she could observe Bret in detail as he reviewed the match footage on television screens throughout the venue. She recounted how Bret repeatedly rewound sections of the match to point out perceived mistakes or shortcomings in Davey's performance, criticizing how Davey looked or executed certain moves. According to Diana, Bret presented these criticisms as facts rather than opinions.

However, Diana countered with her own observation about the match itself. She pointed out that it was actually Bret who made a technical error when executing his signature finishing move, the Sharpshooter, as he mixed up Davey's feet during the submission hold. Rather than viewing this as a flaw, Diana interpreted it as part of the beauty and improvisation inherent to professional wrestling, particularly in the Stampede Wrestling tradition where wrestlers performed without scripts or extensive pre-planning and relied on natural athleticism and storytelling instincts.

Diana challenged Bret's apparent need to dissect and criticize the match, noting that throughout their careers, wrestlers like Davey, Owen Hart, Dynamite Kid, and numerous other greats were able to perform at the highest level without detailed pre-match choreography. She referenced examples including Davey's hour-long matches in Calgary against Japanese opponents who didn't speak English, and the acclaimed encounter between Dynamite Kid and Tiger Mask, both executed without extensive planning.

Most significantly, Diana expressed anger over Bret's alleged comment that the match "would have been even better if he had not been on drugs." She viewed this statement as disrespectful to Davey, particularly given that he was actively dealing with necrotizing fasciitis during his performance. Diana concluded her posts by emphasizing that Davey gave his complete best effort to the match while dealing with a serious medical condition, and that Davey had always been good to Bret throughout their professional relationship.

The conflict represents a broader pattern that Diana has identified, noting that Bret has engaged in one-sided narratives about various wrestlers, including some who are no longer alive to defend themselves. She expressed frustration that Bret continues to push these narratives further with each retelling, despite her family's requests for him to stop. Diana suggested that given everything Davey did for Bret, the appropriate response would have been nothing but praise for the match and Davey's contributions to it.

WrestleCon promoter discusses TNA pulling Leon Slater

WrestleCon promoter Michael Bochicchio recently spoke with Post Wrestling to address the controversial decision by TNA to pull X-Division champion Leon Slater from a highly anticipated match against AEW star Ricochet at the Mark Hitchcock Memorial Supershow on Thursday, April 16 during WrestleMania week. The decision drew significant criticism from fans, wrestlers, and promoters who felt TNA had reneged on a previously cleared match.

Bochicchio explained that the match came about through a shared agent connection between Slater and Ricochet who is a friend of his. The primary objective of the matchup was to elevate Slater's profile during a week when multiple wrestlers increase their standing through high-profile contests. "The whole goal of this whole match was to make Leon Slater kind of a made man. Every WrestleMania weekend, aside from what happens with WWE, there's always a few people that really increase their profile by having really tremendous matches, and Leon was certainly slated to have that this week," Bochicchio stated.

According to Bochicchio's account, both companies had been informed of the match's finish to protect both competitors. He emphasized that Slater was not scheduled to lose the match, directly countering speculation that TNA pulled him due to concerns about the X-Division champion being defeated. "We went directly to Leon's boss (Carlos Silva). We went directly to Ricochet's boss (Tony Khan). We told them what the finish of the match would be to protect the individuals, so I'm not going to go in to more than that. A lot of people just think, 'Well, they didn't want Leon to lose.' Well, guys, I'm going to be honest with you. Leon wasn't losing. So, come up with a different theory on why they pulled him, because that had nothing to do with it. The match was cleared, the finish was cleared, both guys were very excited."

Bochicchio revealed that he did not receive direct communication from TNA about the pullout. Instead, he first learned of the decision through internet reports. When wrestling journalist Dave Meltzer reached out for comment, Bochicchio contacted the shared agent to confirm what he was hearing. TNA's decision was verified, but Silva did not personally contact Bochicchio until several days after the match had been changed and replaced. Silva's only contact came in a text message sent two hours before the Revolution PPV, asking to get in touch. Bochicchio indicated he responded by telling Silva to call anytime, but has not heard back since.

Silva had publicly attributed the decision to partner conflicts, which led many observers to conclude that WWE had ordered the pullout. However, Bochicchio dismissed this theory. He believes the match was not on WWE's radar during their most significant week of the year. Instead, Bochicchio theorized that Silva's frustration stemmed from AEW's decision to air Collision programming on Thursday nights, which he speculated was eating into TNA's Impact ratings and creating competitive tension between the promotions.

The Mark Hitchcock Memorial main event was ultimately restructured into a six-person tag team match featuring The Demand consisting of Ricochet, Bishop Kaun, and Toa Liona against "Speedball" Mike Bailey, Kevin Knight, and Michael Oku. The event streamed live through the TrillerTV+ monthly subscription service.

Vampiro discusses JAWS as creative inspiration

Vampiro, the former WCW star, is wrapping up his American wrestling career with a retirement run that culminates on April 17 during WrestleMania weekend in Las Vegas. JCW will host StrangleMania, where the lucha libre legend will face PCO and Big Vito in triple threat action. The card is stacked with notable names including Rob Van Dam, Vince Russo, EC3, Nic Nemeth, and Matt Riddle.

Vampiro isn't focused on waxing poetic about hanging up his boots. Instead, he's viewing this retirement run as an opportunity to connect with fans and enjoy the experience. JCW Lunacy has been gaining traction as a cult favorite on YouTube, similar to what Lucha Underground accomplished on the El Rey Network, where Vampiro played an integral creative role.

During an interview, Vampiro reflected on the downfall of Lucha Underground, attributing it to ignorance and stupidity in the decision-making process. He recalled receiving a call from Glenn Danzig, the legendary rock and roll musician and wrestling fan who was organizing heavy metal horror festivals. Danzig wanted to bring Lucha Underground to his shows, but the promotion's economic demands were six to seven times higher than the cost of the entire tour, causing that opportunity to fall through.

Vampiro expressed frustration about how the situation unfolded afterward, noting that the broadcast partner and investor was Mark Burnett, essentially the owner of MGM. He questioned how decision-makers could fail to see the obvious problem of having MGM's backing while simultaneously allowing talent and storyline heat to be redirected to other networks and shows, ultimately leading to Lucha Underground being beaten by competitors that weren't even major players in the industry.

Earlier in the conversation, Vampiro discussed how Abdullah The Butcher taught him the storytelling aspects of violence. This education sparked Vampiro to study horror films and reconsider how heel wrestlers should operate. He examined what made horror movies effective and found his answer in Jaws.

Vampiro explained his philosophy using the first Jaws film as the prime example. In that movie, the audience knows the shark is in the water and that danger lurks below the surface. The shark isn't revealed until the end, yet viewers know not to go in the water from the beginning. Vampiro applied this logic to professional wrestling heel work, arguing that a heel's job is to make the audience understand that the babyface will get hurt at some point. The key question becomes when the shark emerges from the depths.

From his conversations with Abdullah and through developing his Vampiro persona, Vampiro emphasized that the approach was always about less is more. Being authentic outside of wrestling became central to his character development, proving that sometimes what you don't show is more effective than what you do.

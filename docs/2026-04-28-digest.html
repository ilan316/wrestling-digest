Motor City Machine Guns Expected to Land in AEW Following WWE Release

The Motor City Machine Guns are no longer part of World Wrestling Entertainment. Chris Sabin confirmed on social media that he and Alex Shelley were among the names released as part of the company's post-WrestleMania 42 roster cuts on April 24. The duo originally joined WWE in the fall of 2024 and quickly made an impression, capturing the WWE Tag Team Championships in just their second match with the company. Despite the fast start, their run proved to be relatively short-lived.

The release comes as part of a sweeping wave of cuts that saw approximately 25 wrestlers let go in a single day. Reports have suggested the moves were tied to WWE reshaping its roster, including making room for emerging talent and adjusting to WWE SmackDown returning to a two-hour format, which reduces the need for a deeper roster. The cuts also included several notable names across the main roster, such as Kairi Sane, Aleister Black, Zelina Vega, Apollo Crews, and Santos Escobar, among others, marking one of the more significant roster reductions in recent years.

Despite the setback, it appears Shelley and Sabin aren't sweating it, with the duo having plenty planned for their 90 days of downtime and beyond. Sabin and Shelley's immediate reaction after their release was a calm one, as the duo believe they will do well outside of the WWE umbrella. Those that we spoke to in WWE said that the duo seemed like they knew they would continue to do well and stay busy outside of WWE, and will continue to wrestle.

Part of that confidence is because wrestling is not the only thing Sabin and Shelley have on their plate, as they'll continue work with their band GRPPLING. While confirming their release on social media, Sabin revealed the band had released a four song EP and encouraged fans to check out their music. The creative projects will continue as they move forward in their careers.

As for their next move wrestling-wise, it's believed the duo will have several options, most notably TNA and AEW. Those close to TNA said that the duo would always be welcome back with the promotion, where they worked on and off from 2007 to 2024, winning the TNA Tag Team Championships 3 times. However, those same sources believe that Shelley and Sabin are more than likely set to join AEW, though it remains unconfirmed if the two sides have had talks.

There is a strong history between the Motor City Machine Guns and AEW. Though Shelley and Sabin have never signed a contract with AEW, the duo did briefly work for the promotion in 2022, teaming with Jay Lethal at All Out 2022 to take on Wardlow and FTR in a losing effort. Prior to signing with WWE in 2024, reports emerged that Shelley and Sabin were expected to sign with AEW, and it was later confirmed that the promotion did offer the duo a deal. In the end, the tag team chose to sign with WWE after they matched AEW's offer.

Now with both sides free of that situation, the expectation is that AEW could revisit that interest. At this time, there has been no confirmation that AEW has made direct contact with the team following their exit. With their non-compete clauses expected to expire in the coming months, Sabin and Shelley are likely to attract strong interest across the wrestling world, given their reputation and proven track record as one of the industry's top tag teams.

Backstage sentiment within WWE suggests the duo handled their release professionally, with those familiar with the situation noting that Sabin and Shelley appeared confident about their future and their ability to remain active at a high level outside the company. While they had hoped for more opportunities during their run, they were said to be satisfied with how they were used overall, even as their appearances slowed in recent months. Notably, they were not scheduled to travel for the most recent episode of SmackDown prior to their release.

Widely regarded as one of the most accomplished tag teams of their generation, the Motor City Machine Guns built their legacy primarily in Total Nonstop Action Wrestling. Their partnership dates back to 2006 and has been a staple of tag team wrestling for nearly two decades, including a lengthy return run in TNA prior to signing with WWE. With AEW reportedly viewed as a likely destination and TNA remaining a familiar option, the Motor City Machine Guns suddenly find themselves among the most talked-about tag teams available outside WWE.

Tony Khan on wrestling media and dirtsheets

AEW President Tony Khan has shared his perspective on wrestling media and so-called dirtsheets, offering a nuanced view of their role within the professional wrestling industry. Speaking on the Marking Out podcast, Khan acknowledged that while misinformation can sometimes circulate, he believes the overall influence of wrestling media is more positive than negative.

Khan explained that there can be misinformation or even disinformation where people put incorrect things out into the world in wrestling for their own benefit. However, he noted that sometimes there's a lot of truth in these stories, and sometimes there's not. When you know things firsthand, it can be interesting to see how information is reported and disseminated through the wrestling community. Despite these challenges, Khan emphasized that there is a net benefit and a lot of good that comes from wrestling media coverage.

A significant part of Khan's perspective stems from his own experience as a wrestling fan. He highlighted the crucial role wrestling newsletters played in shaping his own fandom, particularly when it came to discovering international wrestling. Khan noted that wrestling newsletters were instrumental in helping him learn about wrestling beyond the United States. There is so much wrestling in the world, and for Khan, newsletters were where he learned a lot about international wrestling. He was able to read about Japanese wrestling and first discover what was happening in Japan and in Mexico through these publications. As a big fan of lucha libre, Khan learned a lot about it from wrestling newsletters, which helped broaden his understanding of wrestling styles and talent from around the globe.

Khan emphasized that these exposure opportunities have real value. You can read about wrestlers in newsletters, then go out and watch them, and they can be very influential on your appreciation and understanding of the sport. This democratization of wrestling information allows fans to access talent and styles they might not otherwise encounter through mainstream Western wrestling coverage.

However, Khan did not shy away from acknowledging the structural differences between wrestling journalism and traditional sports reporting. He pointed out that wrestling journalism operates under a different standard compared to traditional sports coverage like football. The sourcing and verification of rumors has a much higher standard in something like football than it does in wrestling. He attributed this difference to the nature of the business, where much of the reporting revolves around locker room developments rather than publicly verifiable information that can be confirmed through official sources.

Khan explained that the kind of sourcing you need to report what are essentially locker room rumors is very different from traditional sports reporting. You don't see legitimate football writers reporting those kinds of things as frequently or with the same level of speculation that wrestling writers employ. In wrestling, the journalistic standard is generally much lower when it comes to that sort of reporting, though Khan did not frame this as necessarily negative given the unique nature of the industry.

Despite these challenges with verification standards, Khan remained positive about wrestling media's contribution to the industry. He stated that there are definitely positives and negatives, but he believes the positives far outweigh the negatives when it comes to wrestling newsletters and wrestling media. He compared wrestling coverage to sports journalism more broadly, noting that just like in any other sport, you want top writers talking about what they see and giving their opinions. Khan acknowledged that he doesn't always agree with football writers, and he doesn't always agree with wrestling writers, but he respects that people follow something year-round.

Khan particularly emphasized the demanding nature of wrestling coverage. Wrestling truly never stops, which means those who follow the sport closely face a unique challenge in staying current. If you follow wrestling closely, it's a similar situation to following football closely. In both cases, there are always going to be some writers who set a higher standard for their work and for their ethics, while others may operate with less stringent standards.

Khan's comments offer a rare inside perspective on how one of the industry's top executives views the media landscape that both promotes and critiques professional wrestling. His balanced approach acknowledges real flaws in wrestling journalism while recognizing the genuine value that passionate, dedicated coverage brings to the industry and to fans worldwide.

If you followed wrestling in the 2010s and after, you likely came across the Young Bucks at some point. Matt and Nick Jackson are more than another successful brother tag team. They became a focal point for what modern wrestling looks like, both in the ring and beyond. Their story goes beyond titles and standout matches. It reflects a shift in how the business itself operates.

Reinventing Modern Tag Team Wrestling

Before the Bucks broke out on the independent scene, tag team wrestling in the United States had drifted into the background. It was reliable and sometimes very good, but it rarely drove ticket sales. Most matches followed a familiar structure, and fans could often predict the timing of the hot tag before the match even started.

The Bucks took that structure and reshaped it. Their matches leaned on constant movement, inventive double-team offense, and extended closing stretches that felt fast and chaotic but still controlled. Signature moments like the Meltzer Driver, More Bang For Your Buck, and the Superkick Party did more than get reactions. They introduced a new rhythm and visual style for tag wrestling. You could see other teams begin to adjust. What they were doing in PWG, ROH, and NJPW started to show up across the industry.

They also helped bring tag wrestling back into the main event conversation. For years, that idea had quietly faded. The notion that a tag match could close a major show without feeling like an exception started to feel normal again, and the Bucks played a major role in that shift.

Branding, Merch, and the Business Side of Wrestling

One of the most important parts of the Young Bucks story has little to do with what happens in the ring. It comes down to branding. For a long time, the path for wrestlers felt straightforward. Sign with WWE, get television exposure, sell merchandise, and hope it leads somewhere. The Bucks showed there was another option.

Through Pro Wrestling Tees, they built a brand around themselves with logos, catchphrases, and designs fans actually wanted to wear. When their merchandise appeared in Hot Topic stores nationwide, it signaled something bigger. Wrestling outside the WWE system could connect with a wider audience. That moment did not go unnoticed. Independent wrestlers saw it. Talent in Japan saw it. Even people within WWE paid attention. It opened the door for wrestlers to build careers on their own terms instead of waiting for a contract to define their value.

In many ways, the Bucks helped push the idea of wrestlers as entrepreneurs before that mindset became common.

A Career Filled With Championships

Alongside their influence, the Young Bucks built a resume that stands out across multiple promotions and countries. In Ring of Honor, they captured 3 ROH World Tag Team Championships and 3 ROH World Six-Man Tag Team Championships. In New Japan Pro-Wrestling, their accomplishments included 7 IWGP Junior Heavyweight Tag Team Championships, 1 IWGP Heavyweight Tag Team Championship, and 3 NEVER Openweight Six-Man Tag Team Championships.

Their run in NJPW coincided with the rise of Bullet Club, which became one of the most recognizable factions in wrestling and a major force in merchandise sales. In All Elite Wrestling, they won 3 AEW World Tag Team Championships and 2 AEW World Trios Championships with Kenny Omega.

Few teams can point to repeated success across multiple major promotions while also contributing to each company's growth.

Being The Elite and a New Kind of Storytelling

When the Bucks launched Being The Elite on YouTube, it felt like it was aimed at a niche audience. The show was loose, unpredictable, and often strange in a way that traditional wrestling rarely allows. It mixed travel clips, humor, cameos, and eventually full storyline development. Wrestlers who later became central figures in AEW, including Hangman Adam Page and Kenny Omega, developed character arcs on the show before they reached national television.

🔥 WWE post-WrestleMania roster cuts

WWE held its post-WrestleMania roster cuts on Friday, April 24, 2026, impacting both main roster and NXT talent with approximately 25 confirmed departures. The cuts sparked significant discussion in the wrestling community, particularly regarding the circumstances surrounding several high-profile releases.

Among the main roster releases were Aleister Black, Zelina Vega, the entire Wyatt Sicks stable (Uncle Howdy/Bo Dallas, Nikki Cross, Joe Gacy, Erick Rowan, and Dexter Lumis), Kairi Sane, Santos Escobar, Zoey Stark, Alba Fyre, and Apollo Crews. The Motor City Machine Guns (Chris Sabin and Alex Shelley) were also released despite only being under contract for approximately a year and a half. NXT releases included Andre Chase, Tyra Mae Steele, Tyson Dupont, Tyriek Igwe, Dante Chen, Sirena Linton, Malik Blade, Trill London, Luca Crusifino, Chris Island, and Carlee Bright.

The release of the Wyatt Sicks proved particularly controversial. The faction debuted to widespread acclaim as a tribute to late WWE star Bray Wyatt and quickly became television regulars. Joe Gacy and Dexter Lumis held the WWE Tag Team Championships for a six-and-a-half month reign, the second-longest in the title's decade-long history. However, the group struggled to connect with audiences once their entrance concluded, with creative frequently lacking direction for individual storylines. Despite this, the stable demonstrated commercial value through strong merchandise sales and even inspired a Wyatt-themed attraction at Universal Studios' Halloween Horror Nights event. Bully Ray criticized the releases, suggesting WWE should have retained Bo Dallas and kept Uncle Howdy searching for The Fiend as a long-term quest, maintaining the emotional connection to Bray Wyatt's legacy. Alexa Bliss, who worked extensively with Bray Wyatt and was rumored as a potential sixth member of the Sicks, posted on social media thanking the group and specifically Nikki Cross, her former tag team partner with whom she won two WWE Women's Tag Team Championships.

The release of Kairi Sane shocked many observers. She was actively featured on television and positioned for a potential storyline with Asuka, with expectations she would turn face and challenge Asuka at WrestleMania. The release cut this angle short without explanation. Reports indicate Sane has returned to Japan, though sources note she travels home monthly and this may not be directly connected to her departure. Stars like IYO SKY reacted strongly to the news on social media.

Zoey Stark's release also drew criticism for its timing and circumstances. Stark had spent nearly a year rehabilitating a serious knee injury sustained in May 2025 involving tears to her ACL, MCL, and meniscus. Reports confirmed she had been medically cleared to return to in-ring action prior to her release but was not being actively used on television. Bully Ray challenged WWE's decision, emphasizing that despite some concerns about her promo delivery and presentation in the Pure Fusion Collective, she deserved at least one match back after dedicating a full year to recovery. He stated: "I tried for a whole year to come back only to have you tell me you're not even going to give me an opportunity… Not even one match back. To me, that's not right." Stark confirmed her departure on social media, calling it "God's plan" while thanking WWE for allowing her to achieve her dreams.

Santos Escobar's situation differed significantly from Stark's. Unlike Stark, Escobar was not medically cleared at the time of his release. He sustained a triceps injury at an AAA show approximately one month prior and remained sidelined during his departure.

According to Bryan Alvarez of F4WOnline, more NXT cuts may still be forthcoming. He stated: "I'm just waiting to see if there's any more coming. The NXT list was said to be bigger than what's out there. They've got a lot of people at the Performance Center and they're always bringing in new talent, so I'm not convinced this is the end of the developmental cuts. Andre Chase's release came with specific context regarding his original hiring. Several released talents announced their next moves. Luca Crusifino, formerly of the D'Angelo Family on NXT, announced his retirement from professional wrestling entirely. In a statement on social media, he wrote: "After much reflection, I've decided it's time to close this chapter of my life. Being a part of the WWE has been a dream ever since I was a child." He expressed gratitude to the organization while noting it was the right decision for him. Crusifino had signed with WWE in early 2022 and made his in-ring debut in February 2023, joining the D'Angelo Family in 2024 before transitioning to WWE EVOLVE following the family's breakup.

Carlee Bright, a former NXT and WWE EVOLVE wrestler, confirmed her departure on Instagram with an uplifting message. Bright wrote: "This wasn't how I imagined this chapter ending, but if there's one thing this journey taught me, it's how to fight through uncertainty and the moments that test who you really are." She signed with WWE in 2022 and appeared on NXT television two years later before becoming a regular on EVOLVE. Despite her release, she remained optimistic, stating: "If there's one thing I know about myself, it's this: mom & dad didn't raise a quitter. This chapter may be closing, but my story is far from finished."

Industry observers noted potential landing spots for the released talent. TNA Wrestling, which has a working agreement with WWE and previously signed several departed stars, would be a logical destination for names like the Wyatt Sicks and the Motor City Machine Guns. AEW was also mentioned as a possible option, though the company faces its own roster depth challenges. The Motor City Machine Guns, in particular, had previously appeared in AEW at All Out in 2022.

The broader context of these releases reflects WWE's annual post-WrestleMania roster management. The company uses this period to make room for recent call-ups to the main roster and to cycle new talent through its developmental system. However, these cuts raised questions about creative decisions, specifically why the company would release established television performers like the Wyatt Sicks and Kairi Sane while facing merchandise success and proving commercial viability. The situation sparked renewed debate about WWE's decision-making given the company's record financial performance.

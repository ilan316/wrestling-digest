🔥 D-Von Dudley Steroid Use in TNA

D-Von Dudley, a WWE Hall of Famer, recently gave candid commentary about steroid use during his wrestling career. In an appearance on VladTV, Dudley admitted to taking steroids during the early portion of his time with TNA Wrestling, a period he had spent ten years with overall.

"I actually took it during my TNA run, but it wasn't long-term," Dudley explained. "I did indulge in it for probably about three years. I spent 10 years in TNA, and it was about three, three-and-a-half years of using it."

Dudley did not shy away from discussing the significant physical consequences that followed his decision to stop using performance-enhancing drugs. "The only bad thing was that when I stopped using it, my body fell off—I wasn't looking jacked anymore, I was looking flabby," he said. "Then I had to train all over again the way I used to when I was younger. I had to go back to calisthenics, do everything, and even get a trainer to help get my body right."

The physical toll extended beyond just appearance. Dudley revealed that his steroid use coincided with a dramatic increase in body weight. "I think that's where the knee problems came into play, because I got up to almost 315 pounds. I was just solid, and carrying all that weight around hurt my knees," he explained. "I wore knee pads and braces my whole career, and I kept asking myself, 'How am I having knee problems carrying all that weight and bouncing around?' You can't do that and expect nothing to happen."

Throughout his explanation, Dudley emphasized personal accountability. "So yes, I did use it when I left WWE and went to TNA for the first three years, but after that, I stopped. I don't blame TNA—I blame myself. I indulged in that stuff. Nobody put a gun to my head and forced me. I did it. I'm not ashamed. I did what I did in terms of the enhancements."

When asked about the severity of his steroid use, Dudley stated definitively that it did not develop into a serious problem. "Did it become a problem? No. Was I addicted to it? No." He also revealed that his family played a crucial role in his decision to stop using steroids, noting that thinking about his sons and wanting to be present as they grow and potentially pursue their own paths in the wrestling business motivated him to cease the practice.

The topic of steroid use in professional wrestling remains controversial and has been debated for decades, with roots tracing back to the early 1990s when WWE Chairman Vince McMahon faced a high-profile trial centered around performance-enhancing drugs. The conversation has continued to be tied to both the physical demands of the business and the long-term health risks faced by performers.

In a separate portion of his VladTV appearance, Dudley also addressed another controversial moment from his time away from WWE. During the 2005 Survivor Series PPV event, a backstage segment showed Vince McMahon interacting with John Cena, during which McMahon used a racial slur while complimenting Cena's work.

Dudley's reaction to the incident was unequivocally negative. "I was totally disgusted by it when I saw it. I didn't like it at all. At the time, I was in TNA, but I remember my head just dropping, you know, because at that time, shock TV—it felt like nobody could do shock TV anymore. Everything had already been done," he said.

He was adamant that the use of such language was inappropriate regardless of intent. "I definitely feel that it was for shock value. I definitely feel that it was meant to get a reaction—and it did—but I don't think it was the right reaction. I don't think it was the reaction they thought they were going to get. Because again, in my opinion, to use that word in pro wrestling at all is wrong."

The incident affected Dudley deeply enough to impact his viewing habits. "So to use that word, you know, in this type of business, I thought it was absolutely wrong, and I was offended by it to the point where I stopped watching WWE for a while. I just concentrated on TNA and what I was doing there," he revealed.

Dudley concluded his thoughts on the matter by taking a firm ethical stance. "I'm sorry, but that type of stuff has no business in pro wrestling. You can create shock value in other ways, but to use a racial term like that—one that has been historically used to degrade Black people—I think it's disgusting for business."

🔥 Pro Wrestling General History and Features

Pro Wrestling's Unluckiest

A couple of weeks ago, a column was posted about the luckiest people and moments in the history of pro wrestling, timed conveniently to St. Patrick's Day. However, luck isn't always good, and in many cases, bad luck proves far more relatable to audiences than good fortune. When examining pro wrestling's unluckiest individuals, it becomes important to distinguish between genuine misfortune and self-inflicted problems. Career-ending injuries suffered in the middle of the ring and issues stemming from substance abuse or legal problems represent bad decision-making rather than bad luck. Rob Van Dam being forced to lose the WWE and ECW Titles due to drug possession arrest and Jeff Hardy's multiple DUI and public intoxication arrests are examples of personal choices rather than unfortunate circumstances. These individuals would be the first to admit they screwed up their own opportunities.

Mr. Kennedy stands out as one of pro wrestling's most genuinely unlucky figures. Between 2005 and 2009, the hype surrounding him was insane, with fans loudly proclaiming he should be a World Champion based purely on his charisma and character work. However, he found stretches of bad luck that hurt him repeatedly. Three months after his main roster debut, he faced Eddie Guerrero on SmackDown. Eddie's "Lie, Cheat, Steal" schtick led to Kennedy being disqualified, and after the match, Kennedy attacked Eddie with a steel chair shot to the head. Tragically, Eddie passed away two days later, and many people incorrectly attributed Kennedy's chair shot to being responsible for Eddie's death. Ken Anderson received considerable hate for a long time because of this unfortunate sequence of events.

The bad luck continued relentlessly. A month after the Eddie Guerrero incident, Kennedy suffered a back injury at a house show that kept him sidelined for six months. Six weeks after returning, he took a head-first shot into the ring steps, suffering an exposed cranium laceration that required nearly two dozen stitches to close. Things appeared to finally be on track when he won the Money In The Bank Ladder Match at WrestleMania 23. However, five weeks later, he would lose a match to Edge that saw Edge become the new briefcase holder. Kennedy had suffered a torn triceps that would require at least six months away from action. When the injury was further evaluated, it was discovered that instead of a torn triceps, he had a hematoma that would only need about a month to heal.

Kennedy's misfortune persisted even after his return. Three months after returning from the hematoma, he was suspended in storyline by Vince McMahon. In reality, the suspension was due to Ken Anderson violating the WWE Wellness Policy after being named in a steroid scandal. In 2008, he missed more action after dislocating his shoulder at a house show, requiring nearly ten months away from the ring. Through all these injuries and setbacks that were beyond his control, Kennedy's promising push was continually derailed by circumstances that had nothing to do with his abilities or his character work.

WrestleMania 37's Main Event Triple Threat Match

A little over a year after the world was forced indoors and wrestling audiences were restricted by social distancing measures, WWE WrestleMania 37 took place at Raymond James Stadium in Tampa Bay, Florida on April 10 and 11, 2021. While the venue was capped at 25,000 fans for each night due to social distancing rules, it was simply nice to hear an audience in full voice enjoying live wrestling again. The match that closed the entire weekend was particularly memorable.

Edge had only been back in action after a nine-year medically enforced retirement for 15 months when he won the 2021 Royal Rumble match, earning himself a WrestleMania main event for the first time since George W. Bush was in office. He decided to challenge Roman Reigns for the WWE Universal Championship, but Daniel Bryan was also pursuing the Tribal Chief. Bryan had gotten Reigns to tap out to the Yes Lock at Fastlane, but the referee didn't see it, and with help from Edge, Reigns retained his title. Bryan then went to SmackDown General Manager Adam Pearce and convinced him he deserved another chance at Reigns, resulting in a Triple Threat Match being booked for WrestleMania 37's main event.

The Triple Threat Match between Roman Reigns, Edge, and Daniel Bryan for the WWE Universal Championship felt like a match shot out of a cannon. Thanks to Triple Threat matches having no disqualifications, all three men bullrush each other immediately, with Jey Uso meeting Daniel Bryan and Edge on the outside with Superkicks. Within minutes, the announce desk was stripped, and the ringside area was covered with bodies, chairs, and steel steps. Roman Reigns and Jey Uso directed traffic on the outside while the action unfolded.

In the ring, all three men played their roles perfectly. Daniel Bryan, despite feeling so mentally checked out at this point in his WWE career that he thought he was going to die, was on fire in the match. He balanced the face and heel dynamic perfectly, having the majority of fans on his side while being vicious enough to generate heat. Edge was the clear fan favorite, a strange sentence to write given his history, and the people genuinely wanted him to have a feel-good moment. This match probably ranks in the top ten when considering the best WrestleMania main events in history, though it doesn't receive as much recognition as it should. Many fans had tuned out of wrestling during the Thunderdome Era when there were no crowds, making this match feel less celebrated than it deserves to be.

Crazzy Steve Legally Blind Wrestling

Crazzy Steve On How He Wrestles While Being Legally Blind: I'm Partly Talented But I'm Mostly Obsessed

The Canadian professional wrestler is unique in many ways, but none more so than his ability to compete in the squared circle despite being legally blind. While speaking to Denise Salcedo in a new interview, Crazzy Steve explained how he manages to be an active professional wrestler despite his disability.

"The way I put it is I'm partly talented, but I'm mostly obsessed," Steve said. "Wrestling, despite my eyesight, came easy to me while training, and I think I put it to the idea that it's a very hands-on way of performing. So I don't necessarily have to see you in order to know what we're doing. For anybody who's training professional wrestling, you know it's a dance to a certain extent, and so just going through repetition after repetition after repetition and kind of becoming a robot in that sense where it's all second nature is kind of how I do what I do."

The wrestler noted that certain environmental factors make his job easier or more difficult depending on the venue. "Certain areas and places make it a little bit more difficult or a little bit easier in some cases, like for Impact or TNA. Their production value is great. Currently, they have the yellow ropes. That really helps me," Steve explained. "But I've worked in there when they have black ropes. Or I've worked for Tommy Dreamer's House of Hardcore, which is black ropes, black canvas, black everything, which he finds hilarious. Or again, on the independents, if you're in some sort of smoky bar and it's not really well lit, they come with their challenges."

Steve emphasized his philosophy for overcoming obstacles in his wrestling career. "But in wrestling, you learn to roll with the punches and you play to your strengths and hide your weaknesses, and I've been able to do that for the past 23 years now," he concluded.

The interview also touched on how Steve connects with fans despite his disability, showcasing his resilience and dedication to the professional wrestling industry.

WCW Documentary

A comprehensive documentary from WWE Vault provides viewers with an in-depth examination of World Championship Wrestling's complete history. The documentary delves into the key periods, major storylines, and influential figures that defined WCW throughout its existence. It explores how the company grew to become a serious competitor in the wrestling industry, featuring some of the biggest names and most memorable moments in professional wrestling history. Through archival footage and historical context, viewers gain a deeper understanding of the events that shaped the company's trajectory.

The film chronicles WCW's eventual decline and the factors that contributed to its ultimate downfall. By examining the complete arc from inception through closure, the documentary provides wrestling fans and historians with a thorough account of one of professional wrestling's most significant organizations. This comprehensive look serves as both a historical record and a study of business decisions, talent management, and creative choices that impacted the wrestling industry for decades.

Mercedes Moné Title Loss

Mercedes Moné's championship collection has taken another significant hit. The international wrestling star dropped the PTW Women's Championship on March 29 at Prime Time Wrestling in Poland, losing the title to Diana Strong. With the defeat, Moné is now down to just two active physical championships, which are the WPW Women's Championship in Canada and the APAC Women's Championship in Malaysia, as her once-loaded title resume continues to thin out in recent months.

The loss marks another chapter in what has been a challenging period for Moné despite her status as one of professional wrestling's biggest international attractions. Her title collection, which once spanned multiple promotions and continents, has been steadily diminishing as she navigates her dual commitments between independent promotions and major wrestling organizations.

Still, Moné won't have much time to dwell on the loss. She is scheduled to defend the WPW Women's Title against Jody Threat on March 31, in what could be a pivotal moment as she looks to maintain at least one of her remaining championships. This bout represents a critical juncture in her current run, as consecutive title losses could significantly impact her standing and momentum moving forward.

Following that bout, Moné has stated that she plans to return to AEW programming. That return has been anticipated, as Moné has been absent from AEW television since early in the year. Her last appearance came at Dynamite: New Year's Smash, where she lost the AEW TBS Championship to Willow Nightingale, a result that marked a significant turning point in her current run.

What comes next for Moné remains to be seen, though her upcoming WPW defense and subsequent return to major television programming should provide clarity on the direction of her career moving forward.

WWE RAW Star Didn’t Want Any Part of Infamous Ozzy Osbourne Segment

Chris Masters Opens Up About Infamous Ozzy Osbourne WWE RAW Segment

This former WWE RAW Superstar wasn't a fan of an infamous segment involving Ozzy Osbourne. The guest host era of WWE had its fair share of memorable moments. On November 2, 2009, Vince McMahon brought in Ozzy Osbourne and his wife, Sharon Osbourne, as the special guest hosts. The Osbournes were tasked with judging a performance segment featuring The Great Khali, with Jerry Lawler hosting the so-called auditions.

As the segment unfolded, Chris Masters appeared on the stage and performed his signature pec-popping routine to Ozzy's "Crazy Train," a moment that visibly amused the rock legend. Speaking on INSIGHT with Chris Van Vliet, Masters admitted he wasn't a fan of the concept from the outset, as he preferred to avoid comedic segments. However, once the decision was made, he committed fully and aimed to deliver the best performance possible, despite his initial reservations.

"Oh, boy. I don't know whose idea that was, but I didn't like it right from the start, because I didn't want to be doing comedic stuff really. But I also knew that there was a point where I was like, All right, well, if I'm going to do this, let me just try to do it the best I can," the former WWE RAW star said.

While the crowd responded positively, he later felt that the success of the bit worked against him, as it kept getting repeated to the point where it became excessive and lost its appeal. "This is funny once or twice, but to do it every week, if I was a fan, by the third time of it I'm like enough. So when I always think about that, it was funny, but I always hate the fact that it's like okay, now let's do this every week in some fashion. As a fan, I don't think I'd like this," Masters recalled.

The real-life Chris Adonis also reflected on the moment involving Ozzy Osbourne, noting that what stood out most was Ozzy's genuine enjoyment, which he found meaningful after his passing. He added that Sharon Osbourne's playful interaction added to the humor of the segment. "So we lost Ozzy not that long ago, so a lot of the clips came up of that segment. One thing I will say that I love about that is the genuine enthusiasm. When you see Ozzy's face, he just loves it. To me, I thought that was really cool. After his passing, I thought about this, and I watched it, man, it's really cool that I entertained Ozzy to that extent. He just thought it was hilarious. Yeah, it's cool. And, you know, Sharon was very flirtatious. It was pretty funny," he said.

He further revealed that there were plans for a specific finish to the segment, but from his recollection, he ended up winning it outright, even though he doesn't clearly remember how it was ultimately executed.

AEW Dynamite Viewership Decline

AEW Dynamite Viewership Shows Contrasting Trends in Latest Episode

Wednesday night's episode of AEW Dynamite on TBS drew mixed results in its viewership metrics, presenting a complicated picture of the show's current health. The episode averaged between 509,500 and 590,000 viewers depending on the source, with reports showing a decline from the previous week's performance. The viewership represented the lowest audience for the show since January 21st of this year.

In the key 18-49 demographic, however, the show demonstrated improvement week-over-week. Dynamite averaged a 0.10 rating in the demo, which marked a 25% increase from the previous week. This demo rating tied the second lowest rating the show has recorded in that category since March 11th. The disconnect between overall viewership and demo performance reflects a common trend in cable television where certain programming attracts more of the desirable younger audience despite broader audience erosion.

The episode faced considerable competition from major sports programming on cable television. The show went head-to-head with NBA Playoffs on ESPN and NHL Playoffs on TNT, which occupied the top two spots on the cable charts for the evening. As a result, Dynamite finished 9th for the night on cable, representing a significant drop from its usual placement in cable rankings.

Collision, which immediately followed Dynamite on the programming schedule, averaged 429,000 viewers with a 0.07 rating in the 18-49 demographic. This performance showed growth of 23.6% in overall viewers and 16.7% in the demo compared to the previous Saturday's show. Collision tied for 16th place on the cable charts for the evening.

Year-over-year comparisons paint a more concerning picture for AEW's flagship program. Compared to the same week in 2025, Dynamite was down 6.2% in overall viewers and a more troubling 37.5% in the 18-49 demographic. The May 6, 2026 episode drew between 509,500 and 590,000 viewers against the May 7, 2025 episode's 629,000 viewers, representing a 19% decline in viewership. The demo rating comparison was even more dramatic, falling from 0.16 in 2025 to 0.08 in 2026, a 50% decrease in the key demographic.

When measured against recent performance trends, this week's episode underperformed. The show was down 12% in overall viewers and 16.7% in the demo compared to the 10-week average of 0.12 demo rating and 670,400 viewers. This represents a continued downward trajectory for the program after several weeks of relatively stronger performances.

Looking at the broader context, Dynamite has shown volatility throughout 2026. The highest viewership of the year came on March 25, 2026, when the show drew 765,000 viewers with a 0.14 demo rating. The lowest viewership came on January 21, 2026, with 498,000 viewers. The May 6 episode places itself near the lower end of this range, suggesting that viewer retention remains a persistent challenge for the program.

Collision's performance alongside Dynamite showed promise with gains from its typical Saturday placement, indicating that audience interest in AEW programming may vary based on day and time slot. However, the overall viewership trends for both shows suggest that AEW faces headwinds in maintaining audience size as the company navigates a competitive television landscape with major sports programming occupying viewer attention.

Harley Cameron Wardrobe Incident on AEW Dynamite

Harley Cameron is addressing the chaotic moment from AEW Dynamite that left fans stunned, and she's making it clear the whole thing was far less dramatic than people thought.

During the May 26 edition of Dynamite, Mina Shirakawa accidentally sent the segment off the rails when she ripped Harley Cameron's top off during an interview segment. The reveal showed Cameron wearing a bra underneath instead of her wrestling gear, immediately catching Renee Paquette off guard on live television. The moment instantly exploded online, with many fans comparing it to a wardrobe malfunction similar to high-profile incidents in entertainment history.

Now Cameron is laughing the whole thing off. While speaking with Good Karma Wrestling and ESPN South Florida on May 8, 2026, Cameron explained that Shirakawa simply assumed she already had her wrestling gear underneath the shirt. Cameron stated, "Look, Mina got all riled up and she just thought I was wearing my gear underneath, which I wasn't."

Cameron then pointed out that her actual wrestling gear is already very similar to what viewers saw during the segment anyway. She emphasized, "For everyone who had a heart attack over it, let me just preface the fact that my wrestling gear is actually like just a bra. I wrestle in a bra, if anyone takes care to notice."

She also joked that fans were overreacting considering Shirakawa was standing right beside her in similar ring gear. Cameron explained the logical comparison: "So for everyone that was like, 'Oh my gosh,' I'm like, Mina is next to me in gear. Our gear is bras."

According to Cameron, the biggest issue was simply that she was wearing the wrong material at the time. Once the shirt came off unexpectedly, Cameron figured she had no choice but to fully commit to the moment. "When she ripped the shirt off, I was like, I gotta take the rest of it off now, you know," she said.

Harley Cameron closed the topic with one final joke directed at anyone still shocked over the segment: "For anyone that was a little bit taken back, I mean guys, just watch my matches. I wear a lot less."

The entire situation quickly became one of the most talked-about moments from Dynamite that week, especially because of how visibly stunned Renee Paquette looked during the live segment. Still, Harley Cameron clearly isn't embarrassed about what happened. If anything, she seems to think everyone else made a way bigger deal out of it than she did, maintaining her composure and sense of humor throughout the explanation.

Released AEW Stars To Join WWE

WWE has been getting plenty of attention and headlines thanks to their recent mass talent cuts that saw 24 wrestlers being released. While it has not happened quite as often as it has in WWE, All Elite Wrestling has had its fair share of released or departed stars from their roster over the past few years.

With the success of former AEW stars in WWE recently, it would make sense for the company to consider bringing in other names who were previously part of Tony Khan's promotion in the near future. Here are six released AEW stars to potentially join WWE.

Matt Taven, listed as the sixth prospect, has an impressive resume stemming from his time in Ring of Honor. Following the closure of Ring of Honor and its eventual purchase by Tony Khan, the company saw former champions making returns to the promotion. Taven is the second ever ROH Grand Slam Champion, holding pretty much every title in the company. Throughout his runs with the promotion, Taven is a former ROH World Champion, multi-time ROH Tag Team Champion, former ROH TV Champion and ROH World Six-Man Tag Team Champions. With his resume and experience, Taven feels like someone who can be a solid veteran presence in WWE moving forward.

Leyla Hirsch is someone who has impressed wherever she has appeared throughout her career whether it be for All Elite Wrestling or Ring of Honor. With her amateur wrestling background, Hirsch was a unique performer thanks to her small stature and underrated strength. After being a standout in ROH for a few years, Hirsch officially left AEW in February 2025 and has been a free agent ever since. Now that she is available, Hirsch feels like someone who can offer something new and different to the WWE NXT roster in the near future.

Mercedes Martinez represents another viable option for WWE recruitment. In early January 2026, it was revealed Mercedes Martinez's AEW contract had expired after a four-year run with the promotion. The longtime veteran was not used on AEW and ROH TV for the better part of two years before becoming a free agent. Looking to finish out her impressive career, Martinez could join WWE as a coach or even an in-ring competitor sometime soon. Whether it be working with the NXT roster or even getting a shot on the main roster, Martinez making her return to WWE would be nice to see after her short stint with the promotion in 2020 and 2021.

Stu Grayson has a notable history with AEW dating back to the company's founding. When All Elite Wrestling first started, one of the key acquisitions in 2019 was the Dark Order, consisting of Evil Uno and Stu Grayson. Widely regarded as one of the most underrated tag teams on the independent scene for years, Uno and Grayson were one of the more featured tandems in the early days of AEW. After being released from the company in 2022, Grayson made his return and re-signed in 2023 before leaving again in 2024. While he has found home back on the indies over the past couple of years, Grayson is someone who could be a valuable asset as a backstage coach for younger stars on NXT or even as an in-ring competitor where he can deliver consistently.

The Righteous, consisting of Vincent and Dutch, recently became available talent. After a two year run with the promotion, The Righteous' AEW contracts expired in 2025, seeing them leave the company to become free agents. Vincent and Dutch stood out thanks to their eccentric personalities and signature entrance theme. Since leaving AEW, The Righteous have since made their presence felt in TNA Wrestling where they are currently embroiled in a rivalry with The Hardys. Despite their success in TNA, the company's working relationship may lead to The Righteous making their way to WWE where they could become even bigger stars in the process.

KJ Orso, formerly known as Fuego Del Sol, completes the list as a talent with unrealized potential in AEW. Back in 2021, Fuego Del Sol signed with All Elite Wrestling on the very first episode of AEW Rampage. Despite this high profile signing, Fuego couldn't quite find a break during his time with the promotion before his three-year deal expired in 2023. Since then, Fuego has undergone a fascinating character change, becoming KJ Orso. Now one of the most entertaining and featured heels in companies like GCW, Orso has proven that he had so much to offer than what was displayed during his AEW tenure.

WWE SMACKDOWN 5/8/2026 EPISODE COVERAGE

The May 8 episode of WWE SmackDown took place at the VyStar Veterans Memorial Arena in Jacksonville, Florida, serving as the final go-home show before the Backlash premium live event on May 9. The episode opened with a respectful in-memory graphic tribute to Ted Turner, the late media mogul and former WCW owner who passed away on May 6 at age 87. Turner's impact on professional wrestling was significant as the businessman who funded WCW and provided the company with the resources to challenge Vince McMahon's WWE dominance during the Monday Night Wars. The tribute came directly before WWE's standard "Then. Now. Forever. Together." signature opening.

The most talked-about segment of the night was undoubtedly the funeral ceremony for the Gingerbread Man costume, which had become the focal point of the Sami Zayn versus Trick Williams feud. The storyline began weeks earlier when Zayn, in his more aggressive heel persona, destroyed the Gingerbread Man mascot that Williams had been using to irritate him. Last week's show saw Williams treating the incident seriously, throwing up the X sign and showing genuine concern before the character was officially announced as deceased.

Throughout the May 8 broadcast, WWE presented multiple segments centered around the Gingerbread Man's wake and funeral. The company aired tribute videos that had been digitally created using AI technology, showing the mascot edited into famous wrestling moments including Hulk Hogan slamming Andre the Giant, Stone Cold Steve Austin getting arrested, Kurt Angle's milk truck segment, Cody Rhodes winning the Undisputed Championship against Roman Reigns, and numerous other historical WWE moments. The videos also featured entirely AI-generated scenes showing the Gingerbread Man water skiing, climbing mountains, DJing, and relaxing with Williams and Lil Yachty.

Backstage segments during the show included funny moments between Sami Zayn and R-Truth at the funeral, which became one of the few highlights praised by reviewers. Zayn also interrupted Trick Williams during the ceremony, angry about the money spent on the expensive funeral setup, mocking the solid oak casket, the abundance of roses, and what he called the "off-key" choir. Zayn complained that SmackDown main events used to feel important and accused Williams of disrespecting the United States Championship. He promised revenge at Backlash before sarcastically apologizing for Williams' loss.

The main event segment featuring the funeral came to a dramatic conclusion when Sami Zayn attacked Trick Williams in the ring. As Zayn stood posing with the United States title, the Gingerbread Man suddenly rose from the casket Undertaker-style. The costume head was removed to reveal Grammy Award-winning rapper Lil Yachty underneath, who had been taken out by Zayn weeks earlier. Yachty grabbed a candy cane kendo stick and struck Zayn multiple times, allowing Williams to recover and connect with his Trick Shot finisher. Williams and Yachty stood tall over Zayn to close the show.

The heavy use of AI-generated imagery in the tribute videos became controversial online, with fans expressing frustration that WWE would resort to what many called "AI slop" instead of the high-quality video packages the company is historically known for producing. Viewers pointed out that WWE had created legendary promo videos and Photoshop edits over the years, making this tribute feel cheap in comparison. Some fans suggested that WWE was spending money on AI videos while simultaneously letting go of talented wrestlers. Critics noted that even though the segment created significant buzz online ahead of Backlash, the execution felt inferior to WWE's usual production standards.

Ted Turner Tribute on WWE SmackDown

Ted Turner is a Surefire Hall of Fame Contender

With Ted Turner's passing on Wednesday at the age of 87, it is only natural to think about his contributions to pro wrestling and his qualifications for the Wrestling Observer Hall of Fame. To many, it's astonishing he isn't already in considering he's been on the ballot for a number of years. In 2025 he did well, earning 55% but ultimately falling short of the 60% required to be inducted. Considering the bump some candidates get after their passing, Turner shouldn't have any problem reaching that percentage this year.

When thinking about Turner's contributions, he should have been in years ago. He was the one that brought pro wrestling to cable television, something he would go on to dominate in the 1970s and 80s. Adding Georgia Championship Wrestling to what would eventually become TBS gave pro wrestling a new home after being shunned by major television networks. After Vince McMahon's attempt to take over TBS' coveted World Championship Wrestling timeslot failed in 1985, Jim Crockett Promotions took over, bringing back wrestling for fans who didn't enjoy the cartoonish antics of the WWF.

By 1988, JCP was struggling and time was running out to save the last major wrestling promotion in the United States that wasn't owned or bought out by Vince McMahon. Turner opted to buy the struggling promotion, keeping wrestling on TBS and paving the way for World Championship Wrestling. Although Turner saved WCW, many within his media empire didn't want anything to do with pro wrestling and didn't see why Turner chose to save something that struggled business-wise. There were many times when he could have listened to those voices and pulled the plug. But he didn't, and with the arrival of Hulk Hogan and other former WWF stars in 1994, business began to boom and by the time the nWo angle kicked off a few years later professional wrestling catapulted itself into a new era of cable dominance. None of that would have been possible without Ted Turner's resolve to keep pro wrestling on his television networks.

It wasn't until 2001 that he no longer had the ability to shield WCW from corporate interests, and the company's dismal track record in its final years ultimately sealed its fate. But the company's enormous success years prior wouldn't have been possible without Turner. Granted, his true hands-on involvement with pro wrestling is a blip compared to the rest of the media empire he built which includes TBS, TNT, CNN, Cartoon Network, Turner Classic Movies, and so on. But the choices he made not only gave professional wrestling a national cable platform, but also prevented it from becoming a monopoly. If he hadn't bought JCP, Vince McMahon may have gotten full control of the industry. Ted Turner's involvement with pro wrestling gave it a new home on television, helped it become a cable juggernaut, and most importantly provided an alternative for both fans and wrestlers.

The Founder of the Superstation: How Ted Turner Changed Wrestling Forever

Since the creation of television, wrestling and TV have been a big part of each other's story. From the DuMont Network airing Wrestling from the Marigold starting in 1949, to Vince McMahon securing cable TV deals with USA to jump-start his national expansion of WWF in the early 1980s, wrestling has always used TV as a way to ensure people will want to sell out the arenas across the country. Few people have been as important to the relationship between TV and wrestling as Ted Turner.

A businessman through and through, Turner redefined television by revolutionizing just how far a local broadcasting network can go. Having already owned a collection of radio stations across the South, Turner purchased a struggling TV station on a UHF signal. For those unfamiliar, UHF stations are the stations above channel 13, which have low viewership compared to the main channels that the larger companies broadcast from. Buying channel 17 in Atlanta in 1969, Turner slowly created a giant in broadcasting.

WTCG, which stood for Watch This Channel Grow, was Turner's first TV station in Atlanta. WTCG was a low-budget station, one which Turner used creative gimmicks to draw audiences to watch the local news. From having news broadcasters have dogs on TV, to other things that made the station stand out, Turner tried it all. In 1971, Georgia Championship Wrestling aired for the first time on TV. For over 25 years, GCW was the main wrestling promotion in Atlanta, and their deal with WQXI-TV, an ABC affiliate, felt like it would boost them into a larger audience. But almost as quickly as they started on WQXI, they left in 1972 to be shown on WTCG.

WTCG, being an independent station, had the freedom that a network station like WQXI didn't have. Also, Turner wanted to expand his audience. By the end of 1972, he had taken his small, independent station that no one watched and was now airing the Atlanta Braves, Hawks, and GCW. But he wasn't done yet.

In 1976, during the 200th anniversary of the United States, Turner made a move that would change television forever. Working with satellite distributors, Turner would start airing WTCG on satellite and cable, creating what he called the Superstation. In 1978, he would change WTCG to WTBS, Turner Broadcasting System. Now the Atlanta Braves, Hawks, and GCW would be broadcast to homes across the country. To ensure he would keep the Braves and Hawks on his network, Turner purchased both in 1976 as well. Within seven years of entering television broadcasting, Turner would become the most powerful owner of a TV station in the nation. While companies were buying multiple stations in hopes of expanding their reach, Turner overnight eclipsed everyone and put himself at the same table as ABC, NBC, and CBS.

WWE Media Rights Contracts Public Disclosure

WWE's previous media rights deals for both Raw with NBCUniversal and SmackDown with Fox were made public during an ongoing shareholder lawsuit against the company, which is set to go to trial in June. The contracts, both dated between 2019 and 2024, provide a rare glimpse into one of WWE's most significant revenue sources.

According to available reports, both contracts confirm previous reporting about WWE's compensation for these flagship shows. Over the five-year period, WWE received $1.325 billion for Raw and $1.025 billion for SmackDown. WWE retained final say on creative decisions for both shows, though the company could not change the basic format of the red or blue brands. An example of a prohibited change would be converting a show into a highlight reel format.

The contracts also confirm that the company had exclusive rights with its broadcast partners, preventing those networks from airing other wrestling shows during the deal's duration. However, the Fox agreement included exceptions for amateur wrestling and other combat sports such as UFC or Glory Kickboxing. For NBCUniversal, the ability to broadcast lucha libre produced from outside the United States on its Spanish language channel was permitted.

In the Fox agreement, WWE was prohibited from disparaging any of the network's other partners, including the NFL or NASCAR, during SmackDown broadcasts. Fox, in turn, agreed to promote SmackDown during its other sports broadcasts, most notably during NFL games. The promotion agreement allocated $125 million over the five years, separate from the media rights payment itself.

The disclosed contracts revealed additional key provisions beyond basic broadcast rights. These included extra content outside of SmackDown on Fox, such as the seven-month run of WWE Backstage and other shows that ultimately never came to fruition. The agreements also established guidelines for how clips from both shows could be shared online, including on YouTube.

For Raw on NBCUniversal, clips could be posted beginning at midnight following the show's conclusion, with specific parameters on how much of the full episode could be shown and time limits on individual clip lengths. The Fox deal for SmackDown contained similar restrictions, with clips posted online before the show's 10 p.m. EST conclusion not permitted to exceed one minute in length.

Regarding renewal procedures, both Fox and NBCUniversal were required to notify WWE by March 15, 2023, during an exclusive negotiating window, whether they were interested in renewing their respective deals. According to available information, there is no public explanation as to why Fox chose not to renew its agreement for SmackDown. In contrast, NBCUniversal received an extension of its exclusive negotiation period, as this timeframe coincided with the ongoing sale of WWE.

The NBCU deal for Raw also provided WWE with the flexibility to offer the company either a two-hour or three-hour version of the program as part of any renewal negotiation. It was announced in January 2024 that Raw would transition to Netflix following the expiration of the NBCUniversal contract. Raw officially left cable television for the first time in its history in January 2025, beginning its Netflix run with a flexible format runtime.

ECW Historical Features

ECW Original The Sandman opened up about two pivotal moments from his time in Extreme Championship Wrestling during a recent appearance on INSIGHT with Chris Van Vliet, revealing details about his involvement in controversial segments and the organic origins of his most recognizable presentation choice.

The Sandman reflected on the infamous crucifixion angle that aired in 1996, in which Raven's flock tied him to a wooden crucifix in one of the most shocking visuals in wrestling history. According to Sandman, he was far more involved in the segment than many fans realize. When asked if he remembered Raven pitching the idea to him, Sandman admitted he couldn't recall the conversation itself, but revealed he personally built the crucifix used in the angle. He explained that he constructed it from the same four-by-fours he had used to build a scaffold for a Tommy Dreamer versus Brian Lee scaffold match, noting that creating the crucifix was relatively easy given his prior construction experience. The segment remains one of the most debated angles in wrestling history, with many believing it crossed a line even by ECW standards.

Sandman made it clear that the controversy was part of the appeal and expressed no regrets about his participation. He stated that he had never been a God-fearing person and didn't believe in traditional Christian concepts like "the Father, the Son, the Holy Ghost." To him, the angle wasn't blasphemous. More importantly, he viewed it through the lens of professional wrestling and dismissed critics, saying "f*ck anybody that didn't like it."

The fallout from the segment became almost as legendary as the angle itself. Following backlash from fans and critics, Raven publicly apologized for the segment. Sandman strongly opposed this decision and believed nobody involved should have backed down. He revealed that he told everyone involved—including Paul Heyman, Todd Gordon, Shane Douglas, Kurt Angle, and Raven—that they were making a mistake. According to Sandman, he said, "You're all f*cking assholes. This is bullsh*t," and opposed Raven going out there to apologize.

The controversy also famously played a role in Kurt Angle distancing himself from ECW at the time. Angle, who had only recently won Olympic gold and was exploring a future in professional wrestling, reportedly wanted no association with the angle after witnessing it firsthand. Sandman said he understood Angle's perspective, especially considering it was his first exposure to the wrestling business. While he couldn't blame Angle or anyone else for their religious objections, Sandman expressed confusion and anger over Paul Heyman's decision to allow Raven to apologize. He stated that he could understand Angle being taken aback by the segment during his initial foray into professional wrestling, but felt that Heyman's handling of the situation was "f*cking bullsh*t."

Beyond the controversial angle, Sandman also discussed how one of wrestling's most iconic entrance themes came to be. Few entrance themes in professional wrestling history are as instantly recognizable or emotionally tied to a performer as Metallica's "Enter Sandman" is to the ECW Original. For generations of fans, hearing the opening riff immediately brings back memories of the Singapore cane-swinging hardcore icon stumbling through the crowd inside the old ECW Arena with a cigarette in one hand and a beer in the other. Yet according to Sandman himself, the now-legendary pairing almost never happened.

Sandman detailed the surprisingly simple origin story behind using the Metallica classic as his entrance music during his rise in Extreme Championship Wrestling. At the time, he had a job working for the Philadelphia Inquirer. He operated what was called the Sandman van, a custom vehicle equipped with four captain's chairs, a bed in the back, a television, and other amenities. Sandman would pile 10 to 12 kids into the van and drop them off in neighborhoods where they would knock on doors to get people to sign up for the Philadelphia Inquirer. One of the kids working for him kept telling him about a particular song, but Sandman didn't initially understand what he was talking about. Eventually, the kid brought him a tape—an eight-track—and when Sandman heard "Enter Sandman," he immediately recognized its potential. At that time, he was coming out to "Big Shot" by Billy Joel, but he switched over to Metallica's "Enter Sandman." As Sandman reflected, "Look how great that worked out."

The change would ultimately become one of the defining presentation choices of the entire ECW era. While modern wrestling entrances are often carefully focus-grouped and produced, Sandman's connection to "Enter Sandman" felt organic, chaotic, and authentic, which perfectly matched the identity of ECW itself. The entrance eventually evolved into a full-on spectacle. Fans inside the ECW Arena would sing along word-for-word while Sandman made his way through the crowd, often taking several minutes to even reach the ring. In many ways, the entrance became bigger than the matches themselves and helped cement Sandman as one of hardcore wrestling's most unforgettable stars.

The pairing also became one of the most expensive unofficial music usages in wrestling history. ECW famously used licensed music without securing long-term rights deals, something that added to the gritty underground atmosphere of the promotion during the 1990s. Even decades later, the combination of Sandman and "Enter Sandman" remains inseparable in the minds of wrestling fans worldwide. What started as a simple recommendation from a kid working alongside him at the Philadelphia Inquirer ultimately turned into one of the most iconic entrances the business has ever seen.

An Unlikely Beginning

Born April 30, 1993, in Vienna, Austria, Thekla holds a Master of Fine Arts degree from the University of Applied Arts Vienna. She describes herself as a creative kid who tried everything — comics, guitar, painting — before finding her true calling in the most unexpected of venues. She stumbled into her first taste of wrestling at a local punk-rock show at age 19 and swiftly became obsessed with the bizarre nature of what she witnessed. That obsession would reshape the rest of her life.

She began her wrestling journey in 2017 in Vienna's underground scene — a world of pub basements, no ropes and loosely enforced rules that bore little resemblance to mainstream professional wrestling. Her unconventional entry into the business turned out to be an asset. If Vienna gave Thekla a foundation, Japan built the house. She considers herself Japan-bred, having worked within the wrestling-obsessed country as early as late 2017. Being the only foreigner in promotions stacked with elite talent forced rapid development. Thekla officially completed her contractual obligations with Stardom following the promotion's All-Star Grand Queendom event on April 27, 2025. She chose AEW, making her on-screen debut May 28, 2025, on Dynamite.

The Toxic Spider

What separates Thekla from the rest of the AEW women's roster is not just her background — it is how all of that background manifests the moment she steps through the curtain. She carries herself with the effortless menace of someone who has nothing left to prove and everything left to take. Her in-ring style is chaotic and precise in equal measure — limb-targeting submissions wound around spider-like movement, sudden bursts of violence punctuated by a cold, unhurried composure that makes her more unsettling than any screaming heel on the roster. The exclusion of one of the biggest unresolved controversies in wrestling history raises important questions about editorial decisions and what defines a comprehensive portrait of Hogan's life and career. Whether Netflix made the right call by prioritizing chronological structure over historical significance remains a point of debate among wrestling fans and documentary enthusiasts alike.

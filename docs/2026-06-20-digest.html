🔥 Cash Wheeler AEW Tag Team Thoughts

FTR's chapter as AEW Tag Team Champions has likely closed following their loss to Adam Copeland and Christian Cage in an "I Quit" match at AEW Double or Nothing last month. According to Cash Wheeler, the team is approaching the end of their in-ring careers and does not believe they will pursue another AEW Tag Team Championship run in the future.

Wheeler explained their current mindset in recent interviews, stating that there is very little they are entertaining doing outside of AEW at this point, even when it comes to new signings. He acknowledged that they are getting close to the end of their careers and no longer aspire to win major championships like the AEW Tag titles. Instead, the focus has shifted toward elevating other wrestlers and pursuing meaningful matches that benefit both FTR and their opponents. This mentality has led them to accept selective bookings on the independent scene.

One such booking includes a match against the Young Guns at Revolution Pro Wrestling's 14 Year Anniversary show in London on August 29, just one day before AEW All In. The Young Guns, consisting of Luke Jacobs and Ethan Allen, are rising stars on the UK indie circuit and former RevPro Undisputed British Tag Team Champions. Wheeler emphasized that the goal of these bookings is to help both RevPro and young talent rather than to pursue championships.

Beyond RevPro, FTR has aspirations to compete in NJPW's World Tag League, a round-robin tag team tournament where the winners earn an IWGP Tag Team Championship match. However, Wheeler made clear that winning the tournament is not the primary objective. Instead, he simply wants to experience the tournament before FTR's in-ring career concludes. He humorously noted that he might not even make it through the entire tournament, joking that he could take a bad bump and decide to head home, having given his best effort.

The CMLL Tag Team Championships also remain on FTR's radar, but again, the experience matters more than the gold. Wheeler specifically expressed a desire to perform in Arena Mexico, which is the only "big four" venue FTR has yet to touch. The big four venues are considered to be Madison Square Garden, Wembley Stadium, the Tokyo Dome, and Arena Mexico. Should FTR achieve a match in Arena Mexico, Wheeler believes he and Harwood would become the first tag team to have wrestled in all four of these legendary venues.

Despite being on a hiatus from AEW, Wheeler has remained active in following the broader wrestling world. He has been watching matches from the 1990s and All Japan Pro Wrestling, though he selectively cherry-picks modern matches that interest him. One recent match that caught his attention was Chad Gable's mask versus mask encounter against Ludwig Kaiser's version of the character at AAA Noche de los Grandes.

Wheeler expressed genuine enthusiasm about Gable's recent work, saying he was impressed enough to watch the match twice. He praised Gable for finally receiving more recognition and spotlight, noting that Gable has been an exceptional performer for a long time and deserves the attention. Wheeler also reflected on FTR's history with Gable, recalling their three-match series against American Alpha when the group was known as the Revival in WWE ten years ago.

Wheeler marveled at how well those matches with Gable and Jason Jordan have held up over the decade, particularly their NXT TakeOver: Dallas encounter. He expressed a wish that he had access to the house show matches the teams worked together, as he believes those performances would also be well-received if more people could see them. Wheeler specifically mentioned the two-out-of-three falls match, suggesting that it would be more talked about today if it had been featured outside of Full Sail, the venue where many NXT matches were recorded.

Looking toward his eventual retirement from television wrestling, Wheeler has indicated that he plans to pursue a singles career on the independent scene once FTR's run as a tag team concludes. He mentioned that there have been times when he considered taking singles matches in AEW to reduce the physical toll on Dax Harwood's body. This included discussions about a potential singles match against CM Punk in AEW that never came to fruition. Wheeler explained that after FTR's feud with the Briscoes and subsequent program with either Punk or the Young Bucks, he did not feel that wrestling Punk as a heel in a singles match was the appropriate next step, and the idea was scrapped. 🔥 John Cena WWE Status and Career

John Cena's retirement from professional wrestling marks the end of an era for WWE, but the legendary performer is not looking back. When asked about a potential return to the ring, Cena has been explicit about the astronomical financial barrier he has set. This approach allows him to keep his promise to the audience that molded him into who he is today—a promise made when he recognized it was time to step away because he had become a step too slow.

What truly sets Cena apart from many other wrestling legends is his philosophical approach to legacy and relevance. During an interview with Good Housekeeping, Cena revealed a surprising perspective that contradicts the typical desire of wrestling veterans to be remembered forever. His primary goal throughout his 23-year wrestling career was never to build a personal legacy but rather to leave the business in a better place than he found it. According to Cena, his résumé is merely a byproduct of this mission, not the ultimate goal.

Cena articulated his hopes for the future by stating that he wants people to forget about him. This seemingly counterintuitive statement is rooted in his belief about what constitutes true success in the wrestling industry. In his view, the ultimate measure of success would be seeing a new generation of WWE stars become so influential and relevant that they capture the audience's attention and loyalty, rendering past legends like himself irrelevant. If the current WWE stars have enough gravity and relevance, they should be the ones influencing audiences and people in the arena. Cena expressed amazement at his own hope that fans will forget about him, indicating that this philosophy represents a genuine shift in how he views his contributions to wrestling.

Despite stepping away from in-ring competition, Cena has remained actively involved with WWE in various capacities. Most notably, at Backlash, he announced the creation of the John Cena Classic, a work-in-progress tournament that represents his latest contribution to WWE's future. TNA Creative Direction and Problems

TNA's Creative Direction and Problems

The old saying goes you want to leave a place better than it was before you got there. But once you leave, who will replace you? TNA turmoil has been in the news this week with Tommy Dreamer, seen as one of TNA's key creative figures in recent years, suddenly departing from the company. It was probably the right time to do so; as mentioned in previous opinion coverage, TNA needs a fresh creative reboot because frankly, the company's current direction in that department is dire. His departure, however, highlights a larger issue across the pro wrestling spectrum: wrestling companies are increasingly dependent on only a handful of well-known, established creative names, with little indication of who will step up in the future.

Hunter Johnston, also known as Delirious, will be leading TNA's creative team in Dreamer's absence. He ran Ring of Honor for a long time and his track record is divisive to say the least, particularly towards the end of ROH's time under Sinclair. The bigger question here is why people in wrestling keep turning to the same pool of names over and over again. It becomes even more apparent when one of the names being talked about to helm TNA creative is Brian James, also known as Road Dogg. He decided to leave WWE earlier this year, and towards the end of his run often got accused of being the person behind all of the bad SmackDown angles and storylines since he was the head writer. Considering SmackDown's problems are still continuing, those accusations were likely unfair in hindsight. But again, this is a familiar, safe name that reinforces how small the creative talent pool is.

The larger problem lies in the lack of creative minds in pro wrestling these days. Post-WCW, much of the creative behind WWE and TNA were Vince McMahon and Vince Russo, respectively. Underneath them were a bunch of writers who, over time, became a revolving door of faceless people who came and went. People got promoted, people left, no one was really a constant beyond who ultimately made the final decisions. Even in Ring of Honor, once Gabe Sapolsky left it was Jim Cornette who ran things for a while before Delirious was brought on board.

Triple H now helms WWE creative but much like Vince McMahon, there's no one really underneath him to take his place if TKO sours on him. Who would they bring in, Bruce Prichard? AEW has some creative people but much like McMahon, Tony Khan is such a big part of AEW's creative that if something happens, there will be a vacuum of power. There are many independent promotions out there run by creative people that have the potential to do a great job if given a new opportunity on a bigger platform. But that highlights another problem in pro wrestling as there are so few promotions that have national weekly television and in the rare instance where there's an opening, those in charge want someone who has that experience, limiting the talent pool considerably.

Across the spectrum pro wrestling needs to do a better job of developing tomorrow's creative minds, not just focusing on today just to get by. While Road Dogg could potentially do well as TNA booker, the question remains whether that represents the best option available to Anthem. It's not just TNA facing this challenge, though; it's a bigger problem across the industry. Every promotion must ask itself the same question: what's the plan for the future? We know who is in charge now, but succession planning and creative development remain largely unaddressed concerns that could have serious consequences for wrestling's television landscape.

Why Carlos Silva's Latest TNA Wrestling Pivot Could Be His Last

If you gave TNA president Carlos Silva some truth serum, or perhaps a relaxing beverage or two, he would likely admit this isn't how he expected things to turn out. He took over the company in February 2025, injecting new leadership from outside the world of wrestling, though at least from someone with combat sports experience that Anthem Sports & Entertainment wanted to support. After the sting from Scott D'Amore's dismissal wore off, things actually seemed to be going alright. Silva became more present, donning his now notable TNA Wrestling hat at ringside and was talked about positively online, a rare feat on social media. TNA made their WWE partnership official, attendance was up, and there was eventually the AMC TV deal. Positive things were happening.

Then, as every executive in wrestling eventually does, inching in front of the camera always eventually turns into full frame. He gave out backstage awards, presented title belts to champions, and even did an extended interview with Ariel Helwani. He became the face of the company and fans seemed to embrace that. Silva had to be loving life and living the dream with TNA. An eventuality with TNA, however, is that even with an upswing, it always gets pulled back down to Earth with the speed of a balloon being tied to a kettlebell, all of which happened in the last few months.

There was negative press about TNA pulling talent from matches with AEW wrestlers due to partner conflicts. Silva made bizarre assertions that AEW decided to go head-to-head with TNA on a few Thursdays at a time when that traditionally happens due to NHL postseason games. The afterglow faded from the AMC launch at a time when no one has any idea what ratings even mean. Plus, there's the constant narrative about WWE pulling all the strings from afar, and now talent is both leaving and likely to leave.

The TNA life became more real for Silva this week when Tommy Dreamer and Sami Callihan were let go in a move called workforce reduction, designed to streamline operations and sharpen strategic focus and profitability. It's everything wrestling fans love to hear: corporate jargon reminiscent of what TKO's Mark Shapiro would approve of.

Sixteen months after he took over, Silva is now face-to-face with TNA's reality and the weight of all the years that have come before it. He made a pivot like a sports general manager would do when things aren't working out: he fired the manager, which in this case was Dreamer. The move isn't necessarily bad, but it's unclear how it will change anything fundamentally. Now, there's no other pivot to make other than for Anthem to make their own change if Silva's next course of action doesn't work, the likelihood of which seems very improbable. Silva also could decide to bow out on his own, which wouldn't be unprecedented.

That's not to say Silva is incapable, but TNA finds itself in an impossible spot as does every other company not owned by WWE or AEW. The wrestling landscape is besieged with content every night of the week, sometimes multiple shows on weekdays and weekends. This month alone features AEW Forbidden Door, TNA Slammiversary, and NXT Great American Bash running nearly head-to-head-to-head, which feels like more of a punishment to fans than a reward.

Silva is charged with making TNA stand out in an already overcrowded marketplace. The challenge is daunting because great wrestling already exists in other spots, huge spectacle events with big stars are done elsewhere, and exiled former WWE and AEW names getting opportunities are also available at MLW and a dozen other super indies who do the same. The fundamental problem is that why would anyone watch WWE Lite when there's already more than enough of the real thing available.

All of this is to say that there's hope Silva has some bold moves planned. However, hiring a former WWE creative member and Triple H associate in Brian James doesn't seem like one of those moves, nor does an affiliation with OVW. Since TV is already taped through Slammiversary, whatever changes come next will take time to materialize on screen, leaving the question of whether Silva and TNA have enough runway to make their next chapter count.

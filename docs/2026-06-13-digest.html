🔥 Chris Jericho AEW Plans and WWE Lawsuit Comments

Chris Jericho Discusses Future Plans, WWE Lawsuit Strategy, and Career Reinvention

At 55 years old, Chris Jericho has made it abundantly clear that retirement is not in his immediate future. During a recent interview with Dan Marston, the veteran performer explained his motivations for continuing to work across multiple creative platforms. Jericho emphasized that he still loves doing cool things that audiences will appreciate, whether in wrestling, music, acting, podcasting, or other ventures. He expressed that creative stimulation and motivation are essential to him, and he views his various projects as interconnected elements of his overall body of work.

When asked about slowing down, Jericho was direct about his mindset. "What am I gonna do? Retire from doing everything, and go take this box and put it over there, and grow flowers for the rest of my life? I have no intentions of slowing down," he stated. He acknowledged that while he may not wrestle forever, he has no desire to do so anyway. According to Jericho, activities like playing music, acting, doing podcasts, and other projects can continue well into his 80s, and he plans to keep pursuing cool opportunities across all these mediums for as long as possible.

Jericho's recent babyface return to AEW in April has been well-received by fans, marking a significant shift from his previous Learning Tree gimmick, which had drawn "please retire" chants. The shift in fan reaction suggests that audiences have embraced his evolution and are interested in seeing what he does next, whether it's related to the Jericho Cruise, his Talk Is Jericho podcast, television appearances, or his band Fozzy.

Beyond his in-ring plans, Jericho recently shared insights into WWE's corporate approach to legal matters. During an episode of Talk Is Jericho, he revealed that WWE had a firm policy of refusing to settle lawsuits out of court. According to Jericho, the company believed that settling even one case could open the floodgates for additional claims from people seeking quick payouts. "Because if you settle for one, then everybody's going to start breaking through. They would rather pay, you know, $30,000 than give out 10 because if you give out 10, then everyone for the low-hanging fruit is going to start trying to get involved," Jericho explained.

This policy reflected WWE's broader philosophy of using litigation as a deterrent against what the company viewed as opportunistic claims. Rather than viewing settlements as cost-effective solutions to individual disputes, WWE considered fighting cases—even expensive ones—as preferable to establishing a precedent that might encourage future lawsuits. Jericho's comments came in the context of recent developments involving Janel Grant's sex trafficking lawsuit against WWE, Vince McMahon, and others, with the defendants seeking to move the case to arbitration.

One of the most significant topics Jericho addressed was his decision to abandon the Y2J character, which he described as probably the biggest creative risk of his career. When Jericho returned to WWE in 2007, he felt the character had become stale and was concerned about becoming a nostalgia act rather than continuing to evolve as a performer. The Y2J persona, complete with the iconic countdown timer and associated presentation, had defined much of his earlier WWE success, but Jericho recognized that times had changed and the character no longer carried the same energy.

To commit fully to his reinvention, Jericho made drastic changes to his presentation. He cut his long hair, switched from long tights to trunks, explicitly told announcers to stop calling him Y2J, created an entirely new video package with none of the old branding, and eliminated the countdown that had become so synonymous with his identity. Jericho compared the boldness of this move to Kiss removing their iconic makeup—it was a clear signal that something fundamentally different was happening.

Rather than worrying about how fans might initially react to such a dramatic change, Jericho focused on committing fully to the new direction. "Whether you like it or don't like it, that's not for us to decide, but here's where we're going. Here's the path we're taking. Come on board if you want to," he explained. While the move represented significant risk, Jericho emphasized that it was necessary and ultimately reinforced an important lesson that has guided the remainder of his career.

Through this reinvention, Jericho learned that he could evolve whenever he began feeling stale, a principle that has defined his Hall of Fame-worthy career. From the suit-wearing "Best in the World," to the eccentric "List of Jericho" era, to his self-proclaimed "Demo God" persona, to his current AEW character, Jericho's ability to adapt and reinvent has kept him relevant across multiple decades. He emphasized that while people will have mixed reactions to his various iterations, he cannot allow external opinions to dictate his creative direction. "I just have to worry about what I can do to be the best personality I can be," he concluded, underscoring the personal evolution that continues to drive his professional decisions.

🔥 Paramount Skydance Acquisition of WBD and AEW Media Rights

PARAMOUNT SKYDANCE MERGER WITH WARNER BROS. DISCOVERY APPROVED BY JUSTICE DEPARTMENT

The U.S. Justice Department has given the green light for Paramount Skydance to acquire Warner Bros. Discovery in a landmark $111 billion merger that will reshape the media landscape. After an extensive eight-month review, the DOJ's Antitrust Division determined the transaction did not pose a threat to competition and declined to challenge it. The department approved the merger without requiring any divestitures, behavioral remedies or concessions, according to multiple sources familiar with the decision.

This approval represents a significant victory for Paramount and the Ellison family, particularly given the relationship with the Trump administration and the current regulatory climate. The decision came days before the UFC held their inaugural event at the White House in honor of Donald Trump's birthday. According to Politico's reporting on the decision, "After an extensive review, DOJ officials determined the transaction did not pose a threat to competition and declined to challenge it. The department approved the merger without requiring any divestitures, behavioral remedies or concessions, according to one of the people."

The approval marks a major step forward for what would become one of the largest media mergers in recent years. The deal is expected to close in September, and both sides are now looking to close up the deal as soon as possible. The transaction will bring together Paramount's assets with Warner Bros. Discovery's massive library and platforms, including CNN and HBO Max. The merger would combine HBO Max with Paramount+ to create a new streaming offering boasting approximately 200 million subscribers.

However, the merger is not completely out of danger despite the federal approval. The deal still faces potential legal challenges from state-level regulators. California Attorney General Rob Bonta is still reviewing the deal and could potentially sue to block it, even after federal regulators gave their approval. Bonta's office made it clear that the state-level review remains active, stating "The Paramount acquisition of Warner Brothers remains an active investigation." Other state attorneys general can also still sue to block the deal despite the Antitrust Division's decision.

The approval goes against an open letter opposing the deal that was signed by more than 5,500 people, who stated that the merger would result in reduced competition in the entertainment space, less jobs, and higher prices for the average American customer. Some signatures came from notable figures including Robert De Niro, Edward Norton, and Ben Stiller. The merger has also drawn concern from Hollywood workers and entertainment industry critics, who fear it could lead to layoffs, fewer opportunities for creators, and even more power concentrated among fewer major companies. Those concerns have only grown because Paramount has already talked about more than 6 billion dollars in expected synergies within three years of the deal closing.

The DOJ approval also comes after a heated public fight involving Netflix. Paramount recently accused Netflix of running a "scorched-earth campaign" to damage the WBD deal, claiming the streaming giant was encouraging opposition from groups like the Teamsters. Netflix denied the accusation and called it absurd.

For AEW, the implications of this merger are significant, though still uncertain. AEW currently maintains a media rights agreement with Warner Bros. Discovery that runs through the end of 2027 and includes an option year for 2028. The company's fate won't be fully known until both the merger is complete and AEW's current media rights deal with WBD expires in December 2027, or if the extra year option is activated in December 2028.

AEW President Tony Khan is reportedly very optimistic about the merger and being under the Paramount-WBD umbrella. It was reported at the start of June that Khan saw Paramount as the more favorable option when it came to the WBD bidding war, with the other major bidder being Netflix, which would have likely been worse for AEW given that service's partnership with WWE. Khan looks forward to the future under the new corporate structure.

Should the merger officially close, industry observers expect that Paramount's streaming platform and WBD's HBO Max service will eventually be combined into a single streaming destination. Such a move could potentially affect how AEW programming is distributed and consumed in the future, although no changes regarding AEW's current media rights arrangement have been announced. Paramount currently has no wrestling on any of its networks or services, but does have an extensive and expensive set of deals with TKO for UFC, Zuffa Boxing and PBR. TKO, of course, also owns WWE.

For now, AEW's current relationship with WBD has not changed, but this federal approval makes the bigger media picture a lot more real. If Paramount and WBD do combine, Tony Khan may soon be dealing with a much different company on the other side of the table, and that could shape AEW's television and streaming future in a significant way. The merger remains on track for completion, with the DOJ's approval removing one of the biggest obstacles standing in its way, though final closure depends on whether state-level legal challenges succeed or fail.

Karrion Kross AEW Conversation and WWE Return Status

Karrion Kross Says He's Had Private Conversations With AEW And Reveals WWE Return Status

He currently holds the MLW World Heavyweight Championship along with three additional titles across different promotions. His journey following this departure has sparked considerable fan speculation about his potential landing spot in professional wrestling.

I'm not one of those guys that wants to drop a title or go missing because a big opportunity comes up."

BIG BILL HEADING TO WWE WITH ENZO AMORE REUNION IN THE WORKS

Big Bill's time in AEW is coming to an end. According to reports from Fightful Select, the former AEW star's contract is set to expire within the next week, and he has officially given notice to the company that he will be departing. The 6-foot-10 standout is expected to return to WWE, where he previously competed as Big Cass.

Big Bill rose to prominence in WWE as one half of the popular Enzo and Cass tag team alongside Enzo Amore. Both men were released from WWE in 2018. Following his WWE departure, Bill competed on the independent circuit and in TNA Wrestling before signing with AEW in 2022. During his time in AEW, he won the tag titles with Ricky Starks before Starks left to become WWE's Ricky Saints. Most recently, he had been working tag team matches with Bryan Keith as part of Chris Jericho's Learning Tree disciples, though that storyline has not been actively pursued since Jericho's return earlier this year.

A reunion with Enzo Amore appears to be on the horizon. WWE intends to sign Enzo as well, although he had not officially agreed to a contract as of mid-June. WWE reportedly wanted to wait until Big Bill's AEW obligations had concluded before bringing both men back to the company. The belief within WWE is that the company did not want to sign Amore and then find itself in a situation where Big Cass wouldn't be able to sign. If the plans come to fruition, it would mark the long-awaited reunion of one of WWE's most popular tag teams from the mid-2010s.

The timing of this move coincides with WWE's reported efforts to rebuild its tag team division following recent releases that saw multiple tag teams being let go or disbanded. Big Cass has demonstrated growth during his AEW tenure, overcoming personal struggles that plagued his initial WWE run and proving he is in a much better position to succeed. He is married to AEW interviewer Lexi Nair, whom he met while working with her step-father Diamond Dallas Page early in his sobriety.

The question remains how Amore and Cass will fit into the current WWE landscape, more than a decade since their peak popularity. There is no doubt there will be buzz when they return, but the challenge will be sustaining that momentum over time. Both performers will face pressure to perform to modern-day wrestling standards, though signs have been positive regarding Cass's in-ring work during his AEW run.

BRIAN CAGE EXPLAINS HIS DECISION TO RE-SIGN WITH AEW

In contrast to Big Bill's departure, Brian Cage has revealed why he chose to remain with AEW despite receiving significant interest from WWE when his contract expired in 2023. Speaking on the Insight podcast with Chris Van Vliet, Cage confirmed WWE pursued him and admitted he seriously considered making the move before ultimately deciding to stay.

"There was a lot of interest, and it's funny because I wanted to go there," Cage explained. "You don't know what you're going to get either place. There's no guarantees. They could say that we're going to use you this way, that way, whatever, but obviously there's you don't know anything. And after going back and forth, all the different pros and cons, a lot of stuff with AEW just seemed more safe, if you will, which is pretty hard to have some sort of security in this business."

The primary factor in Cage's decision came down to job security and long-term stability. He noted that WWE has a history of randomly releasing talent, whereas he feels more secure under Tony Khan's leadership. "The longer I stay, the longer I feel I can just keep staying to have longevity," Cage said.

Several aspects of AEW's working environment factored into his decision. Cage highlighted the superior travel arrangements as a significant perk. "Travel is way better. He pays for all the travel, so hotel, ground transportation, and airfare, where WWE is just airfare," Cage explained. In contrast, WWE typically only covers airfare for talent.

Another important factor is the creative freedom AEW provides. "We're an actual independent contractor, so I can do anything else outside of AEW," Cage noted, emphasizing that this additional flexibility was a major plus. He also expressed confidence that he would not face the threat of sudden release, a concern he associates with WWE's approach to talent management.

Looking ahead, Cage said his goal is to establish himself more as a singles competitor rather than being featured primarily in tag team or multi-man matches. "I've always wanted to do more solo stuff because I feel that's how I get my stuff in. I feel like that's how I stand out more," he stated. "There's so, so much I feel like I haven't done in AEW, especially compared to my time in Lucha Underground and Impact and anywhere else."

Cage noted that many of his AEW appearances have come in tag team or multi-person matches, leaving plenty of potential singles matchups still on the table. "There's just so many matches I haven't had. The few that I have, the matches with Ospreay and Swerve and Hangman and stuff, obviously been great," he said, indicating that he hopes for more singles opportunities in the future.

Regarding his ultimate aspirations in AEW, Cage acknowledged that winning the world title is obviously a goal. However, he expressed a more immediate ambition to be part of the Continental Classic tournament, which takes place at the end of the year. "That's something definitely I want to," he said. "I would love to build up enough repertoire to have an awesome Continental Classic run by the end of the year."

BRIAN CAGE RESPONDS TO CRITICS AND RECEIVES PRAISE

Brian Cage has addressed criticism of his unique in-ring style, explaining why he believes his athleticism should be celebrated rather than questioned. Speaking on his podcast appearance, Cage noted that fans often underestimate him because of his muscular physique before seeing what he can do in the ring.

"When I first started doing that, that is what really stood out and got me over, especially in PWG. They see me and they're like, 'All right, this guy's awful,' and then they see me work, and it's like, 'Holy crap, this is insane,'" Cage recalled.

Cage received one of the biggest compliments of his career from WWE Hall of Famer Mick Foley during WrestleMania weekend. "He looks at me and goes, 'Man, Brian, anyone who looked like you never made it back in my day, and if they did make it, they weren't good.' He goes, 'Not only are you good, then you can do stuff in the ring that nobody your build can do. You are one of a kind, definitely cut from a different cloth,'" Cage recounted.

Cage went on to defend his high-flying offense, arguing that his athletic feats are a product of natural ability rather than cooperation from opponents. "When I flip over the top rope to my feet, that's just me being physically, athletically gifted enough to do that. No one's helping me do that. So that should be celebrated," he asserted.

While standing by his style, Cage admitted there is one aspect of his wrestling he continues to work on. He acknowledged that he can sometimes be too selfless by giving and taking too much in an effort to produce the best match possible.

BRIAN CAGE JOINS STEVEN SPIELBERG'S NEW FILM

In an unexpected crossover between wrestling and Hollywood, Brian Cage appeared in director Steven Spielberg's latest film, "Disclosure Day," which was released in 2025. The movie opens with a wrestling scene featuring Cage and Lance Archer.

Cage explained how he was cast in the project. He noted that Chavo Guerrero, who serves as a coordinator on wrestling-related Hollywood projects, initially contacted him about a television show that ultimately got postponed. When that fell through, Guerrero suggested Cage contact someone else about a different role that would be a good fit.

"I'm in the gym, all of a sudden I get an email," Cage said. "And it's, 'Hey, I just sat down and watched everything with the director. He absolutely loves you, your look, your persona, your charisma, everything you do, you're exactly perfect and you're exactly what he wants.' Great email to get. And he goes, 'Give me a call immediately.'"

When Cage called to learn more details, he discovered that the director was Steven Spielberg himself. "I'm in the gym, all of a sudden I get an email... He keeps saying Steven. I go, 'Steven, who's Steven?' And he goes, 'Oh, the director, Steven Spielberg.' And I went, 'Wait, what?' And I go, Steven Spielberg is watching my stuff. He's like, that's it, I need Brian Cage in my movie. I go, 'This is amazing,'" Cage recalled.

Spielberg's team gave Cage the opportunity to select another wrestler for the film. They wanted another big guy with a different look than Cage. Cage recommended his tag team partner Lance Archer, who was already visible in some of the photos Cage had submitted during the casting process. Chavo Guerrero also appeared in the movie as the referee of their match.

Describing Spielberg as "super nice," Cage said the director was impressed when he saw that a big man like Cage was able to hit a moonsault. "When I did the one moonsault too, he goes, 'Whoa, I didn't know you could do that kind of stuff.' The way he popped for it," Cage said. "He wasn't the smart mark like, 'Oh, just stick to big man stuff.' He's like, 'Whoa, that was amazing.'"

Cage noted that Spielberg was surprised he had never incorporated wrestling into any of his previous films. "He was a huge wrestling fan and he was talking about how he's surprised he's never done, this is the first time he's ever done anything wrestling related in any of his movies," Cage explained. "And he's talking about all the throwback stuff."

Like with many Hollywood projects, Cage said he filmed three times more material than ended up in the final cut. He hopes some of that deleted footage gets released as bonus scenes. "There's a couple of specific shots he wanted us to do," Cage said when asked about being directed by Spielberg. "Like, he wanted this one cool diving elbow or headbutt off like how it was filmed. He wanted the POV shot."

During their conversations on set, Spielberg expressed interest in attending an AEW event in the future. When Cage mentioned that AEW had a pay-per-view scheduled in Los Angeles around that time, Spielberg said he would have loved to attend but was still filming at that time. However, he made it clear he would be interested in coming to a show if the timing worked out in the future.

MICK FOLEY'S WWE DEPARTURE AND AEW SIGNING

In a significant move related to roster changes, Mick Foley recently opened up about the financial losses he suffered from his decision to walk away from WWE. According to Foley, the decision was prompted by the company's ties to President Donald Trump.

Foley had publicly supported former Vice President Kamala Harris during her presidential run and was later critical of Trump and what he deemed to be overly aggressive actions by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agents. However, the final straw came when Trump made insulting comments about the deaths of actor-director Rob Reiner and his wife, Michele Singer Reiner.

"I no longer wish to represent a company that coddles a man so seemingly void of compassion as he marches our country towards autocracy," Foley said in a statement in December. "Last night, I informed WWE talent relations that I would not be making any appearances for the company as long as this man remains in office."

He added: "Additionally, I will not be signing a new Legends deal when my current one expires in June. I love WWE, will always treasure my time with them, and I am deeply appreciative for all the opportunities they afforded me. But, in the words of Popeye the sailor, 'I stands all I can stands, and I can't stands no more.'"

Speaking to Going Ringside about the financial impact of his decision, Foley explained: "It was a difficult decision to make, to kind of cut ties with them. I hadn't been employed by them for many years. I was an independent contractor, but I did have a merchandising deal. I had a couple really easy, high-paying events at WrestleMania weekend. As it turned out, I had to work 24 hours on my own to make what I would have made in four with WWE. But I'm okay with that. I believe if you want something, you go after it and you work hard for it."

When asked about Trump's mocking comments regarding Reiner's death, Foley responded: "Oh, man. There's just a type of discourse to me that is unacceptable from anyone, let alone the most powerful man in the world. You don't mock people when they die. And why people in his party don't call him out on that type of behavior, it's not acceptable. That was the straw that broke the camel's back, but there were an awful lot of straws on that camel's back already."

Foley has since signed with AEW and made his debut for the promotion at this year's Double or Nothing event, where he got physical with MJF (Maxwell Jacob Friedman). When asked if there were any AEW stars he'd like to wrestle, Foley left the door slightly open to a potential ring return.

"You never know what the future might bring. I'm down a lot of weight, but my cardio is suspect at best, and I'd still have to lose another 30 pounds just to hit poor conditions. So, we'll see if there's some changes in the next year," Foley said.

🔥 AEW Forbidden Door Event

This injury forced him off the card, and Bandido took his place in the Continental Championship match against Moxley. Newman subsequently teased an AEW return by posting on social media asking "Is the door still open?" following his missed Forbidden Door appearance.

Gabe Kidd was originally slated to challenge IWGP Global Heavyweight Champion Shota Umino at Forbidden Door, but visa issues prevented his appearance. Kidd, who was returning from a shoulder injury, has since been scheduled to face Umino on July 6 in Tokyo instead. Kidd is also expected to miss Night One of the G1 tournament beginning in Chicago on July 11.

Veteran NJPW star Tomohiro Ishii was reportedly penciled in for Forbidden Door before a neck injury sidelined him. While unconfirmed, it is believed Ishii would have competed in the 12-man Death's Door steel cage match alongside The Conglomeration. To cover for his absence, Ishii was written off AEW television several weeks prior to the event in storyline fashion.

However, AEW did not medically clear Sareee to compete, leading to Maya World taking her place. Khan further praised the new TV Asahi ownership for how they have promoted NJPW over the past year, expressing relief that Gedo and Tanahashi remained in leadership positions. Several AEW talent currently hold championships in NJPW, including Mistico as one-half of the IWGP Junior Heavyweight Tag Team Champions, Konosuke Takeshita as NJPW World Television Champion, Will Ospreay as a NEVER Openweight 6-Man Tag Team Champion, and Alex Windsor as Strong Women's Champion.

🔥 Austin Theory WWE Career Reflections

AUSTIN THEORY WWE CAREER REFLECTIONS: THE CENA MENTORSHIP, MISSED OPPORTUNITIES, AND VINCE'S BACKING

Austin Theory has opened up about some of the most significant moments and decisions from his WWE career, offering insight into his development as a performer and the challenges he faced navigating the company's shifting creative landscape. Speaking candidly on Logan Paul's ImPaulsive podcast, Theory reflected on personal mentorship, missed opportunities, and the exact moment he realized he was being positioned as a future main event star.

THE JOHN CENA INFLUENCE AT WRESTLEMANIA 39

One of the most touching revelations came from Theory's recollection of his WrestleMania 39 encounter with John Cena, when Theory defeated his childhood idol for the United States Championship. What made the moment even more significant was the genuine mentorship Cena provided both before and after their match.

Theory explained that growing up, Cena was always his idol. He recalled having a cardboard cutout of Cena in his bedroom and shared a particularly emotional family story. His grandfather had made one specific request if Theory ever faced Cena in the ring: that he would get a hot dog and a Coke in the crowd. Theory's girlfriend made sure that wish came true on WrestleMania day.

Before their match, Cena took Theory to dinner and provided guidance that initially confused the young star. At that point in his WWE tenure, Theory was performing as a cocky, obnoxious heel character, and he wasn't sure how to reconcile that with Cena's advice.

"I just remember him telling me, 'You're not a bad guy. Like I want you to be you,'" Theory said. "I was like, 'Man, I'm this obnoxious, over-the-top, cocky heel.' And he's like, 'I feel like that's not going to last too long. You got to be yourself.'"

The mentorship didn't end with dinner conversation. During their actual match, Cena continued to guide Theory, even asking if he knew who Evander Holyfield was before suggesting they incorporate that reference into the match by having Theory bite Cena's ear. Cena also called spots during the match itself, helping Theory navigate the bout.

However, one piece of advice resonated more deeply than anything else. As Cena reflected on their encounter, he emphasized the magnitude of what they were doing. "Just remember every show we do is a live event. This is the biggest show we have. We treated it like a live event," Cena told Theory. That lesson stuck with Theory throughout their match and beyond.

After the match concluded with Theory's victory, Cena's support extended even further. According to Theory, Cena went directly to Vince McMahon and advocated for him. "After the match, I remember Cena telling me he was like, 'Hey, I went and talked to Vince. And I told him, You're not a bad guy. So hopefully that helps you become a good guy,'" Theory recalled, laughing at the memory.

Despite Cena's efforts and endorsement, Theory never actually turned into a babyface character as Cena had suggested. Instead, Theory continued his heel run as a singles competitor, eventually teaming with Grayson Waller before ultimately joining The Vision stable. Still, Theory clearly appreciated the genuine care and mentorship Cena showed him during that pivotal moment in his career.

REALIZING VINCE MCMAHON'S BACKING

Theory also revealed the specific moment when he truly understood that Vince McMahon had serious plans for him and was actively pushing him toward main event status. During his time in NXT, Theory said he was simply finding his footing. Things began to change when he joined The Way, where he started showcasing more of his humor and personality. That became a turning point in his development as a character.

When Theory arrived on the main roster, however, he didn't immediately grasp that McMahon was behind him. The realization came after one specific moment that made everything click.

After Theory won the United States Championship and became the youngest U.S. Champion in WWE history, McMahon orchestrated a particular celebration that changed everything. "I remember when I won the US title, Vince wanted all the heels to come out, hold me up in the ring, and then as I left the ring, he came out at the end on the stage and held my arm," Theory recalled. "And I think in that moment, I was like, 'Whoa, like something's going on here.'"

That public endorsement was the clearest signal Theory received that McMahon saw him as a cornerstone of WWE's future. Theory acknowledged that while the signs had been there, this moment made him realize the scope of McMahon's investment in him. He became one of WWE's most prominently featured young stars during that era, even being present with McMahon at WrestleMania 38.

THE MONEY IN THE BANK FRUSTRATION

However, not every decision made during Theory's push under McMahon's watch left him satisfied. When Theory won the Money in the Bank briefcase in 2022, the final premium live event booked entirely by McMahon before his initial retirement announcement, Theory had high expectations for what could come next.

Theory was one of the few Money in the Bank winners who didn't cash in on the world championship. Instead, creative had him pursue Seth Rollins' United States Championship. Theory not only failed to win that match, but the entire segment lacked the dramatic payoff he felt it deserved.

When asked if he felt "robbed" by the failed cash-in, Theory answered with an emphatic "Yes." He explained that while he knew going into it that he wouldn't defeat Roman Reigns for the Undisputed WWE Universal Championship, he felt there could have been more storytelling and emotional resonance to the attempt.

Theory referenced a specific moment with Reigns at Barclays Center where Reigns hit him with the line, "Your daddy's not here anymore," referring to McMahon. In that moment, Theory wanted to build toward something greater, even if the end result would still be a loss.

"I felt like there could have been a moment; I knew I wasn't gonna get the title off Roman, but even just building it to seem like 'oh, here we go'. I can lose to him, whatever, but just give it more of a payoff, because it felt cool man; I did a lot of cool things with it," Theory said.

The failed cash-in attempt served as a turning point. When Triple H took over as head of creative following McMahon's initial retirement, Theory's position shifted significantly. Though he eventually won the United States Championship at Survivor Series 2022, the momentum from his Money in the Bank push had dissipated.

Theory kept the Money in the Bank briefcase as a memento, and it now hangs in his office, dented from use. When people ask him about the damage, they often assume he used it on opponents. "It's funny how I have it in my office hanging, and it's real dented, and people see it and go 'You murdered people with that huh?' I'm like, 'No, Brock Lesnar murdered me with it, and it sucked,'" he said.

THE BROADER PICTURE

Theory's reflections paint a picture of a young talent who was genuinely invested in and mentored by some of WWE's biggest names, yet found himself caught in the transition between creative regimes. His career has been marked by significant highs, such as defeating Cena at WrestleMania and receiving the backing of McMahon, but also frustrating lows where creative opportunities didn't fully materialize as he hoped.

At 28 years old, Theory still has time to reach the pinnacle of WWE, and he remains among those cited as potential future main event stars. He has supporters in high places within the company, and his recent spotlight as part of The Vision stable suggests there may still be chapters yet to be written in his WWE story. Theory himself acknowledged that career trajectories have ups and downs, and he's focused on reflecting on where he can improve and what character changes might help him move forward. Despite the disappointments and missed opportunities, Theory's journey thus far reflects the complex reality of being a young star in a major wrestling promotion, where guidance from mentors like Cena can be invaluable, but where creative decisions and timing ultimately shape the trajectory of a career.

Boxing Momentum Article

Boxing can be very straightforward when viewed from a distance. You have two boxers, a ring, several minutes-long rounds, strikes measured by judges and remembered by fans. However, any person who pays closer attention will know that even before the scorecards change, a fight can change drastically. A boxer may still land his punches, move effectively and appear confident while something has changed inside already.

Momentum in boxing is subtle. It lies in breathing, positioning, rhythm, hesitation and how one boxer behaves after missing his strike by an inch. That is also the reason why boxing is attractive to all those people who enjoy decoding subtle signs. There are those who watch tapes, there are those who compare styles and there are people who bet on boxing matches. The main thing here is that boxing never rewards lazy assumptions. A good puncher, noisier crowd and a well-known boxer can form a story before the fight starts but the fight will keep rewriting it anyway.

The first few rounds can be a mystery to most people. People usually expect action to start immediately, particularly if there are two belligerent boxers on the ring. But the truth of the matter is that some boxers will try to use the first six minutes of boxing as a private investigation. Boxers will find out the distance between them and their opponent. Boxers will also study reaction times of the opposition. Boxers will also throw punches but do not expect them to land. This is because they want to get some information from the exchange. This is what makes early momentum an interesting concept in boxing. It is possible that the boxer that seems busier is only being tested by his counterpart. The quiet boxer might have been giving up the round while collecting valuable information. Boxing fans who know this watch the feet rather than the fist. It is possible that one fighter is dictating the place of the exchanges without throwing a single punch.

Boxing pressure is usually associated with walking forward, throwing combinations, and fighting exchanges. This is one type of pressure which will easily break down an opponent. But pressure does not always have to be so aggressive. It can be very technical. It can also be counter-punching or body punching. Pressure does not always need to involve the same old clichés. The most fascinating pressure is pressure that alters the opponent's style or even causes the fighter to change his game plan due to pressure. For example, when the fighter stops throwing the jab because it continues to be countered, this is pressure. Or when a boxer circles into the wrong direction because he has lost his safer side, that too is pressure.

Small signs often matter most: The fighter begins breathing with an open mouth earlier than expected. The lead foot starts landing wider after each exchange. The jab becomes a push instead of a snap. The guard returns slowly after combinations. The fighter looks to the corner before the bell. None of these signs guarantees a finish. Boxing is too unstable for that. Yet they show where momentum may be moving. A fight can still look even while one boxer is paying a higher physical price.

Body punching is one of the least glamorous aspects of boxing from the perspective of spectators. Head punches are loud. Knockdowns are clear. A good hook on the jaw will be a highlight right away. But body punching takes time, requires patience and, often, seems unnoticed during the first viewing. This is precisely why it works. A good body punch attack does not necessarily have to end a match right away. It robs a person of his movement ability. It robs him of his volume. It makes him second guess any action that he wants to take forward or backwards. Body punches make legs not as loyal. Arms feel heavy. The brain makes smaller decisions. A boxer who invests in body work systematically degrades his opponent's ability to function at full capacity.

During the media scrum following Forbidden Door, Tony Khan was asked about WWE's counter-programming strategy against All In. Khan acknowledged that he was aware of WWE's announcement and had seen it announced during AEW's own show that evening. When discussing the situation, Khan stated according to the AEW Forbidden Door Scrum (h/t Wrestling Inc.): "There's a lot of prestige going back to Wembley Stadium. I think it's going to be a great, great night for AEW. I can't speak to what the other promotions are doing, but I did see that announcement. I saw that with our own show going on tonight. Not a lot that I can do other than I just hope we have a very good show and to plan and prepare that we have a very good show for AEW All In London."

Khan's response reflected a confident but measured approach to the situation. Rather than engaging in public criticism of WWE's tactics, he focused on AEW's own preparations and the significance of returning to Wembley Stadium for the company's flagship event.

WWE has a well-documented history of counter-programming AEW pay-per-views. A notable example occurred when WWE ran Wrestlepalooza on the same day as All Out 2025, which also happened to coincide with WWE's debut on ESPN.

🔥 AEW and NJPW Forbidden Door

AEW x NJPW Forbidden Door 2026 struggled to match the company's usual pay-per-view performance, according to reports from Dave Meltzer in the Wrestling Observer Newsletter. While complete buy rate figures remain unavailable, early indicators suggest the event finished as AEW's lowest-performing PPV of 2026. Meltzer noted that early numbers suggested it would finish last in what has otherwise been a strong year for the company, though he framed this as a relative dip rather than a disaster given the show's typical appeal to a hardcore fanbase.

The primary factor cited for the underperformance was the absence of an AEW World Championship match. MJF, the AEW World Champion, participated in a 12-person steel cage match between teams led by Mark Briscoe and the Don Callis Family, but did not defend his title. Meltzer theorized that MJF would have generated significantly more buys in a straightforward singles match compared to his involvement in the crowded tag match. The executive noted that the card had the shape of a super show on paper, but lacked the centerpiece that typically drives PPV sales. MJF later defeated Briscoe in a title match that opened the July 1 episode of AEW Dynamite instead.

HBO Max numbers, the key distributor for AEW programming, were comparable to Dynasty, which drew around 145,000 buys, though slightly lower given decreased performance on other distribution platforms. External competition also played a role in the PPV's performance. NXT's free Great American Bash special likely siphoned viewership during the go-home stretch. A Sunday night baseball game between the New York Yankees and Boston Red Sox aired opposite the show, though it was not expected to overshadow the entire event. Unlike some previous years, there was no primetime World Cup match competing for audience attention.

Additional complications contributed to the weaker card. Visa clearance issues prevented numerous New Japan Pro Wrestling stars from attending, which reduced the cross-promotional emphasis that typically highlights Forbidden Door events. Late card changes tied to injuries and travel issues further impacted the lineup. The absence of Yota Tsuji and the missing IWGP Championship match, the first time an IWGP title bout has not appeared at Forbidden Door, represented unusual circumstances for the event.

AEW President Tony Khan addressed the logistical challenges of coordinating with NJPW during the post-show media scrum. Khan expressed interest in potentially booking a tournament featuring stars from both companies but acknowledged that such an endeavor would present significant complications. He explained that his preference would be to bring NJPW wrestlers to AEW further in advance of the PPV to build storylines earlier, though this often proves impossible due to various constraints. Khan noted that NJPW requires its wrestlers for their own programming commitments, a situation he understands given that AEW runs 52 weeks of television annually.

Khan pointed to Nick Wayne's involvement in the last two editions of NJPW's Best of Super Juniors tournament as an example of successful cooperation between the companies. He reiterated that while sending talent across promotional lines is beneficial, AEW's demanding television schedule sometimes makes it challenging to send stars away for extended periods. Khan maintained that a dual-company tournament remains something he would love to execute if the logistics ever align properly, emphasizing that Forbidden Door's appeal lies in getting everyone under one roof.

The event also featured a major storyline development involving Will Ospreay and Jon Moxley's Death Riders faction. During an interview on 107.7 The Bone, Ospreay explained his controversial decision to align with the group that had attacked him at Forbidden Door 2025, causing a serious neck injury that sidelined him for six months. At Forbidden Door 2026, the Death Riders supported Ospreay as he defeated Swerve Strickland to win the Owen Hart Foundation Tournament. Since April 2026, Ospreay's association with Moxley and company has intrigued and confused AEW fans worldwide.

Ospreay justified the partnership by explaining that the Death Riders were the only ones who came to his aid when no other backstage allies, teammates from United Empire, or Swerve Strickland offered assistance. He stated that rather than seek revenge against Moxley by using a chair on him as he had done to Ospreay, the wrestler chose to accept help and transform himself into something greater. Ospreay noted that he previously hesitated when delivering crucial finishing moves, specifically the Tiger Driver '91, but that hesitation no longer exists thanks to Moxley and the Death Riders.

While Ospreay emphasized he has not forgiven Moxley for the 2025 injury, he compared his situation to Batman learning martial arts from Ra's al Ghul and the League of Assassins. This metaphor captured his view of the relationship as one of necessary mentorship and transformation despite the violent origins of their association. The wrestler characterized the arrangement as accepting that he needed help to reclaim what was rightfully his and to become a more lethal competitor in the process.

🔥 Will Ospreay AEW commentary and matches

Will Ospreay has praised AEW's Skye Blue for the efforts she has put into her wrestling development. While signed to AEW since 2021, Skye Blue has also appeared for multiple independent promotions during this time, including Pro Wrestling Eve, where Ospreay serves as a producer. Recently, Ospreay shared a message on social media and praised fellow AEW wrestler Skye Blue for her work in AEW and Pro Wrestling Eve.

In response to a social media post talking about Blue's improved mic work, Ospreay hoped to see people notice Blue's efforts and improvements. He wrote: "Quietly Skye Blue has been putting in a lot of work into her game and the improvements she's been making is a testament to her. Hopefully more people will take note of effort she is putting into the craft. Glad to host her again at Pro Wrestling Eve."

Blue recently defeated Emersyn Jayne at EVE 151: Beers, Brawls And Burlesque, where she also cut a promo about showing she has the spirit of EVE and expressed hopes to have a prize waiting for her when she returns to the promotion over All In London weekend. Skye Blue currently serves as one third of the Triangle of Madness on AEW television alongside Julia Hart and Thekla, with Skye being the only member of the group yet to capture championship gold in the company.

Ospreay's support of Blue comes as part of his continued involvement with Pro Wrestling Eve, an organization that hosts a number of current AEW women's stars. The growth of AEW's women's division has accelerated in recent months, with the recent introduction of Women's Tag Team Championship title belts providing more opportunities for wrestlers at various card levels to shine on Dynamite and Collision.

Beyond his work mentoring talent, Ospreay has established himself as one of the most accomplished wrestlers in professional wrestling history. Following his match against Swerve Strickland at AEW x NJPW Forbidden Door, which itself received a 5.5-star rating from Dave Meltzer, Ospreay now has an unprecedented 55 matches that have reached the 5-star threshold or higher throughout his career.

His five-star-plus matches span multiple promotions including PWG, NJPW, Rev Pro, Impact, TNA, and AEW. Some of his most acclaimed performances include his legendary encounters with wrestlers such as Kenny Omega, Bryan Danielson, Kazuchika Okada, Shingo Takagi, and Zack Sabre Jr. His highest-rated match came against Bryan Danielson at AEW Dynasty in March 2024, which received a 6.5-star rating. Other notable performances include his 6.25-star match with Kenny Omega at NJPW Wrestle Kingdom 17 in January 2023 and his 6-star match against Michael Oku at RevPro High Stakes in February 2024.

Ospreay is currently 33 years old and has racked up quite a few top draw matches across the likes of New Japan, Rev Pro, and AEW. He recently won the 2026 Owen Hart Foundation tournament and is scheduled to challenge for the AEW World title at All In 2026, signifying his main event status within the promotion.

Beyond his current AEW pursuits, Ospreay has also expressed interest in continuing his legendary rivalry with Ricochet. This past May, the two men celebrated the 10th anniversary of their viral clash at the NJPW Best of the Super Juniors 2016 tournament, where Ospreay walked away with the victory at the Korakuen Hall in Tokyo, Japan. The match is often credited for playing a role in the evolution of professional wrestling over the last decade, with their high-flying style and fast-pace athleticism impacting the sport in mainstream companies.

Since both men signed with AEW, they've only had two singles matches against each other. While celebrating the 10th anniversary on "Talk Is Jericho," Ospreay expressed interest in renewing the rivalry. He explained: "The idea of him being in the shape that he's in now and me being in the shape that I'm in now, I want to do it one more time. I'll always have that love, that respect and that gratitude towards him for being so patient with me. I don't think there's going to be a day where we don't do this. I think honestly we're probably going to do this when we're 50."

Ricochet also reflected on how the match has continued to be part of wrestling conversations today, while also touching on his real-life friendship with Ospreay. He stated: "Going through that together and being part of a little, I don't want to say revolution, but being a part of a little bit of history that's going to live on forever, and like I said, a conversation piece forever. Outside of the ring, we just had a great connection and just our personalities go so well against each other."

Ricochet also expressed that his friendship with Ospreay has grown over the last 10 years, with both men agreeing that entering battle together made them closer as friends and competitors. Their mutual respect and willingness to work together again demonstrates the lasting impact their initial encounter had on professional wrestling and their personal relationship.

🔥 Vince Russo and JCW

During a live stream promoting his book Total Non-Stop Agony: The Rise and Fall of TNA, Vince Russo discussed JCW's prospects in detail when asked by Violent Jay to identify the promotion's two biggest strengths and weaknesses.

Russo did not hesitate when naming JCW's primary strength, offering high praise for Violent Jay's creative capabilities. Russo stated that nobody working for any major wrestling company right now possesses the creative mind to generate and develop characters the way Violent Jay does. He went further by comparing Jay's abilities directly to Vince McMahon, claiming that even McMahon at his best could not create the characters that Violent Jay brings to the table.

According to Russo, Violent Jay's creative process gives him a significant advantage as a writer. Jay conceives the complete character package, including the gimmick, look, and gear, before handing it to Russo for scripting. Russo emphasized how valuable this setup is for a television writer, pointing to examples like zombies and the Brothers of Construction as characters he has been privileged to write for. Russo called this collaboration a gift that allows him to do his job at the highest level.

The second major strength Russo identified is that JCW is charting its own course rather than copying WWE, AEW, MLW, GCW, or any other promotion. Russo was clear that JCW stands apart because nobody else is presenting wrestling the way they are, making their uniqueness a competitive advantage in a crowded landscape.

However, Russo also identified significant challenges facing JCW. The biggest weakness, in his assessment, is the fundamental difficulty of convincing people who stopped watching wrestling years ago to give the product another chance. Russo explained that the wrestling business has sustained massive damage over the years, and simply getting lapsed fans to watch represents the hardest part of JCW's mission.

Russo provided context for this problem by discussing the historical decline of wrestling viewership. He noted that wrestling on Monday nights used to draw approximately 10 million combined viewers between WWE and WCW, while Raw on Netflix currently draws only 1.5 million viewers worldwide over an entire week. This represents a loss of roughly 8.5 million viewers. Russo emphasized that those fans did not disappear by accident but left because wrestling shifted its focus away from characters and storylines toward match-oriented content.

According to Russo, the wrestling business abandoned what made it compelling to audiences. Rather than focusing on characters, storylines, cliffhangers, and reasons for viewers to return the following week, modern wrestling became overly concerned with the wrestling itself. Russo noted that since professional wrestling is not real, audiences who left did so precisely because the character-driven and story-focused elements disappeared.

Once wrestling stops being part of someone's weekly routine, Russo explained, it gets replaced by other entertainment options. This makes JCW's task even more difficult because they are attempting to win back viewers who may not even think about wrestling anymore. The challenge is not just offering a good product but making people aware the product exists and convincing them to break their established entertainment habits.

Russo clarified that JCW is not begging anyone to watch but rather inviting lapsed fans to check out the product and decide for themselves whether it hooks them back into wrestling. He expressed the philosophy that if JCW successfully pulls viewers back in, they succeeded, and if they do not, JCW did not do its job well enough.

Beyond the fundamental challenge of audience acquisition, Russo identified a secondary operational weakness related to JCW's current business structure. The wrestlers on the JCW roster are independent contractors rather than performers locked into company contracts. This arrangement creates real challenges when writing long-term storylines, as key performers may suddenly become unavailable for specific tapings or dates.

Russo acknowledged that while JCW is not yet at a position where it can maintain wrestlers under contract, this limitation forces the creative team to get inventive and adjust plans on the fly when scheduling conflicts arise. However, Russo expressed optimism that once JCW secures a streaming deal, the company will be positioned to lock talent down to contracts, which would allow for more stable and ambitious long-term creative planning.

When asked which wrestlers from AEW and WWE he would like to see work with JCW, Russo identified several character-driven performers he believes would thrive under his and Violent Jay's creative direction. He expressed particular enthusiasm about the possibility of working with the Wyatt Sicks, saying he could not imagine what creative heights they could reach together. Russo also specifically named Killer Kross and Scarlett as performers he would love to see in JCW at some point.

These preferences align with Russo's broader vision for JCW. Throughout the conversation, he consistently positioned JCW as a promotion built around wild characters, engaging storylines, and an Attitude Era-style presentation that emphasizes character work above all else. In Russo's view, this approach represents what modern wrestling has lost and what JCW is uniquely positioned to reclaim.

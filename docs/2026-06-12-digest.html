Chris Jericho Addresses Backlash Over Tommy's Mommy Segment On AEW Dynamite

During the June 10 episode of AEW Dynamite, Chris Jericho and Tommaso Ciampa engaged in a heated in-ring confrontation that quickly turned comedic. Jericho began calling Ciampa "Tommy," and after Ciampa insisted that nobody calls him Tommy, not even his own mother, Jericho launched into a series of jokes about "Tommy's mommy." The segment led to considerable discussion online, with many fans criticizing the comedy-heavy exchange and particularly the "Tommy's Mommy" portion of the promo.

However, Jericho defended the segment and said the live audience reacted exactly the way he hoped they would. Speaking on Busted Open, he explained his approach: "Well, you know, you always try different things to have some fun. Sometimes you do a little bit more laughing stuff and sometimes you do a little bit more serious stuff but, it all ends up being good so, the live crowd enjoyed the Tommy's Mommy."

Jericho pointed to the crowd participation during the segment as proof that it worked. The chant became one of the most memorable parts of the opening confrontation, with fans in Cincinnati loudly joining in throughout the exchange. According to Jericho, getting that type of reaction from the audience was the entire objective. He stated: "So, we had a good time. It was a great show, and a great crowd in Cincinnati for sure."

Jericho's comments make it clear that he is not focused on the criticism coming from social media. Instead, he appears to be judging the segment based on how the live crowd responded in the arena, and from his perspective, the audience engagement made it a success. Whether fans loved the jokes or hated them, the segment certainly got people talking, and judging by Jericho's reaction, that alone may have accomplished exactly what he wanted.

Personality and Character Remain Essential in Modern Wrestling

With impressive feats of athleticism being pulled off on a weekly basis on AEW television, Chris Jericho has warned young wrestlers that they need to always prioritize building a connection with the audience above just pulling off flashy moves. The importance of storytelling and attaching meaning to actions inside the ring is something veteran names in the industry have been voicing for years.

While speaking positively about the way the in-ring aspect of pro wrestling has evolved from an athleticism standpoint, Jericho believes that personality, character and charisma are still vital in building a connection with the fans. Speaking with Dan Marston, Jericho said: "You have to connect with the audience, and I'm not going to throw stones at the style of wrestling nowadays because when I started our style was the version of what's going on today. The peak of athleticism in 1991 was a Moonsault. I remember the first guys to do a Moonsault in Japan was myself and Lance Storm. Now a Moonsault is the opening move."

Jericho acknowledged that while athleticism continues to evolve, the fundamental principles of wrestling remain unchanged. He explained: "But that's fine, the athleticism grows but the same principle for wrestling never changes. It's all about your personality and about your character and about your charisma and connecting with the audience."

He emphasized the critical nature of audience connection, stating: "You have to connect. If not, you're just a couple guys out there doing high spots and not getting any reactions because there's so much going on, no one's paying attention because nothing is special. There has to be a reason for what you're doing, a reason why the match is even happening."

Jericho stressed that it falls to all wrestlers to discover their own path to connection with fans. He said: "So, it's up to all the guys and girls to figure that out for themselves. You have to find a character that's going to connect and be a little unique that people are going to remember."

The Biggest Risk of Jericho's Career: Dropping the Y2J Persona

Chris Jericho has done it all during his nearly four-decade wrestling career. When reflecting on his most significant character reinventions, Jericho picked his 2008 WWE heel turn as the biggest risk he's taken with his character throughout his entire career.

When Jericho returned to WWE in 2007, he initially brought back elements of his previous incarnation, but he soon realized he did not want to become a nostalgia act. He explained his thinking at that time: "There was a time when I came back in 2007 where I was kind of a, not gonna say a diluted version of Chris Jericho, but you know, it wasn't the same. I had shorter hair, and it just, times had changed, right? So, I thought, I don't wanna do this anymore, I don't wanna be a nostalgia act."

What followed was one of the most drastic image overhauls in wrestling history. Jericho systematically dismantled nearly every element of the Y2J persona that had defined him for years. He described the changes: "So, I switched from, I cut my hair, like I said. I switched from long tights to trunks. Dropped the Y2J. I told the announcers 'Don't ever call me Y2J ever again.' Did a whole new video package that had none of that. And then, the countdown was synonymous with Jericho. Get rid of the countdown. It's done."

Jericho compared his transformation to one of the most notable image changes in music history. He said: "And, that was kind of the first time where I really took a shot, cause it was like, you know, it's almost like KISS when they took off the makeup."

The goal was to show fans that a new version of Chris Jericho had arrived. He stated: "You're doing something very drastic here, and you want people to understand we're doing something different. Whether you like it or don't like it, that's not for us to decide, but here's where we're going, here's the path that we're taking, come on board if you want to."

While acknowledging that the change was risky, Jericho noted that the experience taught him a valuable lesson about character evolution. He explained: "It was a big risk, but not for long, and it's something that I had to do. And, that's when I realized I can reinvent myself whenever I start feeling a little stale, and people will like some of it, not like some of it, but I can't worry about what people think. I just have to worry about what I can do to be the best personality I can be."

David Bowie as Creative Inspiration

Jericho cited legendary musician David Bowie as one of his greatest creative influences, pointing to Bowie's ability to continually transform throughout his career. When a host noted that "if you're not evolving, you're dying," Jericho enthusiastically agreed and elaborated on Bowie's legacy: "Well, that's exactly right. And, my biggest inspiration in show business is David Bowie. I always was really obsessed and interested in really paying attention to what he was doing, which he always reinvented himself."

Jericho described the breadth of Bowie's transformations: "I just did a podcast a couple of months ago of all the Bowie characters and there's literally a dozen of them. You could go to a Halloween party and see 12 people dressed as Bowie but different versions and you would know it's Bowie."

He then drew a parallel to his own career, explaining: "And it's like that with Chris Jericho as well. Every Halloween people could come as Chris Jericho and there's every era that you can see. So that's what my overall goal was and it kind of worked out."

Over the course of his seven years in All Elite Wrestling, Jericho has continued to evolve his character with various monikers including The Painmaker, Le Champion, The Wizard and The Learning Tree, demonstrating his commitment to reinvention and avoiding stagnation. This philosophy of constant evolution remains central to his approach as a performer, keeping his character fresh and relevant regardless of the wrestling landscape around him.

Cody Rhodes Wrestling Commentary

Cody Rhodes has offered wrestling fans some insight into a commonly misunderstood aspect of professional wrestling psychology. During an appearance on What Do You Wanna Talk About?, the American Nightmare explained that the old-school babyface comeback was not always about pretending a move did not hurt. Instead, it was about showing toughness by refusing to let the opponent see the pain.

Rhodes elaborated on the concept by describing the classic sequence. "I wish more people knew this, but today, if you and I are wrestling in the ring, and you hit me, and I decide not, no more, and I start hulking up, right, and I start moving, and you hit me again, nope, not again, and I start, I wish people knew today, that was very common amongst territory, 80s, babyface, the idea that, nah, it's not a not sell, it's a, I'm pretending not to be hurting. Right, it hurts, actually, but I'm not letting you feel it. But I'm not letting you know that."

The distinction is important: wrestlers were selling the act of hiding pain, not ignoring the pain altogether. This psychological approach allowed babyfaces to demonstrate their resilience and mental fortitude against their opponents, creating a more nuanced emotional narrative within the match.

Rhodes noted that while Hulk Hogan popularized and branded this technique as the "Hulk-Up," the strategy existed long before him among many top babyfaces of the territory era. Chief Jay Strongbow, known for his war dance, and Dusty Rhodes, Cody's father, both employed similar comebacks. Rhodes explained that these figures had their own variations of the approach. "When, as much as Hogan absolutely took over it all, but when he created it as the Hulk-Up, it's now made it so that this thing is a Hulk-Up, which is amazing, a credit to him for creating it, but I wish people knew, that used to be every top babyface, at a certain point, whether it was Chief Jay, with the war dance. Chief Jay, when he used to, of course, the war dance, of course. Or my old man, when it's, nope, we're done, that was your way, and it's different."

Rhodes also addressed a similar misconception regarding Japanese wrestling psychology. Many fans have criticized Japanese wrestlers for not selling moves effectively, but Rhodes explained that this represents a different approach to the craft rather than a deficiency. "In Japanese wrestling, a lot of people talk about how there's not selling, and they don't realize, no, they're selling that they're not hurt. That's what they're selling, I'm good, I'm good, and then you find out they're not."

In Japanese wrestling, the performers are still communicating the story of the match to the audience, but they do so by selling the facade of toughness. The audience later discovers that the wrestler was actually damaged by the moves, creating a different dramatic payoff than traditional American wrestling sells.

Through these explanations, Rhodes emphasized that wrestling psychology is more layered than casual observers often recognize, and that different wrestling traditions employ distinct but equally valid approaches to telling in-ring stories.

WWE Creative and Pitch Process

The ongoing discussion about WWE's creative process and how wrestlers interact with the company's writing team has sparked important conversations about responsibility and accountability in professional wrestling.

AJ Styles recently shared his thoughts on how wrestlers should approach pitching ideas to creative teams and why simply bringing a problem isn't enough. While speaking on the Phenomenally Retro Podcast, Styles explained that talent should come prepared with more than just complaints about what isn't working. "If you're going to go in there with an idea, don't just tell them what's wrong. Have a solution. Have something to bring to the table. Don't just say, 'I don't like this.' Give them something else to work with." Styles said performers should understand that creative teams are managing storylines involving many different people at the same time. "You've got to remember they've got a lot going on. They're not just worried about you. They're worried about everybody else too. So if you're going to pitch something, make sure it's something that can actually work."

The former WWE Champion noted that wrestlers should be willing to contribute ideas while also understanding that not every suggestion will be used. "Sometimes they're going to like it. Sometimes they're not. That's just part of it. You can't take it personal every time somebody doesn't use your idea." Styles also emphasized the importance of confidence when presenting an idea. "If you believe in it, then explain why you believe in it. Tell them why it works. Tell them why it makes sense. If you're passionate about it, let them know." According to Styles, the best pitches are often the ones that help everyone involved rather than focusing on a single performer. "You want something that's going to help the story. You want something that's going to help everybody involved. That's when you've really got something."

Styles concluded by reiterating that wrestlers should always be thinking creatively and looking for ways to improve what they're doing. "Don't be afraid to come up with ideas. Don't be afraid to speak up. Just make sure when you do, you've got something to offer."

Speaking on his Hall of Fame podcast, Booker expressed strong opinions about the relationship between creative writing and performer success, arguing that writers can create a character but it is ultimately up to the wrestler to make it work. You're at risk of being one of those guys that's going to get released. That's just the bottom line."

Booker said wrestlers have to stay ready because preparation is the only real luck they will ever have in the business. "Preparation is the only luck you're ever going to have. Booker made it clear that a wrestler's ability in the ring and their star power are what truly decide their future. "Your in-ring acumen and your star power is the only thing going to keep your job. Bottom line."

To illustrate his point, Booker discussed The Undertaker's original character, noting that the concept sounded peculiar on paper. Booker T's fundamental point was straightforward: a writer can hand someone an idea, but the wrestler has to make fans believe in it. In The Undertaker's case, what could have been a strange short-term gimmick became one of the most iconic characters in wrestling history because the performer behind it was skilled enough to carry it. This perspective emphasizes that performer quality transcends creative constraints and that talent, preparation, and star power remain the deciding factors in a wrestler's longevity and success.

Together, these perspectives paint a picture of professional wrestling's creative ecosystem where both contributions matter: wrestlers should engage constructively with creative teams by bringing solutions rather than just problems, but ultimately their own performance, dedication, and star quality determine whether they will succeed regardless of the material they're given.

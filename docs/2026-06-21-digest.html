🔥 FTR Stepping Away from Wrestling

Since the summer of 2014, FTR has maintained a grueling schedule without substantial time away from the wrestling business. For the first time in over a decade, the decorated tag team of Dax Harwood and Cash Wheeler is hitting the brakes. Following their loss to Adam Copeland and Christian Cage in an I Quit match for the AEW World Tag Team Championship at Double or Nothing last month, FTR has been absent from AEW television. The team's absence, combined with reports about their approaching contract option year, sparked speculation about their future. Now, Harwood has provided clarity on the situation by explaining their decision to step away.

In a detailed Instagram post, Harwood revealed the reasons behind FTR's absence and the toll the constant work schedule has taken on both members of the team. He wrote: "Since the summer of 2014, The Revival, FTR, Dawson & Dash, Dax & Cash, whatever you want to call us, we've had the pedal to the floor. Aside from my bicep tear in 2017, we've taken no time away from the job we've dreamed of having. Physically & mentally, we both became exhausted."

Harwood provided an extensive list of injuries and physical ailments he has endured throughout their career. He noted that as two of the few talents working both AEW Collision and AEW Dynamite, they maintained an exceptionally demanding travel and work schedule. Among the injuries he catalogued are three hematomas on his lower back, another bicep tear for which he declined surgery, a labrum tear extending from his groin to his hip, and a shoulder that requires replacement. He also mentioned a potential laundry list of other health issues he has been too afraid to get checked out.

Despite the seriousness of his physical condition, Harwood's tone remained characteristically candid and somewhat humorous about the situation. His willingness to publicly detail these specific injuries underscores how significant the cumulative toll of continuous wrestling work has become. The decision to step away represents a major moment for a team that has prided itself on their work ethic and dedication to the profession.

Regarding their future, Harwood expressed considerable uncertainty. He stated: "For the first time in 12 years, we've decided to step away and take some time for ourselves & for our families. I'm not sure when we'll be back, what we're going to do, how much longer we have, or if we even need to prove anything at all anymore." This candid admission suggests that FTR is grappling not only with physical recovery but also with existential questions about their career trajectory and remaining ambitions.

However, FTR did not commit to a complete hiatus. Harwood announced that the team will fulfill two wrestling dates later in the summer: a match against The Good Brothers in Corbin, Kentucky on August 22 for Appalachian Mountain Wrestling, and a RevPro Undisputed British Tag Team Championship match against The Young Guns at Wembley Arena on August 29. These bookings indicate that while FTR is stepping back from regular AEW programming, they are not entirely removing themselves from wrestling.

The timing of this announcement carries additional significance given FTR's contract situation with AEW. Reports indicate that the team is approaching the option year of four-year deals they signed back in 2023. According to industry reports, WWE has shown preliminary interest in the team, with this interest being communicated to FTR through mutual contacts. However, no indication exists that a departure from AEW is imminent, and Harwood's statement appears to prioritize personal well-being over contract negotiations or corporate positioning.

The decision by FTR to step away raises questions about the broader patterns of wrestler health and sustainability in professional wrestling. As one of the few teams working both AEW's major television programs, FTR has operated at a level of intensity that many in the wrestling community cannot maintain indefinitely. Harwood's detailed account of his injuries serves as a public acknowledgment of wrestling's physical demands and the cumulative impact of a relentless schedule.

For AEW, the absence of FTR removes one of its most accomplished and respected tag teams from active competition. The team has held the AEW World Tag Team Championship multiple times and has been central to the company's tag team division since its formation. Their absence from the current product creates space for other teams, and reports suggest that the tag division may see new additions in the coming months.

As FTR takes this unprecedented time away after 12 years of continuous work, the wrestling community awaits clarity on what comes next for the veteran team. Whether they return to AEW, explore other opportunities, or reconsider their long-term involvement in the business remains to be determined. For now, Harwood and Wheeler are prioritizing recovery and family time, recognizing that the professional dreams they have pursued for over a decade must be balanced against personal health and well-being.

🔥 Owen Hart Cup Finals

In one of the most compelling stories of the Owen Hart Foundation Women's Tournament, Maya World defeated ROH Women's World Champion Athena in a stunning upset on AEW Collision on June 20, 2026. World, who entered the tournament as a last-minute injury substitution for Sareee, will now face Mercedes Moné in the finals at AEW x NJPW Forbidden Door on June 28. The winner of that match advances to August's All In and receives a shot at the AEW Women's World Championship.

World's run through the tournament has been nothing short of remarkable given the circumstances surrounding her life. She entered the competition unexpectedly after Sareee was not medically cleared to compete, and she did so while dealing with the recent tragic loss of her brother. Despite these hardships, World defeated Skye Blue in the quarterfinals to earn her shot against Athena in the semifinals.

The matchup between World and her mentor carried significant real-life and professional history. Athena trained World when she was breaking into the wrestling business, establishing a genuine connection between the two wrestlers. Their relationship had previously played out professionally in ROH, where they feuded with Athena ultimately prevailing. Most recently, World took Athena to a draw in March 2026, after which Athena defeated her in a title defense. The semifinal match marked their third significant encounter.

The tournament match itself played out with Athena as the dominant veteran and World as the scrappy underdog, keeping the contest competitive throughout. Athena controlled much of the action down the stretch and appeared poised for victory when she hit the O-Face. World, however, kicked out at the three-count, shocking Athena and the audience. Once Athena regained control, she channeled Shawn Michaels' famous last words to Ric Flair, telling World, "I'm sorry. I love you, but it's my time." Athena charged forward for a clothesline, but World ducked low and trapped her mentor in a bridging roll-up. World secured the pinfall victory with a 1, 2, 3 count, pulling off what announcers described as a major upset.

The emotional aftermath of the match reflected the significance of World's journey. After the bell, the wrestlers appeared to break character to share a meaningful moment. Jinder Mahal Explains WWE Contracts

Former WWE Champion Jinder Mahal, whose real name is Raj Dhesi, recently surfaced on social media with a comprehensive video breaking down step-by-step how WWE contracts work for talent in the company. The explanation addresses what has long been a source of confusion regarding WWE wrestler compensation, including pay-per-view bonuses, merchandise royalties, and downside guarantees.

Mahal began by explaining the basic structure using a hypothetical example: a 3-year contract worth $1 million per year as a downside guarantee. WWE pays its talent every single week, so the downside guarantee is divided by 52 weeks. In this example, that breaks down to approximately $19,230 per week, which Mahal rounded to $20,000 for simplicity. The critical point is that this $20,000 check comes in every single week regardless of whether a wrestler is injured or wrestles seven days a week. It is a guaranteed minimum payment.

However, wrestlers can absolutely out-earn their downside guarantee. The downside guarantee only represents a minimum, with no set maximum ceiling. To track earnings beyond the guarantee, WWE employs what Mahal calls the "bucket system." This system is imaginary in nature and essentially off the books. While a wrestler receives the same amount every single week, the bucket keeps track of how much money WWE is paying out versus how much the wrestler is actually earning through various avenues.

Mahal walked through the bucket system week by week using specific examples. In week one, $20,000 goes out as the weekly guarantee, and if a wrestler earns $5,000 from wrestling on Raw, their bucket balance becomes negative $15,000. However, this negative balance should not alarm wrestlers because their weekly payment remains unchanged. By week two, after another $20,000 payout and $5,000 in earnings, the balance drops to negative $30,000, but again, the weekly check stays the same.

The bucket system becomes more interesting when significant earnings occur. In Mahal's week three example, a wrestler earns $50,000 from a pay-per-view appearance and $10,000 from a main event Raw match while in a sold-out arena, bringing the bucket balance to positive $10,000. Importantly, this positive balance does not result in immediate additional payment. The weekly check remains constant, and the bucket balance is purely an accounting mechanism.

Merchandise represents another significant revenue stream that flows into the bucket system. In Mahal's example, a $100,000 merchandise check does not go directly to the wrestler. Instead, it goes into the bucket, creating a positive balance of $95,000 after the $20,000 weekly payout. This demonstrates how various income streams accumulate in the bucket without affecting the weekly payment schedule.

What happens when a wrestler gets injured or falls out of favor and stops appearing on television is also important to understand. The weekly paycheck continues unchanged, but the bucket balance begins to shift negatively since earnings are not coming in while payments continue going out. A wrestler who was previously positive in the bucket could quickly move into negative territory, and one already in negative could fall further behind.

Mahal illustrated the potential for recovery with a WrestleMania main event scenario. Six months after an injury, if a wrestler main events WrestleMania and earns $500,000, that money does not get paid directly. Instead, it goes into the bucket, where it offsets any negative balance accumulated during the inactive period. If the wrestler then wrestles regularly on Raw for the remainder of the year, earns additional merchandise payments, and has a few pay-per-view matches, the bucket could end the year in positive territory.

Using his example, by week 52 of the contract, a wrestler would have received $1 million in guaranteed downside payments while carrying a positive bucket balance of $325,000. This $325,000 functions as a bonus, bringing the total annual earnings to $1.35 million. However, Mahal noted that for the vast majority of wrestlers, he estimates over 90 percent, the bucket balance ends up negative rather than positive.

The crucial aspect of the bucket system is what happens when the balance is negative at the end of the contract year. Wrestlers do not owe WWE any money if their bucket is in the negative. The negative balance simply resets, and the contract enters year two at zero. The wrestler continues to earn their guaranteed $1 million downside in year two regardless of what happened in year one.

Mahal provided important context about how the bucket system has evolved and its implications for wrestlers. He noted that when he first signed with WWE, downside guarantees were much lower, but it was more common for wrestlers to out-earn their guarantees if they stayed healthy, remained booked, and worked a full schedule. In the modern era, guarantees are significantly higher, making it less common to out-earn the downside, though still possible.

Additionally, Mahal explained that some outside appearances and third-party deals do get added directly to a wrestler's weekly check, or WWE takes a revenue split with the talent on such arrangements. The ongoing bucket balance is shared with talent on every weekly check statement, providing transparency into the system.

The bucket system carries real consequences for wrestlers. As Mahal emphasized, WWE keeps track of the gap between what they pay wrestlers and what wrestlers earn back. This information becomes a significant factor when contracts are being renewed, when roster evaluations or cuts are being made, or when salary reduction discussions take place. A wrestler with a consistently negative bucket has less negotiating power when renewing their contract.

Conversely, wrestlers who consistently out-earn their downside guarantee place themselves in a much stronger negotiating position when their contract comes up for renewal. A positive bucket balance demonstrates that the wrestler generates substantial revenue for the company, giving them leverage to negotiate more favorable terms.

Mahal emphasized that there has always been confusion around how WWE wrestlers get paid, and he created the video for aspiring wrestlers and anyone curious about the business side of professional wrestling. His detailed breakdown makes clear that while the downside guarantee provides security and stability for wrestlers, the bucket system creates a nuanced financial relationship between wrestlers and WWE that extends well beyond the simple weekly check.

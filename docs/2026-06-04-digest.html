Mercedes Mone Makes Surprise Return to AEW Owen Hart Foundation Tournament

After an extended absence spanning several months, Mercedes Mone made her long-awaited return to AEW television during the June 3, 2026 episode of Dynamite, which took place in Richmond, Virginia. She emerged as the mystery wild card entrant in the Women's Owen Hart Foundation Tournament, replacing Willow Nightingale, who was forced to withdraw from both the tournament and vacate the TBS Championship due to a shoulder injury sustained in mid-May.

The announcement of a wild card entry in the quarterfinal round had generated significant speculation among fans about who would face Alex Windsor. When the mystery opponent was finally revealed, it was Mone making her first AEW appearance of 2026. Her return ended a lengthy absence that began after she lost the TBS Championship to Nightingale at New Year's Smash on December 31, 2025. During her hiatus, Mone remained active on the independent wrestling circuit but lost all of her remaining titles outside of AEW, including losses to Persephone, Swan, Mila Smidt, Alexxis Falcon, Diana Strong, and Jody Threat in her final indie match on March 31.

Questions about Mone's status had circulated throughout her time away from the company. However, reports from Fightful Select indicated that she remained under contract with AEW and was expected to return. Sources suggested that her comeback could have major ramifications near the top of the women's division, particularly given the injuries to both Nightingale and former AEW Women's World Champion Toni Storm, who was reportedly sidelined for the remainder of the year.

Rather than easing back into competition after such an extended layoff, Mone was thrust directly into the tournament picture with major championship implications on the line. The winner of the Owen Hart Foundation Tournament earns a guaranteed match against the AEW Women's World Champion at All In: London. This made the stakes particularly high for her return match.

Mone's history with the Owen Hart Cup added another layer of significance to her wild card entry. Having won the tournament in 2025, she now had the opportunity to accomplish a rare back-to-back tournament victory. However, she faced a stern test in Alex Windsor, who had previously defeated Mone for the RevPro Undisputed British Women's Championship in mid-December.

The match itself was competitive throughout, with both competitors trading momentum. Mone started strong, backing Windsor into the corner and delivering strikes before connecting with a dropkick. She maintained firm control in the early going, taunting Windsor by standing over her and getting in her face while keeping the action at her pace. Windsor fought back with arm drags and strikes, but Mone countered with her own offense, including a crossface and multiple Backstabbers that earned near-fall attempts.

As the match progressed, Mone landed several signature offense spots, connecting with multiple Meteoras both in and out of the ring. Before the first commercial break, she sent Windsor into the middle rope and hit a Meteora to the floor. Coming out of the break, Mone attempted a Frog Splash from the top rope but Windsor mounted a comeback, forcing Mone to retreat to the outside. Windsor capitalized with a Cannonball and a clothesline off the apron to even things up.

The match saw several back-and-forth exchanges as it entered its final stages. Windsor managed to avoid a double-knee attack in the corner and attempted a Made in Japan for a two-count. She also went for a Superplex off the top, but Mone countered into a Meteora. The former champion hit a Three Amigos suplex sequence and connected with multiple Backstabbers throughout the bout, staying one step ahead of Windsor's comebacks.

The finish came when Mone locked in her trademark Statement Maker submission hold for the second time during the match. This time, Windsor had nowhere to go and was forced to tap out. Notably, Mone refused to release the hold even after the bell had rung, adding extra emphasis to her return and sending a message to the rest of the women's division. The victory was a measure of revenge for Mone after Windsor's previous success against her on the independent circuit.

With the victory, Mone officially advanced to the semifinals of the 2026 Owen Hart Foundation Tournament. Her next opponent will be determined when Persephone faces Hazuki on the June 6 episode of AEW Collision on Saturday. On the opposite side of the tournament bracket, Athena defeated Mina Shirakawa at Double Or Nothing and will face the winner of Skye Blue versus Sareee in the other semifinal matchup.

Mone's immediate placement into the tournament and her decisive victory over Windsor made it clear that AEW had significant plans for her return. Her positioning as a top contender was reinforced by the fact that she was not easing back into competition but rather being placed directly into a major tournament with championship ramifications. The return also marked notable details such as Mone debuting a new theme song, signaling a potential fresh chapter in her AEW career.

The circumstances surrounding her return proved fortuitous for the company. The loss of both Nightingale and Toni Storm from the women's main event picture had created opportunities in the upper card, and Mone's unexpected return helped fill that void while generating significant fan excitement. Her successful debut match in the tournament suggested that her comeback could indeed have major implications at the top of the women's division as previously reported.

🔥 Mick Foley WWE vs AEW magic

Mick Foley recently shared his thoughts on the differences between AEW and WWE, pointing to what he believes is a unique sense of passion and personal investment behind the AEW product. While making it clear that he still maintains a great deal of love and respect for WWE, Foley explained that one aspect of AEW stands out to him when comparing the two organizations. According to Foley, AEW benefits from a level of personal ownership that creates a distinctly different feel for viewers. "What I feel from watching the two shows is that AEW has that little bit of magic, and WWE has kind of lost it," Foley said during the interview.

Foley attributed much of this difference to corporate influence. "I think when you're part of a corporation, and the bottom line is everything, you take away from your mid-card by making it clear that what you're selling is more important than the product in the ring," he explained. In Foley's view, WWE's corporate structure under TKO Group Holdings prioritizes business concerns over the wrestling itself, which ultimately diminishes the overall product quality.

The hardcore wrestling icon noted that there was a period when he stepped away from watching wrestling altogether. When he eventually returned as a viewer, he found himself gravitating more toward AEW than WWE. This personal shift reflected broader changes he observed in how wrestling was being presented and prioritized across the industry.

Foley also reflected on how wrestling broadcasts were presented during previous eras, suggesting that older matches may hold up better over time because the focus remained squarely on the action inside the ring. He specifically referenced the commentary work of Jim Ross and Jerry Lawler during his career. "Jim Ross and Jerry Lawler were locked in on the matches," Foley recalled. "There weren't so many things they had to go to that would take people out of that suspension of disbelief."

"That's just a theory on my part, but I think 10 to 20 years down the line, the matches that J.R. called, whether it was with 'The King' or whether it was with Jim Cornette in WCW, they'll stand the test of time, I think, better than the current matches in WWE because it seems like the matches are a means to an end instead of something to be enjoyed for themselves," Foley stated.

This observation about commentary led Foley to a broader conclusion about the differences between the two promotions. He noted that young fans who were not even born during his active wrestling career regularly approach him to discuss matches from his era. He theorized that part of the reason younger audiences are drawn to those historical matches is because of the way they were called and presented, with commentary that enhanced rather than distracted from the in-ring action.

Foley ultimately returned to what he sees as AEW's biggest strength. "So I just feel like at this point, AEW has personal ownership that shows in a way. There's a love and a feeling of magic that I don't think the corporate ownership of WWE displays nearly as well," he concluded.

As someone who experienced multiple eras of professional wrestling and enjoyed legendary runs across different promotions, Foley's perspective carries considerable weight whenever he discusses the evolution of the industry.

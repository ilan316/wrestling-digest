🔥 WrestleMania 42 card announcements and matches

WrestleMania 42 Card Takes Shape With Major Announcements

WWE has confirmed 12 matches for its biggest annual event, scheduled for April 18-19 at Allegiant Stadium in Las Vegas, Nevada. The confirmed card includes world championship bouts, multiman matches, and several storyline-driven encounters. WWE is expected to announce two additional matches, bringing the total to 14 across the two-night event.

The championship matches announced include CM Punk defending the World Heavyweight Championship against Roman Reigns on Night 2, while Cody Rhodes will defend the Undisputed WWE Championship against Randy Orton on Night 1. Additional title defenses feature Stephanie Vaquer defending the Women's World Championship against Liv Morgan, Jade Cargill defending the WWE Women's Championship against Rhea Ripley, and Sami Zayn defending the United States Championship against Trick Williams.

The Intercontinental Championship will be decided in a ladder match with Penta defending against Je'Von Evans, Dragon Lee, JD McDonagh, and Rusev. The Women's Tag Team Championship ladder match features Nia Jax and Lash Legend defending against three teams: Charlotte Flair and Alexa Bliss, the Bella Twins, and Bayley and Lyra Valkyria. AJ Lee will defend the Women's Intercontinental Championship against Becky Lynch.

Additional announced matches include Seth Rollins vs. Gunther, Finn Balor vs. Dominik Mysterio, an unsanctioned match between Jacob Fatu and Drew McIntyre, and Brock Lesnar vs. Oba Femi.

🔥 Pat McAfee WWE Return and Randy Orton storyline

Pat McAfee's surprise return to WWE television on the April 3 edition of SmackDown marked a significant shift in the build toward WrestleMania 42, though the path to his involvement proved far more complicated than the on-air narrative suggested. McAfee was revealed as the mystery voice that Randy Orton had been speaking to throughout recent weeks, immediately attacking Undisputed WWE Champion Cody Rhodes and cutting a promo in which he criticized the current state of the company, referenced declining SmackDown ratings based on Cagematch data, mentioned that WrestleMania 42 still had available tickets, and declared that he and Orton would save the wrestling business.

Behind the scenes, the decision to involve McAfee came directly from TKO Group Holdings leadership, specifically from CEO and McAfee's agent Ari Emanuel, rather than from WWE's creative department or Triple H. According to reporting from Dave Meltzer on Wrestling Observer Radio, McAfee was actually the first choice for celebrity involvement in the WrestleMania 42 main event build. However, McAfee initially turned down the offer. The company then scrambled to find alternative celebrities before eventually working out a deal with McAfee that brought him back into the fold. Sources indicated the decision was motivated by corporate synergy between WWE and ESPN, the network that airs McAfee's daily show and has the domestic premium live event contract to broadcast WrestleMania 42.

The angle generated considerable internal friction within WWE. Meltzer stated that multiple sources expressed dissatisfaction with the move, with one person telling him it was "the single most counterproductive thing the company has done in a long time." The segment was not considered a Triple H creative decision and reportedly went over the heads of the creative team. Cody Rhodes' response promo later in the same SmackDown episode, in which he told McAfee and his supporters to kiss his ass, was not originally scheduled in the internal rundown. Reports indicated that Rhodes was instructed by creative team members to speak candidly after seeing strong social media backlash, and his comments appeared to be directed at TKO's interference in storyline planning so close to WrestleMania. Some observers believed Rhodes was shooting during that segment, further fueling the backstage chaos surrounding the angle.

Despite the internal criticism, McAfee has leaned fully into his new role and aggressively defended the decision. On his podcast, he praised Randy Orton extensively, calling him a "6'5, 275 pounds of perfection from the wrestling gods" and suggesting that Orton should be featured prominently on WWE programming despite reportedly being sent home for approximately six months prior to this angle. McAfee claimed that Orton becoming a 15-time world champion at WrestleMania would be remembered by future generations as the moment that saved the entire business. He declared that anything not involving Orton is subpar and emphasized that he and Orton have things in development that fans will want to witness in Las Vegas.

McAfee also directly addressed critical fan reactions on his podcast, firing back at what he called the "IWC dipshits" for their negative response to the angle. He argued that critics did not understand what wrestling was supposed to look like and suggested they were part of the problem. He claimed that the angle was ultimately for the benefit of those fans, even though they did not realize it. McAfee stated that he was simply trying to show wrestling fans what the product should actually be when someone like Randy Orton was at the top of the card and suggested that their opinions would change once they witnessed what he and Orton had planned.

The stipulation surrounding McAfee's involvement stipulates that if Orton fails to defeat Rhodes for the Undisputed WWE Championship at WrestleMania 42, McAfee will never be seen or heard from in WWE again. Given McAfee's high profile and the corporate investment in bringing him into this storyline, most observers believe this stipulation essentially guarantees an Orton victory, as WWE would likely want to maintain the option of using McAfee for future programming and corporate synergy purposes.

Other talent within WWE have provided commentary on the decision. Matt Striker acknowledged understanding the business rationale behind the move, explaining that WWE's bottom line depends on mainstream appeal and celebrity involvement, citing various celebrities like Jelly Roll, Lil Yachty, and others who have been brought into angles. Striker specifically noted that the crossover to mainstream appeal is what WWE is ultimately chasing, even if dedicated wrestling fans have mixed reactions to such decisions.

Looking ahead, McAfee's involvement raises questions about his on-air role as a commentator on both SmackDown and Raw, given that he is now performing as a character actively involved in the narrative while simultaneously being expected to promote WrestleMania 42 through his ESPN platform and podcast. His heel persona contradicts typical promotional expectations, creating a unique dynamic in how WWE will position him for the company's biggest event of the year.

Beyond the immediate WrestleMania 42 build, there is broader speculation about McAfee's long-term media future. Netflix is reportedly emerging as a front-runner to secure McAfee's show for his next deal window as his ESPN contract continues. Ari Emanuel is involved in positioning this potential next step, and the streaming giant's expanding partnership with WWE through Raw, combined with McAfee already working as a commentator on Netflix-distributed broadcasts, creates natural synergy for such a deal. Triple H has described McAfee as the face of WWE in the eyes of many, underscoring his value as an all-around media property rather than just on-air talent.

The McAfee-Orton alliance represents a significant corporate investment in the WrestleMania 42 main event, whether or not it has received universal praise backstage. The angle has fundamentally altered the narrative heading into the company's flagship annual event, and McAfee's willingness to absorb criticism while aggressively defending his involvement suggests he intends to be a major element of the Orton versus Rhodes story through April 18 and 19 in Las Vegas.

🔥 Hulk Hogan Netflix documentary announcement

Netflix has officially announced "Hulk Hogan: Real American," a new documentary series that will premiere on April 22, 2026. The announcement came on April 6, 2026, when Netflix released the official trailer for the project, which marks a significant moment for wrestling fans as it features Hogan's final recorded interview before his passing.

Hulk Hogan, whose real name was Terry Bollea, passed away on July 24, 2025, at the age of 71 from cardiac arrest. Before his death, Netflix had been filming extensively with the wrestling legend, capturing approximately 20 hours of interview footage. The documentary will feature this final interview material, making it a poignant capstone to Hogan's storied career and life.

The four-part docuseries is described as an unfiltered look at the man behind the legend. Netflix's official synopsis states: "Before he was Hulk Hogan, he was Terry Bollea. Uncover the man behind the legend — featuring his very last interview." The documentary promises to explore Hogan's childhood, his wrestling career, and his transition into a major pop culture icon, while also examining the more controversial aspects of his life and legacy.

The trailer for "Real American" sets a reflective tone, with Hogan's voiceover stating that "in wrestling, you should be remembered for all the wars." Throughout the preview, the documentary contrasts archival footage from his prime in the 1980s with more recent imagery showing Hogan struggling physically, creating a sobering portrait of his later years. The trailer also includes home videos from earlier in his life, though his adult children Brooke and Nick are notably absent from the preview. Hogan's ex-wife Linda appears in the footage.

Hogan's legacy is presented as complex and multifaceted in the trailer. His final WWE appearances are featured, during which he was booed by audiences, reflecting the shift in public perception he experienced in his later years. Despite his iconic status as one of wrestling's biggest stars and a key figure in WWE's rise during the 1980s boom period, Hogan faced numerous controversies throughout his life that affected how he was remembered by mainstream audiences and wrestling fans alike.

The documentary includes interviews with several major figures from Hogan's life and career. Bret Hart appears briefly in the trailer, infamously telling Hogan, "you're a liar." Other interview subjects include Jimmy Hart, Kevin Nash, and Scott Hall, providing multiple perspectives on Hogan's life, career, and impact. In one dramatic moment from the trailer, Hogan himself states threateningly: "you want me to tell the truth? Okay, I know where all the bodies are buried."

The timing of the documentary's release is particularly significant for wrestling fans. "Hulk Hogan: Real American" will premiere just five days after WrestleMania 42, which takes place on April 17, 2026. Additionally, Hogan's iconic match against Andre the Giant from WrestleMania III is being inducted into the WWE Hall of Fame as the second annual "Immortal Moment" award winner. This means April will see multiple tributes to Hogan across WWE and Netflix platforms.

The documentary appears designed to present a balanced and unflinching view of Hogan's life rather than a purely celebratory one. The trailer hints at exploring controversial elements of his career and personal life, suggesting the project will address both the highs of his wrestling dominance and Hollywood success, as well as the scandals and personal conflicts that marked later chapters of his story. The intimate moments shown, including his struggles with physical decline and family estrangement, add emotional weight to the narrative.

Hogan's professional wrestling career was remarkably prolific. He made his pro wrestling debut in 1977 and first wrestled for WWE in 1979. Over his career, he held the WWE Championship six times and won the Royal Rumble match twice, in 1990 and 1991. He was inducted into the WWE Hall of Fame in 2005 by Sylvester Stallone as a singles competitor and again in 2020 as part of the legendary nWo faction alongside Scott Hall and Kevin Nash.

The documentary also reflects personal conflicts that emerged in Hogan's later years. His daughter Brooke has been public about her estrangement from her father, including his absence from her wedding and his decision to remove her from his will. Notably, Brooke did not attend WWE's tribute to her father following his death, though her brother Nick did.

Netflix Vice President of Sports Gabe Spitzer addressed the documentary in remarks to media outlets, noting that the extensive 20 hours of filmed interviews with Hogan provided substantial material for the final project. Spitzer indicated that the documentary represents part of Netflix's broader sports documentary strategy, alongside other major projects like its Mike Tyson and Deion Sanders documentaries.

The premiere of "Hulk Hogan: Real American" on April 22 represents one final opportunity for audiences to hear directly from the wrestling icon, as the documentary preserves his reflections on his own life, legacy, and the complex circumstances that defined his remarkable but controversial journey from Terry Bollea to Hulk Hogan.

🔥 Shawn Michaels NXT management and recruiting

SHAWN MICHAELS' APPROACH TO NXT MANAGEMENT AND TALENT DEVELOPMENT

Shawn Michaels, WWE's Senior Vice President of Talent Development Creative, is redefining how leadership handles wrestlers in developmental by prioritizing emotional honesty and creating safe spaces for frustrated talent to express themselves without judgment.

During an appearance on 7PM in Brooklyn with Carmelo Anthony, Michaels explained his philosophy for managing talent experiencing high emotions. He emphasized that his approach centers on trust and openness, allowing wrestlers to feel comfortable speaking candidly about their concerns and frustrations.

"Again, some of it is the experiences you have. I always try to tell them, you're not going to have a thought in your head that I haven't had at one time. So if you're comfortable with it, you can be honest with me and open," Michaels stated.

THE IMPORTANCE OF VULNERABILITY AND EMOTIONAL SAFETY

Michaels highlighted a generational shift in how wrestlers are managed. He believes his generation lacked proper mentorship regarding emotional vulnerability, and he is intentionally creating a different culture in NXT.

"One of the things our generation didn't have was the old-timers saying that it's okay to be scared, to cry and be emotional, intimidated—it's okay to say, 'I don't know.' To know that everything is okay—you're not coming to be judged, certainly by me, because I don't have that right and I have made every mistake," he explained.

Rather than dismissing passionate or angry wrestlers, Michaels validates their emotions while channeling them productively. He understands that frustration and drive often come from the same place and can be redirected toward improvement.

"So that's something that I do use, as best I can, to help them and guide them. I understand the people that are passionate, or when they get angry or they've got a chip on their shoulder—they're all things that I can relate to, and I can at least validate the way they're feeling and then do my best to try and help guide them in the midst of those emotions."

NAVIGATING COMMUNICATION WITH UPPER MANAGEMENT

Michaels also coaches talent on how to express their frustrations appropriately. He encourages wrestlers to speak freely in safe spaces with him while also teaching them how to communicate concerns to higher management in strategic ways.

"And try to get them to, 'It's okay to say to me right now, we're in a safe space—go ahead and lay it out on me.' But if you want to go to upper management, I would suggest maybe saying it this way," Michaels noted.

Demonstrating self-awareness, Michaels acknowledged that his approach can be criticized and remains open to that criticism. He told talent that calling him a hypocrite is fair if they felt that way, recognizing the bigger picture of professional wrestling extends beyond individual relationships.

KURT ANGLE AS THE RECRUITING STANDARD

When it comes to talent recruitment for NXT, Michaels named Kurt Angle as the measuring stick for evaluating prospects. Angle's seamless transition from amateur wrestling to professional wrestling represents the gold standard Michaels seeks in recruits.

"Kurt, easily the best transition from what he did, to our stuff. Did it seamlessly, what a stud. He absolutely is the guy. He is the standard, certainly from an NXT standpoint when we're recruiting now, using Kurt as the guy who's the best to have done it in such a seamless, quick manner."

Michaels praised Angle's natural ability to master professional wrestling's nuances while maintaining authenticity and versatility in character work.

"He was just a natural at it, Kurt was. He was the real deal, and could just come in and do our stuff, and wear a little cowboy hat and be funny, then go out there and stretch somebody like nobody's business."

WWE currently recruits through multiple pathways including the WWE ID program for independent wrestlers and the NIL program targeting college athletes. The diverse backgrounds of current talent have proven beneficial for on-screen product quality.

MICHAELS' OWN CAREER PERSPECTIVE

Beyond his management philosophy, Michaels reflected on his own wrestling career when asked about his best era. While acknowledging his 1990s work as professionally impressive, he stated the 2000s were superior for him personally and artistically.

"I'll lean towards the 2000s. I know the 90s, from a professional standpoint, I would arguably say that I felt like I was better than a lot of people in the game then, but me personally, I was not in a great place."

During the 2000s, Michaels' wrestling improved as he gained maturity, understanding momentum and psychological storytelling at a deeper level. More importantly, he could actually enjoy his success.

"But in the 2000s, my work was so much better. As you become a more mature player, you understand momentum, picking your spots, being smarter with the game. I was able to do that in the 2000s, and also be able to enjoy it."

Michaels attributed much of his 1990s struggles to self-imposed pressure and defensive attitudes, believing everyone was competing against him. By contrast, his 2000s tenure coincided with personal stability including family and children.

"That's the thing that I didn't do early in the 90s, allow myself to enjoy it, because I felt so much pressure. Majority of it I put on myself and felt like everybody was sort of coming after me, and you're very defensive. The 2000s, I would easily say it was, certainly for me personally and professionally, a much better time, because I was in a much better place, with a family and children and all of that, it's hard to beat that time for me."

Former WWE Champion explains WWE contracts

Former WWE Champion Jinder Mahal, now competing under his real name Raj Dhesi, recently offered fans a detailed look at how WWE talent contracts are structured and how wrestlers can earn beyond their guaranteed salaries. In a video posted to Instagram, Dhesi used a hypothetical contract to explain the system WWE has traditionally used to compensate performers.

Using an example of a three-year contract worth 1 million dollars per year as a downside guarantee, Dhesi noted that WWE talent are paid on a weekly basis. He explained that to calculate weekly payments, wrestlers divide their annual guarantee by 52 weeks. With a 1 million dollar annual guarantee, that equates to approximately 19,230 dollars per week, which he rounded to 20,000 dollars for his example.

According to Dhesi, the downside guarantee functions as a minimum salary, meaning the performer receives that weekly amount regardless of circumstances. "Now with a downside guarantee, that 20,000 dollar check will come in regardless if you are injured or if you wrestle 7 days a week. Same amount every single week."

Dhesi then addressed a common question among fans: whether WWE wrestlers can earn more than their guaranteed contracts. He confirmed that additional earnings are absolutely possible. "A downside guarantee is only a minimum. There is no maximum. There is no set maximum."

To explain how additional earnings are calculated, Dhesi described what he referred to as WWE's "bucket system." According to Dhesi, wrestlers continue receiving the same weekly pay, while the bucket tracks earnings generated through various revenue streams. "Television tapings, live events, pay-per-views, merchandise, everything goes into this bucket."

Dhesi explained that the bucket system is unofficial in nature and operates off the books. Wrestlers may initially operate at a negative balance in the bucket before eventually becoming profitable through major appearances and merchandise sales. Using a series of examples, he illustrated how different events and merchandise sales would contribute to the bucket throughout a contract year.

Importantly, he stressed that negative balances do not mean talent owe WWE money. "Again, don't be alarmed by the negative amount. You do not owe WWE this money." He later illustrated how a major event such as WrestleMania could dramatically alter a wrestler's standing. "So week 30, 6 months later, you come back and you main event WrestleMania. You earn 500,000 dollars, except you do not get paid 500,000 dollars. It goes into the bucket. Whatever you were negative by, this will offset it."

If a wrestler finishes the contract year with a positive balance in the bucket, that amount is paid out as additional compensation above the downside guarantee. Using his example, if a wrestler was positive 325,000 dollars in the bucket by week 52, they would receive their full 1 million dollar downside guarantee plus the 325,000 dollar bonus, totaling 1.35 million dollars for that year.

Dhesi noted that most wrestlers never exceed their downside guarantee. "Now, for most wrestlers, I would say over 90 percent of wrestlers, this number is going to be in the negative." However, he emphasized that negative balances are wiped clean when a new contract year begins. "And if it's in the negative, does not matter, because after week 52, we go back to week 1, and your balance starts at zero. You do not owe WWE any money."

Dhesi concluded by noting that the process repeats for each year of the contract, with the performer continuing to receive their guaranteed salary while having opportunities to earn additional money through strong performance, merchandise sales, and high-profile appearances. The system essentially allows top performers with strong merchandise sales and frequent premium live event appearances to significantly exceed their downside guarantee, while the majority of wrestlers receive their guaranteed minimum regardless of their bucket balance.

Matt Hardy Praises Tommy Dreamer's Work in TNA Creative, Says Criticism Was Unfair

Matt Hardy has come to Tommy Dreamer's defense regarding the criticism the veteran wrestler received for his work in TNA's creative department. Speaking on The Extreme Life of Matt Hardy, Hardy expressed that Dreamer's reputation during his time helping lead the company's creative direction has been unfairly judged.

"I think so. I think it has been a little unfair. No doubt. I mean, I had said that earlier today. I said, you know, him and Delirious obviously quite overworked. You know, I mean, they're very busy because there's only two guys doing that," Hardy explained during the podcast.

Hardy gave credit to Dreamer for the significant behind-the-scenes work he contributed to TNA during his tenure. "I think he has done a lot of good for TNA and I'm very curious to hear the motivation behind all this, why he is stepping away," Hardy said, acknowledging Dreamer's recent departure from the creative role.

The veteran wrestler also reflected on his working relationship with Dreamer dating back to their time together on House of Hardcore. "I certainly enjoyed working with Tommy. I enjoyed working with Tommy all the way back to the House of Hardcore days whenever he was doing that show. I always thought he had a good pulse on kind of where pro wrestling is and where it's going, and he stayed pretty current with things," Hardy noted.

Hardy outlined what he perceived as Dreamer's overall philosophy when it came to booking wrestling programming. "I think he tried to parallel or mirror as much as he could of kind of the ECW mindset about, 'This is what the fans think of these people, so let's give them that as much as we can,' or let's make the most out of whoever we can by highlighting their strengths and trying to hide their weaknesses," Hardy explained.

He drew a comparison to Paul Heyman's legendary work in ECW as an influence on Dreamer's creative approach. "Paul Heyman and ECW was a masterclass on taking guys that weren't extremely talented and highlighting their strengths and getting them over. Paul positioned people where they could succeed because he highlighted their strengths and protected their weaknesses as much as possible," Hardy said.

Hardy concluded by emphasizing his personal respect and appreciation for Dreamer's contributions to professional wrestling. "I got lots of love for Tommy, man. No doubt about it. I loved working with Tommy. Really enjoyable working with Tommy," Hardy said.

Taya Valkyrie reacts to Perros del Mal reforming

Perros del Mal has been reformed in AAA with a new roster taking on the legendary stable's mantle. The group made their debut on Saturday's live edition of AAA on Fox, featuring members Karmen Petrovic, Daga, Angel Garza, Berto, and Bronco Nima. The segment concluded the show when the newly formed stable attacked El Grande Americano before unveiling the classic Perros del Mal t-shirts that have become iconic in lucha libre history.

Taya Valkyrie, who was a prominent member of the original incarnation, took to social media to share her perspective on the reformation. She posted: "For those that don't know the history, it goes deep. What we made, what we created, the cheers, the boos, the impact that Perros del Mal had on lucha libre history is undeniable. @SoyHijoDelPerro created a movement and I wouldn't be where I am without him. Learn about the history and enjoy it. Because I will always treasure @PerrosdelMalMx. It was magic."

Valkyrie's connection to Perros del Mal spans over a decade. She joined the stable in 2012 and achieved significant success during her tenure, winning the AAA Reina de Reinas Championship in 2014. Her reign with the title lasted 945 days, which stood as a record at that time, underscoring the group's ability to elevate its members to championship prominence.

The historical significance of Perros del Mal cannot be overstated in the context of lucha libre. During the group's peak years under founder Perro Aguayo Jr., the stable achieved a level of popularity that few factions have ever reached. The merchandise associated with the group became ubiquitous throughout Mexico, drawing comparisons to some of the biggest merchandise movements in professional wrestling history. Arena Mexico experienced extraordinary attendance figures whenever Perro Aguayo Jr. appeared on programming, consistently drawing 10,000 fans on Friday nights and regularly hitting 15,000 or above when he faced top opponents like Santo or Mistico. For a singular venue that ran multiple times per week, this attendance run represents one of the greatest in professional wrestling history.

Perro Aguayo Jr.'s tragic death in 2015 marked a significant moment in lucha libre, and his legacy continues to influence the promotion years later. The decision to revive Perros del Mal in 2026 represents an acknowledgment of the stable's enduring importance to AAA and Mexican wrestling culture. With Valkyrie's endorsement and emotional connection to the group's original mission, the new incarnation enters with the blessing of someone who was central to its greatest success.

Eric Bischoff says Road Dogg would be huge leap forward for TNA

Eric Bischoff believes Road Dogg could thrive in TNA Wrestling, arguing that the WWE Hall of Famer would bring valuable experience and instincts to the promotion's creative team. Speaking on 83 Weeks, Bischoff admitted he has never worked directly with Road Dogg but still spoke highly of what he believes he could contribute.

"I've never worked with him, so I absolutely have no idea. Certainly know of him. So my opinion doesn't really mean much," Bischoff said, before going on to explain his assessment of the veteran's situation in WWE.

Bischoff suggested that Road Dogg's struggles in WWE may have been more about fit than ability. "I think clearly out of place in the TKO version of WWE. Clearly out of place. Square peg, round hole. Just never going to work," he explained. However, he emphasized that the issue wasn't talent, but compatibility. "It's not who's right and who's wrong. It's just the chemistry just doesn't work."

Bischoff also described Road Dogg as someone whose approach to wrestling was developed during a much different era of the business. "I think Road Dogg is old school. His perspective, his sensibilities, his creative instinct were all developed and learned through trial and error during a period of time that was so different than the period of time that we're currently operating in."

Drawing from his own experience returning to WWE, Bischoff said adapting to the modern corporate structure can be difficult. "I understand the adaptation challenge and the cultural adaptation part of it. It has nothing to do with Road Dogg's abilities, his skill, his instinct, his relationship with talent, his ability to motivate and direct talent to become better than they might be without a guy like Road Dogg." He added that he has no doubts about Road Dogg's capabilities. "I 100% believe he has all those qualities."

Bischoff then turned his attention to TNA, saying Road Dogg could represent a significant upgrade in the company's creative process. "In a place like TNA, quite frankly, I think he'd be a huge leap forward from a Tommy Dreamer who doesn't really have any television experience, and what experience he has is extremely limited and not really what I would consider television experience as far as creative wrestling."

He concluded by encouraging any promotion interested in Road Dogg to pursue him. "I think someone like Road Dogg is extremely valuable to TNA, to Tony Khan or to whoever else would be interested in talking to a guy like Road Dogg. I would encourage it. I hope he gets that opportunity because I think he's got a lot left in the tank and somebody would benefit greatly from tapping into it."

Martha Hart receives award for Owen Hart Foundation work

Dr. Martha Hart has been named the 2026 Calgarian of the Year in recognition of her outstanding work with the Owen Hart Foundation. The honor was presented at the 2026 Calgary Awards ceremony held Wednesday, June 17 at City Hall in downtown Calgary.

According to the City of Calgary's official press release, "Dr. Martha Hart, PhD, is a Cambridge-trained researcher, philanthropist, and advocate for education, housing stability, and food security. After her husband Owen Hart's death in 1999, she founded the Owen Hart Foundation. Its home ownership and scholarship programs help low-income Calgarians buy homes and pursue post-secondary education."

Hart was one of 13 honorees at this year's ceremony and received the award for her extraordinary contributions to the community. The Calgarian of the Year award is among the highest and most respected prestigious individual civic honors presented to an exceptional citizen who improves the quality of life for others.

The Owen Hart Foundation was established following Owen Hart's death at the WWF Over the Edge pay-per-view on May 23, 1999. Since its inception, the foundation has focused on three main areas of impact. The first is a scholarship program designed to help low-income high school students pursue post-secondary education. The second is a home ownership program designed to assist new homeowners in achieving financial stability. The third is a partnership program aimed at fostering relationships with charities such as the Alberta Children's Hospital and the Calgary Zoo, expanding the foundation's community reach and impact.

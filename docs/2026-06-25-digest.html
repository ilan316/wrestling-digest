🔥 AEW TBS Championship Survival of the Fittest Match

AEW is creating historic wrestling history with the announcement of the first-ever Survival of the Fittest match, designed to crown a new TBS Champion following the injury-forced vacancy created by Willow Nightingale's shoulder injury. The match takes place next Wednesday, July 1, in San Diego, California, on AEW Dynamite.

On the June 24 episode of AEW Dynamite, the company held two qualifying matches to determine additional participants for the landmark bout. In a major upset, Harley Cameron defeated Marina Shafir in the show's first hour. Cameron entered the match as a heavy underdog, but managed to overcome Shafir's sustained offense throughout the encounter. The decisive finish came when Shafir attempted to lock in her signature Mother's Milk submission hold. Cameron reversed the submission attempt into a backslide pin and scored the three-count victory, earning her first AEW singles win since March 2025. The victory was particularly impressive given Shafir's dominance through most of the match, where she controlled Cameron with knees to the midsection and near-submission holds. Cameron managed to show her resilience, hitting a belly-to-back suplex earlier in the contest and surviving Shafir's sleeper hold with a reversal of her own before ultimately catching Shafir in the backslide.

Later in the same Dynamite broadcast, Queen Aminata earned her spot in the Survival of the Fittest match by defeating ROH Women's Television Champion Red Velvet. The match between Aminata and Velvet was action-packed and competitive throughout, with both competitors trading momentum. Early in the bout, Aminata displayed impressive strength by taking Velvet down with a suplex. Velvet nearly caught Aminata off guard with a roll-up pin after helping her up, showing some good sportsmanship. The match continued with Velvet slamming Aminata to the mat and connecting with a scissor kick to her back, but Aminata refused to stay down and continued fighting. Both women collided on the ropes, and Velvet executed a powerful slam. Later, Velvet dodged an attempted DDT from Aminata, resulting in both competitors being taken down. Aminata regained the momentum and hit a sliding boot in the corner followed by a double stomp. Velvet answered back with a hurricarana that knocked Aminata off the ropes, and then connected with a frog splash that looked like it could finish the match. The finishing sequence saw the two women engage in a strike battle before Aminata scored the decisive victory with a big kick to Velvet on the bottom rope. This marked Aminata's first AEW singles win since June 2025, showing her momentum heading into the title match.

Cameron drew particular attention for her ring gear choice, arriving at the ring in Supergirl-inspired attire. The creative choice seemed to energize the competitor as she took the fight to Shafir throughout their encounter. While the supergirl-themed outfit generated discussion, it was Cameron's technical wrestling ability and resilience that secured her advancement, not high-flying aerial maneuvers.

With the victories of Cameron and Aminata, the Survival of the Fittest field now includes five confirmed competitors: Hikaru Shida, former AEW Women's World Champion; Kris Statlander, also a former AEW Women's World Champion; CMLL Women's World Champion Persephone; Harley Cameron; and Queen Aminata. The qualifying matches made clear that AEW is stacking an impressive roster for the historic tournament to determine Willow Nightingale's successor as TBS Champion. As of the current reporting, only one final spot remains to be filled in the match. AEW has stated that additional participants and segments will be announced as the build toward the July 1 Dynamite continues, keeping fans in suspense about who will claim the final qualifying spot and round out the field for this inaugural championship-determining match.

WWE Title Changes History

June 24th stands out as one of the busiest days in professional wrestling history when it comes to championship title changes. The date has hosted major shows including WWF King of the Ring 2001, WWE Vengeance 2007 – Night of Champions, ROH Best in the World 2012 and 2016, plus numerous episodes of RAW, WCW Nitro, AEW Dynamite, Collision, Rampage and SmackDown. Over more than five decades, this single calendar date has witnessed a remarkable number of title transfers across virtually every major wrestling promotion.

The earliest recorded title change on June 24th dates back to 1972, when Abdullah the Butcher won the NWF World Heavyweight Title. The following year, Johnny Powers captured the NWF North American Heavyweight Title. El Halcón's 1977 Mexican National Heavyweight Title victory and Wahoo McDaniel's 1984 NWA United States Heavyweight Title (Mid-Atlantic Version) win followed, establishing the date's significance in wrestling history. Cien Caras won the NWA World Light Heavyweight Title in 1987, continuing the pattern of major championship changes.

1988 proved to be an exceptionally busy year for title changes on June 24th, with three championships switching hands. Shiro Koshinaka won the IWGP Junior Heavyweight Title, Fabuloso Blondy captured the NWA World Light Heavyweight Title, and Tatsumi Fujinami won the IWGP Heavyweight Title. This cluster of changes demonstrated the date's importance across multiple wrestling territories and promotions.

The 1990s and 2000s continued the trend with significant title changes. Booker T and Stevie Ray won the WCW World Tag Team Titles in 1996. Yoshinobu Kanemaru's 2001 GHC Junior Heavyweight Title victory marked the beginning of a new wave of changes. In 2007, two major WWE titles changed hands when John Morrison won the ECW World Title and Candice Michelle won the WWE Women's Title. Taylor Wilde's 2008 TNA Women's Knockout Title triumph added to the growing list.

The 2010s saw continued championship activity on this date. Kenny King and Rhett Titus won the ROH World Tag Team Titles in 2012, while Tigre Uno captured the TNA X-Division Title in 2015. These changes reflected the ongoing relevance of June 24th as a date for major wrestling broadcasts and events.

2019 produced one of the most unusual days in wrestling history when five title changes occurred, all involving the WWE 24/7 Championship. The 24/7 rule allowed for rapid-fire title changes, with Heath Slater, EC3, R-Truth, Cedric Alexander, and R-Truth again each holding the title on the same date. This sequence demonstrated how the 24/7 championship's unique stipulation could create multiple changes within a single day.

More recent years have continued the tradition. In 2022, Magia Blanca won the Mexican National Welterweight Title. Three titles changed hands in 2024: MUSASHI and Seiki Yoshioka won the All Asia Tag Team Titles, Fuminori Abe won the GAORA Television Title, and Finn Bálor and JD McDonagh won the WWE World Tag Team Titles. The most recent entry is from 2025, when Channing Lorenzo won the NXT Heritage Cup, proving that June 24th remains an active date for championship changes in professional wrestling.

Wrestling is great, but no longer makes for great TV

There is always competition in wrestling as territories were built on both that and local TV. The WWF/E Attitude Era was driven by two billionaires wanting nothing more than to beat the other. Even today, AEW and WWE go head-to-head on what seems like a bi-monthly basis. In today's age, however, wrestling has a daunting new foe to compete against: everything else.

We live in a time where every TV show, movie, game, comic or book ever made is available at our fingertips 24/7 in seconds. The instantness of our entertainment is overwhelming, but also makes attention spans shorter and the call for quality that much louder. This is especially true when it comes to TV.

I've watched wrestling in one form or another for almost my entire life. I've also watched TV for almost my entire life and have seen both grow and change over the years. I've also worked in TV development and something has been becoming very noticeable: simply put, wrestling isn't great TV anymore.

TV has evolved, but wrestling hasn't evolved with it. TV has been at its peak for several years. Studios, streamers and networks are pouring money into creating high-quality shows and entertainment that people clamor for, discuss and binge in hopes they become moments in popular culture. Wrestling, by nature of its weekly production, can't really do that.

When TV wrestling numbers were in the millions on a weekly basis, it was because people were tuning in to see what happened and what might happen next. Now, that's just not how we watch and consume TV. The days of being home to watch something at a specific time are long over and wrestling doesn't really have an answer for that.

Sure, with Raw on Netflix, it's easy to watch after the fact but by that time, it's been spoiled for you and unless you are already a superfan or something huge happens, people aren't going to go back and check it out just for the sake of it. It'd be like watching Monday Night Football on a Thursday after reading about it for two days.

TV shows today are also produced with cinema-level stories, effects and talent. Every new season of Stranger Things was treated like a pop culture event that you just had to be part of. That show had the benefit of being able to put out 8-10 episodes and call it a year. Wrestling needs new content every week. I don't dispute that it's hard to maintain quality in that situation, but the difference between the two has never been more glaring because in wrestling, I'm not getting a full story, I'm not seeing characters develop, and I'm not really surprised by any twists or really shocking moments.

That's on you, Creative. Yes, this is all a criticism of the way wrestling creative is currently presented. It could be due to corporate mandates, injuries derailing plans, or just an attempt to appeal to the widest possible audience, but WWE is flat as a pancake, AEW is overstuffed, and TNA or NXT seem like they are trying to recapture 90s stories without doing the 90s homework. This does not make for great TV.

This was all very noticeable when Tony Khan was trying to get a TV deal for Ring of Honor. I was covering ROH at the time and not at all surprised that it didn't happen because no one outside of diehard wrestling fans would watch it. There's some really great wrestling in ROH so if you're a fan of the art form, it's worth watching. But to the casual fan that would be who they are targeting as an audience, there's nothing. No stories, no outstanding characters, no hooks or reasons to tune in every week on a TV watching basis.

Studios and TV executives aren't willing to take a chance on anything they think might develop over time, so with ROH, they would never spend the millions to put it on the air just because.

The piece concludes by noting that wrestling is at its best when treated as a real sporting event, though the full recommendations for how wrestling can make this work are not included in the available excerpt.

Heyman emphasized that Cesaro had all the necessary qualities to become a top-tier performer. "You look at Cesaro and he checked, especially in that moment in time, he checked every box to become a top star," Heyman said. "Everyone who got in the ring with him came back to Gorilla and said 'give me him.'"

Heyman pointed to John Cena's experience working with Cesaro as a prime example of the wrestler's ability to work main event level matches. According to Heyman, after working with Cesaro on television, Cena returned to Vince McMahon and expressed that he could main event WrestleMania with Cesaro, and probably should have.

However, Heyman explained that Cesaro's positioning in the company was primarily utilitarian. The wrestler was placed in that position the day after Brock Lesnar conquered the Undertaker's streak so that when Lesnar took a hiatus, Heyman would have an excuse to be on television. "I had an excuse to be on TV to say, 'My client Brock Lesnar defeated conquered The Undertaker's undefeated streak at WrestleMania,'" Heyman explained. Heyman was candid about the magnitude of the mistake. "When you realize what he's capable of, what an opponent he could have been for Brock Lesnar.

🔥 MJF interview and comments

During a recent appearance on The Ariel Helwani Show, AEW star MJF opened up about several significant topics affecting his career and the wrestling industry. The interview came ahead of his main event appearance at AEW Double or Nothing on May 24, 2026, where he was set to defend his AEW World Championship against Darby Allin in a Hair vs. Title match.

When discussing the current state of AEW's locker room, MJF pointed to a significant culture shift that has improved the company's atmosphere. He credited this improvement to the departure of several talents who viewed AEW as merely a stepping stone back to WWE. According to MJF, the biggest offender in this category was Aleister Black, formerly known as Malakai Black in AEW. While MJF clarified he doesn't personally dislike Black, he stated that Black was primarily motivated by wanting his WrestleMania moment in WWE. MJF explained that Black and similar-minded wrestlers have since left the company, with some discovering that "the grass isn't greener" after their WWE departure. However, MJF acknowledged that some wrestlers did find more success in WWE than they had in AEW.

As a team captain and someone who has been with AEW since day one, MJF made clear his position on such talent departures. He stated that many wrestlers who left AEW with the intention of using it as a stepping stone to WWE ended up requesting significant pay cuts when they returned to the WWE fold, or were ultimately released by the company. MJF emphasized that wrestlers who work hard and show genuine commitment to AEW have no need to burn bridges. He reiterated that his criticism came from feedback he received from a majority of the locker room about Black specifically. MJF made his stance abundantly clear: he doesn't want to deal with wrestlers who aren't team players, though he expressed he would be happy to be proven wrong if anyone were to return and demonstrate genuine commitment to AEW.

Beyond the Aleister Black situation, MJF discussed the overall improvement of AEW's programming and atmosphere. He argued that AEW currently has "bar none the best wrestling on television" and stated this as fact rather than mere opinion. While acknowledging that WWE, TNA, and NXT all feature incredible wrestlers and matches, MJF maintained that AEW's product is superior. He noted that AEW faced criticism in the past, but that criticism was justified by legitimate issues occurring behind the scenes at that time. However, in 2026, he argued there is nothing substantive to criticize about AEW's product. The company has seen increased house show attendance year-over-year in most U.S. territories, elevated pay-per-view buy rates, and MJF himself was headlining what would become the second-biggest grossing domestic pay-per-view in company history.

MJF discussed how AEW has successfully positioned itself as an alternative to WWE by blending young, hungry talent with established wrestling legends. He emphasized that younger wrestlers in AEW are presented as top-tier competitors rather than as projects, which contributes significantly to the company's distinct identity. Additionally, MJF credited Tony Khan's evolution as a leader and his genuine passion for professional wrestling. Despite not claiming to personally love Khan, MJF acknowledged Khan's growth as a boss and his significant contributions to wrestling. He pointed out that Khan, as a billionaire, could dedicate himself entirely to his other sports ventures but instead chose to invest in professional wrestling out of authentic passion for the industry. Khan has developed into one of the best minds in wrestling, according to MJF.

When asked about his own future, MJF discussed his deliberate decision to remain committed to AEW rather than pursue WWE opportunities. He referenced a hypothetical WWE offer that would have been substantial financially, but explained that his priority is building his legacy and going down as the greatest of all time. MJF finds something particularly special about potentially achieving GOAT status without ever having worked for WWE. He views this as akin to the Mount Rushmore of professional wrestlers-turned-actors, which he lists as Dave Bautista, John Cena, and Dwayne "The Rock" Johnson. His aspiration is to become the fourth member of that Mount Rushmore based solely on his own name and legacy, what he calls "the Maxwell machine," rather than relying on any company's machine. He added the standard wrestling caveat that "never say never," but emphasized his current mindset at age 30 is firmly focused on AEW.

MJF also addressed his complicated relationship with former AEW rival CM Punk, who currently works for WWE. When asked if he was surprised by Punk's success in WWE, MJF made clear that while Punk is succeeding professionally, MJF still has significant personal disdain for him. He called Punk a "fucking cunt" and "a bad guy," but immediately balanced this by acknowledging Punk as "an incredible professional wrestler." Throughout the interview, MJF repeated this sentiment, stating that while Punk "sucks" as a person and is "a piece of shit," he is simultaneously "a great wrestler." Their feud during Punk's initial AEW tenure was considered among the company's best programming, culminating in a violent Dog Collar Match. The rivalry never reached continuation after Punk's departure from AEW, making the subject a lingering element of wrestling history.

On the topic of wrestling legends, MJF also offered his assessment of Bret Hart. While not claiming to have any beef with Hart, MJF referenced his opening promo at the very first AEW Double or Nothing event. He acknowledged Hart as one of the greatest wrestlers of all time but ranked him below Shawn Michaels in the all-time hierarchy. MJF attributed some of Hart's recent behavior and statements to the lingering effects of his injury from his match against Goldberg, suggesting Hart is "punch drunk." He explained his reasoning for placing Michaels above Hart, pointing to Michaels' superior mic skills, athleticism in the ring, wrestling ability, and ability to draw audiences. MJF argued that while Hart was excellent at his particular time period due to his work-rate abilities, Michaels was the more complete performer and more exciting presence.

Beyond wrestling-specific topics, MJF engaged in a serious discussion about antisemitism and the Israel-Gaza conflict. When the conversation turned to antisemitic comments online, MJF spoke candidly about his personal experiences. He expressed that he is not in support of the current situation in Israel and stated he is not a fan of Benjamin Netanyahu, describing much of what is happening as "super inhumane." However, despite making these critical statements about Israeli government actions, MJF reported that he still receives antisemitic abuse online from people who attack him specifically because he is Jewish. This phenomenon frustrated MJF, who pointed out that even when expressing criticism of Netanyahu and Israeli government policies, he still encounters hateful messages. MJF questioned the logic of holding Jewish Americans responsible for the decisions of the Israeli government, emphasizing that he was born American and has no connection to Netanyahu or the Israeli government.

MJF elaborated on this point by stating clearly that he is American and not part of the Israeli government, nor does he have any relationship with Netanyahu or influence over his decisions. He found it particularly strange and illogical that Jewish people worldwide are often blamed collectively for Israeli government actions. MJF also shared that antisemitism is not a new experience in his career but something he has dealt with for most of his time in professional wrestling, long before the Gaza conflict became a major global issue. He referenced painful experiences from his younger years that shaped him mentally. Throughout this portion of the interview, MJF emphasized the importance of separating legitimate criticism of government actions from attacks on Jewish identity and Jewish individuals.

Regarding AEW's current momentum and position in the wrestling landscape, MJF painted a picture of a company in its strongest position. He credited the weeding out of wrestlers who weren't committed to AEW's long-term vision, the quality of wrestling being produced, and the improved leadership and direction from Tony Khan. He noted that while wrestling fans have some frustration with WWE and TKO, AEW is positioned to capture that audience. He used the analogy of someone willing to try an alternative product, comparing it to someone switching from Diet Coke to Pepsi. The key difference, according to MJF, is that AEW is now producing consistently excellent wrestling shows that provide genuine value to fans seeking an alternative to WWE's offerings.

🔥 Chris Jericho AEW character evolution

Chris Jericho recently appeared on Ringer Wrestling's The Masked Man Show to discuss his character evolution in AEW and the reasoning behind his decision to drop "Chris" from his ring name. When asked about his most important eras, Jericho explained that he always credits his current work because if he didn't, it would suggest he wasn't fully invested in what he's doing now.

The name change to simply "Jericho" was intentional and strategic. By not using his first name as frequently, Jericho created intrigue online that prompted fans to question what he was doing and why he made the change. He drew inspiration from self-titled albums released by legendary artists, explaining that this stripped-down approach allows him to leverage the equity built into his name over decades of professional wrestling. Jericho compared his branding strategy to Metallica's self-titled album, commonly known as "The Black Album," and The Beatles' "The White Album." According to Jericho, these albums didn't need additional titles because everyone already knew who the artists were, and the name alone conjured individual memories and associations for each listener. He applied this same philosophy to his wrestling persona, reasoning that everyone knows who Jericho is, and when they hear that name, they can recall whatever memories they have on their own.

Jericho expressed satisfaction with his current direction, stating that he likes what he's been doing and is going with the flow to see what works and what doesn't. He emphasized that the decision wasn't about creating something entirely new but rather stripping away elements to focus on the essence of his character.

Beyond discussing his current work, Jericho reflected on various underrated or overlooked eras throughout his career. He spoke fondly of "The Wizard" character, which involved throwing fireballs during matches. He noted that this was something that hadn't been seen in professional wrestling for years, and he took the commitment seriously. When performing this character in Las Vegas, Jericho had to obtain a real magician's license to legally throw fireballs, as required by local commission rules. He proudly noted that he still renews this license every year and carries it with him at all times, making him an officially licensed magician in Nevada.

Another era Jericho highlighted was his early work as a crybaby character, marked by tantrums and chair-slamming segments against the ringpost. He recalled ripping off Dave Penzer's tuxedo during this period, which helped establish the character archetype of asking "Who is this guy?" He referenced his "Monday Night Jericho" era as a particularly good time in his career that fans still bring up fondly.

Jericho also defended his "Learning Tree" persona from 2024 and 2025, which served as a stable or faction in AEW. He described this era as "really going left field" and focusing on the haters of the world, which paradoxically made them hate it even more. Rather than viewing this as a failure, Jericho compared the vibe to controversial or polarizing albums like The Rolling Stones' "Dirty Work," Kiss' "The Elder," and Metallica's "St. Anger." Despite these projects being divisive among fans and critics, Jericho considered "The Learning Tree" era to be genuinely cool and worth exploring.

The legendary wrestler was emphatic about his professional commitment across all character iterations. He stated that whether audiences liked his work or not, he had given one thousand percent effort for every single version of Chris Jericho he has portrayed. Jericho denied ever having a character that he thought wouldn't work and subsequently deprioritized. Instead, he emphasized that everything he has done has received a million percent of his effort, intent, passion, and focus. While acknowledging that some iterations have worked better than others, he stressed that the effort level and commitment have remained consistent throughout his career.

This interview reflects Jericho's philosophy of constant evolution and reinvention while maintaining dedication to his craft, regardless of how each chapter of his career is received by the wrestling audience.

The Evolution of WWE: From Kayfabe-Breaking Moments to Modern Legacy Building

Wrestling has produced countless memorable moments, matches and shocking incidents that have shaped the industry. Thirty years ago, on May 19, 1996, one particular moment would become a watershed event that arguably helped pave the way for the WWE Attitude Era and the eventual rise of Stone Cold Steve Austin as we know him today.

The Background and The Kliq

In 1994, real life friends and WWE wrestlers Kevin Nash, Scott Hall, Shawn Michaels, Triple H and Sean Waltman formed The Kliq. It wasn't an official on-screen stable like DX but existed backstage among the friends. The group became highly influential backstage due to close friendships and their growing star power in WWE. They traveled together for tours and other wrestling events, influencing creative decisions and developed a reputation for holding significant power backstage.

What Happened on May 19, 1996

On that fateful date, Hall and Nash competed in their final WWE matches before becoming free agents and eventually signing with WCW. At an event in Madison Square Garden, Hall faced Triple H while Michaels defended the WWF Championship in a steel cage match against Nash. The shocker came after the main event when Hall entered the ring and embraced Michaels. Later, Nash and Triple H joined in the group hug, bidding farewell to the two who were departing the company.

The incident pre-dated the social media era when it was difficult for videos and photos from wrestling events to quickly spread. However, a couple of fans had snuck in their camcorders into the venue and captured the incident on tape, causing it to spread like wildfire. The crowd gasped and were confused at what unfolded. It was an unusual sight because they were breaking a sacred WWE rule by breaking kayfabe in public. Vince McMahon was particular about feuding wrestlers being spotted together at outings so as to maintain their rivalries and good and bad guy personas they had going. The curtain call incident shattered the illusion of wrestling which undermined the product's presentation.

The Fallout and an Unexpected Consequence

Nash and Hall escaped any form of punishment since they were done with WWE. Michaels was the WWF Champion and a top star in the company. So the only person who took the fall was Triple H. The irony is that without the curtain call incident of 1996, there would not be a Stone Cold Steve Austin or the birth of the Attitude Era.

Triple H became the scapegoat since he lacked championship status and did not have an expiring contract. On June 23 that year, WWE held their annual King of the Ring event. Triple H's growing stature was leading to him winning the tournament and skyrocketing his career. He did not get a suspension or indefinite leave of absence after the controversial incident, but instead was punished by not winning the tournament. That accomplishment went to Austin who defeated Jake Roberts to win the event, followed by him proceeding to give the legendary and revolutionary Austin 3:16 promo in a post-match interview.

The following week, fans started bringing "Austin 3:16" posters to events signifying the impact it had among WWE fans which led to Steve Austin's trajectory as the next top star to take over the WWE landscape. All of that seemed unimaginable back 30 years ago. This historical moment demonstrates how unexpected circumstances can reshape the entire trajectory of a wrestling company and launch new eras.

This caused Haas to get distracted and lose by count-out. WWE wanted to keep building the character, and Rico knew he needed another memorable moment to keep fans talking.

In the match, Rikishi was in line for the Tag Team Championship against Charlie Haas and Rico. When Rikishi put Charlie in the corner for the Stinkface, Rico jumped in and dragged Haas out of the ring, taking the move himself. 🔥 WWE house show expansion and scheduling

WWE is ramping up its live event schedule again this summer, marking a significant shift from the company's previous strategy under the Endeavor era. One of the biggest benefits discussed was giving talent more in-ring reps outside of television. This experimental environment enables talent and producers to work through creative ideas with different crowds across the country before they potentially make their way to televised programming.

There is also said to be a strong emphasis on developing younger talent through these additional live events. By pairing developing wrestlers with proven performers, WWE aims to accelerate the growth of its younger roster while maintaining the quality of the shows. WWE management also stressed the importance of finding the right balance with scheduling in order to avoid increasing the risk of injuries for talent, recognizing the dangers of overworking wrestlers.

When Endeavor acquired WWE back in 2023, one of the immediate changes made under the new regime was scaling back the company's house show schedule. DOES WWE HAVE "BLUE DOT FEVER" FROM DECLINED ATTENDANCE VERSUS HIGHER PRICES?

If you've been following the music industry lately, you may have heard about "blue dot fever," a phrase describing what happens when there are so many unsold tickets on Ticketmaster (shown as blue dots for open seats for sale) for a particular artist that tours get cancelled. Artists then release statements about "working on a new album," "spending time with family," or other excuses to cover up poor ticket sales. Notably, these artists never acknowledge that higher ticket prices might be driving away consumers. They refuse to bite the hand that feeds them—the Live Nation corporation, which manages most top musical acts while owning Ticketmaster. This company was recently found to be in violation of anti-trust laws for its strong-armed control over larger arenas, and many argue it engages in price gouging with artist approval.

The simple law of economics explains what's happening. The Law of Demand states that price and quantity demanded have an inverse relationship: when prices go up, quantity demanded should go down. Several determinants of demand—including income, tastes and preferences, confidence about the economy, and prices of related goods—determine whether consumers will pay more. Yet current economic conditions suggest they won't. Gas prices hover around $4.56 per gallon according to GasBuddy.com. Both CPI and PPI inflation numbers from April 2026 were significantly high, piling on top of inflation from 2022-2023 that never truly dissipated following the excessive printing and borrowing of money in response to the 2020 COVID-19 recession. Real, inflation-adjusted incomes haven't kept pace for years. Additionally, 2025 and 2026 have seen companies scaling back jobs due to automation and artificial intelligence implementation.

The music industry provides clear examples of this pricing problem. Post Malone had to cancel multiple shows to "devote more time to his next album," yet Ticketmaster seating charts showed unsold seats everywhere. Jennifer Lopez and Meghan Trainor struggle with sales despite their names. Meanwhile, Post Malone and Jelly Roll, both well-liked with crossover appeal, should theoretically sell well—but they don't. The formula is simple: lack of quality music combined with lack of good recent releases equals low sales in 2025-2026. Prices are simply too elevated.

This phenomenon has already impacted other sports for years. MLB, NHL, NBA, and UFC events all saw prices rise excessively while quality remained stagnant or declined—not enough to justify the higher costs. Compare this to the fast food industry. Wendy's and McDonald's are getting crushed financially right now with massive same-store sales declines, specifically because their mediocre fast food doesn't taste good enough to justify higher prices. The economics are undeniable.

For TKO and WWE specifically, the concern is real. While executives prioritize maintaining the $546.2 million net income earned during 2025, they tell Triple H to release wrestlers rather than give away pennies. Yet if attendance continues declining and ticket sales plummet—if blue dots start appearing on those arena seating charts—the real problem won't be external economic conditions alone. It will be that the product, at the price point being charged, no longer feels worth the cost to consumers already struggling with gas prices, job losses, and eroded purchasing power.

THE PSYCHOLOGY OF THE NEAR FALL: HOW TIMING AND SILENCE BUILD WRESTLING DRAMA

A near fall works because everyone in the building agrees to hold their breath for the same three seconds. The wrestler on top hooks the leg, the referee drops down, and the crowd starts counting before the hand even hits the mat. Wrestling drama depends on timing more than noise. A kickout at one feels routine. A kickout after two and a half makes people lean forward, because the finish suddenly feels possible.

The pause matters because it gives the audience time to process what they're watching. When those critical three seconds stretch out, the emotional weight increases exponentially. The best near falls usually share several key details: the move fits the story being told, the cover looks tight, the referee's count feels clean, the crowd already believes the match could end at any moment, and the kickout comes late enough to sting. When those pieces align, the audience reacts before thinking consciously about what they saw.

A small shoulder lift can feel bigger than another high-impact move simply because of how the near fall is constructed. The pause gives the crowd time to choose a side again. In that split second, even a quiet viewer decides whether they want the match to end or if they need it to survive one more turn. Silence can hit harder than a chant. A loud crowd helps, but silence can do more damage in the right spot. After a finisher, there is often a tiny drop in sound as people stop chanting because they are watching the count. That quiet second gives the producer room to cut close to the faces. The pinned wrestler looks finished. The opponent looks desperate. The referee's hand becomes the main object in the frame.

Good wrestling storytelling does not require constant explanation. It trusts the audience to remember the injury, the missed chance, or the move that already failed once. A kickout needs a reason. A late kickout lands better when it says something about the wrestler—maybe pride keeps them alive, maybe the rival waited too long before covering, or maybe the earlier damage made the move weaker than usual. The same principle applies to any structured entertainment: clear structure reduces confusion and builds comprehension.

A near fall should never feel random. It should feel earned, painful, and close enough that the next one might truly end it. When wrestlers understand this psychology, they create moments that transcend the physical performance and tap into something deeper in the audience's collective consciousness—the fear that someone they care about might not get up.

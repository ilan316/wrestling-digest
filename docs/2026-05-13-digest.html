THUNDER ROSA RANKS WRESTLING'S BEST FACE PAINTERS

During an interview with Under the Mat, AEW Women's World Champion Thunder Rosa discussed the art of face painting in professional wrestling and her own history with the practice. Rosa explained that a promoter initially suggested she paint her face to attract more fans as Dia De Los Muertos gained popularity. She only wore it at that specific promotion initially, but when she traveled to Japan and struggled to connect with audiences, she resumed the practice. She has been painting her face consistently since 2015.

When asked to rank her top five face painters with no particular order, Rosa immediately named Darby Allin and Sting before attempting to include herself. Host Simon Miller corrected her, noting she could not pick herself. Miller then suggested Legion of Doom, asking whether she preferred Animal or Hawk's face paint. Rosa chose Animal's design.

When Miller mentioned Jeff Hardy as another great face painter, Rosa enthusiastically agreed. "He's very artistic and I love the fact that he always paints his face very differently based on what he sees," she noted, highlighting Hardy's ability to evolve his designs based on creative inspiration rather than relying on repetitive patterns.

MVP QUESTIONS TRIPLE H'S ABILITY TO ELEVATE TALENT

MVP, now an AEW star, has reignited his ongoing conflict with WWE Chief Content Officer Paul "Triple H" Levesque by questioning how many wrestlers the WWE executive actually helped elevate during his in-ring career. During an appearance on the Marking Out podcast, MVP compared Triple H's legacy to that of The Undertaker, sparking significant discussion about their respective contributions to developing future talent.

MVP argued that Undertaker consistently helped wrestlers get over across multiple generations in ways Triple H did not. "You can go down the list and think of talents that Taker worked with that he elevated," MVP said. "I'll give you Randy. I'll give you Batista. Who else can you remember Triple Nose getting over?" He further elaborated by noting that Undertaker worked with and elevated difficult cases like Giant Gonzalez and Mr. Kennedy, making them legitimate stars.

MVP pointed to Jeff Hardy as a specific example of someone he believes never received full credit for defeating Triple H. According to MVP, Triple H protected his own image even in defeat rather than fully selling Hardy's victory. "Jeff Hardy — I remember the finish," MVP explained. "He sat up smiling like, 'Gosh, golly, he got me,' instead of looking hurt."

MVP acknowledged Triple H's success as a top attraction during WWE's boom periods, stating both men drew significant money and held important positions. However, he questioned overall value when comparing the scope of their contributions. "If Undertaker made — let's just say for the sake of our conversation, through the course of his career, 10 other guys. And 'The Nose' got two or three other guys over that he elevated them. In the big scheme of things, the big picture, who was more valuable for the company?"

MVP based his criticism on firsthand experience working around Triple H backstage, suggesting that helping create future stars matters as much as financial drawing power at the top of the card. The latest remarks continue a pattern of public criticism from MVP regarding WWE and Triple H, with the AEW performer making clear his issues extend beyond corporate philosophy into wrestling legacy itself.

The professional wrestling community is mourning the loss of Barry Blaustein, the visionary filmmaker behind one of the industry's most celebrated documentaries. He notably wrote for Eddie Murphy on Saturday Night Live and co-wrote the film "Coming to America" alongside David Sheffield.

Despite his success in comedy and mainstream entertainment, Blaustein called his wrestling documentary "Beyond the Mat" his favorite thing he had ever done. Released in 1999 during the height of the Attitude Era, the groundbreaking documentary served as both a writer, director, producer, and narrator project for Blaustein. The film presented an unfiltered and humanizing look at professional wrestling, blending humor with a genuine exploration of the physical and emotional toll the industry takes on its performers.

"Beyond the Mat" focused on the lives and careers of several larger-than-life personalities including Mick Foley, Terry Funk, Jake "The Snake" Roberts, and New Jack. The documentary showcased Blaustein's ability to approach the wrestling business with both critical eye and genuine affection for the art form. He pulled all advertising for the film from WWF broadcasts and threatened legal action to prevent it from ever being released to the public. McMahon allegedly instructed WWE wrestlers not to speak about the movie publicly.

Despite these efforts to suppress the film, "Beyond the Mat" became a critical and commercial success. The documentary earned praise from numerous wrestlers in the industry, including Roddy Piper and Hulk Hogan. The film's unflinching examination of wrestling resonated with audiences, and it ultimately earned a spot as a finalist for the Academy Award for Best Documentary, finishing among the final twelve selections, though it did not receive a nomination.

The tagline "The Movie Vince McMahon Didn't Want You to See" became a defining characteristic of the film's marketing and legacy. Rather than diminishing the documentary's appeal, McMahon's opposition to the project inadvertently boosted its cultural cachet and ensured its place in wrestling history.

According to Swerve, his generation had to constantly hustle just to get noticed, whether it meant sending DVDs to promoters, emailing companies, or traveling endlessly across wrestling scenes trying to improve and gain exposure.

"Wrestling now is a different world than when I was coming up. I still had to get DVDs and shop my stuff, send emails to promoters and all that. Now it's like you can go viral and be on TV or getting a contract just from going viral. I think everybody's getting contracts before they master their craft first. And that's killing their growth," Strickland stated.

The former AEW World Champion then explained why he still believes struggling through the independent grind is critical for long-term success in wrestling. He emphasized that wrestlers need to develop their skills before chasing audience metrics and social media validation.

"Before you get the audience—the likes, the clicks, the retweets—you've got to grind it out first. You've got to hone in on your craft first. You've got to suffer. Because as quick as you get it is as quick as you'll lose it if you're not good enough and experienced enough to keep carrying it. So master the craft first," Strickland explained.

He also became nostalgic while discussing the independent wrestling era that helped launch stars like Bryan Danielson, Kenny Omega, Penta, Will Ospreay, Mustache Mountain, GUNTHER, and Ricochet. According to Swerve, that era felt different because so many future stars were simultaneously pushing each other across packed indie cards in smaller venues.

"I miss seeing a Bryan Danielson being the indie star, the Kenny Omegas being indie stars. I miss seeing the Lucha Underground guys, Penta being a star on the indies. Man, that era was so tough and so competitive, but we were all working together at the same time. Will Ospreay was coming from the UK and we were like, 'Oh, that's our UK brother.' Then Mustache Mountain was coming over, and then Gunther finally came over, and we were just trading guys," Strickland recalled.

He continued, "The indies were healthy. The best wrestling you were seeing wasn't in stadiums—it was in these little clubs, because it was all of us at the same time. We were all rock stars in that era. Ricochet was on the card and you're like, 'Oh, he's on the card—I'm going to this indie show,' and it's 50 bucks around the corner. That kind of is dying now—the indie star isn't really having that anymore."

Strickland's perspective highlights a fundamental shift in how the wrestling industry operates. The rise of social media and viral content has created new pathways to prominence that bypass the traditional independent wrestling circuit. While this democratization of opportunity might seem beneficial on the surface, Strickland argues it comes at a cost to the actual development and sustainability of performers' careers.

The bottom line of Strickland's argument is that modern wrestling has become too focused on viral popularity and instant success, while the long grind that once created elite-level performers is slowly disappearing. His commentary raises broader questions about whether the current model, driven by algorithms and social media trends, is ultimately beneficial or detrimental to the long-term health of the industry and the careers of individual wrestlers who rise too quickly without proper foundational training.

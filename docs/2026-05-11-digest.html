AEW Jazwares Layoffs

AEW's action figure future suddenly looks very shaky because the entire AEW team at Jazwares was laid off Friday, and fans are now bracing for the possibility that the company's wrestling figure line is finished.

Wrestling figure reporter and collector Kyle Peterson reported late Friday that the full AEW division connected to Jazwares was let go as part of company cuts. Peterson called the situation the "worst news" possible for fans of the AEW Jazwares line.

In his report, Peterson noted that corporate layoffs typically happen on Fridays, comparing the situation to WWE-related job cuts fans have seen before. However, he stressed that the real damage affects the employees who suddenly lost their jobs. "Very unfortunate news for us fans of AEW Jazwares, but even worse news for all those designers and that team over there at Jazwares having their income basically disappear overnight," Peterson said.

For months, collectors have questioned whether the AEW figure line would continue, especially as rumors circulated about negotiations, slowing releases, and uncertainty surrounding the license itself. Peterson admitted he originally believed both sides were still trying to work something out behind the scenes.

"We've talked about it at length—ad nauseam, dare I say—here on the channel about Jazwares and AEW. Where does the license go? Are they still negotiating?" Peterson asked.

But after hearing the entire team was reportedly removed, Peterson said the answer suddenly became obvious to him. He then flat-out suggested the AEW Jazwares relationship is essentially over. "Well, by eliminating that entire team at Jazwares, I think the answer is out there. Jazwares' AEW line is officially done—or for all intents and purposes, the relationship is done. If you let go of the team at Jazwares, there's nobody to work on it anymore. So, it's all but over at this point," Peterson explained.

At this stage, neither AEW nor Jazwares has officially confirmed the reported layoffs or the status of the toy license publicly. If Peterson's information is accurate, one of AEW's biggest merchandise partnerships may have quietly collapsed behind the scenes. For collectors who have followed the line since AEW's launch years ago, this could end up being a major blow to the company's memorabilia business.

🔥 Asuka's WWE Status and Semi-Retirement

ASUKA'S WWE STATUS REMAINS UNCLEAR FOLLOWING EMOTIONAL BACKLASH LOSS

Following the conclusion of WWE Backlash on May 9, 2026, significant speculation erupted regarding the future status of Asuka after her loss to IYO SKY. The match itself was historic, marking the first time two Japanese wrestlers had competed in a singles match on a WWE premium live event. After the match concluded with SKY's victory, Asuka and her former mentee shared an emotional embrace, with both appearing tearful. Asuka hugged and kissed SKY, raised her hand in respect, bowed to her, and waved to the fans before exiting the ring. The moment was widely interpreted by observers as a potential passing of the torch or farewell.

The emotional nature of the post-match segment immediately triggered speculation across social media and wrestling media outlets about whether this marked the end of Asuka's in-ring career. Additional fuel was added to this speculation when Asuka had posted to her Instagram story before the match a pensive photo of herself with Frank Sinatra's "My Way" playing in the background, with lyrics about facing "the final curtain." This selection of music and imagery contributed significantly to the narrative that something final was occurring.

Dave Meltzer reported on the post-WWE Backlash edition of Wrestling Observer Radio that he had been told Asuka is now "semi-retired." However, Meltzer openly acknowledged that he has not received clear details on what semi-retired means in Asuka's specific situation. Meltzer stated: "So, the story I heard is that she is semi-retired. I haven't really gotten an exact meaning of what semi-retired means, but I guess people were wishing her goodbye and things like that backstage. So I don't know if that means she's going to Japan and still wrestle. I don't know if that means she's going to still do stuff here and there, but I was told semi-retired." Bryan Alvarez responded to this report by saying "That sucks, I'm going to miss Asuka." Meltzer and Alvarez also speculated about the possibility that Asuka may have been part of recent WWE roster cuts, though neither had confirmed information on this matter.

Despite the semi-retirement report, multiple sources indicate significant internal confusion within WWE regarding Asuka's actual status. According to Fightful Select's reporting, Asuka's situation appeared to be a mystery to much of the locker room and to numerous people involved in the creative process. Importantly, everyone that was spoken to by Fightful Select expressed a firm belief that Asuka has not had her final match. One WWE higher-up described Asuka as "always reliable no matter what was thrown her way," praising her work and reliability over the past year. The report noted that WWE has been extremely happy with her performances.

Adding to the uncertainty, Asuka signed a new long-term WWE contract during the summer of 2024. According to Fightful, while the exact length of the deal remains unknown, most WWE contracts signed during that period were believed to be approximately five years in length. This substantial contract commitment would seemingly contradict a complete retirement or separation from the company. The contract signing occurred well before the Backlash match and the subsequent semi-retirement reports, suggesting WWE had plans for Asuka's future at that time.

Multiple WWE wrestlers posted tributes to Asuka on social media following Backlash, which added to the confusion about her status since no official WWE statement had been made explaining the reason for these farewell-type messages. AJ Styles wrote that he "doesn't know the facts," but knows Asuka will be a Hall of Famer. Michin posted "We love Asuka around here." Bayley wrote "No matter what, where, or when…Iyo Sky and Asuka are two of the rarest wrestlers in the entire world." When Charlotte Flair was asked what her tribute post meant, she simply responded "just a fan!!!" Nattie noted that Iyo and Asuka had "such an incredible match tonight. You can feel their passion and how both are true leaders in the ring wanting to GIVE the most to each other." Rhea Ripley commented "Iyo Sky vs Asuka Phenomenal! Best of the best! You did it @Iyo_SkyWWE Proud of you!" Ash By Elegance wrote "Blessed to have been Asuka's first match in WWE! Honored… Legend!"

Big E, during the official Backlash post-show, expressed his perspective on the situation. He stated: "I don't know if this is a goodbye. I haven't really seen Asuka this emotional. If this is goodbye, I know she will be dearly missed. She has been a pioneer; she has done truly incredible things in the ring before she even got to WWE but has further staked her claim as an all-time great here in WWE. I don't know what this means, but shout out to IYO SKY for another incredible performance. She's someone I expect to be in that World Title picture again very, very soon."

IYO SKY issued a public statement following the match in both Japanese and English. She wrote: "Our story comes to its final chapter with today's match. The moment the match ended, my heart was filled with all sorts of emotions. Meeting ASUKA-san, Spending the same time together, Being able to learn so much up close— These are irreplaceable treasures to me. Thank you so much ASUKA-San We love you ASUKA-San." SKY also tagged Kairi Sane in the post, acknowledging that Sane had been a key individual in their storyline before her exit from the company in the latest round of WWE cuts.

The release of Kairi Sane appears to have frustrated some within WWE. Reports indicate that there was disappointment among some backstage personnel regarding the amount of television time invested into the pairing of Asuka and Kairi Sane without ultimately delivering a satisfactory payoff to Kairi's side of the storyline. This frustration may have also contributed to the unclear nature of Asuka's current status, as the Kabuki Warriors storyline involving Asuka displaying a domineering attitude toward Sane was building toward a larger payoff that never materialized due to Sane's release.

Asuka's career achievements in WWE are substantial. She has held the NXT Women's Championship once, with a record-breaking 510-day reign. On the main roster, she has been a four-time women's champion across Raw and SmackDown. She has held the WWE Women's Tag Team Championship five times, including three reigns with Kairi Sane, one with Charlotte Flair, and one with Alexa Bliss. She won the Women's Money in the Bank in 2020 and won the inaugural Women's Royal Rumble in 2017. Her match against Ember Moon at NXT TakeOver: Brooklyn III was rated four and a half stars by Dave Meltzer and is considered one of her best performances. Other highly-rated matches include her TLC match with Becky Lynch and Charlotte Flair at TLC 2018, the Women's WarGames match at Survivor Series 2023, and a match against Rhea Ripley from the September 22, 2025 edition of WWE Raw.

Asuka's wrestling journey began long before WWE. She debuted in the promotion AtoZ in Japan in June 2004 but was forced to retire in March 2006 due to chronic nephritis. Wrestling History and Legacy Retrospectives

There's a reason people who don't even watch wrestling know who Stone Cold Steve Austin is. Or why a match from 1987 still gets referenced in think pieces today. The best feuds in pro wrestling aren't just about two guys fighting — they're about conflict, identity, and the kind of storytelling that gets under your skin whether you want it to or not. The greatest rivalries in wrestling history demonstrate how the sport transcends athletic competition to become genuine art.

Randy Savage and Ricky Steamboat essentially invented what a truly great wrestling match could look like before anyone had figured it out. The setup was simple and vicious: Savage drove Steamboat's throat into the guardrail, then dropped a ring bell onto his larynx from the top rope, putting him out of action. From that moment on, the crowd wanted revenge in the worst way. What they got at WrestleMania III was something revolutionary. In front of 93,173 fans packed into the Pontiac Silverdome in Michigan, the two wrestlers delivered 14 minutes and 35 seconds of pure, breathless wrestling, complete with 22 pin attempts and almost no wasted movement. Steamboat won the Intercontinental title and stole the show from a card headlined by Hogan and Andre the Giant. Savage himself later said that everywhere he went, fans brought up this match above all others. Thirty-plus years later, they still do.

The Sami Zayn and Kevin Owens feud represents something different — a rivalry that never ends but keeps finding new chapters. Most feuds have a beginning and an end, but this one has spanned two decades across multiple promotions. Zayn and Owens first fought each other in 2002 in a small Canadian indie promotion, and went on to tear apart various wrestling organizations across the next two decades. What makes it work is that it's never just about the belt or the spot on the card — it's about two people who genuinely know each other too well. The betrayals hit harder because the friendship was real. The reunions feel earned because audiences have watched them earn it. By the time they stumbled into an unlikely tag team championship run together, partly as a result of Zayn's absurd infiltration of The Bloodline, the whole thing had taken on a dimension no writer could have planned. No modern feud has more history or more heart.

Edge and John Cena didn't like each other, and it showed in ways that elevated their feud beyond standard good-versus-evil storytelling. Edge was the guy who would cash in a Money in the Bank briefcase at 2 in the morning on a wounded champion, while Cena was the company's straight-laced golden boy who couldn't be bought or bent. The contrast was perfect. What elevated this beyond predictability was that Edge was genuinely compelling as the villain, not just cartoonishly evil. He was smart, petty, and opportunistic in ways that felt real. Their TLC matches were some of the most brutal and athletic spectacles of the era, and the feud had enough twists and title changes to sustain multiple years without feeling tired. It's a rivalry that holds up much better than people give it credit for.

Before WWE dominated everything, American wrestling was a patchwork of regional territories that produced some of the most sustained, genuinely emotional feuds the business has ever seen. The crown jewel of that era was Ric Flair against Dusty Rhodes. The contrast couldn't have been sharper. Flair was all private jets, designer robes, and championship gold — a man born to make you despise him. Dusty was the American Dream, a heavyset guy from the Carolinas who talked and fought for everyday working people. It was class warfare turned into professional wrestling, and arenas sold out for it night after night for over a decade. This feud is the reason the NWA still means something to people who weren't even alive to see it.

John Cena versus CM Punk represented another watershed moment for wrestling storytelling. On June 27, 2011, CM Punk sat cross-legged at the top of the entrance ramp and delivered what became known as the Pipe Bomb, a promo that blurred the line between scripted performance and reality in ways that captivated audiences and changed how wrestling approached narrative.

Wight described having a large bandage on his nose with sinuses packed with gauze, and doctors instructed him to avoid bending over or straining. Although the surgery likely saved his life, the effects of acromegaly have continued to haunt Wight decades later. The disorder enlarges bones, joints, organs, and other parts of the body over time, creating enormous long-term physical strain. According to Wight, people with untreated acromegaly typically do not survive past their late 40s as the growth hormone enlarges the heart, fuses joints by overgrowth of bones, and causes organs like the liver and intestines to overgrow.

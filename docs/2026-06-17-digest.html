🔥 Kris Statlander's bed of nails scars tattoo

Kris Statlander found a unique way to commemorate one of the most brutal matches of her AEW career. Back in November 2025, Statlander competed in the first-ever Women's Blood & Guts match in AEW history. While her team came up short in the bout, the former TBS Champion and former AEW Women's Champion made sure the experience left a lasting mark, literally.

The match featured some of the company's biggest names and earned a reputation as one of the most brutal women's matches AEW had ever presented. Mercedes Mone, Marina Shafir, Megan Bayne, and the Triangle of Madness defeated Willow Nightingale, Kris Statlander, Jamie Hayter, Toni Storm, Mina Shirakawa, and Harley Cameron. During the match, Statlander would take a devastating bodyslam onto a bed of nails at the hands of Marina Shafir outside of the ring, one of several brutal spots that characterized the competition. Multiple wrestlers left the event with injuries following their matches.

Speaking with the Daily Star in a recent interview, Statlander revealed that she had the scars from the nail bed spot tattooed onto her back in red ink, creating the appearance that the wounds are still fresh. She explained her reasoning and the humorous aftermath. "My scars that I got from taking the bed of nails at Blood & Guts, I got those scars tattooed on my back in red so they always look fresh," Statlander said. "Everytime I go to the medical room to get my back worked on or something, they see my [tattoo], they always think it's a fresh cut and they're like, 'I can't work on your back.' Then they're like, 'Oh, wait, no, it's a tattoo.'"

The tattoo has become a running joke backstage, with medical staff frequently doing a double take before realizing the marks aren't new injuries. For many wrestlers, scars represent sacrifice and dedication to their craft. Statlander's tattoo does exactly that while also giving everyone backstage a good laugh, turning one of the most painful moments of her career into a permanent tribute that honors her accomplishment in the historic match.

CM Punk's AEW tenure discussion

Matt Hardy has offered his perspective on the power dynamics between CM Punk and Tony Khan during Punk's controversial run in AEW. The current TNA star recently discussed the topic on The Extreme Life of Matt Hardy podcast, providing insights into what he believes was happening behind the scenes at the Jacksonville-based promotion.

According to Hardy, Punk recognized early on that he had Tony Khan's trust and confidence, which gave him considerable leverage within the company. Hardy suggested that Punk used this awareness to his advantage when situations called for it, stating that Punk could "kind of get away with what he wanted" if he was determined enough about something. Hardy elaborated on this dynamic by explaining that Punk could sense Khan's relative inexperience running a wrestling company for decades, which allowed Punk to understand exactly how much influence he possessed.

However, Hardy was careful to clarify that he did not believe Punk manipulated every situation or took advantage of Khan constantly. Rather, Hardy felt that Punk was simply aware of his leverage and occasionally flexed that power when it benefited him to do so. This understanding of his position within the company seemingly allowed Punk to operate with a certain degree of freedom that may not have been available to other talent on the AEW roster.

Hardy drew a stark contrast between Punk's behavior in AEW and his recent return to WWE. According to Hardy, Punk clearly understood that he could not operate under the same mindset in WWE that he had employed in AEW. Many fans and industry observers initially expected Punk's WWE return to be marked by controversy or conflict given his history, but Hardy noted that this did not occur. Instead, Punk adopted a markedly different approach, becoming what Hardy described as "a by-the-book guy" and operating as "a model company employee."

For Hardy, the fundamental difference between Punk's two runs comes down to the environments and leadership he encountered. In AEW, Hardy believes Punk sensed an opportunity to leverage his position and occasionally took advantage of it. In WWE, Punk apparently recognized the different corporate structure and expectations, and accordingly adjusted his behavior to comply with company standards rather than test boundaries.

Hardy spoke positively about Punk's WWE conduct, suggesting that it is difficult to dispute the professionalism Punk has displayed since his return to the company. This observation suggests that Punk is capable of adapting his approach based on the organizational culture and power structure he encounters, demonstrating an awareness of how different companies operate and what each environment will tolerate.

Jack Perry is currently negotiating a new deal with AEW, according to Bryan Alvarez reporting on Wrestling Observer Radio. Throughout his time with AEW, Perry has established himself as a significant figure in the promotion and held multiple championships.

Perry's championship accomplishments in AEW are extensive. He won the AEW World Tag Team Championship alongside Luchasaurus in January 2022, holding the gold for 161 days. WWE King Of The Ring Creator Eddie Andelman Has Passed Away

Eddie Andelman, the legendary Boston sports radio personality credited with creating the concept behind WWE's King of the Ring tournament, has passed away at the age of 89. News of Andelman's passing was announced on Monday by his sons, Dave, Mike, and Dan.

While Andelman was primarily known for his groundbreaking work in sports radio, he also left a lasting mark on professional wrestling history through his relationship with Vince McMahon and WWE. Andelman partnered with WWE to launch the original King of the Ring tournament in 1985. The first two events, held in 1985 and 1986, took place at Sullivan Stadium in Foxborough, Massachusetts, where Andelman owned a portion of the surrounding land.

The tournament was originally conceived as a charity event rather than a wrestling spectacle. Working alongside WWE, Andelman helped raise funds for organizations supporting children affected by serious illnesses, including the Joey Fund and the Jimmy Fund, which benefit those battling cystic fibrosis and pediatric cancer. In a 2022 appearance on The Lapsed Fan podcast, Andelman reflected on the origins of the tournament and explained how the idea came together. "I came up with it because I like to do original things. Vince was into it, and the wrestlers I might add were really into it." According to Andelman, the inaugural event proved to be a major success, raising more than $100,000 for charity.

King of the Ring initially existed as a non-televised live event before eventually evolving into one of WWE's signature premium live event concepts. The first two winners of the tournament were Don Muraco in 1985 and Harley Race in 1986.

Beyond WWE, Andelman also worked with the American Wrestling Association on the 1985 Wrestling for a Cure charity event, further demonstrating his commitment to using professional wrestling as a vehicle for charitable fundraising.

Outside of wrestling, Andelman became one of the most influential voices in Boston sports media. He was instrumental in helping establish the sports talk radio format in the market through his work with WEEI and was later inducted into the Massachusetts Broadcasters Hall of Fame.

Following the news of Andelman's passing, wrestling journalist Dave Meltzer paid tribute on social media and highlighted his important contribution to wrestling history, expressing hope that WWE would acknowledge Andelman as the person who came up with the idea of King of the Ring.

Although Andelman never worked directly in the wrestling business, his influence continues to be felt today through King of the Ring, a tournament that remains one of WWE's most recognizable and enduring traditions.
